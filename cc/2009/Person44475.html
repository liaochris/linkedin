James C. Ayers, Jr.’s Summary

Mr. Ayers is experienced in handling a wide range of litigation matters, and strives to apply his experience to help individuals, families, and small businesses faced with a legal crisis. Over the course of his career, Mr. Ayers has successfully litigated over 1000 cases, has tried over 150 cases to verdict, and has handled 20 or more appellate matters including writing the briefs for a landmark U.S. Supreme Court appeal which changed the law of arbitration. Mr. Ayers has represented clients in over 100 venues across Alabama and Tennessee. He has lectured to various professional groups and published materials in the areas of personal injury trial and settlement, trial evidence, expert witnesses, trial practice and presentation, arbitration, and employment law. He is a certified arbitrator for the Association of the Better Business Bureau, and a graduate of the National Institute for Trial Advocacy.

James C. Ayers, Jr.’s Education

  • The University of Alabama School of Law

    JD, cum laude , 1990 — 1993

    Mr. Ayers obtained his J.D., cum laude, from The University of Alabama School of Law in 1993. While in law school he served as a Senior Editor of the Alabama Law Review, was accepted into the Bench and Bar Legal Honor Society, served as Treasurer for the International Law Society, and worked as a research assistant to Professor, now Supreme Court Justice, Harold See. Member Phi Delta Phi.

    Activities and Societies:
    Alabama Law Review; Bench and Bar Legal Honor Society.

  • The Ohio State University

    B.S., cum laude , Economics , 1985 — 1990

    Mr. Ayers received his B.S., Economics, cum laude, from The Ohio State University Honors College in 1990. While in college he was selected as a scholar for the Mershon Center for International Studies, received the Summa Academic Achievement Award, and was placed on the President's List. During his Junior and Senior years he was employed by the Ohio House of Representatives where he worked to help constituents who had questions or problems with state agencies.
