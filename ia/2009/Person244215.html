Rama Mavuduru’s Summary

I am passionate about taking software product ideas from vision to reality fast. It is hard to predict what will win in the marketplace. I believe in doing rapid low cost experiments to discover the path to delightful products. I win only if the customer wins.

At Intuit I play a key role in developing platform technology for the next generation products. I invented the 'MDA Toolkit', tools and developer workflow to generate source code from domain models. I also developed a reference application to showcase the architecture and best practices. I work with product teams to evangelize and on-board the new platform technology.

My life goal is to build and nurture an 'innovation factory' - a small team of highly engaged individuals that create delightful software products.

Rama Mavuduru’s Experience

  • Intuit

    (Public Company; Computer Software industry)

    March 2005 — Present (3 years 11 months)

    I play a key role in developing the new technology platform that enables a SaaS business model for future product offerings.

    I invented ‘MDA-Toolkit’ – developer tools and process for model driven development.

    I developed a reference application to showcase the architecture and development best-practices.

    I evangelize the new platform through presentations, training sessions, and 1-1 discussions.

    Early in my tenure at Intuit I was also part of the team that moved QuickBooks from a flat-file storage system to a RDBMS.

  • Stentor

    (Privately Held; 1001-5000 employees; Telecommunications industry)

    November 2003 — December 2004 (1 year 2 months)

    I contributed to the development of the next-generation patient information and image management system that Stentor was building in collaboration with Medicalis of Canada.

    We were a bunch of smart committed people. Yet we were unsuccessful in delivering a quality product on time. I learnt the value of leadership, management, communication, and relationships. I took some hard life lessons and walked away after a brief stint. Later Stentor got acquired by Philips Medical Systems.

  • Advent Software

    (Public Company; 501-1000 employees; ADVS; Computer Software industry)

    September 2000 — October 2003 (3 years 2 months)

    In the second half of my tenure at Advent I evolved to the role of development manager and lead developer for Moxy, trade order management product.

    I delivered 7 releases of Moxy on schedule while improving product robustness and quality.

    Over 850 money management firms in US and Europe use this product in their daily stocks and bonds trading. I worked with a product development team of 21 individuals leading the engineering efforts and developing key sections of the code.

    I also worked with management and engineers to evolve an engineering vision and roadmap for Moxy that met the near term business needs without compromising the long-term architectural integrity.

  • Advent Software

    (Public Company; 501-1000 employees; ADVS; Computer Software industry)

    September 1997 — September 2000 (3 years 1 month)

    Advent is one of my best employment experiences. Why? Two reasons: 1. I had a great boss, Max Nassiri.
    2. We delighted the customers and Advent made boat-loads of money selling the product.

    I worked on Moxy, a client-server application that runs on Windows. It is built using Visual C++ and SQL Server. It is used by investment managers to manage stock and bond trades.

    During my tenure I implemented major functionality enhancements that increased product sales and added 450 new customers.

    In 1998 I developed FIX messaging that enabled realtime messaging of stock trades between the portfolio managers and the stock exchanges. Based on this feature Advent sold Moxy to Wells Fargo Bank.

    In 2000 I developed a real-time extensible messaging capability that opened up Moxy for integration with both internal and external software applications. This capability resulted in several major business deals and the most significant deal was with SEI, the ASP leader in portfolio management.

  • XOX Corporation

    (Computer Software industry)

    January 1996 — April 1997 (1 year 4 months)

    One of my advisors at University of Minnesota introduced me to XOX. XOX's core asset was SHAPES library for geometry modelling. When I got there the company was looking to build end-user applications based on the SHAPES technology, especially in computer gaming. It also went public while I was there. The IPO was lukewarm. I drove some of the product prototypes. Financially the company was not doing well. I quit to finish my MBA and came to the bay-area in August 1997 and joined Advent.

  • Cray Research

    (Public Company; 1001-5000 employees; CRI; Computer Hardware industry)

    April 1994 — December 1995 (1 year 9 months)

    I worked on Unichem, a molecular modeling application for the supercomputers. I contributed to several features that required 3D graphics expertise.

    Cray was going downhill by the time I got there. Economy was also bad. I was happy to get a job at THE Supercomputer company. Years ago US government had denied supercomputers to India. (That's why C-DAC had started.) Now I got a chance to work at the company that made them.

    In December 1995 Silicon Graphics bought Cray and laid off the entire applications division. The lesson learnt for me was that I need to be close to the core of a company. The core of Cray was the hardware. Software was given away for free. In bad times all non-core is killed.

  • Center For Development of Advanced Computing (C-DAC)

    (Computer Software industry)

    June 1990 — January 1991 (8 months)

    Prior to my formal employment at C-DAC I worked there as a summer intern in 1988 when it first started. Later I also did my BE project work there.

    C-DAC's mission was then to build the first supercomputer for India. The people, the facilities, and the mission, were world class. I was in love with this place.

    My BE project was to port SPICE, the ciruit simulation program, to Transputer, the building block of parallel supercomputer. The bigger goal was to get several compute intensive applications running on the supercomputer. After a lot of late nights spent debugging and some help from a CDC Cyber computer expert, at TIFR Bombay, I and my project buddy got the code running on the supercomputer.

    The brief research associate role was the continuation of the BE project work to tie off the loose ends.
