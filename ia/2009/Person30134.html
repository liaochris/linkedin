Bob Brooks’s Education

  • University of Massachusetts, Amherst

    MLA , Masters in Landscape Architecture , 1978 — 1981

    I was in some pretty inspiring company at UMass, and the pace was rigorous. While there I worked at an architectural firm in Northampton and so was very much connected to the world of real-life design practice. It went to my head and I thought of myself as quite the professional, I carried an attache case rather than a backpack! I've learned a lot since then, had some successes and some that I'd love to do over, but I've run into a lot of fellow UMass alums and we are all grateful for what we were given by a dedicated group of educators there.

    Activities and Societies:
    Teaching Assistant; Stockbridge School of Agriculture

  • Boston University

    BS , Geography , 1974 — 1978

    Geography is an unusual major but it's one that prepared me perfectly for subsequent pursuits. With a foundation in global political and economic systems, transportation; as well as natural systems such as geology and meteorology, the program prepared me well for a specialty in landscape architecture, and later in environmental sustainability.

    Activities and Societies:
    Weather Station Manager

  • Franconia College

    1967 — 1969

    For a college-age person, the 1960's were a time of Haight-Ashbury and Woodstock, Jimi Hendrix and Timothy Leary. Franconia College, an experimental college in the White Mountains of New Hampshire, at times seemed more like an academic commune. The curriculum was focused around an interdisciplinary core study which examined a particular subject from many perspectives. Other than that, students designed their own program and were expected to work to support the operation of the college. My time there impacted me in ways I'm only now beginning to realize. There was a strong "Back to the Land" movement in America, with the Whole Earth Catalog, the beginnings (or revival) of organic farming and alternative energy. Sustainability was not defined yet but many of us were practicing it. Often students were living in wood-heated cabins built in the woods from used lumber and windows. Franconia College didn't last long; like a hippie-Camelot it had it's brief moment in time and then was gone.
