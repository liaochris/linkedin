Clay Wilson, Ph.D., CISSP’s Summary

Clay Wilson received his Ph.D. from George Mason University in 2001. From 2001 to 2003, as a professor at the University of Maryland University College, he provided in-person instruction to military officials at Fort Gordon, Georgia, and online to other graduate students located in multiple countries on the topics of computer security and risk analysis. He is a member of the advisory board for the Security Studies Laboratory at the University of Maryland, and has been a past member of the Virginia Commonwealth Joint Commission on Technology and Science Subcommittee on Nanotechnology for 2005.

As a specialist in Information Technology and National Security for the Congressional Research Service (CRS), he has written numerous policy reports for the U.S. Congress and explained the enabling technologies for High-Altitude Electromagnetic Pulse, Directed Energy Weapons, Electronic Warfare, Terrorist Cyber Capabilities, Improvised Explosive Devices, and Avatars and Virtual Reality. He has presented technology lectures at the U.S. National Defense University and at various international conferences on computer crime, computer security, terrorist computer skills, roadside bombs in Iraq and Afghanistan, and electronic warfare. His writings appear in numerous conference proceedings and books, including the Cyber Conflict Studies Association proceedings, and the National Defense University publication on Cyberpower.

He has interacted directly with policy makers for the U. S. federal government, and influenced committees and members in the U.S. Congress as they have confronted new challenges for international relations. His work has involved explaining how new technologies that are now interwoven into industry and the critical infrastructure may also have hidden vulnerabilities that may be attacked or misused by terrorists or international criminals.
