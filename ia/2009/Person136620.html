Lance Walley’s Summary

I started working with computers in junior high school (1982), and although I've been a software and hardware developer about 50% of the time, my interest is more on the business and organizational side of things. This leads me to start and grow businesses with partners who have greater technical vision. I started an embedded computing business with a friend in 1987, then followed with several businesses: 1) mobile phone text messaging; 2) billing systems for instant-messaging; and 3) staffing & recruiting for Perl and Ruby on Rails. I co-founded Engine Yard in 2006 with Tom Mornini, Ezra Zygmuntowicz, and Jayson Vantuyl.

Lance Walley’s Specialties:

I seem to be good at working with people and seeing things through. Also good at understanding technology with moderate depth and bridging to less technical audiences.

  • Partner

    FaceBridge Research, Inc.

    (Privately Held; 1-10 employees; Internet industry)

    June 2003 — July 2006 (3 years 2 months)

    My friend, Tom, and I went to MacWorld in 2003 and saw high-quality, easy-to-use AV transmission over regular IM networks - in this case, over AIM.

    In other words, one could plug in an Apple video camera and do video chat about as easily as text chat, and it was actually quite good quality.

    We went to dinner and realized that some % of people would want to bill for their "presence" via this kind of medium.

    We formed FaceBridge Research to develop our first prototype and to file patents on various parts of the idea.

    The system worked very well, but frankly, the world didn't seem to care :-(.

  • Product Manager

    Quios.com, Inc.

    (Privately Held; 51-200 employees; Wireless industry)

    March 1999 — March 2001 (2 years 1 month)

    Quios.com was a great experience! Marc Vanlerberghe was on the same track as me in terms of mobile information offerings, but he had a number of things I lacked :-)... Stanford grad, larger view of the world, good connections, etc.

    Marc contacted me from Belgium and we later met at Stanford. He asked if I'd come on board once he got angel/VC. He ultimately secured funding and I became his first employee. My prior software became the start of Quios.com.

    Quios needed more people, so I brought in my friends, Tom and Dan, from Sacramento and Chicago. We all moved to SF and began a very interesting journey.

    Unfortunately, Quios fell victim to the same forces that were affecting many other businesses around 2000-01. The company still exists today, but all but a few people were laid off over a 6- or 12-month period.

    I wound up doing some web consulting for Quios and a few other small customers, which kept me afloat during the early-2000s recession.

  • President/Programmer

    StayTuned (Sole Proprietorship)

    (Sole Proprietorship; Myself Only; Wireless industry)

    March 1997 — December 1998 (1 year 10 months)

    After leaving Parallax, I become interested in mobile pagers and phones. The ability to receive text messages was new in the US, and 2-way models were just on the horizon.

    I bought two servers and started learning the technologies required to serve a website that would have database and data feed connectivity.

    This all coalesced in late 1997 to form a system where a web user could pay $7.95/month to receive personalized stocks, weather, horocopes, lottery, and server status messages on his pager or cell phone.

    But, alas, I was either too early, or in the wrong country, or didn't have the marketing muscle to attract enough users - or probably all of the above! I ran out of money and folded the company.

    This negative outcome ultimately led to very positive experiences, as I was called upon to help a brand new VC-funded startup with similar aspirations in San Fran.

  • CEO

    Parallax, Inc.

    (Privately Held; 51-200 employees; Computer Hardware industry)

    September 1987 — December 1996 (9 years 4 months)

    This is the company I started with a friend when we were both 19. Parallax developed hardware and software tools to help other techies do their job... products like microprocessor programmers and emulators (hardware), and assemblers and simulators (software). We later developed a 1-square-inch "BASIC Stamp" embedded computer that really broke the price/performance/sexiness barrier in its market. For $50, one could automate all sorts of simple processes. Before the BASIC Stamp, such computers were clunky and $200.

    When I left in Dec, 1996, the tiny computers were our mainstay and were being sold around the world through distribution channels in Europe and Asia, as well as well-known US channels, such as Radio Shack and Digi-Key.
