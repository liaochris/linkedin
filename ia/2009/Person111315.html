Jessamy Kelly’s Summary

In 2006 I set up a creative venture called Juo Ltd with designer Joanne Mitchell. Juo is a contemporary fused glassware brand aimed at the design-conscious consumer and is based at the National Glass Centre, in Sunderland.

In 2009 I completed my practice based PhD (Part-Time) at the University of Sunderland. My research examines the combination of glass and ceramics in a hot state, within my studio practice as a means of artistic expression; offering a potential new model of practice for artists working in glass & ceramics, as very few artists work in both materials.

In 2002, I completed my Masters degree at Edinburgh College of Art. This involved an Industrial placement at Edinburgh Crystal as a student glass designer. The placement was in collaboration with Edinburgh College of Art, and went towards my Masters Degree in ‘Glass Product Design’. This opportunity gave me the freedom to research and develop ideas within Edinburgh Crystal’s production facilities, allowing a creative and experimental approach within the reality of a commercial environment.

Until July 2006, I worked as an in-house glass designer; I then decided to focus my practice on my studio work and own business. I occasionally work on a freelance basis with industry; this collaboration informs and influences my studio work in a unique and innovative way. I am currently artist in residence at Edinburgh College of Art.

Jessamy Kelly’s Specialties:

Design and specification of industrial glass product. Specialist Glass sector knowledge and expertise in sourcing and supplier relations. IT skills: Microsoft office, Autocad, Rhino & Photoshop. Studio skills and specialist techniques: glass blowing, casting, fusing. Cold working techniques: sandblasting, engraving and diamond cutting.

Jessamy Kelly’s Education

  • University of Sunderland

    PhD , Glass & Ceramics , 2004 — 2009

    This practice-led research investigated the feasibility of combining glass and ceramics in a hot state, as a means of artistic expression in studio practice. Glass and ceramics have many related material qualities and are processed in similar ways. Chemically they are alike however structurally they are very different, which creates compatibility problems when they are combined in a hot state. Through controlled processing, material properties have been altered to partially convert materials into each other to improve compatibility. A new material has been developed that is suitable for studio practitioners; the research offers a new model of practice which has unique practical and visual benefits.
