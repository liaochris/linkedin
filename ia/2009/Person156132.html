Matthew Burgess’s Summary

Chief Marketing & Analytics Officer and co-founder at Maverick Internet Ventures, Inc.

Over the past 4+ years, first as COO, then as CMO, I have had P/L responsibility for everything we take to market. I've been responsible for product development, site launches and optimization of customer acquisition. I've worked in close partnership with our CTO to build technology, systems, and processes to manage very large and complex SEM/PPC campaigns, and to create an innovative approach and a platform for parallel, multivariate testing of websites. The work spans across both the creative and the analytical, and I am regularly challenged, which is very important to me.

I'm blogging at Creatalytics.com - discussing this convergence of creative and analytical thinking in our work.

I am also the owner of a services firm that I founded in 2002, Cross Canyon Corporate Services, Inc., that provides high end business entity formation services.

  • Company Makers, LLC

    (Management Consulting industry)

    February 2002 — December 2002 (11 months)

    A Business Process Outsourcing provider, Company Makers provided business entity formation services as well as a suite of back office services for small to medium businesses, including corporate legal services, controller/accounting/bookkeeping functions, human resources, recruiting, web site and ecommerce implementation, public relations management, finance, capitalization strategy, investment due diligence, administrative staffing and facilities management.

    The company was founded by Robert Gryphon, a successful technologist and entrepreneur who founded Octane Software and sold it to E.piphany for $3.2 billion. He and the company were based in Incline Village, NV, and I was brought on to run the company and build the practice in Los Angeles and the Bay Area.

  • Zone Ventures / Zone Reactor

    (Venture Capital & Private Equity industry)

    September 2001 — February 2002 (6 months)

    I was Entrepreneur-in-Residence and Vice President of Intellectual Property Asset Acquisition at Zone Reactor, a technology commercialization firm created by Zone Ventures. Zone Reactor was a division set up in partnership with Draper Fisher Jurvetson to build and launch new companies from technologies under development within world-class R&D facilities such as TRW, Raytheon, and Lockheed Martin Aeronautics. As EIR, I reviewed technologies, researched and proposed product and market launch strategies, and wrote business plans.

    As VP of Intellectual Property Acquisition, I conceived and led a project to find and acquire software and related intellectual property in the wake of the Internet crash.

  • Venice Networks (SuperSig)

    (Internet industry)

    August 1999 — March 2001 (1 year 8 months)

    In 1999, I moved to LA to co-found SuperSig with Mark Jeffrey, now the CTO of Mahalo. We had a vision for applying server-managed dynamic HTML content to email. We raised several million dollars in startup capital from a group of top Silicon Valley entrepreneurs and angel groups, including Angel Investors LP, Argus Capital, Ron Conway, Bob Bozeman, Greg Gretsch, Andrew Beebe, Ariel Poler, and Thomas Layton. We began the company as a consumer play, and launched an email personalization service. Then, like a lot of early stage Internet companies in early 2000, we changed gears and re-engineered our technology and our company to provide a productivity and customer acquisition suite to businesses. We inked deals with ITXC Inc, Williams Sonoma and others.

    My work involved oversight of all operations, legal, capitalization, marketing, public relations, business strategy, and human resources.

  • Communities.com

    (Internet industry)

    June 1997 — August 1999 (2 years 3 months)

    fka Electric Communities.

    After investing in and working with Electric Communities as a portfolio company of Agincourt Partners, I was hired, first to re-write the business plan in preparation for a round of financing, then as a manager of business development and marketing strategy.

    The company was ambitiously creating the Internet’s only secure, fully-distributed, multi-user application platform. I was focused mainly on server software licensing, channel marketing, web developer outreach, and strategic alliances for subsidiaries ThePalace.com and OnLive Technologies. ThePalace was consumer, and OnLive was H.323-compliant online conferencing for the enterprise. This work melded sales with strategic marketing; everything from software demos at major conferences to negotiating license agreements, partnership contracts, and strategic alliances.

  • Agincourt Partners (fka Volpe Welty Asset Mgmt)

    (Venture Capital & Private Equity industry)

    May 1996 — June 1997 (1 year 2 months)

    After business school, I became a venture capital associate for Bill Welty at Agincourt Partners LLP, formerly a division of the investment bank Volpe Welty. My research involved cutting edge developments in network software and technologies. We published a report on the key emerging technologies in the then-nascent segment of online communities.

    I was fortunate to learn under Bill Welty. He was a tough boss who would not tolerate intellectual laziness, and encouraged a broad-based, whole thinking view. As part of my efforts to understand technology, business, and the qualities that drive success, regular reading topics included evolutionary biology, bionomics (economy as a self organizing ecosystem), chaos theory, emergence (complex systems arising out of simple rulesets and interactions), complexity theory, string theory, and even a fair amount of science fiction and cyberpunk. This reading, reasearch, and the long conversations with Bill, helped shape my thinking to this day.

  • BU MBA Applied Securities Analysis Program

    (Educational Institution; Higher Education industry)

    1995 — 1996 (1 year)

    While in the MBA program at BU, I founded a new program to give students an opportunity to manage a fund of capital as portfolio managers. Conceived as an advanced course coupled with an actual fund, I wrote fund raising proposals, worked with the Dean to choose a professor and to raise capital, and build a syllabus.

    In 1996, the program launched. Within a few years, it had become the most popular course in the MBA program, and was transformed into an entire degree program: MS in Investment Management.
