Nicole Herbots, i.r., PhD UCL’s Summary

Nicole Herbots and her students research and model low temperature (T<480 K) methods of synthesis and characterization of new semiconductor nanophases such as sub-nanometer ordered silicon dioxides (US Patent 6,613,677, Herbots et al.), SiGeO2 (US patent 5,124,421, Herbots et al.), SiGe, SiGeC, SiGeC oxides, and GaAs Oxides. The synthesis methods used include CIMD (US Patent 4,800,200, N. Herbots et al.) which combines molecular beams, ion beam deposition, and IBO (Ion Beam Oxidation), and new low temperature surface processing techniques such as the Herbots-Atluri Clean, the EpOX™ process, EpOxNOx™ and SilOxSi(S)™to produce either templates for (hetero) epitaxy in her clean-room laboratory, of "nanostacks" of ultrathin films to create high performance gate oxides, peroskvites and photovoltaic surfaces.

Most Recent Work: Medical Physics AY 2007-08, AY 2008-09

Our most recent work (Technology Disclosures M6-060, which led to a US patent filed 4/27/2007; M8-040, filed 11/7/2007, and M9-035P filed 9/22/2008 ) is at the boundary of condensed matter physics, biology and medical electronics, with applications in medical devices implants such as integrated bio-sensors/pump implants and lenticular implants. Our latest advances are in the design of bio-compatible and hermetic organic molecular nanofilms to bond bio-sensors supporting substrates to their controllers and create thereby permanent single-device medical implants for chronic disease control such as recurrent cancers, diabetes and high blood pressure.

Most Recent Discovery

We have recently discovered that we could bond at room temperature Si(100) supporting electronic devices to a wide rane of silicate wafers using our most recently patented Langmuir-Blodgett molecular nanofilms as nucleating precursors to synthesize a bonding SilOxSi nanophase, a class of new materials which we call "interphase" to contrast with the geometrical concept of interface. We call the silicate-based interphase NanoOxides™
