Chinki Sinha’s Summary

I covered the refugee and immigrant community in Utica in addition to government and politics in Town of New Hartford, a fast-growing suburb.
Utica's demographics began changing in the 1970s when a lot of refugees from Vietnam and Cambodia came to the small factory town. A refugee resettlement agency began operating in 1979 and over the years many Bosnians, Somali Bantus, Russians, and Burmese came to the city. The refugee groups alone account for almost 15 percent of the city.
I write about the issues and challenges faced by them and also the impact on Utica's economy, mindset and culture because of such demographic changes.
Interesting stories that I have covered include the Bosnian community separating from the larger Muslim mosque and buying a former Methodist Church to convert it into a place of worship. There was a lot of negative reaction and some even went to the extent of calling the move as similar to having a terrorism training institution.
But this pointed to the Bosnian community's coming of age. Another big story was the Burmese Buddhist community's purchase of city property to build a monastery. In Utica, these are significant changes. With a population of only about 60,000 people, such diversity is fascinating and also attracts a lot of criticism.

Chinki Sinha’s Education

  • Syracuse University

    Master's in Journalism, Newspaper, Online and Magazine journalism, 2005 — 2006

    While at the university, I had a chance to do beat reporting. I reported extensively on the lesbian, gay, bisexual and transgender community and discrimination against them. One of the stories featured a transgender woman and her struggle to keep her job. It was a difficult beat because many LGBT members weren't "out" but it taught how to be sensitive to your sources and yet remain objective.
    I also covered Islam in America as a beat. It included writng about the local muslim community and their lives, especially after 9/11. A news feature on Madrasa or Islamic Schools was one of the my stories. I visited an Islamic high school in New England for the story. It certainly was not an easy story to write because for many Muslims in a post 9/11 America, talking to the media wasn't easy. They feared being protrayed negatively.
    On my beat now, which includes reporting on minority issues and refugee spot stories, gain trust of my sources and report accurately and with sensitivity.

  • Xavier Institute of Communications, St. Xavier's College, Mumbai

    Post Graduate Diploma, Journalism and Mass Communications, 2003 — 2004

    The course required a 10,000 word thesis and I chose to study the coverage of domestic violence by three leading daily newspapers in the country.
    It was definitely a learning experience as it helped me use research tools. Through the project, I not only learned how to dissect news, but how advertising and dominant ideologies play a role in news judgment.
    For example, the newspapers I studied often carried reports on domestic abuse, even dowry-related deaths, in the city briefs section, which measured only 4 or 5 inches in column space.
    They treated it as a routine item rather than a social ill that needs to be addressed.
    I also had a chance to attend P. Sainath's talk on media coverage, which broadened my perspective. Sainath makes compelling arguments using facts and statistical data and that was certainly a lesson.
    During my work at Utica O-D, I used census data for a news feature on the lack on funding for minority-owned businesses and to show how race was a determinant.
