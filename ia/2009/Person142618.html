Liz Birkholz’s Summary

I enable people to live, work and play in significantly more sustainable urban environments. I enable the vision. I get the word out.

Liz Birkholz’s Specialties:

Registered landscape architect, urban planner, public relations, communications, GIS spatial analysis, LEED® AP, public speaker; LEED-CS, LEED-EB O+M, LEED-NC, LEED-ND project certifications; sustainable site development strategies; eco-charrettes, climate change and greenhouse gas emissions reduction policy; urban development related to corporate, higher education, health care, public, bioscience and e-commerce industries; Twitter

Liz Birkholz’s Experience

  • 2010-2011 Vice President-Elect Communications

    American Society of Landscape Architects

    (Non-Profit; Non-Profit Organization Management industry)

    May 2009 — Present (4 months)

    From September 2009 through November 2011 I will support ASLA's President in communications and serve as a liaison to standing councils, committees and special study groups, task forces and representatives; monitor the management and administration of ASLA's operating plan and budget; represent and act for ASLA as directed by the President and consistent with ASLA's policies, and serve as a Director for the ASLA Fund's Board. The ASLA Fund supports ASLA research, library, archives, awards and professional education programs. It also supports the ASLA Awards program including the Firm Award, the Landmark Award, the Community Service Award and Professional Awards. At year-end 2008, ASLA membership was 17,000+ strong.

  • Chair, P.R. + Communications Advisory Committee

    American Society of Landscape Architects

    (Non-Profit; Non-Profit Organization Management industry)

    October 2008 — Present (11 months)

    The Public Relations & Communications Advisory Committee advises ASLA's Public Relations staff on media relations strategies, publicity materials and outreach activities; forming communication plans for policies and programs; developing national and chapter public relations efforts; suggesting opportunities to generate positive coverage of the profession; evaluating publicity efforts; reviewing and recommending improvements to online communications; all to better serve landscape architects, students, allied design and construction professionals, the public and media.

  • Advisor, Annual Meeting Education Advisory Committee

    American Society of Landscape Architects

    (Non-Profit; Non-Profit Organization Management industry)

    October 2006 — Present (2 years 11 months)

    I make recommendations on keynote speakers and continuing education sessions and review, evaluate and recommend the balance of the educational content from ASLA's call for proposals.

  • Planner and Landscape Architect

    NBBJ

    (Privately Held; Architecture & Planning industry)

    April 2006 — January 2009 (2 years 10 months)

    I focused on strategic, time-phased, sustainable approaches to suitability analyses, site selection recommendations, development concepts, development feasibility approaches, master planning, zoning analyses, code review, and phasing and entitlement strategies, so that my clients could make the best possible decisions about their development plans. I headed GIS spatial analysis for the firm. I was a member of NBBJ's Sustainable Design Group and I paid much attention to ASLA's Sustainable Sites Initiative, the Living Building Challenge, the Living Site Challenge, LEED certification costs and fees, and the future with respect to climate change policy and sustainable development. I managed and administered LEED-NC, LEED-CS, LEED-EB O+M and LEED-ND certification efforts.

  • Advisor

    South Lake Union LEED-ND Advisory Committee

    (Architecture & Planning industry)

    February 2007 — December 2008 (1 year 11 months)

    The City of Seattle's Department of Planning and Development is evaluating how Seattle's urban centers perform with respect to the U.S. Green Building Council's LEED-ND (Neighborhood Development) standard, and has submitted the neighborhood of South Lake Union as part of the USGBC pilot phase for LEED-ND. I reviewed LEED-ND prerequisites and credits that support neighborhood and City goals, provided direction on documenting credits, evaluated credit performance, and discussed opportunities to leverage standards and credits for public policy development.

    (Non-Profit; Non-Profit Organization Management industry)

    ASLA folded the Online Advisory Committee into the PR Advisory Commitee. As Chair of the PR Online Subcommittee, I thrived in engaging ASLA's broader public relations concerns through the lens of its online communications.

  • Council Member, Professional Practice Network Council

    (Non-Profit; Non-Profit Organization Management industry)

  • Professional Awards Chair

    Washington Chapter of American Society of Landscape Architects

    (Non-Profit Organization Management industry)

    August 2007 — March 2008 (8 months)

    I led the 2008 Professional Awards Committee through a series of firsts: 1) the first time any chapter of ASLA implemented a virtual jury for professional awards -- pulling distinguished jurors from Philadelphia, San Francisco, Minneapolis and Austin; 2) the first time the competition was open to all landscape architects registered in the state of Washington with projects submitted from across the United States; and 3) the first time the Chapter printed a Summary of the Awards.

  • Landscape Architecture Staff

    Hewitt Architects

    (Privately Held; 11-50 employees; Architecture & Planning industry)

  • Board Member

    Kruckeberg Botanic Garden Foundation

    (Non-Profit Organization Management industry)

    July 2002 — June 2005 (3 years)

    I guided efforts towards transitioning a 4.3 acre private residential garden in Shoreline, Washington, designed and planted by master botanists Art and Mareen Kruckeberg, into a public park, through a Deed of Conservation Easement which protects the Kruckeberg Botanic Garden in perpetuity from development. The E.B. Dunn Historic Garden Trust and Cascade Land Conservancy were the Easement's co-donees. My service was true labor of love and fascination: in addition to reviewing and commenting on multiple drafts of the Easement, while I was pursuing my MLA degree I did extensive research on and wrote a history of the garden and on Mareen's contributions to Pacific Northwest horticulture through her MsK Rare and Native Plant Nursery.

    My first gig in the field of landscape architecture, working for a venerable master of the profession. I worked with Haag on competition entries and on notable high-end primary and vacation residences throughout the Puget Sound area. I absorbed his tenets.

  • International Program Manager

    Amazon.com

    (Public Company; 10,001 or more employees; AMZN; Internet industry)

    This is where I truly came to respect a project's impact on revenue. I kept close tabs on web site features that our teams launched on the U.S. website that resulted in the most beneficial impact to the bottom line and cost us the least in terms of time and resources. Some features were astonishingly successful, others weren't. I collected the best as high-priority launch features, guiding international product managers in the United Kingdom, Germany, France and Japan to get those launched on our international web sites.

  • Content Project Manager

    Amazon.com

    (Public Company; AMZN; Internet industry)

    October 1999 — June 2000 (9 months)

    I dove into project management, and what a terrific stealth project I landed: working with a witty team of product managers, marketers and editors, I started with streamlining editorial content processes and ended up managing the launch of the first web site feature that allowed other merchants to customize their virtual storefronts on Amazon.com's web site.

  • Assistant to General Counsel and Legal Systems Lead

    Amazon.com

    (Public Company; AMZN; Internet industry)

    December 1997 — October 1999 (1 year 11 months)

    After a great go of it, I decided to give up my art career in order to earn home equity and make mortgage payments. This was just before the height of the dot com boom and everyone I knew in Seattle seemed to be working for high tech. I was the second hire for the legal department. By the time I moved on to my next role we had grown to 22 legal staff. The company was getting big fast. This is where I learned to love the energy of the fast-paced corporate world. I witnessed dozens of acquisitions and I contributed to intellectual property efforts including domain name registration, patent administration, trade dress, as well as document retention and service agreement administration. I coordinated with technical teams to configure and deploy new legal matter management software for increasing case loads.
