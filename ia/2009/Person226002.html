Sue Black’s Summary

Dr Sue Black is Head of the Department of Information and Software Systems at the University of Westminster. She has a PhD in software engineering and is interested in practical research which focuses on improving software and/or helping others to improve their software. If you are interested in improving the quality of your company's software or are interested in discussing the possibility of enrollling for a PhD in this area please do get in touch.

Sue is keen to get more women into technology and has set up several online networks for women in computing since 1998, the most well known of which is BCSWomen which is very active and currently stands at around 1000 members, all women in tech.

Sue also campaigns to make sure that Bletchley Park, the fundamental, historical, codebreaking site near Milton Keynes is saved for posterity, she blogs about this at savingbletchleypark.org.

Sue was recently put forward as one of ten women IT role models by ITPRO to celebrate Ada Lovelace Day 2009:

Ada Lovelace Role Models: http://www.itpro.co.uk/610290/ada-lovelace-day-celebrates-women-in-it

Sue Black’s Specialties:

Software engineering, software measurement, software maintenance, software evolution, software quality, Bletchley Park, women in computing, social media.

    British Computer Society

    (Non-Profit; Information Technology and Services industry)

    2003 — Present (6 years)

    About the BCS Advisory Council

    The Council is a representative body of the membership that advises the Trustee Board on the direction and operation of the Society.

    It also has a constitutional role in electing the Honorary Officers and representatives to the Trustee Board. It comprises 32 elected members, as well as the President, the Deputy President, the Vice-Presidents, the immediate past President and the past President. The elected members comprise:

    6 elected by Branches Congress (the regional constituency)
    6 elected by the SG Assembly (the Specialist Groups constituency)
    6 elected from the Forum Strategic Panels, by the Professional Membership
    2 elected by the young professionals constituency; and
    12 elected by the Professional Membership from the general membership of the Society

    All paid up members of the Society, except Students, are eligible for election (i.e. all AMBCS, Companions, MBCS and FBCS).

    Terms of office for Council members are for three years, unless filling a casual vacancy in which case it is to the end of the original term in respect of which the vacancy arose. Terms of office start at the first Council meeting after the Society's AGM1.

    Council meets up to three times a year, at the moment in January, May and September. The President chairs both Trustee Board and Council.

  • Crucible Fellow

    NESTA

    (Higher Education industry)

    1986 — 1987 (1 year)

    http://www.nesta.org.uk/crucible/

    Ever wondered what a space scientist and a biologist might have in common or how an artist could help a particle physicist? Crucible is your chance to stop wondering and discover what great minds, inspiring speakers and creative thinkers can do when they spend three weekends together.

    Day to day you're faced with demanding challenges in your own field. But what about the wider world? What about more complex challenges that can't be solved by one discipline alone. By bringing bright thinkers together, Crucible is designed to help you see the bigger picture.

    Crucible aims to help you discover:

    * how other early to mid-career researchers are tackling the same issues as you;
    * how evidence-based policy is made;
    * how to communicate with the media and public;
    * skills and attitudes likely to make your research more innovative;
    * how thinking creatively can really make a difference to your work and career.

    The challenges facing society today require people from different disciplines to collaborate and think outside the box. Crucible is just the start.

    The ripple effect measures impact, or how likely it is that a change to a particular module may cause problems in the rest of a program. It can also be used as an indicator of the complexity of a particular module or program. Central to this paper is a reformulation in terms of matrix arithmetic of the original ripple effect algorithm produced by Yau and Collofello in 1978. The main aim of the reformulation is to clarify the component parts of the algorithm making the calculation more explicit.
