Chris Carvalho’s Summary

Chris Carvalho has almost twenty years of professional and ten plus years of Digital Media experience. He currently serves as the General Manager of SmartyCard.

SmartyCard, winner of the People's Choice award at DEMO 09, is a first of its kind web site that has created an entirely new approach to the online entertainment ecosystem. The learn-earn-and-play experience creates a parent approved payment mechanism for tweens. Parents purchase points and kids unlock them by completing age appropriate lessons. Points can be used to redeem rewards from popular games, toys and virtual items - music from iTunes, online memberships to ClubPenguin - and much more.

Prior to SmartyCard, Chris headed up Lucasfilm Business Development for nine plus years and the company's internet division. In his business development role he led the company's strategic analysis of new technology and Digital Media. Additionally, he worked closely with senior management of all divisions identifying new business opportunities leveraging the Lucasfilm intellectual property and crafting and refining strategic plans. Chris was also a THX Board advisor -- helping set and execute that company’s strategy and goals.

For the Lucasfilm internet division, Chris had operational and P&L responsibility for Lucas Online, and crafted all of the major business deals, including ones with established companies like Yahoo, Apple, and AOL and startups like Eyespot, Zazzle, and TalkCity. Chris was the driving force behind Lucas’ Digital Media initiatives – identifying new opportunities and creating new site features that delivered additional content and revenue.

Prior to coming to Lucasfilm, Chris spent seven years as a management consultant, with Deloitte & Touche, consulting to executives in a range of companies, from startups to the Fortune 500. Chris has served as a speaker at numerous events including the Stanford Graduate School of Business Future of Content and Digital Hollywood conferences.

The concept behind SmartyCard is built on the dramatic changes going on in the online space for tweens -- over 200 virtual worlds in development or live -- and the reality that tweens don't have a payment mechanism.
