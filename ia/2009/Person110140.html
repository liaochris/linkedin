Jenny Ryan’s Summary

My interests lie in the intersections between people, culture, and technology. For the past five years, I have engaged intensely with the field of cyberanthropology and written several virtual ethnographies, and now seek to apply my deep understanding of online communities through writing, consulting, and collaboration with groundbreaking community initiatives.

I am currently seeking consulting opportunities that will allow me to utilize my insights regarding new media in creative ways. I am particularly interested in helping with research projects pertaining to cyberlaw and media policy.

Past Research:

• Online Social Networks as Vehicles for Individual and Collective Remembrance: Ethnographic project concerning the proliferation of “online shrines” for deceased individuals (primarily young people) on online social networks.

• Armchair Anthropology in an Era of Interactive Technologies: Exploring modern visual “ethnographies” on YouTube.

• Eating Disorder Communities on the Web: Posted a series of questions to a dozen eating disorder communities on the web (both pro-recovery and pro-disorder), presented in a high-level psychology course on eating disorders.

• Being, Knowing, and Being Known on the Facebook: Conducted and analyed 6 intensive interviews, both online and in-person, concerning identity, community, and ethics surrounding use of the social networking site Facebook.com.

• The Trance Dance Experience: Posted a series of questions about trance and dance to 8 different trance communities on Tribe.Net. These responses were pieced together to create a poetic tapestry of perspectives, performed for the final class.

• Bridging the Virtual and the Real: Identity and Community on the Facebook: Myriad interviews supplemented this research, which also drew heavily from an extensive literature review.

Jenny Ryan’s Education

  • Wesleyan University

    MA, Anthropology, 2007 — 2008

    Based on five years of participant-observation on the social networking sites MySpace, Facebook, and Tribe.net, The Virtual Campfire explores the increasingly blurred boundaries between human and machine, public and private, voyeurism and exhibitionism, the history of media and our digitized future. Woven throughout are the stories and experiences of those who engage with these sites regularly and ritualistically, the generation of "digital natives" whose tales attest to the often strange and uncomfortable ways online social networking sites have come to be embedded in the everyday lives of American youth.

    The Virtual Campfire: An Ethnography of Online Social Networking can be read at http://thevirtualcampfire.org.
