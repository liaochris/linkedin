W. Warner (Bill) Wood’s Summary

University professor specializing in anthropology and museum studies with emphasis on the history and culture of environmentalism, material culture and identity, globalization, and tourism. Previously Assistant Curator of Latin American Anthropology at the Natural History Museum of Los Angeles County; Academic Programs Coordinator at the Center for Latin American Studies, University of Illinois, Urbana-Champaign; and Visiting Fellow in the Anthropology and Global Studies Programs at Johns Hopkins University. Most recent work is focused on the transnational lives of Zapotec weavers and textiles, representations of nature in museums, zoos, and other public institutions, as well as eco-tourism and biodiversity on the Pacific Coast of Oaxaca. Co-director (with Andres Lopéz Peréz) of the Binational Initiative for the Study and Conservation of the Coral Reef Communities of Hautulco, Mexico.

Select publications include: "Picturing Pristine Coral Reefs and the Government of Eco-Friendly Tourism at Las Bahías de Huatulco Resort" (for The Art of Governance: Visual Culture in Greater Mexico, Mary Coffey and Sarah Hill, eds. Minneapolis: University of Minnesota Press, forthcoming); and "Textiles oaxaqueños, el arte indígena ‘falso’, y la ‘invasión’ mexicana de la Tierra del Encanto." Cuadernos del Sur 9(19): 19-33.

Select exhibits include: "Conversations" (February 20– June 19, 2005), an interpretive, object-based 3000 sq. ft. exhibit exploring the museum’s science, social science, and history collections though art co-curated with a team of 5 curators and 6 artists; and “What is Mexican Folk Art?” (October 19, 2003 – January 4, 2004) an interpretive, object based 750 sq. ft. introductory exhibit to accompany the "Grandes Maestros de Arte Popular Mexicano" traveling exhibit.
