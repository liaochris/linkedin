John Gotts’s Experience

  • Inventor & Startup Work

    John Gotts

    (Internet industry)

    May 1976 — Present (33 years 4 months)

    John currently is working with BijoBee, a smart search tool, another web business that will be unveiled in August and and energy company that he's starting with a retired U.S. Senator. More information on both of the new companies will be made public soon.

  • Founder & CEO

    Gotts Hydrogen

    (Internet industry)

    June 2008 — May 2009 (1 year)

    John Gotts and his brother-in-law created a hydrogen generator that ran from energy in stored deep-cycle batteries, charged from any electrical socket.

    John and Gabe were able to produce around 24 liters per minute, which increased horsepower and engine torque while greatly reducing emissions, and figured a complete system would produce around 60 liters per minute.

    This project continues with a focus on how this could be used to dramatically reduce soot emissions on long-haul trucks and other diesel internal-combustion-engines.

  • Founder

    Gotts Electric Vehicles

    (Internet industry)

    January 2007 — June 2008 (1 year 6 months)

    John looked seriously into the conversion of gas-powered-vehicles to electric as fuel prices soared up through most of 2008. The idea was to take low-cost small cars, new from the factory and after removing the internal-combustion-engines and controls the cars would be re-fitted with electric motors and batteries.

    As the economy slumped nobody was buying new vehicles and as fuel fell by 70% the return-on-investment became nonviable as a plausible cost benefit to the consumer.

    John learned a lot working with experts in this field.

  • Founder & CEO

    BitZip

    (Internet industry)

    June 2006 — December 2006 (7 months)

    John made deals totalling nearly $80 million dollars to purchase the top eight BitTorrent sites in an attempt to legitimize and capitalize on the traffic, which at the time totaled nearly 100 million monthly unique visitors at a time when Facebook and MySpace were selling for billions with equivalent total users.

    An agreement was made to sell the company to InfoSearch Media, where George Lichter (ex-CEO of Ask.com) met with John and the MPAA to facilitate a deal. The MPAA agreed that it was interesting but the studios did not come back with a yes in time to close the deals. Hulu.com came out soon after using a business model nearly identical to the one John put forth to the MPAA and affiliate companies.

  • Founder & CEO

    Searching.com

    (Internet industry)

    January 2005 — June 2006 (1 year 6 months)

    Searching.com was John's first attempt at the search space. At the end of 1999 John created a business plan to provide better search results and started negotiations to buy the domain SearchMe.com. A trademark was filed under that name for, "What are you search for?" with the USPTO. John dropped the idea when convinced by an advisor that Yahoo owned the search space and that the search race was over. SearchMe.com is now a new search engine with no ties to John and everyone knows how Google came from nowhere to take the market.

    Part of Searching.com's traffic plan included a signed contract to acquire StumbleUpon.com for ten million dollars. John was unable to secure funding only to see Ebay acquire the company months later for seventy-five million. John sold the searching.com domain and website in 2008.

    John learned a great deal about the search space and traffic that serves him well at Byjobi.com.

  • Founder & CEO

    Spy-Assassin

    (Internet industry)

    January 2004 — March 2005 (1 year 3 months)

    John's most-successful venture to date was Spy-Assassin, which saw over ten million downloads from early 2004 to early 2005. John used domain names such as adware.com, virusfilter.com and other type-in traffic to attract people looking for spyware removal software. At its peak sales reached nearly $500,000 monthly.

    John's father still owns adware.com and runs PCSafe.com, which continues to sell malware removal software since 2001.

    John and his father created software to remove or warn users about bad websites but didn't follow up on the provisional patent. Two years later a trio from M.I.T. were paid nearly $50 million (as reported) by McAfee who named the product SiteAdvisor.com.

  • Founder & CEO

    InboxCop

    (Internet industry)

    September 2001 — December 2003 (2 years 4 months)

    John Gotts and his father created one of the first anti-spam products for Windows with InboxCop. The software still works today and can be downloaded at SpamSafe.com, which is owned by PCSafe.com.

    McAfee soon after purchased NovaSoft's anti-spam and made it McAfee's SpamKiller for an estimated $35 million.

  • Founder & CEO

    Network Rewards

    (Internet industry)

    December 1999 — August 2001 (1 year 9 months)

    John Gotts and his father created one of the first rewards programs for credit, debit and retail cards for tracking referrals. Fleet Boston discussed distributing 150,000 cards just before the crash of the dot com market and Fleet's legal department ended relationships with all new start-ups.

    After demonstrating the system to ServiceMaster and ChemDry franchises both companies started their own similar reward programs.

    These programs are now ubiquitous across card issuers and many other product and services providers.

  • Founder & CEO

    PowerBingo.com

    (Internet industry)

    May 1998 — December 1999 (1 year 8 months)

    John started and then sold PowerBingo to Border Capital of Canada. The six-figure profit gave John his first small domain success and showed John the value of domains as real estate.

  • Founder & CEO

    FreeCashGames

    (Internet industry)

    February 1995 — November 1999 (4 years 10 months)

    John Gotts and his father created a website where they proposed creating free games users could play for real cash prizes, supported by banner ad sponsors.

    A director from a large Palo Alto VC suggested to John that there was no real future in online games as it wasted people's time and wasn't a way for people to interact. He suggested it would lead to anti-social behavior because people would not be interacting. This may have been the single worst advice John ever took.

    Years later John paid the now late Gary Gygax to consider creation of a world-of-warcraft-similar game but unbelievably the largest game makers didn't think people would know who he was. It was great to meet Gary and he is missed.
