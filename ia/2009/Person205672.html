Ruth M. Shipley’s Summary

SMR Information Solutions provides customized business research to support companies in the biological, biomedical, biotech, medical devices, pharmaceutical, and health care industries.

The founder and owner, Ruth M. Shipley, has a BA in Biology and Chemistry, a Master of Science in Biochemistry and a Master of Science in Library Science. She has been a science librarian, a clinical medical librarian and a medical writer. She has over 20 years of experience in researching, analyzing and summarizing biological and biomedical information.

We are part of a global association of independent researchers, many of whom are former reference librarians. We research business information for a fee. Do you really think you can find all the information you need for free on the Internet? We think not.

So if you're tired of getting 1,000,000+ hits on Google, please contact us. Even if you are not in our target industries, we may be able to find someone else who can help you if we cannot.

    Information Access Company (IAC) was bought by Gale Research in 2000. The new company was named The Gale Group. But my job remained the same: indexing and abstracting articles in medical journals, magazines and newsletters.

    Gale even let me work at home! They gave me a computer and paid for my Internet connection. So I could sit around all day in my PJs and read medical articles! If they hadn't shut down their entire U.S. indexing & abstracting department and sent all the work overseas, I would still be working there. See my blog entry, "Is There Life After Outsourcing?" at http://smrinfosolutions.blogspot.com

  • Several titles in Editorial Dept.

    Information Access Company

    (Publishing industry)

    February 1990 — January 2000 (10 years)

    IAC was the first U.S. company to create a computerized index to the periodical literature. It was called Magazine Index on CD-ROM. Eventually, MI became InfoTrac, which you would have found in many public libraries.

    I started out at IAC managing a $2.5 million contract with another company that provided consumer abstracts of articles in medical journals for a consumer health database IAC had created. IAC was one of the first - if not THE first - company to create a consumer health database back in the late 1980s. Initially, it was called Health Periodicals Database, then Health Reference Center and then Health Resource Center.

    After a few years, IAC canceled the contract and starting indexing and abstracting medical journals in-house. I trained a group of indexer-abstracters, and eventually became an indexer-abstracter myself. My job title changed periodically, but my overall duties did not. Eventually, I was allowed to work at home. IAC also pioneered telecommuting, which was very unusual back in the 1980s.

    IAC no longer exists. It was bought by Gale Research in 2000 and the combined companies were called The Gale Group. Originally part of Thomson, Gale is now owned by Cengage.

  • Clinical Medical Librarian

    University of Missouri-Kansas City

    (Educational Institution; Higher Education industry)

    September 1986 — September 1990 (4 years 1 month)

    Truman Hospital in Kansas City, MO, was one of the first U.S. hospitals to implement a clinical medical librarian (CML) program in the early 1970s. A CML doesn't just sit at the reference desk and passively wait for people to ask for help finding information. S(he) goes on patient care rounds in the hospital and actively seeks opportunities to provide information to the health care team that will improve patient outcomes.

    But that all changed when Grateful Med came out in the late 1980s. Now doctors and med students could search MEDLINE themselves! So the faculty would do their own search, and then send their students to me with a list of articles they wanted. My job was to photocopy the articles or get them on interlibrary loan and deliver them to the team.

    At that point, I saw "the handwriting on the wall" and knew I would just be an overeducated photocopy clerk. That's why I left clinical librarianship and UMKC.

  • Science Librarian

    Juniata College

    (Educational Institution; Higher Education industry)

    September 1984 — August 1986 (2 years)

    Juniata College is a small, liberal arts college in the Allegheny Mountains of central Pennsylvania. As the Science Librarian, I was responsible for searching the scientific literature for students and faculty.

    Unfortunately, most of the students only used the library as a study hall. And most of the faculty hardly ever visited the library. It was my first lesson in supporting the information needs of a large group of people. What did they do when they needed information?

    The faculty probably just relied on their professional colleagues and their own personal journal subscriptions. And if the faculty never used the library, why should the students?

    I only spent two years at Juniata. But in that time, I completely reorganized the periodical collection, which was located in the basement. The previous librarians felt it was better to leave all issues of a magazine together, even when the publisher changed the magazine's title. Not such a bad idea!

    But I was down in the stacks looking for a magazine one day, and the jumble of letters left me very confused! I saw different letters of the alphabet all around me. I never did find the magazine I was looking for, because at that point I stopped and decided to put all of the magazines in strict alphabetical order.

    The library director gave me a “thumbs up,” so I mentioned the idea to the periodical clerk under my supervision, and she and her student helpers eventually rearranged the entire collection.

    I also decided to automate the periodicals list. It was a huge, printed book consisting of photocopies of the catalog cards. Four cards to a page. But it’s so hard to update a printed book. We had a VAX minicomputer on campus, and there was a terminal in the library.

    So I contacted the computer department, told one of their students what I wanted, and I don’t even remember who entered all of that data! But I remember seeing a beautiful computer printout before I left Juniata College.
