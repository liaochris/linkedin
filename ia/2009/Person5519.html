Andrew Griffiths’s Summary

I get a kick out of rethinking old problems and crafting new, more successful responses when others are stymied. I love consulting – particularly in industries that are new to me – and people say I’m an unusually creative strategist. I enjoy the intellectual stimulation of seeing and applying the Big Picture.

These days consulting has taken a back seat. I now have a start-up business, FindWAtt, built on a 25 year old fascination with the attributes of things. We focus on product attributes and the potential to radically improve ecommerce with structured data and metadata. Attributes have become such a constant theme in my life that I now apply a technique I call Shadow Price Management to every decision I make.

In my personal life, I am celebrating a year of fives: 45 years since I entered high school, 40 years since my first consulting assignment, 35 years since I graduated from Cambridge, 30 years since I graduated from Wharton, 25 years since I got married and left McKinsey (not causally connected), 20 years since I moved to the wine country, and 10 years since I decided that at some point I would build a start-up company.

Andrew Griffiths’s Specialties:

Strategy, marketing, pricing, purchasing, non-linear thinking, altering perspectives, puncturing conventional wisdom, thinking creatively; product attributes – particularly those of consumer product goods, supermarkets, railroads.

Andrew Griffiths’s Experience

  • CEO

    FindWAtt

    (Internet industry)

    2007 — Present (2 years)

    We Fix Findability

    FindWAtt was created when we ran into the roadblock that stopped GroceryPower in its tracks: ecommerce everywhere suffered from a lack of product data plus attributes. Even though Internet retailers (including the top 100) are often equipped with wonderful software that allows buyers to ‘drill-down’, find what they want and make comparisons, this software in many product categories is severely hamstrung by the lack of structured and attributed data. Drilling down can be useless when the customer finds a ‘dry hole’ – product information that is meaningless to her or simply not what she wants. The upshot for the retailer is no sale.

    We’ve spent the last 2 years developing self-learning, human-supervised systems that supercharge product data processing, standardization, and attribution. These systems require minimal product knowledge and steadily diminishing human involvement as they learn. Our tag-line is “We Fix Findability”. We can help e-Retailers satisfy their customers’ needs to find and compare attributes of products they want in ways that are meaningful to the customer. The result is higher immediate sales and customers who keep coming back.

  • CEO

    Grocery Power

    (Internet industry)

    June 2003 — Present (6 years 5 months)

    Powering Consumer Choice

    I started GroceryPower in the shadow of the dot.com boom and crash and, in particular, Webvan’s failure. The Internet’s penetration of typically low value grocery and other consumables such as drugstore items was, and still is, pitiful in comparison with high value durables such as computers and cameras. I figured there had to be a “pony in there somewhere,” although simply porting over the durables model would never amount to much except in niche markets. The “pony” we identified was based on the ongoing, repetitive nature of consumables purchases versus single, infrequent purchases of durables. So our initial model in 2003 was to put continual, real-time professional purchasing optimization in consumers’ pockets in ways that are meaningful to them: minimize costs, allocate dollars most effectively (e.g. which organic fruits to buy), maximize nutritional efficiency, and so on.

    We spent a long-time in part-time prototype mode to keep our burn rate very low but by 2005 we’d ramped up to the point of needing production-level databases of products and their attributes. We’d known that there wasn’t a universal database of products, particularly with attributes, but we hadn't anticipated the enormity of building our own, limited version. After both learning painfully that any solution relying on humans (notoriously inaccurate creatures) wasn’t scalable and investigation of 3rd party solutions, we placed the GroceryPower concept on hold to develop an automated solution to product attribution – this is now FindWATT.com

  • CEO

    The WIKID Group

    (Privately Held; Management Consulting industry)

    1995 — Present (14 years)

    WIKID is an acronym for Wisdom – Insight – Knowledge – Information – Data. It is represented in pyramid form with Wisdom at the top: data is distilled into information, hopefully then into knowledge, but rarely into insight and wisdom.

    Much of our consulting work has been in the railroad industry, starting in 1990 with Canadian National Railways (CN) which challenged the applicability of value propositions to the ‘commodity’ business of railroading. CN was a fantastic client undergoing an amazing transition from a high cost state-owned corporation to the most profitable public railroad in North America today. With CN carrying a significant portion of Canada’s GNP, the company provided me with an intellectual playground to refine my earlier understanding from department stores of the impact of product attributes on supply chain design. In particular, CN exposed me to the consumer goods channel and the supermarket industry.

    I’ve long thought that much of what passes for management theory is hogwash. One of the benefits of consulting as a sole practitioner was the freedom to take mini-sabbaticals for reading and thinking, particularly about strategy. I have accumulated a lot of material that will slowly make its way onto my long-standing (but sorely neglected) strategy blog (www.blackboxstrategy.com). I was also able during these sabbaticals to fill in some of the holes left by my excessively narrow British education.
