David Wright’s Summary

I am very lucky: I am a public librarian, and find that the people I serve and the colleagues I work with are a really great bunch of folks, when they're on their meds. It also seems to me the kind of work that, the better you try to be at it, the better you become as a person. Which is nice. I am also fortunate to specialize in the field of reader's advisory, which means conversing with people about books and reading and helping them find other good stuff to read. Which is really fun and challenging work. Another great thing I get to do, both for the library where I work and for some other folks (see below), is write articles and book reviews and columns and blog posts, about books and culture and stuff that I find interesting, or that my patrons are interested in. All in the city I grew up in and (mostly) love. Plus I get to go around and speak to groups and conventions all around the country about our shared work and interests, which is also something I enjoy and am pretty good at.

David Wright’s Experience

  • Reader's Advisory Librarian

    Seattle Public Library

    (Government Agency; 501-1000 employees; Libraries industry)

    January 1998 — Present (12 years )

    I work in the Fiction/Teen/Library Equal Access Program department, on the third floor of The Seattle Public Library's Central Branch, which is that extraordinary and curious structure at the corner of 4th and Spring in downtown Seattle. If you're visiting, drop by and say 'hi,' I'd love to see you. Aside from the day to day work of a librarian and reader's advisor, I write and edit book lists and posts for one of our library's blogs - SHELF TALK (http://shelftalk.spl.org), and do an Adult Storytime called Thrilling Tales on the first and third Monday of every month.

  • Reviewer and Columnist

    NoveList, Booklist, Library Journal, Kirkus, Real Change, Alki, etc.

    (Publishing industry)

    1997 — Present (12 years )

    Kaite Stover and I write an irregular column to Booklist magazine called He Reads / She Reads, which plays around with gender stereotypes. It is fun to do, and is the kind of thing we don't see much of in bookish circles- not the male angle, but the guy angle.
    I wrote an article in RUSAQ: 'Mark Page, Finder of Lost Books in The Mystery That Wasn't There,' on how we track down imperfectly-remembered titles for patrons, written in the form of a Chandler detective story. I also wrote for the book 'Research-Based Readers' Advisory,' a chapter titled 'Zen and the Art of Reader's Advisory.' My first title was 'Your Perfect Orgasm'- a craven ploy to trick people into reading it. It is still a pretty good chapter, albeit much less orgasmic. I've also done articles for the NoveList database called 'Hot Topics,' which address areas such as Urban Fiction, Cult Fiction, Slipstream or Graphic Novels, that I see as high-interest among younger readers and less well known by your average librarian.

  • Speaker/Presenter

    ALA, PLA, WLA, OLA, BEA, CAL, Bouchercon, Ontario & Albany (NY) Library Associations, etc.

    (Libraries industry)

    December 1997 — Present (12 years 1 month)

    I actually enjoy going to speak before groups, especially if I can distract them while I do so with slides and sight gags. Sounds hokey, and I suppose it is, but I've gotten pretty good reviews from groups and conferences I've spoken to, so if you're interested in a presenter, I love to visit new places I've never been (and old favorites such as Toronto) and generally work pretty cheap if you'll fly me in and get me a hotel stay. I have done presentations on men and reading, both solo and together with Kaite Stover (see columnist, above), as well as the reading interests of 20-somethings, and a program on how to create and produce Adult Storytimes, which I recently presented at the Ontario Library Association Conference, the Toronto Public Library, the Oregon Library Association Conference and the Colorado Library Association Reader's Advisory Day.
