Stephen McConnell’s Summary

Responsible for new technology developments within the Australian, North American and European IT industries. Has successfully managed the delivery of commercial products in the Computer Aided Design industry, high-end proprietary simulation applications in the pharmaceuticals industry (Pilkington Glass, US), production systems in the ceramics industry (Caroma, Aust), and world-class innovations in simulation related to underground and open-cut mining industries (Newmont Mining, Aust).

He has led many international projects dealing with distributed service management in the B2B and financial services domain (OSM, France).

Chaired the Object Management Group Electronic Commerce Domain Task Force from 1996 through to 2001 during which time he was responsible for establishment and adoption of international standards for a common reference architecture and technology adoption process covering B2B community collaboration standards (OMG Distinguished Service Award in 2000).

Coordination of advanced research and development activities related to the delivery of a distributed service management platform (OSM, France) and the osm.planet global service management architecture.

Founder of the DPML and responsible for overall DPML technology and service strategy development.

Stephen McConnell’s Specialties:

Component architectures and component management solutions; distributed systems, service management and Peer-to-Peer architectures; service and Process Oriented Frameworks and Adaptive security strategies; Java, J2EE, JMX, JNDI, JNLP, CORBA, UML; team management, motivation, coordination, mentoring; business planning and market strategies.

    (Information Technology and Services industry)

    April 1997 — January 2004 (6 years 10 months)

    With support from the NSA and EDS combined with financial backing from European Commission under OSM AC211, I established OSM SÀRL as a vehicle to promoting and leverage ICT assets developed within Europe to a broader international community.   • Chairman

    OMG Electronic Commerce Domain Task Force

    (Non-Profit Organization Management industry)

    1997 — 2000 (3 years)

    I was responsible for the development of the OMG Electronic Commerce roadmap, involving the coordination of contributions from the UK, USA, Germany, France and Japan. This work established the groundwork for the launch of a Negotiation Facility Request For Proposal (RFP) with backing from the W3C (via Dave Hollander). The proposal was subsequently issued under the OMG Montreal Meeting in June. International promotion of an Electronic Commerce Reference Model commenced in 1998 (Salt Lake City). I was responsible for the delivery of the OSM business object interoperability service implementation. By the middle of that year I was involved in the development of RFP dealing with Trust Management and Public Key Infrastructure (PKI) standardization processes. The PKI RFP was successfully issued in Washington (1999) and the OSM submission to the Negotiation Services RFP was adopted as a formal standard in Philadelphia during April 1999.

  • Project Director

    OMI Software Architecture Forum

    (International Trade and Development industry)

    1995 — 1997 (2 years)

    The OSAF project addressed cross-industry standardization in the embedded systems sector, and was initially composed of the European member organizations Entoteam (Italy), Trialog and Chorus (France), IONA Technologies (Ireland), and Interglossa and the X/Open Group (UK) and quickly expanded to encompass the sponsoring of other international projects and initiatives resulting in participation of Tandem, Southwestern Bell, and MITRE (USA), Hitachi (Japan), CommerceNet, and the Object Management Group.

  • Project Director

    Tachosmart 1B

    (Transportation/Trucking/Railroad industry)

    1995 — 1996 (1 year)

    The TachoSmart project involved coordination between government agencies across Eastern and Western Europe with the aim of facilitating consensus on future digital driving hour monitoring technologies in the transport industry. The project involved the coordination of incumbent industry players, new entrants representing smart card based technologies and in-vehicle intelligent devices, and a myriad of government agencies covering vehicle and driver licensing, vehicle inspection and enforcement agencies, agencies concerned with digital evidence as a part of judicial processes, and agencies dealing with the monitoring of dangerous and hazardous goods. The end results of the project was the delivery of an agreed technology strategy that contributed to the establishment of 1998 EU Council Regulation 2135, and subsequent 2005 Regulations covering the mandated usage of smart-card based identification and data-collection mechanisms in all new trucks and buses across the European Community.

  • Senior Consultant ICT

Over the four years with company I developed a service delivery model which served as a framework for contract negotiation and service delivery towards company clients and the European Commission.

  • Project Administrator

    GOAL (Esprit 6283)

    (Information Technology and Services industry)

    April 1992 — June 1996 (4 years 3 months)

    The CEC Project GOAL (Esprit 6283) aimed to develop generic software tools to support a new project management paradigm, in which projects are collaborative, decentralised and inter-organizational. To support inter-organizational interaction, communication and cooperation, the project developed a design framework for formalizing the flow of information between organizations, specifying access to and provision of project services, and defining project-wide standards and procedures. The framework was based on normalizing interactions between autonomous software agents by specifying messages and protocols for inter-agent communication and cooperation.

These activities led to contacts and involvement with the French company Matra (defence and automotive interests), the development of a number of contracts with companies in the USA, Europe and Japan, and local partnerships with Digital Equipment Corporation in Australia linked to projects on emerging workstation technologies – perhaps best exemplified by the underground mine 3D flight simulators developed in conjunction with Newmont Mining (Australia).
