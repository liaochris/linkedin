Branko Gerovac’s Summary

I work at the forefront of technology and market advances, and regularly produce game changing innovative products across the interplay of computing, communications, and media. I hold senior technical executive positions, and provide leadership in technology development, business planning, product development, and customer and partner relations.

Branko Gerovac’s Experience

  • CEO

    Video in the Cloud

    (Internet industry)

    2009 — Present (less than a year)

    Video in the Cloud is dedicated to developing the technologies, markets, and business opportunities for next generation cloud computing infrastructure that delivers high bandwidth access to the massive amounts of data required to support a broad class of data intensive Internet scale applications.

  • Chief Strategy Officer, Board Member, and Founder CEO

    Blackwave Inc. (formerly Acinion Inc.)

    (Privately Held; Computer Hardware industry)

    2006 — 2008 (2 years )

    We established Blackwave to build Internet video servers that would provide customers with substantially higher performance at lower cost with a smaller footprint and reduced power consumption. As founding CEO and then CSO, my role was to foster the key business, market, technology, and product strategies. We were very successful in identifying market timing and evolution, business opportunity, and innovative technology, and in building a strong engineering team. Within the first year, we developed and delivered full alpha systems demonstrating the key unique features to customers. This was accomplished via an innovative development approach employing test driven design and agile methodologies. The work produced several patent applications.

  • Senior Partner

    GCTRP LP

    (Computer Software industry)

    2004 — 2006 (2 years )

    GCTRP was formed to consult to cable and telco clients, and to develop the technology, market, and business concepts that would lead to the formation and funding of Blackwave. Key developments included the definition of system testing and design methodology and the business modeling of the emergence of Internet video.

  • CTO and VP of Research

    SeaChange International

    (Public Company; SEAC; Computer Software industry)

    1999 — 2004 (5 years )

    SeaChange was a spinoff of work that I and colleagues began at DEC in the late 80s and early 90s to bring about the emergence of digital television. My major projects at SeaChange included: the early development of 1GbE for video delivery, advanced targeted advertising methods, compressed domain video switching, media server integration, and automatic content propagation. I was an executive liaison to and worked extensively with domestic and international customers. I negotiated key joint development ventures, performed due diligence for investments, contributed to investor relations, and managed the corporate patent portfolio, contributing several patents. During this period, SeaChange doubled revenue, and became the dominant supplier of Video On Demand and Ad Insertion equipment to the cable television industry, for which we received a Technical Emmy award.

  • Founding Member and Associate Director, RPCP

    MIT

    (Educational Institution; Higher Education industry)

    1994 — 1998 (4 years )

    The MIT Research Program on Communications Policy provided a unique and productive platform to investigate the broad impact of emerging digital media and electronic commerce. The program was funded by DARPA. I led the development of an award-winning game-changing progressive-scan HDTV video camera, the development of an Internet video server for the Army, and the investigation of emerging broadband to the home communication services. I also contributed to projects on electronic commerce, electronic publishing and copyright, digital libraries, and educational uses of internet video.

  • Founding Member

    Committee on Open High Resolution Systems (COHRS)

    (Internet industry)

    1990 — 1997 (7 years )

    COHRS was an ad hoc group formed to promote interoperability, scalability, and extensibility of emerging digital media standards, particularly HDTV and digital television, and thereby, to bring more Internet compatibility to digital television standards. COHRS had a broad, diverse, international membership representing television, film, computer, and communications industries and academia. The work, demonstrations, and publications of COHRS members were very successful in raising the visibility of the broader cross industry potential of making media digital. Through the efforts of COHRS, many of these concepts are now commonplace.

  • Member, Advisory Committee on Advanced Television Service (ACATS)

    FCC

    (Government Agency; Telecommunications industry)

    1990 — 1997 (7 years )

    ACATS was the FCC's industry advisory committee chartered to oversee the definition of HDTV. As the computer industry representative, I promoted the importance of interoperability and the move to an all digital standard. These aspirations of digital television came at a time when the broader implications of the convergence of computing, communications, and media were just becoming recognized. During this period, I participated in several negotiations among broadcast, consumer electronics, computer, and motion picture industries.

  • Visiting Scientist, Media Lab

    MIT

    (Educational Institution; Higher Education industry)

    1989 — 1994 (5 years )

    In order to provide DEC with a window into the impending convergence of computing, communications, and media, I moved my offices to the MIT Media Lab. This was an active period in transforming consumer electronics and media. I participated in Lab research, and promoted these technologies and market directions throughout DEC.

  • Consulting Engineer, Corporate Research

    Digital Equipment Corporation

    (Public Company; DEC; Computer Hardware industry)

    1981 — 1994 (13 years )

    In the late 80s and early 90s, I pursued the emergence of digital television, cable television, cable modems, and convergence technologies, as well as the impact across the corporation of these new markets and technologies. I was a strategic advisor to the Chief Counsel and the Executive Committee, and also regularly advised and collaborated with the Corporate Strategy Group, the Government Relations Group, and the Cable Business Unit.

    During most of the 80s, I led engineering development of high performance workstations including: 3D graphics, windowing systems, and VLSI for geometry, rendering, and video. My corporate responsibilities included the system architecture review board and the sponsored university research committee. I oversaw DEC's sponsorship of university research in graphics, visualization, and digital media; one notable project led to the development of the X Window System, where I was a founding member of the X Window System Consortium.

  • CoChair SIGGRAPH 89; and other positions

    ACM SIGGRAPH

    (Non-Profit; Motion Pictures and Film industry)

    1987 — 1992 (5 years )

    The SigGraph organization represents a unique multidisciplinary interplay of science, technology, and entertainment. Its annual conference is an eclectic gathering, sharing, and showcase of the year's developments in computer graphics and animation. Each year's conference at that time was its own organization with a planned 5 year life cycle. A fulfilling aspect of my role was to build and manage the committee of volunteers and contractors who together run all aspects of the annual conference. The multimillion dollar conference produces a revenue surplus that funds other programs and functions of the overall SigGraph organization.

    Through 1999, I contributed to SigGraph in a variety of capacities including: Conference Planning Committee; Program Director for GraphiCon’91 (SigGraph-Moscow); SigGraph-SigCHI Liaison; Strategic Planning Committee (Snowbird); and organizer of technical sessions and special projects at several SigGraph conferences.

  • Technical Director, Behavioral Sciences Department

    E.K. Shriver Center

    (Educational Institution; Research industry)

    1971 — 1981 (10 years )

    The Behavioral Sciences Department was a nationally-renown research group funded by NIH to investigate basic learning theory with an emphasis on automated methods of language acquisition and social behavior modeling in children. To create the research platform, I worked with early microprocessors, personal computers, objected oriented programming, real time process control, and film and video production. Notably, I also originated an area of behavioral research called learning by exclusion.
