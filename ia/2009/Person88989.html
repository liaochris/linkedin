Grant Canary’s Summary

Hello! I'm a rising developer of sustainable technologies.

I get my kicks designing and developing projects that end with the implementation of new sustainable technologies in the field. Especially when these technologies solve larger environmental problems.

The Black Soldier Fly project I am managing feeds food waste that would otherwise be landfilled to black soldier fly larvae. When the flies are fattened and mature, they are a product substitute for fishmeal. Income streams both from receiving the food waste and selling the larvae-meal.

The project is looking for grants, angel investors, and a larger location and partner with whom to scale the existing pilot factory.

This project specifically responds to the pain being felt in the aquaculture industry today. Aquaculture has grown at a rate of 8-11% per year for the last thirty years (in comparison with land based meat and plants which have grown at 1-2% per year). It is the industry responsible for keeping world food supplies semi-adequate as population grows. However, it is dependent upon fishmeal (chopped up small fish) as a protein source for feed. In 2006, the sustainable limit of fishmeal production was reached and prices of fishmeal doubled from $600 to $1200 per ton.

  • Marandua Project

    (International Trade and Development industry)

    July 2006 — September 2006 (3 months)

    The Marandua Project entered the cost estimation phase in May of 2006. It was an integrated bio-diesel plantation and carbon sequestration project that scaled an existing UNEP project (las Gaviotas) to 200,000 Hectares.

    As the project was set for implementation in a fragile eco-system without utilities, the job of the BioSystems team was to design self-sustaining systems for food production, electricity production, and waste treatment and then estimate their cost. Four systems emerged that were replicated throughout the plantation. They utilized Biodigester Technology, Integrated Manure-Aquaculture-Algae systems, Solar Sterilization, and waste treatment with Black Soldier Fly Larvae.

    JP Morgan would come on board to fund the project as a part of the emerging carbon credit market; however, the Colombian government would turn it down due to its size and the public services that would be caused by 40,000 employees and 100,000 people arriving in a previously barren region.
