John Reid’s Summary

As Director, Product Technology and Innovation at the Moline Technology Innovation Center (MTIC), I am currently responsible for redefining the MTIC as a support network for internal technology leverage and a linkage to the external environment to accelerate innovation for John Deere to support business growth. With my colleague in Advanced Marketing, we have created and jointly orchestrate an Accelerated Innovation Process that intends to deliver new innovations to the marketplace.

In previous roles, I have provided enterprise-wide support and coordination of John Deere’s development of the technology development process in automated and unmanned vehicle development. From 2000 to 2005, I provided enterprise support for John Deere’s technology strategy in automated and unmanned vehicle systems.

I came to Deere and Company in January 2001 after a 14-year career at the University of Illinois where I was recognized internationally for my contributions in robotic applications for off-road equipment. My expertise includes machine vision perception, controls, and hardware-in-the-loop design. Previously, I served as a consultant to agricultural and military industries in their effort to deploy advanced technologies into precision agriculture and systems automation.

At the University, I was research director and chairman for 6 completed M.S. degrees and 15 completed Ph.D. students. My awards have included the Japanese Society for the Promotion of Science Research Fellowship (1998), the University of Illinois University Scholar Recipient (95-97) and the Karl Gardner Advising Award (1997).

John Reid’s Education

  • Texas A&M University

    PhD , Agricultural Engineering , 1983 — 1986

    I was part of a project to develop machine vision guidance for an agricultural tractor. My role included the development machine vision algorithms based of detecting crop rows relative to the vehicle which were used to create a guidance directrix that resulted in a steering control signal. These algorithms were integrated into the control of a Ford 7710 tractor by the team.

  • Virginia Polytechnic Institute and State University

    MS , Agricultural Engineering , 1980 — 1982

    Development and validation of low-temperature grain drying models for bin drying of shelled corn. The goal of the research was to understand the potential for alflatoxin development when applying low temperature grain drying control strategies in warmer climates like Virginia.

    Development of a weather model for providing estimates of psychometric conditions based on historical data records. The dry bulb model was based on principle component determined from spectral analysis. The web bulb model, completed by a colleague, was based on a Markov random process and correlation with the dry-bulb data.
