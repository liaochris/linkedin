John J. Parman’s Summary

Educated as an architect and planner, with a research focus, I was recruited by EHDD in San Francisco as its first marketing director in 1977. I spent the next 20 years in A/E firm marketing, ending up at SOM/SF.

In 1998, I joined Gensler's Communications group, led at the time by Helen Dimoff. We founded "Dialogue," Gensler's client magazine, which I've edited since 1999. As editorial director, I work with a team of editors and designers on print and digital publications (with creative direction by Mark Coleman).

In parallel with my marketing/communication activities, I published the award-winning journal "Design Book Review" (1982-1999). I've been involved with an online design journal, "LINE," since 2000, serving as its cochair through 2004 and again in 2009. I'm also an editorial adviser to the west coast edition of "The Architects' Newspaper."

UC Berkeley Professor Richard Bender and I have collaborated on writing and research since 1972. In 1989, we founded a think tank, the Urban Construction Laboratory, as a vehicle for these projects.

I've written articles and reviews for various magazines, including "Aquapolis" (Italy - RIP), "ArcCA," "Architectural Record," "Architecture" (RIP), "Bay Architects' Review" (RIP), "California Management Review," "Catalyzer," (Japan), "Design Methods Group Newsletter" (RIP), "Industrialization Forum" (RIP), "Journal of Architectural Education" (RIP), "Journal of Architectural Research" (RIP), "Places," "Portus" (Italy), "Rassegna di Architettura e Urbanistica" (Italy), "Space & Society" (MIT edition), and "The Architects' Newspaper" (CA and NY editions).

John J. Parman’s Education

  • University of California, Berkeley

    M. Arch., Building Technology, Planning, 1972 — 1975

    In the Ph.D. Program, 1975-1978. While in graduate school at Berkeley, I worked with Professor Richard Bender on evaluations of HUD's Operation Breakthrough and a Federal Solar Energy Demonstration Program led by the same group (ex-NASA). The latter was for the Joint Economic Committee, U.S. Congress; the former for the National Science Foundation. I was Professor Bender's principal research associate, the beginning of our friendship and collaboration.

  • Washington University in St. Louis

    B.A., Architecture, History, 1966 — 1970

    Jim Fitzgibbon, who worked with Buckminster Fuller, was a big influence. I had him in fourth year. He suggested that I go to Berkeley. I also benefited from Udo Kultermann and Norris Kelly Smith, the two (very different) historians teaching at the school. I was involved with design methods in this period, writing for the "Design Methods Newsletter." At Berkeley, I became friends with Horst Rittel, the critic of first generation design methods, and took his class (with Mel Webber) on planning theory.
