Alexa B. Johnson’s Summary

As a marketing masseuse, I passionately specialize--not in Shiatsu or hot stone therapies--but in creative concepts and copywriting.

Like a bodyworker who talks to a client about where it hurts and what he wants to achieve before realigning his muscles and connective tissue, I partner with my colleagues and my clients to really wrap our brains around our pressures, problems, strengths and goals. Then, I "massage" the organization's visual and verbal marketing messages. After all, the messages are the connective tissue between an organization and its audiences.

**********************************************************************************
My other passions involve plants and gardening. I share that information here because the work I do in the garden informs the work I do in marketing, and vice versa. As a creative person -- not just a creative businessperson -- my thinking processes are a mix of methods, principles and intuition. I also think gardening, like marketing, is filled with wonder and play, experimentation and reflection, and the darn hard work of just digging in and getting it done.

    What the heck is Instructional Design, Alexa?

    Instructional Design (ID) is an art and a science, much like Sales and Marketing in fact. You use your theoretical and practical knowledge to plan and create educational and/or communications tools. Then you put those systems and tools in play, you analyze their effectiveness and you adjust them based on feedback and results.

    The thing that separates good ID from great ID is also what separates okay marketing from powerful marketing: your student or your target audience has to recognize, "What's in it for me?" and be called to action.

  • Instructional Designer

    Element K

    (Privately Held; 501-1000 employees; E-Learning industry)

    June 2005 — May 2006 (1 year)

    Instructional Designers, like writers and marketing creatives have to think big picture, from soup to nuts:

    What are the goals of the organization sending the message? Who do we want to reach, and what do they know, believe? What are their attitudes and habits and motivations? What are the goals of the audience and how does/will the audience relate to the message? How can we really impact behavior? So, let's make a good plan, probably with a bunch of smaller plans inside it and let's figure out who's going to contribute what, by when and how. How are we going to define and measure success? Time is money: how can we get the quickest and best ROI? Along the way, are we using our resources wisely and moving in the right direction? What adjustments do we need to (be ready to) make? Have we covered all of our bases? Sweet, good work. What's next?
