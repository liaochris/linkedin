Dung Le’s Summary

Bioscience and Biotechnology have been contributing to the improvement of human health and well-being. Such improvements have been achieved via adequate and appropriate nutrition and excellent health care. Thus, my career objectives have been to use special knowledge and technical expertise in Biochemistry and Molecular Biology to improve human health and her well-being.

While a graduate student at Chungbuk National University, Korea, I had an excellent opportunity to exercise Protein Engineering Techniques. My research subject was the enzyme Acetohydroxy acid synthase (AHAS). Being a key enzyme in the biosynthesis of branch-chain amino acids, AHAS had been a target for several classes of herbicides.

My brief stay at the National Food Research Institute of JAPAN allowed me to familiarize with microbial engineering via Ribosome- and RNA Polymerase- targeting techniques.

At the University of Nebraska-Lincoln, I worked on the topics of methionine oxidation, detection and repair. Methionine sulfoxide is a small molecule and all conventional approaches failed to produce specific antibody. Using a non-conventional approach, I was able to develop an antibody specific for methionine sulfoxide. In another direction, I studied the enzyme free-methionine sulfoxide reductase from yeast.

My current research at the National Agriculture Research Center of Japan is aimed at developing RNAi-mediated virus-resistant rice and analysis of its resistant mechanism with emphasis on Rice Tungro viruses.

With my knowledge and expertise mentioned above, I am looking for opportunities that employ molecular biology and protein biochemistry to work on subjects that ultimately contribute to the improvement of human health and well-being. Presently, my special interest is given to agricultural biotechnology.

Dung Le’s Experience

  • JSPS Postdoctoral Fellow

    National Agriculture Research Center - Japan

    (Biotechnology industry)

    November 2008 — Present (5 months)

    My research at the Research Team for Vector-Borne Diseases of the National Agriculture Research Center of Japan is aimed at developing RNAi-mediated virus-resistant rice and analysis of its resistant mechanism with emphasis on Rice Tungro viruses. Using pANDA vector, I planned to deliver a 500-base inverted repeat of each of every gene of the viruses into japonica rice and then study how the transgenes can trigger the degradation of virus RNA when the transgenic plants is exposed to live viruses and finally how it could confer resistance to the viruses.

    My other interest is to look for evidence insight into the mechanism by which Rice Tungro Spherical Virus assists the Rice Tungro Bacilliform Virus to transmit via insect vector.

  • Postdoc

    University of Nebraska-Lincoln

    (Educational Institution; Higher Education industry)

    April 2006 — March 2008 (2 years)

    In certain proteins, methionine residues are positioning close in proximity to the active site, the binding site or sites that are critical for correct folding. Existing evidence proved that, when these methionine residues are oxidized, the proteins become malfunction and consequently lead to development of diseases.

    My studies involved in both development of novel detection tools for methionine sulfoxide (antibodies, biosensor, etc) and identification of enzymes that reduce methionine sulfoxide back to methionine. The successful stories had been published in Biochemistry and Journal of Biological Chemistry.
