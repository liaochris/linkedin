Nathan de Vries’s Summary

The social aspect of the web is something that has always drawn me in, not only as a developer but also as a passionate user. For this reason, much of what I enjoy doing focuses on user experience at the core, and connecting people in new and interesting ways.

My interest in "social software" has driven me to participate in the web standards development process as a web standards advocate, and to use those skills to develop social tools in an attempt to bring people closer together. I have an active interest in Microformats, and how they can help us bring the web forward from the dark ages of the late 90s.

I also thrive on working with talented people & fellow early adopters, both as a leader & part of a team. Working with equally passionate people both professionally and through web-focused user groups is something I take great pleasure in doing.

Nathan de Vries’s Specialties:

Frontend web dev. with standards compliant and semantic HTML, CSS, unobtrusive Javascript & Adobe Flash/Flex. Also experienced with dev. for mobile devices.

Backend web dev. with Ruby/Rails & PHP.

Building flexible APIs for mobile devices (e.g. J2ME/iPhone).

Integration with Facebook, Youtube, Flickr & Google APIs.

Utilisation of Amazon Web Services for scalability (EC2/SQS/S3).

Deployment with Capistrano & Slack/Puppet/Sprinkle.

System admin. of Debian/Ubuntu servers.

Nathan de Vries’s Experience

  • Lead Server Engineer

    Storyz (was Tilefile)

    (Computer Software industry)

    January 2008 — January 2009 (1 year 1 month)

    Early in 2008, Tilefile made the decision to refocus their product, change the company name to Storyz, and cut down on development overheads. We decided to rewrite our primary Python application in Ruby on Rails, based on the success of the search, mapping & metrics projects I'd worked on.

    I was responsible for leading the team of 4 Rails developers to build a mobile-focused group messaging application (i.e. group MMS). The application could be accessed across many platforms, including the web, an iPhone application available in the AppStore, J2ME applications, WAP devices, a Facebook application and widget frameworks.

    The application supported transcoding of images and video into a wide variety of formats for viewing across platforms, a flexible JSON API for non-web/WAP access, email/SMS notifications, and integration with third parties like YouTube and Flickr for media importing.

    I also assisted in moving the development team from Subversion to Git.

  • Primary Architect & Senior Software Engineer

    Tilefile (now Storyz)

    (Privately Held; 11-50 employees; Computer Software industry)

    September 2006 — January 2008 (1 year 5 months)

    I was initially hired by Tilefile as a Ruby on Rails developer, working on search, mapping & metrics projects. This role quickly expanded into a role as the primary architect, overseeing and guiding the development of an in-house Python application being built by a team of 10 developers.

    As a Rails developer, I wrote Facebook applications & iPhone web applications using iUI. I also worked extensively with the APIs of services such as MSN, Google Talk, Youtube & Flickr.

    I drove the adoption of Amazon's Web Services (EC2, S3 & SQS) for Tilefile's media storage, transcoding & streaming system, and Ubuntu as a homogeneous server platform.

    I was also responsible for switching the Flash development team from legacy Actionscript 2 to Actionscript 3 & Flex, and performed prototyping of desktop Flash development with AIR.

  • Co-Founder, Director & Software Engineer

    Duck New Media

    (Computer Software industry)

    February 2006 — April 2007 (1 year 3 months)

    The Duck New Media business was inspired by the writings of Seth Godin. Specifically, the concepts introduced in his book, "Flipping the Funnel: Give Your Fans the Power to Speak Up". We saw a market in companies who didn't know how to give their customers a voice to talk to other potential customers.

    My role was to find those companies and give voice to their customers via the web. This typically involved building websites focused on customer opinions, built as rapidly as possible. Playing the role of businessman and technician really furthered my experience in the areas of sales and technology.

    In this agile environment, I picked up Ruby (the programming language) and Ruby on Rails (the web framework), and haven't stopped using them since.

  • President

    UTS Programmers' Society

    (Information Technology and Services industry)

    March 2006 — March 2007 (1 year 1 month)

    This role involved organising events for a membership body of approximately 400 CompSci students, as well as recruitment of new members. Society events ranged from purely technical, software development meetings and hack-a-thons, through to debates and social meets.

    During the same period, I continued to play a role as an administrator of the society's computer network, and liaised with technical staff of the university.

  • Computer Systems Officer (CSO)

    UTS Programmers' Society

    (Educational Institution; 201-500 employees; Information Technology and Services industry)

    March 2005 — March 2006 (1 year 1 month)

    Maintenance of the network & server infrastructure for a 400+ member society, as well as management of a team of 10 telecommuting administrators.

  • MySource Matrix Developer

    Squiz

    (Privately Held; 11-50 employees; Information Technology and Services industry)

    December 2004 — February 2006 (1 year 3 months)

    My primary role was in the development of MySource Matrix, an open source content management system (CMS) used in government and big business. Clients included Austereo (2DayFM, TripleM), The Australian Federal Government, Oxford University and many others.

    One of the major projects I was responsible for was to build the bulk-emailing system being used by Austereo's TripleM radio station for their email subscription system. The system needed to send out highly-customised text & HTML emails to their email lists. These lists generally had somewhere in the order of 100,000+ members, so the project included flexible rate control systems to ensure compliance with regulations and to reduce system load.

    I also worked on the PHP_CodeSniffer project which is now available as an open source project from the PHP Extension and Application Repository (PEAR).
