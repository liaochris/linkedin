Frederick W. Chapman’s Summary

My mission is to empower people to achieve their goals and realize their dreams by harnessing the power of technology. I have reaped the benefits of technology for many years, and I can help you do the same. For example, do you do a lot of math? By selecting the best tools for the job, I can give you wings to soar through your calculations -- you will make fewer mistakes, get better results, and do it all more quickly and easily.

I am a mathematical software specialist with 5 years of postdoctoral training and over 20 years of professional experience. I am an expert in Maple and Maple TA and have strong connections to both the mathematical software industry and the academic world. My areas of expertise include computer algebra, numerical analysis, and hybrid symbolic-numeric methods -- especially Geddes series methods for solving hard problems in computational functional analysis.

I invented Geddes series in my PhD thesis in 2003. I completed my PhD under the supervision of Prof. Keith O. Geddes, a co-founder of the Maple research project. I spent 11 years in residence at the University of Waterloo, the place where Maple was invented. I was affiliated with Waterloo's Symbolic Computation Group for 11 years and the Ontario Research Center for Computer Algebra for 7 years. Before that, I worked at the Lehigh University Computing Center for 9 years as a full-time consultant specializing in mathematical software and high-performance computing.

As a research mathematician, I have dedicated my career to inventing new mathematics and developing new algorithms to solve hard problems. As a technical consultant, I have devoted myself to empowering engineers, scientists, mathematicians, students, teachers, and researchers alike with modern software tools for exploring and applying mathematics. In all my work, Maple has proven to be an indispensable tool for quickly gaining insight into complex mathematical phenomena and rapidly prototyping new algorithms.

Frederick W. Chapman’s Education

  • University of Waterloo (PhD, 2003)

    Doctor of Philosophy , Applied Mathematics , 1998 — 2003

    In my 326 page doctoral thesis, "Generalized Orthogonal Series for Natural Tensor Product Interpolation," I introduced a dozen original mathematical inventions in half a dozen different areas of mathematics, developed rigorous mathematical theories for all of them, and presented a variety of computational applications. The principal invention of my thesis is the "Geddes series expansion" (GSE), which I named in honor of my thesis supervisor, Prof. Keith O. Geddes. GSEs form a vast parameterized family of bilinear series expansions for multivariate functions -- the number of degrees of freedom in the parameter space is uncountably infinite! The terms of a GSE are generated by a simple iterative algorithm based on a nonlinear "abstract splitting operator," which I also invented. GSEs have dozens of computational applications ranging from the fast and accurate approximation of high-dimensional multiple integrals to the automatic derivation and proof of multivariate identities.

  • University of Waterloo (MMath, 1998)

    Master of Mathematics , Applied Mathematics , 1995 — 1998

    In my 178 page master's thesis, "Theory and Applications of Dual Asymptotic Expansions," I introduced an original mathematical invention, developed a rigorous mathematical theory for it, and presented a computational application in Maple. The "dual asymptotic expansion" (DAE) is a new kind of bilinear series expansion for multivariate functions. The terms of a DAE are generated by a simple iterative algorithm based on a nonlinear "asymptotic splitting operator," which I also invented. This work was supervised by Prof. Keith O. Geddes, co-inventor of the Maple computer algebra system. My doctoral thesis (see above) built directly on my master's thesis and extended it considerably.

  • Lehigh University (BA, 1982)

    Bachelor of Arts , Pure Mathematics , 1979 — 1982

    I completed a very demanding four-year bachelor's degree program in three years. I majored in pure mathematics and took an additional concentration of courses in computer science. I was on the Dean's List every semester, graduated Magna Cum Laude, and was inducted into two national honor societies: Phi Eta Sigma (after freshman year) and Phi Beta Kappa (upon graduation).
