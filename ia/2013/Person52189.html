Heather Boerner's Summary

Heather Boerner is an award-winning freelance writer and editor with more than 15 years experience. She specializes in healthcare storytelling that describes how people make tough decisions for themselves and their families. She's worked as a daily and weekly newspaper reporter, in the trenches of a start-up website and as an independent freelance writer who draws on all that experience to produce prompt, accurate and engaging copy for consumer and business publications. Her work has appeared in publications including Better Homes and Gardens, Yoga Journal, The San Francisco Chronicle Sunday Magazine, Prevention, Zocalo Public Square, and custom publications including Lupus Now, HemAware, Diabetes Forecast and National Nurse Magazine. She has recently begun multimedia collaborations with radio producers, creating narrative stories and accompanying video and audio that reaches wider audiences.

She is recently the recipient of a Dennis A. Hunt Grant for Health Journalism, and a winner of an Association of Health Care Journalists Award of Excellence, among others.

In addition to her writing work, Heather has edited everything from ebooks to academic and white papers, focusing on smoothing the rough edges of copy and layout to let the clients' voice and meaning shine.

Specialties: Social determinants of health, healthcare access, health policy, sexual health, women's health, fertility, miscarriage, lgbt/queer issues, men's health, nutrition, obesity, complimentary and alternative medicine, physical activity, yoga, meditation, business development, finance, profiles, features, narrative nonfiction.

Heather Boerner's Projects

  • Rotten to The Core: Oral Healthcare and Overall Health

    • September 2012 to September 2012
    Team Members: Heather Boerner

    Oral healthcare is a key factor in overall health, yet our system is set up so that more Americans suffer from lack of dental care than even medical care. The results? Excruciating pain, long term bad health, loss of job opportunities, and even death. Only an improved Medicare for all can fix the system.

  • Lost Generation

    • January 2012 to February 2012
    Team Members: Heather Boerner

    The hospital industry believes that investing in new nurses hurts their bottom line. So an entire generation of new graduates are struggling to find work. What troubles lie ahead, then, for the future of nursing and healthcare?

  • White Paper: Public Health Prevention Efforts--Saving Lives, Saving Money?

    • October 2012 to October 2012
    Team Members: Heather Boerner

    Chronic conditions are the biggest drivers of health care costs. Expanding health insurance coverage, improving quality of care, or expanding community or behavioral prevention can each save lives, researchers have found. But only community prevention has been found to save lives and money in the long run.

    • National Nurse Magazine
    • June 19, 2013
    Authors: Heather Boerner

    All over the country, RNs are working to provide exemplary care, no matter the age, income, race, or national origin of their patients. But if RNs and other healthcare providers are not sensitive to their patients’ immigration status and understand all the ramifications of living life in fear of government discovery or without access to serv- ices that legal residents take for granted, they can miss things.

  • Excellence in Health Care Journalism, Trade Category

    Association of Health Care Journalists
    • April 2012

    "Squeezed to Death," for National Nurse Magazine, explains why hospital-based skilled nursing facilities around the country are closing. Hospitals claim that they can't support the units, that they're money losers, but this investigation revealed that they support themselves just fine. They just can't support the rest of the hospital. And as they close, the people who need them the most--those ineligible or too noncompliant for home care and other services--are left with fewer options and worse care.
