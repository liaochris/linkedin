Guillaume Paumier's Summary

I'm a science Ph.D who never stopped playing Legos and dominoes. In my work, I build systems out of basic components (like Legos), and I prepare and execute robust plans so that all the moving pieces fall right into place (as with falling dominoes). I love to organize, create, optimize and fix systems.

I have put this to good use in project and product management, and the field of technical communications. At the Wikimedia Foundation, I set up several systems to increase transparency and collaboration between engineers and users. I also led the development of UploadWizard, a step-by-step file uploader, which has been used to upload about 3 million files to the Wikimedia Commons media library.

My interests are many; I'm used to working in an interdisciplinary, international and multicultural environment. Currently, I work at the intersection of communications, website operations and engineering. During my Ph.D., I worked at the crossroads of microelectronics, chemistry and biology.

I like to code, and I've practiced enough languages to be able to pick up new ones quickly. I enjoy simplifying what's complex, notably through user interfaces and information architecture. I like to craft beautiful, easy-to-understand documents that use visual explanations.

I joined the Wikipedia community 8 years ago. During that time, I've written articles, contributed thousands of photos, become a MediaWiki wizard, and held various organizational positions. In 2008, I wrote a Wikipedia handbook with Florence Devouard. I'm passionate about making knowledge accessible to all; I highly value transparency, openness and the respect of users' freedom and privacy.

I enjoy traveling the world, hiking in nature and taking photos, many of which have been reused widely on the Internet and in printed publications.

I also handled project-related communications and management.

  • PNIPAM Technologies for lab-on-chips

    • Ph.D. Thesis — Université Toulouse III Paul Sabatier
    • November 6, 2008
    Authors: Guillaume Paumier

    Labs on chips are miniaturized devices integrating one or several laboratory functions, usually dedicated to the handling of chemical and biological samples. Our work aimed at integrating a smart polymer called poly(N-isopropylacrylamide) (PNIPAM) in microsystems, in order to develop a new technological process for labs on chips.

    PNIPAM is a thermosensitive polymer that undergoes a reversible state transition ; it switches from a hydrophilic, swollen state below its temperature of transition (LCST ~32°C), to a hydrophobic, collapsed state above it. The technology we developed is based on heating elements and a surface functionalization process to graft the PNIPAM layer.

    Our results show that the electro-osmotic flow can be modulated by thermally controlling the PNIPAM, thus paving the way to electrokinetic mixers. This thermal control also enables the adsorption (and partial desorption) of proteins on fonctionalized beads, the main application being sample preparation.

    Keywords: Microfluidics, thermosensitive polymer, NIPAM, lab-on-a-chip
