Justin Rich, PMI-ACP, CSP, CSM's Publications

  • Disillusionment, Protest or Business as Usual: The 2004 EU vote

    • Association of American Geographers, Electoral Geography
    • April 6, 2005
    Authors: Justin Rich, PMI-ACP, CSP, CSM, Fiona Davidson, Tad Sours

    Abstract:
    Since the advent of the European Union Parliament in 1978 the institution has functioned in a manner similar to that of many of its member nation legislative bodies, balancing coalitions that are generally aligned around center-right Christian Democratic parties and center-left Social Democratic parties. However, the presence of minor parties has been increasing over the last two decades and the arrival of Greens, regionalists, nationalists, and now neo-conservative and anti-EU parties has added a new uncertainty to the composition of the parliament and to its governing (and opposition) coalitions.

    This research examines the 2004 elections to the European Union parliament, analyzing the voting data at the NUTS 1 regional level to provide insight into the current pattern of non-traditional voting (i.e. voting for parties that are not aligned in the traditional centre left/center right blocs). Initial results indicate that European elections are being used both as mid term referendums on national ruling parties and as a means for voters to express their dissatisfaction with the traditional opposition parties. In addition, comparison with results from the previous two EU elections indicates that these levels of dissatisfaction have remained relatively constant, as has the electoral expression of the voters' discontent with the EU project as a whole (through anti-EU parties) although there has been considerable spatial shift in that expression.
