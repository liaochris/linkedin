João Ferreira's Summary

I'm currently a Lecturer in Computer Science at Teesside University. I'm a member of the Digital Futures Institute and of the High Assurance Software Laboratory (HASLab – INESC TEC Privileged Partner).

I am interested in mathematical methods that can improve the current scientific standards in algorithm and software design. In particular, I am interested in calculating programs from their formal specifications and in program analysis and verification.

My mid-term goals are to continue my research on software verification and algorithmic problem solving, and to apply my scientific knowledge in industry.

Specialties

Algorithms, Algorithmic Problem Solving, Program Calculation, Mathematical Approaches to Software Quality, Formal Methods, Functional Programming, Separation Logic, FreeRTOS, Java, Haskell, C

Postdoctoral Research Associate

University of Teesside

Educational Institution; 1001-5000 employees; Higher Education industry

February 2011 – September 2011 (8 months)

I was a member of the project Inference Mechanisms for a Separation and Numerical Domain (EPSRC Funded Project EP/G042322), where I conducted research on automated verification of heap-manipulating programs using separation logic. I worked on the verification of FreeRTOS, a real-time, multitasking, preemptive operating system for embedded devices.

Systems Administrator

Theoretical and Computational Physics Research Group

November 2004 – 2011 (7 years)

I was the system administrator (on a consultancy basis) of the Computational and Theoretical Physics Group at the University of Minho. I have installed and maintained three Linux clusters with 144 total computing nodes. I also maintained the group’s network and I provided user support. I have designed, implemented, and installed their web portal, which is based on the Plone CMS and has custom features like the management of scientific publications.

Ph.D. student (comp. science)

University of Nottingham

Educational Institution; 10,001+ employees; Research industry

January 2006 – September 2010 (4 years 9 months)

My thesis was on principles and techniques of algorithmic problem solving. The goal was to articulate principles and techniques that can be used to solve algorithmic problems more effectively.

The results include a new and efficient algorithm to enumerate the positive rational numbers in two different ways and a new and constructive proof of the two-squares theorem. The thesis also includes 12 teaching scenarios that can be used to support the teaching of algorithmic problem solving.

Teaching Assistant / Module convenor

University of Nottingham

Educational Institution; 10,001+ employees; Research industry

September 2006 – January 2010 (3 years 5 months)

I have been a teaching assistant of Algorithmic Problem Solving (APS) and Mathematics for Computer Scientists (MCS) since 2006. These modules are taught to first-year Computing Science students.

From October 2009 to January 2010, I was the module convenor of Algorithmic Problem Solving (APS). This module is about how to solve problems that involve the construction of an algorithm. Many problems are of this nature, so the scope is very broad. The module is structured around the basic principles of algorithm design (invariants, problem decomposition, etc.) with each principle being introduced via well-chosen problems. Appropriate mathematical skills are introduced in the same problem-driven way.

Plone Developer

Eurotux, SA

Privately Held; 11-50 employees; Information Technology and Services industry

November 2004 – December 2005 (1 year 2 months)

I have worked in the web development team at Eurotux, creating Plone templates and products (Python, Zope). I was responsible for most of the Zope Page Templates (XHTML, ZPT) and CSS code. I was also responsible for making the web solutions usable, accessible, and standards compliant.

Junior Researcher

University of Minho

Educational Institution; 1001-5000 employees; Higher Education industry

November 2004 – November 2005 (1 year 1 month)

I was a member of the PPC-VM project, whose goal was to create a parallel virtual machine capable of automatically distribute tasks among computing nodes. I developed tools that allow Java and C# programs to distribute themselves among a set of computing nodes (presented at PaCT ’05). I also developed JaSkel, a framework that provides parallel skeletons to write parallel programs (presented at CCGRID ’06).
