Casey O'Donnell's Summary

I am an Assistant Professor in the Department of Telecommunication, Information Studies and Media at Michigan State University. My research examines the creative collaborative work of videogame design and development. This research examines the cultural and collaborative dynamics that occur in both professional "AAA" organizations and formal and informal "independent" game development communities. My research has spanned game development companies from the United States to India. My research examines issues of work, production, copyright, as well as third world and postcolonial aspects of the videogame development workplace. I am also an active game developer, releasing my first independent game, "Osy," in February of 2011.

Specialties

anthropology, corporate anthropology, ethnography, game development, video game tools development, design automation, open source software, game design

Casey O'Donnell's Projects

  • Diffusion Case Study

    • January 2011 to November 2011
    Team Members: Casey O'Donnell, Jared Jackson, David Ducrest, Thomas Drapela, B. J. Wimpey, Jef Fredyl, Steven Arnold, L. Renee Stander, Jim Moore, Tom Robertson, Mac Smith

    In this case study, students take on the role of a physician's assistant in the local emergency room of a small town, where patients are being adimitted for chlorine gas exposure in the wake of a train wreck in the middle of town in which a chlorine tanker car ruptured. Students 'fly into' the lungs (all the way down to an alveolus) to explore the ill effects of chlorine gas exposure on the diffusion of oxygen into the blood. While in this alveolus, they measure concentrations of oxygen in both the air of the alveolus and in the blood, and learn about the three factors governing diffusion, namely: membrane thickness (the thickness of the respiratory membranes), available surface area (the available surface area of these membranes for diffusion), and concentration gradient (the differences in the concentrations of oxygen in the inspired air and blood). In doing so, students learn the principles of diffusion and are able to compare the effectiveness of three different treatments.
