Jeremy Knop's Summary

Jeremy Knop is an innovative multimedia storyteller and photojournalist who uses his skill set to deliver compelling stories through visual media. He is fascinated by the changes the news industry is currently experiencing and loves to tell stories that help people better understand the more complex issues of our time.

His work has been published with: The Washington Post, The New York Times, The Philadelphia Enquirer, The Arizona Republic, San Diego Union-Tribune, Center for Public Integrity, The Florida Center for Investigative Reporting and many more.

Knop loves crafting stories with his video, still photography, audio and writing skills and is also very interested in accountability journalism and computer assisted reporting.

He is a passionate individual who cares deeply about public service accountability journalism. Knop believes journalists play a vital role in maintaining a healthy, transparent democracy by ensuring public awareness so that citizens can make informed choices about issues that directly affect them.

In 2012, Knop completed an intensive investigative reporting fellowship with News21, running up to the presidential election. The project was an eight-month national investigation into voting rights and election fraud in America. To see the investigation, visit votingrights.news21.com.

Currently, he is the 5:30 and 10:00 p.m. producer for KTVQ-Q2 News; a Cordillera Communications owned station and CBS affiliate, in Billings, Montana.

Specialties: HTML/CSS, Non-linear editing (FCP, Avid), Adobe CS5 software, Photojournalism, Videography (DVCPRO, P2), ENG operations, AP ENPS, Analytics (Google analytics, Sitemeter, Feedburner), Multimedia Storytelling, Newscast Production, Social Media (Twitter, Skype, LinkedIn, FB), Writing (Broadcast, Online, Print, SEO, Hyperlinking).

Montana Center for Investigative Reporting

Nonprofit; 1-10 employees; Online Media industry

May 2012 – Present (1 year 6 months) Billings/Helena, MT

MTCIR is a new nonprofit nonpartisan newsroom in Montana that produces in-depth and enterprise reporting in the public interest on issues crucial to the healthy function of Montana communities.

Funded mostly through philanthropy — by national, regional, community and family foundations and even more importantly by individual donors in our own communities; local accountability journalism is a vital part of every community.

Local accountability journalism maintains a close watch on those with power or influence over the rest of us and sounds an alarm when abuses or betrayals of the public trust are discovered.

Perhaps most important, it sparks conversation about what really matters in our communities and gives citizens the information they need to be civically engaged.

It is the very kind of journalism for which the First Amendment exists and it is invaluable to democracy.

MTCIR's in-depth, innovative and enterprise reporting is published on our website and packaged with multiple plaforms in mind — making our content available to local and national newspapers, television and radio news stations and other online news organizations.

Investigative Reporter, Photojournalist

News21

Nonprofit; 51-200 employees; Broadcast Media industry

January 2012 – September 2012 (9 months) Phoenix, Arizona Area

The 2012 national News21 project will bring two dozen top student journalists from 11 universities to conduct an investigation into the impact on American voters of recent extensive changes in election laws and voting procedures in many states. It is headquartered at the Walter Cronkite School of Journalism and Mass Communication at Arizona State University.

The investigation will be released this August prior to the national political conventions. Content will be distributed through media outlets across the country and will be available at votingrights.news21.com. Follow @WhoCanVote on Twitter, or on the project's daily blog votingrights.news21.com/blog.

Students work under the direction of leading news veterans, including Leonard Downie Jr., former executive editor of The Washington Post, and Steve Doig, Knight Chair in Journalism and an expert in computer-assisted reporting. Their work has been recognized with numerous awards from the Online News Association, the Society of Professional Journalists and the Society of News Design, among others.

Students selected for the News21 program study a topic in-depth during a spring topics seminar, followed by a 10-week reporting fellowship during the summer. Students work out of a newsroom at the Cronkite School and travel the country – and sometimes to other countries – to report and produce their projects.

News21 is a program of the Carnegie Corporation of New York and the John S. and James L. Knight Foundation that is helping to change the way journalism is taught in the U.S. and train a new generation of journalists capable of reshaping the news industry.

Since 2006, nearly 500 top journalism students in the U.S. have participated in the landmark national initiative. Their work has appeared in major national publications, including The Washington Post, msnbc.com, the Center for Public Integrity and many others.

Photographer

Lights, Camera, Compassion!

January 2009 – May 2011 (2 years 5 months)

LCC was a personal project I did for a little over two years that was a chapter out of photographer Jeremy Cowart's book of life called Help Portrait. And for me it was focused on the less fortunate in the Phoenix Metro area.

LCC’s mission is giving back to the less fortunate by doing professional portraits for people that are not able to afford traditional avenues of family portraiture. These people could be single mothers or fathers, war veterans, orphans, the lonely, the sick or suffering, the hopeless, or the homeless.

Poverty steals many things: like your home, car and food; but it also steals something inside. The primary focus of LCC is to nourish the damage on the inside and restore hope.

This is a movement; a shift in thinking about photography. This is not a promotion of free photography, but rather, promoting the value of a great photograph. It's about laughing, connecting, sharing and serving for the pure sake of serving.
