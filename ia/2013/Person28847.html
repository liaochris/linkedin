Dallas McMillan's Experience

Senior Veterinary Surgeon

Cairns Vet Clinic

Partnership; 11-50 employees; Veterinary industry

January 2000 – Present (13 years 8 months) Cairns Area, Australia

As a senior veterinarian at Cairns Veterinary Clinic, I lead our veterinary care team to provide personalised care to our clients & their pets. We help owners keep their pets and family happy & healthy by building a better understanding of pet health care, and the effective use of correct socialisation and training and proven preventative health care like good hygiene, parasite control, vaccination and early attention to any health problems that do occur.
Cairns Veterinary Clinic offers consultations and surgery by appointment.
My Interest areas include skin and ear disease, tick paralysis ultrasound, surgery.

Cairns Veterinary Clinic has worked closely with the RSPCA over the last 10 years providing discounted medical & surgical treatment, preventative health care and surgical desexing to RSPCA shelter animals, and assisting RSPCA with the assessment, treatment and investigation of animal cruelty campaigns.
This has given me extensive experience with early age desexing and complex medical situations in challening situations requiring sensitivity and confidentiality.

Cairns Veterinarians, including Cairns Veterinary Clinic provide discounted surgical desexing and microchipping to pet owners facing financial hardship in collaboration with Animal Welfare. Again this provides plenty of opportunity to develop surgical desexing skills, often with more challenging mature patients which have had multiple pregnancies.

An important part of our work at Cairns Veterinary Clinic is providing a wildlife emergency and referral service. We attend to hundreds of wildlife admissions annually. We work closely with Wildlife Rescue QLD carers to identify animals and birds which will be suitable for release with close attention to the animal welfare and ecological impact of any treatment undertaken.
During my time at Cairns Veterinary Clinic I have also represented North Queensland Veterinarians as president of the NQ branch of the Australian Veterinary Association (2010-2011).

    • New Scientist Magazine 21 September 1996
    • September 21, 1996
    Authors: Dallas McMillan

    Our appetite and metabolism are controlled by powerful hormones. Many of these are peptides - short chains of amino acids. Understanding how these hormones regulate appetite is useful in identifying the causes and possible management of obesity and other metabolic diseases.

    "ANYONE who has faced a difficult exam or job interview knows that stress decreases appetite. Biologists thought they already knew which molecule was responsible. But researchers in California now claim that the main culprit is another chemical.

    When we are stressed, our brain releases a range of molecules called neuropeptides. These prime the body into a state of heightened arousal, to allow us to rapidly switch into a "flight or fight" reaction. "
