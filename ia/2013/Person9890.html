Lee-Roy Lahey's Summary

Lee-Roy Lahey is an artist with a decade of experience in:

• Story development and storyboarding for animated feature film and television, live action shorts and features, commercials, music videos, and titles.

• Editorial illustration, cartooning, comic book art and story, and fine art.

• Designing and illustrating book covers, logos, advertisements, textiles, packaging, and presentations.

A skilled draftsman and figure artist with a tested understanding of the cinematic craft. A cartoonist at heart who enjoys telling stories through the mediums of illustrated books and animation.

A life of drawing and love of cartoons, performance, and story, led Lee-Roy to a career as a visual storyteller. He moved to Los Angeles in 2002 and began as freelance Storyboard Artist on live-action commercials, music videos, and independent films, including two feature films that went to Sundance. These experiences strengthened Lee-Roy's drawing skills, understanding of clear visual storytelling, and the ability to work quickly. Lee-Roy began working as an animation Story Artist in 2005, lending his additional talents in character and story development. He has continued to improve his craft in the field, with work on animated features, shorts, television shows, and commercials as Story Artist, Character Designer, and creator.

In addition to motion pictures, Lee-Roy also works as freelance illustrator and designer in a variety of formats, including comic book projects currently in development.

Technical and Customer Support Supervisor

eNIC Corporation

1999 – 2001 (2 years)

Lee-Roy made a career change from latte-maker when he joined eNIC Corporation as a Customer Service Representative in 1999 and was promoted to Manager within three months. eNIC was the company responsible for making .cc domain registrations available worldwide, as an alternative to the rapidly vanishing .com real estate. Lee-Roy applied creative thinking and deliberation on a daily basis, helping to resolve issues confronting this upstart corporation in a global marketplace. Lee-Roy's artistic talents were known around the office, as evidenced from white boards filled with cartoon characters, and his skills were put to use in the creation of several animated web banners for the company.

Mime Theatre Studio

2009 – 2011

The benefits of studying the art of Mime have been two-fold for Lee-Roy. It is an exploration of, and provides a renewed connection to, theatrical performance, creative movement, and physical expression. These things also inform Lee-Roy's understanding of physical anatomy, expression, and action, as applied to hand-drawn characters who must, in essence, give a physical performance on the page or screen.

American Animation Institute

Storyboards, Figure Drawing (Karl Gnass, Instructor)

2006 – 2009

The American Animation Institute is run by the Animation Guild, Local 839 IATSE, in North Hollywood, California and offers vocational courses aimed at sustaining and improving the art of animation. Classes are regularly attended by industry professionals and aspiring artists alike, seeking to further develop and explore their craft.

Cornish College of the Arts

BFA, Illustration

1994 – 1998

Cornish is a private arts college in Seattle, Washington, first established as the "Cornish School" in 1914. Sergei P. Tschernisch assumed presidency of Cornish in 1994, following a 12-year tenure at California Institute of the Arts. Cornish's Illustration program provided students with a strong foundation in communication design, drafting, color theory and application, painting, and drawing. Particular emphasis was placed on figure drawing and the study of anatomy. Illustration assignments and final projects focused largely on the completion of full-color works which might accompany a text, such as editorial or book illustration. The school's greatest asset: an exceptionally dedicated teaching staff of gifted, experienced working professionals.

Ruth Asawa San Francisco School of the Arts

Visual Arts, Media Arts • Recipient: Russ Farnsworth Award

1990 – 1994

The Ruth Asawa San Francisco School of the Arts (SOTA), is an alternative public high school modeled in part after New York's High School for Performing Arts. Acceptance into the Visual Arts program provided experiences in drawing, painting, and sculpting, and offered a strong early foundation in realism. The Media Arts (aka Film and Video) program was established in 1992 and quickly became popular for providing an environment of lively discussion and creative freedom to high schoolers, and for the art form's unique combination of written word, visual aesthetics, technology, performance, and critical analysis.

(Additional awards: Outstanding Achievement in Media Arts, Outstanding Achievement in English.)

California State Summer School for the Arts

Animation

1993 – 1993

California State Summer School for the Arts (CSSSA) takes place on the campus of CalArts in Valencia, California, and offers courses to high schoolers in a variety of art forms. The 1993 program in Animation provided the opportunity to study under many of CalArts Animation faculty and MFA students at the time, including then Department Chair of Experimental Animation, Christine Panushka. The curriculum included group experiments, walk cycles, bouncing balls, zoetrope strips, flip-books, stop-motion, and pencil animation.

New Conservatory Theatre Center

Stage Acting

1988 – 1992

New Conservatory operates a Children's Theatre Company and School in San Francisco that allows children to express their raw creative energy and love for character on the stage. Enrollment in the program from the ages of 12 to 16 offered weekly vocal, physical, and improvisational exercises, and provided many experiences auditioning and performing in company productions including original works written and designed by students. In 1992, New Conservatory staged a production of The Changing of the Times, written by Lee-Roy Lahey.
