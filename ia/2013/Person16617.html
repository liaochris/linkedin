Edward Branley's Summary

I've been self-employed since 1986, and have done a number of different things in that time, but the common theme/thread to all my professional experience is education. Since leaving the high school classroom in 1984, I've continued to teach various computer-related subjects.

Continuing that theme, I own/operate a social media consulting/training firm, YatMedia LLC. We are taking clients interesting in using Social Media to grow their businesses. I work with a knowledgeable team to develop social media/social networking strategies for clients, and my natural cynicism balances their enthusiasm.

I also teach, 2-3 weeks a month, for HDS Academy (Hitachi Data Systems), through Merit Systems, LLC. I'll teach a wide range of classes for HDS, including both enterprise and modular storage hardware and software.

Additionally, I've been a street railway enthusiast ever since I was a small child, riding the Perley A. Thomas streetcars from the cemeteries to downtown with my parents to go shopping on Saturdays. In 2004, I wrote a book on the history of New Orleans' Canal streetcar line. The plan was to use the book as an impetus to start this effort, but the storm became a bit of a speed bump in the process. As part of the process of rebuilding our city, NOSRA's mission will be to educate on the importance and value of our historic streetcars, and to raise funds to preserve and restore several of the NORTA-owned streetcars currently not in service.

I plan to combine my expertise in education, courseware development, Internet development, business networking, public speaking, and small business management with my passion for history and streetcars to make this venture a success for streetcar fans and the community at large.

BOOKS: Author of the books: New Orleans: The Canal Streetcar Line, and Brothers of the Sacred Heart in New Orleans (both published by Arcadia). Co-author of Choosing Windows NT..

This "Images of America" book traces the line's history from its origins in 1860 as a mule-car service to electrification in the 1890s, through the golden age of electrification, continuing to the discontinuance of streetcars in 1964, their replacement with buses, and the return of streetcars in 2004.
