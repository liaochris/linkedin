Duncan Clauson's Projects

  • The Effect of Policy on the Economic Feasibility of Combined Heat and Power in Washington State

    • August 2010 to March 2011

    Abstract:
    Global climate change is a widely discussed topic in the United States. Yet the task of reducing greenhouse gas (GHG) emissions is not entirely a technological one but one of economics and policy. Wastewater Treatment Facilities (WWTFs) provide a great opportunity to employ combined heat and power (CHP) and reduce greenhouse gas emissions. There are over 16,000 WWTFs in the United States and of those, 3,500 employ anaerobic digesters, which create biogas. Biogas is a fuel that can be used for CHP. Evaluating Washington State's policy for renewable energy revealed gaps between the current available best practices and the State's policies. A 2005 prefeasibility study performed by the United States Environmental Protection Agency Combined Heat and Power Partnership for the Brightwater Wastewater Treatment Facility in King County provided the background information to execute this case study. Using the same assessment tool from the 2005 study, I examined the impact of each of the four scenarios: no incentives with current energy prices, 5 megawatt net metering, a cogeneration credit, and a change in the market price of electricity (e.g., as the result of a carbon tax). After dissecting the proposed policy solutions and determining the impact on prices and incentive payments, the data was then inserted into the tool, where upon economic feasibility was determined. For the scenarios with a positive annual savings an annuity formula was used to calculate the payback period for the combined heat and power system. Findings for each scenario conclude with whether a CHP system should be installed based on annual energy savings over a 20-year payback period with no change in energy prices. As a result, I was able to make recommendations based on which of the policy options were the most effective at improving the economic feasibility of combined heat and power.

  • UW Residence Hall Energy Conservation Study

    • October 2012 to Present

    Buildings are becoming more efficient. Moving forward, the greatest opportunity for increased energy savings will not come from the buildings themselves, but from the occupants. In this project we are using students to isolate the effect of information on energy behavior apart from other factors. Students living in residence halls do not pay energy bills – there is no cost savings associated with less energy use. Therefore, our study will determine if increased information alone can change energy behavior.

  • InSol Energy

    • September 2010 to April 2011

    We’ve built a thermally conductive water block that is attached to the back of the solar panel, drawing heat away for use onsite. We use a proprietary variable-flow pump control to increase efficiency further, providing control over what type of energy (electricity or heat) is generated. The process increases energy generation by increasing panel efficiency and capturing excess heat to use in a closed-loop system. Our product reduces consumers’ payback time by up to 20% and increases their annual energy savings by $276 - $674, depending on their current energy generation costs.

  • Vending Machine Lighting: A Simple Way to Reduce Costs

    • September 2010 to December 2010

    The United States Department of Energy (DOE) reports that, “The current installed base of beverage vending machines operating in the United States consumes more than 12 billion kilowatt hours (kWh) of electricity annually” (United States Department of Energy, 2009). That is enough energy to power over 1 million average US homes for one year. The UDOE estimates that 5-20% of this electricity use can be attributed to the advertising lighting on the front of the machines.

    Through this project I determined that the University of Washington could save $6,700 annually in reduced electricity costs. This can be achieved by removing the advertising lighting from vending machines throughout campus.
