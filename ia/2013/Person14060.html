Biagio "Bill" Sciacca's Summary

• As the author of “GoalsBook: Embracing Personal Responsibility in an Age of Entitlement, Biagio “Bill” Saciacca presents an easy to read book packed full of tips and ideas for success with goal setting.

With chapter titles such as Never Settle when you can Excel and You’ve Got to Feel it, Smell It and See It , the readers are left with a since of attainability and a desire to do and be more than they thought possible.

• Biagio "Bill" Sciacca is a well known Author, Speaker and Professor. He grew up in the coal mining district of Northeastern Pennsylvania and went on to earn a bachelors degree in Philosophy and Sociology and a Masters and PhD in Business Administration.

Even with a full time academic post at Penn State University, where he lectures in Micro and Macro Economics, Marketing, Leadership, Strategic Management and Interpersonal Skills in Business, Bill is also an entrepreneur. He has run a successful business of LMI, Leadership Management International, a chain of donut and ice cream franchises, and several businesses in the automotive/car care industry.

Bill’s consulting company touts several thousand clients from locally owned firms to multinational Fortune 500 companies. Bill divides his free time between research in creativity, Southern Italian cooking, writing fiction, and writing and recording his own songs with guitar. He is also a certified SPIN instructor.

• A motivated, team player, a published author, as well as a dynamic speaker, an astute executive and leader and a gifted teacher. I have many years of managing a sales/marketing organization as well as the profit and loss of various divisions (as well as entire organizations) I have had the charge to lead.

Bottom line performance as well as a motivational flare has been the hallmark of my career. I am well liked by my students and faculty, clients and participants that I have had the privilege to serve. And, I have a great deal of data to back that claim up!

There is no doubt that a bit of truth can be found in this wry observation, but clearly there is very little that is good about office politics. Actually, it can be very bad as honest, church-going, child-rearing, ethical people mutate into beings that reflect the lowest common denominators of human existence—all for the assumed benefits of what the next promotion or raise may bring. Don't pretend it doesn't exist. Have you ever heard anyone say, "She is really more worthy of that new position than I am. I mean, she has it all: the education, the experience, and the people skills?" So why do we hurt each other and make others feel small so we can be perceived as more worthy? Below are some of the root causes of office politics; the motivation behind why it occurs; and some practices to minimize the consequence or, better yet, prevent it from happening.
