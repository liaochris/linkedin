George S. Motto, M.D., CWP's Summary

"We're killing ourselves with the fork!" I remember my father, Salvatore Motto, a pharmacist, saying when I was in grammar school and growing up on Chicago's west side. Unfortunately, events since then (including my 45 years since medical school graduation as a practicing physician and endocrinologist) have shown me that my father was, indeed, ahead of his time. An epidemic of lifestyle related overweight, obesity, and physical unfitness has resulted in dramatic increases in diabetes, high blood fats, hypertension, osteoarthritis, sleep apnea, heart disease, stroke, and diminished quality of life. As with any epidemic, I believe we have to look past the traditional medical model in combating it. By leaving my incredibly satisfying and rewarding private practice of endocrinology and metabolism, I now have time to partner with others of different interests and disciplines who share my life long dream of treating and preventing disease using multiple, workable, lifestyle interventions for health. I'm reaching out to those individuals.

Through SEDHS we were able to help many patients become healthier through behavioral change. SEDHS was the precursor of my current endeavor - The Metabolism, Weight, & Lifestyle Institute, Ltd. (MWLI).

George S. Motto, M.D., CWP's Education

University of Illinois School of Public Health, Chicago

Public Health

1990 – 1991

I entered the U of I School of Public Health with an eye toward obtaining an additional degree, a Master of Public Health degree. However, during the introductory course I realized that unhealthy lifestyle, which results in overweight, obesity, and physical unfitness is our nation's and, indeed, the world's most serious public health problem causing a world wide epidemic of unhealthy lifestyle related diseases - diabetes, obesity, sleep apnea, cardiovascular disease, arthritis, premature disability, and sudden death - all in one way or another directly or indirectly related to my expertise - Endocrinology & Metabolism. I was already involved in Public Health!

It was then that my efforts to do more in trying to get individuals, families, and communities to adopt healthy lifestyles gained momentum and I abandoned my quest for a Masters Degree in Public Health.
