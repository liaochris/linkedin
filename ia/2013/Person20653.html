Carson Kahn's Publications

  • The Disaggregation, Dematerialization & Defocusing of Media in Content-Oriented Industries

    • March 22, 2012
    Authors: Carson Kahn

    This essay examines the effects of three disruptive trends presently transforming content-delivery media and markets through in the context of global consumer behavior. As relevant to six “content segments,” or content-oriented submedia—viz., newspapers, books, music, broadcasting, cable television and cable networks— the study sequentially addresses the forces of dematerialization, a consumer-driven shift from the proprietorship of objects to access to digital data; disaggregation, a provider-driven shift from infrastructural content delivery to an à-la-carte platform model; and defocusing, an industry-driven swing from attention-centric, narrow-outlet media to integrated, polysource multimedia delivery systems. The examination invokes real-world enterprise and consumerist examples of fruitful and fruitless attempts to dematerialize, disaggregate, and defocus mainstream content media, including those of Apple, Google, Netflix, and News Corp.’s iPad e-newspaper app The Daily. This meta-analysis is augmented by the author’s own interviews with and published materials by established experts in related fields, including Dr. Paul de Sa, Chief of the Office of Strategic Planning and Policy Analysis of the United States Federal Communications Commission, Mark Harrison, a social media evangelist employed by Google, and Frank Chimero, adjunct professor of design and technology studies at Portland State University. The author’s own infographic design and some data visualization will graphically support cases cited throughout the essay. The exploration constitutes a reasonable survey of the trends, behaviors and forces that stem from and drive forward the so-called ‘3 Ds’ of content delivery media and its consumers. The most effective facets of the analyses hinge on a holistic, psychosocial and technological approach, in addition to the expectable financial, economic and consumerist elements.
