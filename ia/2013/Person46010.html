Frédéric Verhelst's Patents

  • Ultrasonic characterisation of high concentration particles

    • Europe Patent WO/2003/102550
    • Issued May 27, 2003
    Inventors: Frédéric Verhelst, Arno Volker, Rolf Bartstra

    A method for characterizing a suspension with particles floating therein, which method comprises the steps of generating an acoustic actuation signal and registering an acoustic reflected signal. Further, a first dataset is calculated on the basis of the reflected signal, the dataset containing information which represents an autocorrelation function as a function of time of the reflected signal. Thereupon, first datasets are combined into a second, cumulated dataset, which represents a sum of the autocorrelation functions which correspond with the first datasets. Finally, a representation of a particle size distribution is determined by solving this representation from an expression for the second, cumulated dataset in terms of the representation of a particle size distribution.

  • Use of Agent Structures for Event Detection: Identification of Wells Watering out on Troll

    • SPE 112131 (Intelligent Energy Conference 2008)
    • February 26, 2008
    Authors: Frédéric Verhelst, Marta Dueñas Díez, Randi Helene Halmoy, Martin Halvorsen, Jan-Erik Nordtvedt

    The industry-wide focus on Intelligent Energy in recent years has lead to more and better data being available, both onshore and offshore. Regrettably, processing tools have not seen a proportional improvement, and therefore engineers experience a data overload. Fairly simple alarms along with manual routine checks using independent, “offline” tools are dominating the daily tasks in production optimisation, and more intelligence and flexibility is thus needed. In this paper we suggest an agent structure allowing online, real-time event detection.
    In this work producing oil wells watering out on Trollwere used as an example. The detection algorithm was designed to search for significant drops in the wellhead pressure. By detection of such trends at an early stage, a successful correction and restabilisation is more likely.

  • Soft sensing for gas-lift wells

    • SPE Journal / Society of Petroleum Engineers
    • December 10, 2006
    Authors: Frédéric Verhelst, Hayco Bloemen, Wilco Sturm, Stefan Belfroid

    This paper considers the use of extended Kalman filtering as a soft-sensing technique for gas-lift wells. This technique is deployed for the estimation of dynamic variables that are not directly measured. Possible applications are the estimation of flow rates from surface and downhole pressure measurements or the estimation of parameters of a drift-flux model. By means of simulation examples, different configurations of sensor systems are analyzed. Finally, the estimation of drift-flux model parameters is demonstrated on real data from a laboratory setup.

  • Correlation of 3D-computerized tomographic scans and 2D-colour image analysis of Westphalian coal by means of multivariate statistics

    • International Journal of Coal Geology / Elsevier
    • January 6, 1996
    Authors: Frédéric Verhelst, André Vervoort, Petra David, Willem Fermont, Leo Jegers

    Core material containing Westphalian vitrinite-rich coal from the north of Belgium (Campine Basin) has been investigated by colour ima ge analysis and computerized tomography. The relative 3D density distribution of the investigated core material was determined by non-destructive X-ray computerized tomography. Colour image analyses of polished sections of the same piece of core material provided data concerning the corresponding maceral composition and mineral content of the coal. The application of both methods to the same piece of rock allowed for a first-order cross-correlation of different rock properties by multivariate statistical analysis. Density estimates of macerals were made. The combination of both techniques is very useful in a study of fracture systems in core material and density calibration of computerized tomographic scans of rock constituents.

    • September 25, 1995

  • Sediment-petrological and petrophysical characterization of the Westphalian D sandstone in well KS-161 (Campine Basin)

    • K.U.Leuven
    • July 1, 1992
    Authors: Frédéric Verhelst

    The subject of this MSc thesis was a 125 meter long cored interval from well KS-161. This interval contains the "Neeroeteren Sandstone" (Upper Carboniferous, Westphalian D), deposited in a sandy braided river system. The sandstone was examined from a petroleum geological perspective, including a petrological and petrophysical component. The diagenesis of the sandstone was determined by combining a detailed core description with different analysis methods. This included the investigation of thin sections with polarizing petrographyical microscope and after staining, the use of scanning electron microscopy, and of conventional and cathode luminescence petrography. The petrophysical characteristics were determined using conventional porosity and permeability measurements on core plugs, combined with mini-permeability and X-ray tomography of core samples.

    This thesis was awarded the 1992 student prize of the Koninklijke Vlaamse Ingenieursvereninging (KVIV) (Dutch: Royal Flemish Society of Engineers) as the best MSc thesis in the fields of Civil and Mining Engineering and Architecture.

  • Integrated System Approach to Petroleum Production (ISAPP)

    • November 2004 to August 2006

    The "Integrated System Approach to Petroleum Production (ISAPP)" is a Strategic Knowledge Center initiated by TNO in 2004 in collaboration with Shell and Delft University of Technology. It complements Shell's Smart Field® initiative, aiming to achieve structural and sustainable performance improvements regarding the exploitation of oil and gas reservoirs.

    During a period of five years, 40 PhD students, postdocs and TU Delft staff cooperated with TNO and Shell staff to create an integrated network of skilled specialists in disciplines like geophysics, reservoir characterisation and engineering, production engineering and separation and conversion technology. Their focus will be on devising innovative technologies for the oil and gas industry.

    The main focus of ISAPP is closed-loop reservoir management, by combining model-based optimization and data assimilation (computer-assisted history matching). It is also referred to as ‘real-time reservoir management’, ‘smart reservoir management’ or ‘closed-loop optimization’. Common to all these is the aim to maximize reservoir performance over the life of the reservoir by changing reservoir management from a periodic to a near-continuous process. By making use of real-time production data combined with regularly acquired seismic time-lapse data, the displacement and depletion processes in the reservoir may be controlled in a much better way. It brings together measurement and control theory as used in the process industry and data assimilation techniques as used in meteorology and oceanography.

    My role was business development during the project establishment and TNO's representative in the Management Team once the project was started, with a special responsibility for business development.

  • Snorre time-lapse feasibility study

    • January 1999 to December 1999

    In 1999, Saga Petroleum started a time-lapse feasibility study using two vintage 3D seismic surveys from the southern part of the Snorre Field.

    Snorre Field was discovered by Saga Petroleum in 1979, and came on stream in August 1992. Development of the southern part of the field was aided by a 3-D seismic survey shot in 1983 and another in 1997 for reservoir characterization. Saga used these surveys (known as GE83 and SG9701, respectively) to determine the value of time-lapse seismic data for field management.

    Snorre Oil Field is located on the Tampen area in the Norwegian sector of the Northern North Sea. There are two main reservoirs; the Triassic Lunde Formation and the Triassic-Jurassic Statfjord Formation. Each reservoir consists of a network of fluvial sand bodies in a mudstone matrix, deposited in an alluvial setting. The field has a structural/stratigraphic trap, formed by westward tilting and erosion of a major fault block. Total stratigraphic thickness of the reservoir is more than 1000 m, and 17 zones with varying fluvial styles and complexities have been identified. Both Statfjord and Lunde reservoirs are truncated by the Kimmerian unconformity and overlain by Jurassic and Cretaceous shales. The field is estimated to contain 3.5 10e9 bbl of undersaturated oil in place. Production strategy has mainly been WAG (water alternating gas) injectors and conventional water injectors. Sand connectivity and flow directions are difficult to predict due to the complex geology.

    The analysis has shown that it is possible to identify movement of the oil water contact, water/gas flooding, and the location of flow barriers in several parts of the Snorre Field despite the complex sedimentological character of the reservoir. Use of time lapse date together with traditional dynamic data has increased the dynamic understanding of the reservoir and the study can be regarded as a technical success.
