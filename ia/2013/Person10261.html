Amelia Shister's Summary

Amelia Shister joined the Brookings Institution in 2012 to work on the Global Cities Initiative as part of the Metropolitan Program. Previously, she was at Bipartisan Policy Center (BPC) working on the Nutrition and Physical Activity Initiative. She graduated from Trinity College (CT) in 2009 with a B.A. in International Studies (Latin America and the Caribbean) and Hispanic Studies. She received her M.Sc. in Political Science (Comparative European Politics) from Trinity College Dublin in 2010. Prior to joining BPC, Shister interned at Dynamic SRG, a political fundraising firm in New York City. She also worked as a field organizer on a congressional campaign during the 2010 election cycle. She has worked and studied abroad in Barcelona, Spain and Dublin, Ireland.

Moving forward, Shister aims to substantively engage in research and policy analysis in areas of foreign policy and international political economy, especially with regards to food security and global commodity trade laws. She is currently studying part-time at Johns Hopkins School for Advanced International Studies (SAIS), here in DC and hopes to begin her Ph.D. in the next few years.

Amelia Shister's Projects

  • Global Cities Initiative

    • October 2012 to Present
    Team Members: Amelia Shister

    Launched in the Fall of 2011, the Global Cities Initiative is a five-year effort aimed at helping U.S. city and metropolitan leaders become more globally fluent by providing an in-depth and data-driven look at their regional standing on crucial global economic measures, highlighting best policy and practice innovations from around the world, and creating an international network of leaders who ultimately trade and grow together. Chaired by Richard M. Daley, the former mayor of Chicago, the Global Cities Initiative is a joint project of the Brookings Metropolitan Policy Program and JPMorgan Chase.

  • The Metropolitan Revolution

    • January 2013 to Present
    Team Members: Amelia Shister

    The Metropolitan Revolution is a book set to be released June, 2013. With the book will come a nationwide campaign. In The Metropolitan Revolution, Jennifer Bradley and Bruce Katz position cities and metropolitan areas, already the engines of our national economy, as the vanguard of national progress. We find the revolution present in grand, economy shaping gestures: an Applied Sciences District in New York City, an Infrastructure Trust in Chicago, and a metro-wide transit system in Denver. They also showcase a series of smaller, structural interventions: an intermediary in North East Ohio that helps manufacturing firms retool their factories for new demand; a Regional Export Council in Los Angeles that helps small businesses access global markets; and a network of Neighborhood Service Centers in Houston that gives new immigrants access to low cost banking, education, child care and health care. Jennifer and I use these stories and others to illustrate the emergence of a new growth model: a next economy where we export more and waste less, innovate in what matters, produce and deploy more of what we invent and build an economy that actually works for working families. We show how the revolution is being led by a Pragmatic Caucus who put place over party, collaboration over conflict and evidence over dogma and argue that the Metropolitan Revolution fully aligns with this disruptive moment: crowd sourced rather than close sourced, entrepreneurial rather than bureaucratic, networked rather than hierarchical. The Metropolitan Revolution offers a path to break through our paralyzed politics, reshape our federal republic, and revive our national economy.
