Kira Butler's Summary

My experience providing design solutions to a variety of clients has been balanced between technical proficiency, accountability, creative process, and a driven, ravenous desire to establish resonant brand identities in print and on the web. An aggressive creative professional with uncompromising standards for efficiency and execution, I am committed to producing work that is beautiful, user-friendly, and competitive with the competition. My design process is iterative, often collaborative, and QA is never compromised. I create memorable work that always meets the deadline.

  • Graverobbers Typeface, Website, and Book

    • March 2007 to Present
    Team Members: Kira Butler

    Graverobbers is a study in memory and mortality. A multi-part project that incorporates grave rubbing, type design, book binding, page layout design, photography and creative non-fiction writing, the narrative addresses the way we remember the dead with the hard and dirty process of confronting our own transient nature through the artistic process. Ultimately, Graverobbers is a small slice of the macabre, with a touch of self-deprecating humour to soften the seriousness of the subject matter.

  • Requiescat: A Modern Memento Mori Art Book and Sculpture

    • April 2005 to Present
    Team Members: Kira Butler

    Requiescat involves a process of self-reflection by examining the tangible materials that punctuate mortality. Each page within this book prompts a reflection on the inevitability of the human condition; the experience amplified by handling the raw materials contained within each page. The viewer must dig through layers of grass, soil, and clay wrapped in sealed plastic, thereby removing them from fully experiencing the contents of their suggested “grave.” This project was inspired by Memento Mori artwork.

  • Peek! Book

    • April 2005 to Present
    Team Members: Kira Butler, Josée Bolduc

    Peek! examines the notion of private and public spaces through typography. Created from both graffiti and advertisements found in public restrooms, the book uses acetates, contrasting colours, and small peep holes to reveal different aspects of the typeface. These small, voyeuristic glimpses of the font are reflective of the invasiveness of advertising in presumed private spaces.
