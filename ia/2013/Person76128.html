Joshua Kitlas, MSc's Publications

  • Using Internship Experience to Evaluate a New Program in eScience Librarianship

    • iConference 2012
    • February 7, 2012
    Authors: Joshua Kitlas, MSc, John D'Ignazio, Dr. Jian Qin

    This research project explores the role of internships in a new curriculum designed as part of the eScience Librarianship (eSLib) project. eSLib’s goal is to educate a new type of librarian capable of supporting science researchers who increasingly depend on cyberinfrastructure to conduct their research. Experiential learning was identified early on in the IMLS-funded project as a necessary aspect to give students field exposure that would help prepare them to manage the diverse digital objects being generated throughout an array of disciplines and institutions. Evaluation of student internship experience enhances understanding of the relation between the eSLib curriculum and what is needed by institutions tasked with managing data produced by computer and network-enabled scientists.

    Results showed how well interns in the eScience Librarianship program at Syracuse University's School of Information Studies experiences matched up with the courses taken and professional expectations.

  • eScience Panel - Off-the-shelf Software Packages vs. Open Source Software vs. In-house Software Development: Which Tools Should be Taught?

    • June 2011 to Present
    Team Members: Joshua Kitlas, MSc, Yan Xu, Erin Bartolo, Jeffrey Stanton

    Developed topic, theme and suggested panelists for the Microsoft eScience Workshop at the 8th IEEE International Conference on eScience October 8-9, 2012 Chicago, IL, USA.

    Conference site: http://www.ci.uchicago.edu/escience2012/index.php
    Microsoft Research site: https://research.microsoft.com/en-us/events/escience2012/

    Excerpt from my document:
    Off-the-shelf Software Packages vs. Open Source Software vs. In-house Software Development: which tools should be taught?

    The eScience community is rife with locally developed, open source and off the shelf software products aimed at every segment of the data lifecycle. Software practices differ not just between universities and industries, but also between schools, departments, research centers, and laboratories. The wide-ranging software adoption by institutional leaders can create disparities and difficulties in analyzing and sharing data. Managing the governance of the software and analyzing the impact it has on the institution is often overlooked.

    Inspecting and installing virtual machines, operating systems and complex software packages may prove to be a hurdle to some departments while others deploy various technologies with little effort. The varied protocols, methods, and environments in academic research raises a number of questions about the software they use or intend to use.
    • Can this wildly varied approach to software adoption be sustained in the long term?
    • Should the academic community develop closed standards for different research areas?
    • How much, if at all, should industry be involved in this process?

    These are but a few of the topics that will be debated. Please join us for what will no doubt be a compelling discussion that will directly influence the future of eScience.
