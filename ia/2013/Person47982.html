George Parolalista's Honors and Awards

  • Institutional Medal of Merit

    Santa Casa da Mesericórdia do Seixal (Sacred House of Mercy, Seixal, Portugal)
    • October 2012

    Institutional Medal of Merit in recognition of successful development of the Seixal Futsal project.

    I got involved with this project after meeting a living legend called Carlos Codinha in Milan during the 2009 Homeless World Cup where I was sponsoring the South Africa Team and he was the head coach of the Portugal Team for CAIS (a national charity in Portugal similar to The Big Issue).

    I immediately offered my support to CAIS but they seemed to run a tight ship and didn't really need an external consultant, so Carlos introduced me to his own project in Seixal that needed a bit of attention. I went to Portugal and found out a bit more about him.

    As a child, Carlos found out Mother Teresa was visiting Portugal and decided to force his parents to take him to meet her... he did. Mother Teresa left instructions with local nuns to watch over him because 'he will become a missionary'.

    Carlos worked as a volunteer, between 1986 - 1993 with the Missionary Sisters of Charity (Mother Teresa's gang) in several countries (Portugal, Italy, Morocco and Spain) and he also worked as a missionary with the Fathers of Charity in Rome (Italy) and Tijuana (Mexico) between 1993 and 1996.

    In 1996 he founded the "Casa Alianza Mexico - Mexico City Covenant House" as a Street Educator Project responsible for carrying out activities with children living on the streets in order to create conditions for them to leave that environment and start the process of social reintegration.

    He returned to Portugal in 2004 and took office as socio-cultural project coordinator in Santa Casa da Misericordia of Seixal where he has worked with youth in gang-culture neighbourhoods considered high risk in the South Bank area of the River Tagus, known as Seixal.

    It was an honour to work with Carlos Codinha as well as some incredible youth mentors and futsal coaches (Gonçalo Filipe, Pedro Varela Bola, Leonel Nabais) who made history in winning the Portuguese National Futsal Championship.

  • Homelessness Transition Fund Evaluation Tender Interview Panel

    • February 2013 to Present

    As a member of the Evaluation Sub Group and an expert by experience, HomelessLink felt I would provide an important perspective on the bids, and one which they decided should form part of the decision making process.

    The purpose of this evaluation is to assess the achievements to date of the grants awarded under the HTF Main Grants and Future Ready programmes between 2011 and 2014.

    The evaluation will cover three main areas:

    Achievement of the HTF programme objectives,

    Impact of funding up until December 2014;

    Recommendations for future homelessness policy and funding.


    Within these areas some of the main questions we want to ask are:

    Did the HTF programme achieve its objectives?

    Bearing in mind the challenges of attribution, how successfully has the HTF programme delivered its overall objective to help end rough sleeping?

    How successfully has the HTF programme supported the roll out of the 'No Second Night Out' Standard across England?

    How successfully has the HTF programme supported strategically critical services for single homeless people and in what areas?

    How successfully has the HTF programme supported transition for existing services to sustainable funding arrangements?

    Which interventions and transition approaches were most effective in helping HTF achieve its outcomes?

    Has HTF funding created or strengthened working partnerships across organisations and geographies?

    What lessons can we learn about supporting interventions?

    What good practice and lessons learned have there been through HTF funding?

    What successful projects which could be scaled up or replicated?

    What have we learned from interventions that have tackled specific issues or followed a specific approach?

  • Gemini News Service

    • March 1993 to July 1993

    Given my first break as an Editorial Assistant at what is now considered the legendary Gemini News Service. The London-based agency, which focused on news from the developing world for more than three decades, went out of business in 2002.

    A proud history

    Gemini News Service aimed to improve people’s understandings of the global north and south through good journalism.

    I was lucky to have front-row access to founder, Derek Ingram, as he filled me in on the unique aspects of his idea for Gemini to publish features that “fill in” people’s understanding of the developing world and growing a huge thumbnail library in the process.

    "He thought that most papers were full of catastrophes, coups and cyclones and one needed to help people get behind or below the news to understand why these things happened and spot trends.” Clyde Sanger.

    “Gemini was always focusing on the alternative angles and news. It was as much about explaining the global south to the north as explaining the strange things about the north to the south.” Alan Martin.

    With these objectives in mind Gemini mailed out packages of its writers features to its subscribers throughout the world - I was lucky enough to be part of the process of receiving reports via satellite from correspondents all over the world and adjusting the English and packaging them with a graphic thumbnail and dispatching them to the majors.

    Part of what made it unique in the international media landscape was its writers. Local journalists, local perspectives.

    “For all of the remarkable advances in media technology since Gemini stopped publishing there are still so many stories not being told, so many parts of the world that major news agencies simply don’t cover." Allan Thompson
