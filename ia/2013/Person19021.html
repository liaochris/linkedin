Luke Butcher's Summary

Luke Butcher is a designer with an interest in the built environment and author of The Architecture of the Profession. He is currently a Thouron Scholar the University of Pennsylvania, School of Design, studying towards a Masters in Environmental Building Design (MEBD). Prior to this Luke studied at the Manchester School of Architecture between 2006 and 2012 and currently holds a first-class BA (Hons) Architecture, BArch with Distinction and MA Architecture + Urbanism with Distinction. He has previously contributed to Notes on Metamodernism and Look Up Manchester.

Luke Butcher's Projects

  • Performative Urbanism

    • October 2011 to Present
    Team Members: Luke Butcher

    Performative Urbanism proposes six tactics (seeding, erasure, repair, belts, towers and adaptation) to provide phased development that is socially responsible, culturally aware and both fiscally and ecologically sustainable within the dynamic processes shaping Stoke-on-Trent today and in the future. Instead of proposing an urban core a dispersed field of targeted investment is put forward to develop new synergistic ecologies between the six towns. Stoke-on-Trent is thus reconstituted as a matrix of hybrid landscapes that seek to constantly utilize and then optimise multiple performance specialisms. Located on the edge of Fenton, a former clay pit offers an opportunity to test these strategies. A new tower is tasked with condensing multiple programmes, principally sport and materials science research, so that new unforeseen synergies can develop between the two.

  • Vedado Secondary School

    • February 2011 to March 2011
    Team Members: Luke Butcher, Carrie Bayley, Elke Schungel, Ana-Luise Ibarra

    Cuba is in the process of restructuring it’s schools (2011). Attempts are being made to accommodate three main school types within the city centre and bring education services closer to the urban population. One possible site is situated within Vedado, where the characteristic 100m x 100m grid interfaces with the main coastal road, the Malecon. This site already acts as a social condenser, being close the United States Office of Interests and the city’s main funeral home. Into this area of contrasting human emotions a new secondary school is proposed, with 280 students (aged 11-15) across three years. The secondary school reacts to the existing urban fabric and environmental conditions. Due to seasonal flooding, from coastal surges in the hurricane season, the school is raised above a plinth of adaptable space. It thus becomes a social beacon in the community that can act as a catalyst for further transformation. There is the potential for the space between the supporting columns to be appropriated and absorb existing social functions. The school is structured formally and programmatically by three elements, with these arranged so that a central ‘Heart’ space can provide public functions (library, dining, social space). These services are seen as the first elements of new infrastructural elements within the community that increases the scope of the services offered by a traditional secondary school.

  • Ravensbury Square

    • October 2008 to May 2009
    Team Members: Luke Butcher

    Ravensbury Square is a response to the problems of urban and socio-economic decay occurring in the peripheral areas of Manchester City Centre, focusing on a community of East Manchester - West Clayton. The specific site was the subject of a Compulsory Purchase Order in 2008 and the Victorian terraces that once occupied it have since been demolished. It occupies a key location within the community, adjacent to primary link roads and the Ravensbury Primary School. Despite recent investment in the area the quality of housing and the overall built environment remains poor. Ravensbury Square is a proposal that, through its 54 dwellings and new public spaces, could raise the standard of the built environment within West Clayton. The 54 homes consist of a mixture of 1, 2 and 3 bedroom properties that look to challenge the preconceptions of affordable housing, calling for a new adaptive typology to meet the challenges of contemporary lifestyles. A flexible, adaptive density, allows residents to appropriate the home, developing them as and when required.

Luke Butcher's Publications

  • The Architecture of the Profession

    • Luke Butcher
    • October 4, 2011
    Authors: Luke Butcher

    The Architecture of the Profession is the title of a research project undertaken at the Manchester School of Architecture. It is intended to provoke thought and debate within the wider architectural community, questioning the current model of practice in the UK through historical analysis of both the image and role of architects and an extensive industry survey; revealing a conflicted professional landscape. Prospective forms of practice emerging now will inform the future production of the built environment. There is the potential for an expanded field of architects who will achieve more through greater engagement with both clients and wider society. This new profession must be open to change, optimistic and ready to rise to the challenges facing the world.

  • The Architecture of the Profession Essay

    • Barton Willmore
    • January 26, 2012
    Authors: Luke Butcher

    Luke Butcher was invited by Barton Willmore to contribute an essay on the current state of the architectural profession as part of a book celebetrating their 7th anniversary as a practice, 75. The essay sets out some of the key points made in The Architecture of the Profession and is intended to act as a reference point for the practice when reviewing the long term objectives of their design business as a whole.
