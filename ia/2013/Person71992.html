Kare Anderson's Summary

As an Emmy-winning former NBC and Wall Street Journal reporter I feel passionate about supporting people in becoming more credible, compelling advocates in how they speak, move and appear.

I was a founding board member of Annie’s Homegrown; co-founder of nine PACs; Pacific Telesis’ Cable TV Wideband Division’s first director; and a state-senator’s chief-of-staff.

My clients are as diverse as athletes, surgeons, COOs and startup founders. The root of their new power is not in what they do but rather in what they want to accomplish. What they often share an ardent desire to use best talents around sweet spots of mutual interest.

As an author of: “Moving from Me to We,” “Walk Your Talk,” and “Resolving Conflicts Sooner,” I am co-founder with Lori Ruff, of the video-enhanced ebook publishing imprint, “Mutuality Matters.”

My most popular programs are: “Becoming the unforgettable face of your organization,” “Grow faster with profitable partnerships,” “Storyboard your experience,” and “Turn employees into ardent, articulate ambassadors of your brand.”

Let me help you be more frequently quoted and connective with others to attract greater opportunity and friendship in all parts of your life.

Here are three ways I can support your success:

1. Speak at your conference
2. Coach you, your employees and/or customers face-to-face or by phone
3. Provide actionable insights in my:
. ♦ Books: "Moving From Me to We,” “Resolving Conflict Sooner,” and “Walk Your Talk”
. ♦ Columns: “Quotable & Connective” on Forbes & Huffington Post
. ♦ Blog: "Moving From Me to We"
. ♦ Twitter: @kareanderson and @MutualityBooks or #MutualityMatters

Feel free to contact me at http://SayItBetter.com.

    • Plume

    Authors: Kare Anderson

    From Publishers Weekly:
    Anderson, a California-based communications consultant, here proposes a sensible, three-step model for negotiating that aims to satisfy all concerned parties. Rather than overpowering and outsmarting the opposing side, Anderson counsels negotiators to determine exactly what their party wants, to identify the needs and desires of the other party, and finally to suggest a compromise. Fleshing out this outline are 100 specific techniques ("Start Out Right; Make a Good First Impression," "Stay Flexible," etc.) and exercises designed to translate the techniques to the reader's own situation. This manual is distinguished from others of its type by its particularly illustrative and imaginative examples, which are certain to broaden and quicken negotiating strategies in both business and social settings.
