Matthew DalSanto, Ph.D.'s Summary

I am an economist and attorney specializing in antitrust and unfair competition law, antitrust economics, intellectual property law, complex commercial litigation, public policy law and economics, and the law and economics of federalism.

Previously, I was a lecturer in the economics department at the University of Minnesota and an adjunct lecturer in economics at Hamline University and Macalester College. I also had the opportunity to work as a postdoctoral research fellow at the University of California, Berkeley's Center for Law, Business and the Economy prior to commencing my legal practice.

Recently, my practice has focused on representing multinational technology companies against allegations of cartel activity. I have also assisted with antitrust counseling matters and representing defendants in cases involving issues at the intersection of antitrust and intellectual property law, including claims related to parties' duties as current and former members of standard setting organizations. I assist with the development of my cases' economic arguments and act as the liaison between the lawyers and the expert economists whom we retain.

Matthew DalSanto, Ph.D.'s Publications

  • The Economics of Horizontal Government Cooperation

    Authors: Matthew DalSanto, Ph.D.

    This paper analyzes the economic properties of intrastate and interstate cooperative agreements. These agreements are commonly referred to as compacts or joint powers agreements (intrastate compacts). The compact mechanism allows regional governments to enter into contractual agreements with one another to coordinate policy choices and to engage in cooperative endeavors. Given the inter-jurisdictional nature of the issues that affect horizontally situated governments, this mechanism is a powerful tool to achieve welfare-enhancing outcomes for citizens.

    A review of the legal case law surrounding compacts is conducted to analyze the legal properties from an economic perspective. These economic properties are used to develop a simple game-theoretic environment and a neoclassical growth model. In these two environments, behavior under the compact is compared with regional governments acting in a non-cooperative manner where they take the period actions of the other governments as given. The models demonstrate that the compact mechanism can lead to superior outcomes for the signatory governments' citizenry.
