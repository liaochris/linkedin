Aaron Falk's Summary

I am an engineer, architect, and product manager in the area of communication networks.

I lead small teams to quickly prototype solutions to Internet infrastructure problems.

I look at problems from a systems perspective, keeping the focus on the needs of the customer and user to identify design, execution, programmatic, and social risks.

I excel at communicating across disciplinary and tribal boundaries, for example bridging customers/engineering, software/hardware, development/testing, and management/engineering.

I am an expert and experienced cat-herder, creating and managing distributed cross-organizational technical communities.

I have a history of accomplishments -- including five patents and several innovative, deployed systems -- in Internet research and development with a focus performance and transport solutions.

I have a strong track record where research meets standards as chair of the Internet Research Task Force, working group chair in the IETF, and membership on the Internet Architecture Board (IAB) and RFC Editorial Board.

I'm very proud to have worked on commercial projects at Verivue, PanAmSat, and Hughes Space & Communications and government-funded projects at BBN Technologies, USC Information Sciences Institute, and TRW Space & Defense.

I was the technical lead for National Science Foundation's (NSF's) Global Environment for Network Innovation (GENI). GENI is a effort to design and build a distributed testbed for network research with a particular focus on providing the scale and flexibility to support future Internet architecture development. In my role, I directed a distributed development effort across a multitude of academic/industrial teams taking the project from conceptual definition to a working prototype supporting experimental use. I identified technical risks and developing mitigation plans; established GENI Engineering conference; defined conference session goals and agenda; set direction for over 70 design and prototyping subcontracts (worth over $40M), assessed subcontract progress and made recommendations for continued funding; and ensured that GENI’s development plans were satisfactory to the community.

Ex-officio member

Internet Architecture Board (IAB)

2005 – April 2011 (6 years)

The IAB is chartered both as a committee of the Internet Engineering Task Force (IETF) and as an advisory body of the Internet Society (ISOC). Its responsibilities include architectural oversight of IETF activities, Internet Standards Process oversight and appeal, and the appointment of the RFC Editor. The IAB is also responsible for the management of the IETF protocol parameter registries. The IRTF chair holds an ex-official membership in the IAB. In my role, I provided a research prospective to the IAB on topics such as BoF and working group creation and RFC Editor policy.

Internet Research Task Force (IRTF)

Nonprofit; 201-500 employees; Research industry

March 2005 – April 2011 (6 years 2 months)

The Internet Research Task force (IRTF) focuses on longer term research issues related to the Internet while the parallel organization, the Internet Engineering Task Force (IETF), focuses on the shorter term issues of engineering and standards making. As IRTF Chair, I was responsible for ensuring that Research Groups produce coherent, coordinated, architecturally consistent and timely output as a contribution to the overall evolution of the Internet architecture. Aaron Falk's Publications

  • Definition of an Internet Research Task Force (IRTF) Document Stream

    • RFC Editor
    • December 2009
    Authors: Aaron Falk

    This memo defines the publication stream for RFCs from the Internet Research Task Force. Most documents undergoing this process will come from IRTF Research Groups, and it is expected that they will be published as Informational or Experimental RFCs by the RFC Editor.

  • Naming Rights in IETF Protocols

    • RFC Editor
    • April 1, 2008
    Authors: Aaron Falk

    This document proposes a new revenue source for the IETF to support standardization activities: protocol field naming rights, i.e., the association of commercial brands with protocol fields. This memo describes a process for assignment of rights and explores some of the issues associated with the process. Individuals or organizations that wish to purchase naming rights for one or more protocol fields are expected to follow this process.
