Martin Houston's Summary

I initially intended to be a Biochemist but the first year I was at University the Personal Computer revolution started.My uncle had bought a ZX81 and I was hooked! I Took what programming courses were available as part of my degree and found myself leaning Unix on the QMC PDP11. That gives me over 30 years of Unix experience, not many people can say that!

I found I had more interest in computers than the original course of study and thanks to the flexibility of the course unit system emerged with a BSc 2-1 Honours in Computer Science and Biology.

My first job was obtained for me by my tutor, Colin Lowe, The Hommerton Hospital in Hackney had approached QMC for someone to write software for their newly purchased Sage IV microcomputer. My biochemistry background made me the obvious candidate. The Sage was A 68000 processor powered system using the USCD P-System - an early example of a 'pseudo-code' based system later popularised by Java.

My job was to write software to acquire data from a gel electrophoresis machine over the IEE448 bus. The previous system in use was a Commodore PET so the Sage was quite a leap in processing power!

The job at the hospital was only temporary but stood me in good stead to choose where I wanted to go in my first permanent job. I chose Northern Telecom (Now known as Nortel). (for the rest see below...)

My Goals?

I am returning to the freelance life because I want to be in a position of maximum flexability to use my talents to help in some small way with the crisis that is gripping our planet and threatening our very existence.

I like to see computers being made to do what is needed. To do the mental equivalent of the heavy lifting without having to be so complex to use that we humans have to be in fear of them. Open Source together with the incredible search-ability of the internet has the potential to take software development out of being a black art.

The photo is of me with Tux the Linux penguin.

Specialties

Linux, Perl, python, Internet, firewalls, rapid software development for problem solving!, kernel internals /device drivers.

Xenix software developer.

Apricot Computers

Privately Held; 1-10 employees; Computer Hardware industry

1985 – 1989 (4 years)

I developed the Xenix system software for the Apricot Xen - an advanced NON IBM Compatible 286 based system. Being non IBM compatible (pre PC-AT) meant that many device drivers had to be written from scratch by our small team. A single Xen with terminal cards was capable of supporting a dozen or more users.

The Xen was also a very early platform for Microsoft Windows as well as Microsoft Xenix.

When IBM produced the PC-AT and other manufacturers like Compaq followed suit lack of IBM PC compatability was harming the prospects of an otherwise brilliant (for the time) machine.

The company produced the Xen-i - a PC-AT compatible system that looked like the Xen but was considerably inferior in performance. For instance the ability to choose a modern at the time floppy disk controller meant that the 3.5" floppy drives on the Xen rotated faster than the 'PC' ones and were as fast as the hard disk was on an original PC-XT!

Being PC compatible meant the standard SCO Xenix product was usable and the need for the dedicated development team diminished.

The company wanted its Xenix development staff to retrain to OS/2 but we all decided to resign instead.

Martin Houston's Publications

  • Graphvis visualisation of Nagios Config

    • Nagios Exchange website
    • July 13, 2012
    Authors: Martin Houston

    Graphvis visualisation of complex Nagios configs.
    I have written a couple of utilities one to walk a nagios config tree and turn it into a perl hash (called nagparse)and another to use that hash converting the tree into a graphvis directed graph (called graphit).
