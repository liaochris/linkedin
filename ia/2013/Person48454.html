Greg Herlein's Experience

Vice President Engineering

Cognitive Networks

Privately Held; 11-50 employees; Consumer Electronics industry

September 2013 – Present (2 months) San Francisco Bay Area

Cognitive Networks provides automatic content recognition (ACR) in SmartTV sets using a very lightweight client that communicates with our cloud-based systems to detect exactly what video is playing in real-time on the TV. This enables us to trigger events to apps on the TV to enable real engagement synchronized with the video content - at the same time gathering data on what is really being watched. Our technology heavily uses python (especially twisted), C, ffmpeg, and a variety of big data solutions - all running in Amazon AWS. We're doing over a million transactions a second already and we've only just begun.

Recently consulted with Brightsign (formerly part of Roku), Technicolor and THX on various media projects. Current product designs include a low power radio telemetry system (using Arduino) and a robotic underwater ROV (using Raspberry Pi as a network gateway for live streaming video) aimed at the education markets.

Director, Software and Product Development

Rackspace Hosting

Public Company; 5001-10,000 employees; RAX; Information Technology and Services industry

May 2012 – February 2013 (10 months) San Francisco Bay Area

I joined Rackspace while they were transitioning their product organization and managed the final stages of development and deployment of their new web-based cloud control panel (https://mycloud.rackspace.com). That product team was split across the San Francisco CA and Blacksburg VA offices. I managed the successful launch and then transitioned product responsibility to the Blacksburg team. I then owned full product development and operations responsibilities for two product areas: auto-scale and mobile. The Mobile team delivers iOS and Android apps to enable customers to control their cloud from their devices and provides SDKs for custom development by third parties. The product teams used continuous integration/continuous deployment using a modified kanban agile approach. Notable technologies used (aside from the Rackspace cloud) include Twisted Python, Cassandra, Chef, and Cocoapods.

Vice President, Engineering

IZ-ON (formerly PRN, a Technicolor company)

May 2011 – May 2012 (1 year 1 month) San Francisco Bay Area

Premier Retail Networks is the world’s leader in the production, delivery and operation of digital out-of-home (DOOH) advertising networks. I joined them in 2004 to manage the development and deployment of their CheckoutTV system. I architected the IPTV-based Wal-Mart ‘Smart Network’ – still the largest retail advertising network in the world – and managed it’s successful development and deployment to production. That network delivers nearly 4 billion media plays per month – all analyzed in Hadoop. After developing that network I went on to manage all R&D efforts. My team developed prototype media playback systems using ATSC datacasting as well as low-power ATSC ‘white-space’broadcast technology. I then became Vice President and managed all Engineering, QA and professional services locally and offshore. As a member of the Executive Management team I was responsible for all technology related matters for the business including outward facing business development efforts. My responsibilities also included driving the Intellectual Property program. I devised our patent strategy and personally filed over 30 patent applications and testified for the company in patent litigation.

PRN’s SaaS offering used a Java-based technology stack (especially around SPRING) emphasizing REST/XML web services. Core databases were Oracle with significant LAMP (PHP/Linux/Apach/MysSQL) at the edge, including cloud-based offerings (hosted on the Rackspace cloud). Big data analysis was done with Hadoop and Splunk. Configuration management used Puppet. User facing technologies included javascript, AJAX, CSS, Flex, DHTML, SMIL, and MPEG playback. On-premise systems used IPTV streams as well as HTTP-fetch based MPEG playback.

Director, Engineering

IZ-ON (formerly PRN, a Technicolor company)

Consulting Senior Software Engineer

Genesys Telecommunications Labs, Inc.

Privately Held; 1001-5000 employees; Computer Software industry

July 2000 – June 2003 (3 years)

While consulting at Alcatel Genesys I owned the development of the Meridian and Symposium T-Server product. This product was written in C/C++ for all major versions of Windows and Unix and provided network control for the call flows for large-scale call centers. The product was deployed into multiple major banks and financial institutions. I redesigned the core product and refactored its aging code base to eliminate nearly 100 critical defects and provided a new high-availability/automatic failover version of the product. This refactoring required that I design and implement a full regression test suite to ensure that behavior was consistent after the rewrite. In addition, I created a tool used by support that would take customer logs and generate test cases for the framework to enable automated reproduction of complex customer-location specific failures for much faster responses to the very large customers using the product. These efforts led directly to a major sale to Korea Telecom yielding a multi-million dollar revenue gain in the final quarter of 2002 – a substantial impact during that recession period.

Director, Engineering

Quicknet Technologies, Inc.

May 1999 – June 2000 (1 year 2 months)

Quicknet created the PhoneJack and LineJack low cost PCI telephony cards (both FXO and FXS) and software for low-cost or free Voice over IP calls. I joined Quciknet to lead their ‘MicroTelco’ project. In that role I hired and managed a staff of 18, including Software Engineers and web developers, network engineers, DBAs, and support staff. MicroTelco was a business model to enable the use of low-cost PC systems running Linux and Quicknet’s telephone line interface cards to create an ecosystem for Voice over IP (VoIP) calls. Customers could create a very low cost telephony switching endpoint and Quicknet would create the ‘network’ to match calls to destinations and make money on the arbitrage. This model required a secure Linux implementation with extremely reliable drivers for our custom hardware. I co-designed and co-authored the telephony subsystem and successfully open sourced our drivers and got them accepted into the Linux kernel. Quicknet's technology stack also included highly scalable LAMP server farms and extensive MySQL server clusters.
