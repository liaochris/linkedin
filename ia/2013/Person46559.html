Gabriel Baird's Summary

Win or lose, we want to know why. This is as true in our personal lives and sports as it is in business. Knowing empowers us to improve.

I designed, launched and run Hyland Software's Win/Loss Program. I investigate why deals are lost and won by interviewing external decision makers, consulting with internal stakeholders and analyzing CRM data to glean actionable insights to help us win.

To do this, I leverage my award-winning journalism skills. News outlets across the country have published more than 1,000 of my articles. These articles have improved public policy, triggered mass firings and resignations, sparked two criminal prosecutions and identified $21 million in lost revenue.

The transition from journalism to business has been exciting. However, the most challenging project of my career remains a six-month investigation, written on tight deadlines and published in Ohio’s largest newspaper.

This project required diving into an unfamiliar organization, identifying and earning the confidence of subject-matter experts and then learning convoluted processes governed by complex policies and state laws.

I scrutinized more than 30,000 documents by hand, identifying policy breaches and illegal alterations. I then analyzed databases containing more than 4 million records.

The findings, coupled with information extracted from extensive interviews conducted by me and my team members, were detailed in more than 30 articles written on deadline over a six-month period and published on the front-page of The Plain Dealer and cleveland.com, which together reach more than 1.4 million weekly readers.

The project won statewide awards and was a finalist for a national prize.

Since 2012, I have leveraged the skills that fueled the success of this journalistic project to pinpoint breakdowns in sales cycles, refine sales presentations, isolate best practices, analyze business data sets and identify areas ripe for revenue growth.

  • Revenue Lost

    • The Plain Dealer
    • February 20, 2011
    Authors: Gabriel Baird

    Calculating the cost of corruption and mismanagement was not only a challenging exercise in computer-assisted reporting, it also required me to digest the complex workings of the state’s property tax laws. After hours of interviews with officials, I wrote a step-by-step summary of how I understood the system to work. This summary was way too detailed for the newspaper.   • Many Russo donors got tax breaks

    • http://gabrielbaird.com/Stories/Clips_as_PDFs/donors_benefit_20101128.pdf

  • Plain Dealer investigation uncovers thousands of miscalculated bills

    • The Plain Dealer
    • September 23, 2010
    Authors: Gabriel Baird

    One of the most challenging analyses I have performed was figuring out how much property owners were ripped off on their taxes because of problems with a little-known but very powerful county agency, the board of revision. I started with a database of 95,844 complaints to the board and pulled from it all cases from 2008 where taxpayers succeeded in getting their property values reduced. I then joined these cases with a database of more than 1 million records detailing the appraised value of every property in the county between 2002 and 2009. Every case where a property's 2009 value was higher than the 2008 reduced value represented a likely case of a taxpayer being overtaxed. Lacking a database of each parcel's taxing district, I used ArcGIS to map the nearly 8,000 cases I had identified and then did a spatial join to match up parcel numbers and tax districts. After exporting the results to a database, I updated the records with the year's tax rates and arrived at how much property owners were possibly overcharged on their taxes -- upwards of $7 million. My analysis also found that more than half of the overcharged taxpayers did not file new complaints. County attorneys are investigating whether the county owes it to these people to refund overpayments. (I did the data analysis; Plain Dealer reporters Mark Puente and Henry J. Gomez shared in the reporting and writing.)

  • Illegal Actions

    • The Plain Dealer
    • August 22, 2010
    Authors: Gabriel Baird

    As part of a continuing investigation into the county's boards of revision, I obtained and analyzed a database of more than 78,000 complaints filed by property owners (later updated to include more than 95,000 cases). I found that the property values of nearly 26,000 properties were decided without giving property owners a hearing. In all, these decisions shaved more than $400 million from the county tax rolls. After publication of this story, attorneys for the county concluded that most of the cases violated state law. This opened the door to property owners to have their cases heard. (Plain Dealer reporter Mark Puente shared in the reporting and writing.)

    • The Plain Dealer
    • August 1, 2010
    Authors: Gabriel Baird

    A criminal investigation was launched after fellow Plain Dealer reporter Mark Puente and I reported that a county board of revision member was working a second job during hours he was paid to work for the county. The exciting shoe leather reporting for this story started after I analyzed county parking-garage data and found that the board member averaged just three hours a day at the county building.     • January 14, 2007
    Authors: Gabriel Baird

    I fought for months to make Cleveland officials comply with the state's public records laws and release public records on officers' uses of force.
