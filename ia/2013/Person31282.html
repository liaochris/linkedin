David G. Ross' Summary

I’m a franchise lawyer and business lawyer who is licensed in Maryland, Washington, D.C., and New Jersey – but who has represented clients in various state and federal courts around the country in 15+ years of practice. My work for these clients has exposed me to numerous industries (e.g., hotel, fast-food, retail, senior care, accounting, and health club), franchise systems (or “franchise brands”), and issues.

The Ross Law Firm, which I founded in 2007, offers a variety of services to franchisees and independent business owners. We provide advice on new business opportunities and ventures (including reviews of franchise disclosure documents and franchise contracts), represent clients in transactions such as lease negotiations and sales of businesses, resolve and prevent avoidable disputes (including those involving franchise law and employment law issues), and zealously represent clients in lawsuits and arbitration proceedings.

Litigation, in fact, is our greatest passion. Despite our small size, we have successfully battled big law firms in complex, large-scale matters. We have handled issues related to, among other things, franchise disclosure laws, franchise terminations, and royalties. Moreover, we have years of experience litigating “business law” issues such as breach of contract, unfair competition, misappropriation of trade secrets, non-competes, antitrust, fraud, and consumer protection.

David G. Ross' Publications

  • The Moving Target: System Standards and the Franchisor's "Sole Discretion" to Change Franchisee Obligations

    • franchisehelp.com
    • August 29, 2011
    Authors: David G. Ross, Jeffrey Fabian

    Franchise Agreements typically provide franchisors with virtually unfettered discretion to revise system standards -- and thereby change the rules governing their franchise relationships -- however and whenever they please. This is dangerous to franchisee interests.

  • Is the Protected Territory Really "Protected"?

    • November 18, 2011
    Authors: David G. Ross

    In many franchise agreements, the "protected territory" clause is so watered-down (and/or easy to lose) that it provides the franchisee with insufficient protection from intrabrand competition.

  • Mandatory Arbitration -- Advantage Franchisor

    • December 5, 2011
    Authors: David G. Ross

    Franchisors often tout the mandatory arbitration clause as a mutually beneficial provision, but I find arbitration unfair to franchisees.

  • False Complaint of Discrimination? Don't Retaliate Against the Employee

    • December 8, 2011
    Authors: David G. Ross

    It doesn't matter that your employee's discrimination complaint lacked merit. If you respond by firing, demoting, or otherwise punishing him/her, you could face liability for "unlawful retaliation."

  • Protect Your Trade Secrets (or Else the Courts Might Not)

    • December 8, 2011
    Authors: David G. Ross

    The Uniform Trade Secrets Act, adopted by all but a few states, provides recourse to business owners whose valuable trade secrets have been misappropriated. In order to qualify for this protection, though, you must demonstrate that you took reasonable efforts to maintain secrecy.

  • Be Wary of an Oppressive Franchise Agreement -- Even if You Trust the Franchisor

    • February 8, 2012
    Authors: David G. Ross

    Beware of a franchisor's assurance that onerous contractual provisions are simply mandated "by the legal department" and that power is actually wielded fairly in practice. If the franchisor didn't want to at least reserve the right to enforce unfair, one-sided clauses, those clauses wouldn't even be in agreement. Besides, there's no guarantee that the "fair" people running the franchise system today are the same people who will run it in the future.

  • Preventing "Hostile Environment" Harassment in the Workplace -- and Avoiding Liability

    • February 21, 2012
    Authors: David G. Ross

    In order to avoid liability for "hostile environment" harassment, an employer must be aware of the applicable laws, set down rule to ensure compliance with those laws, remain familiar with its OWN rules, and actually apply those rules in practice.

  • "Put It in Writing": Why It's Important for Business Owners to Document their Communications and Agreements

    • March 7, 2012
    Authors: David G. Ross

    Don't over-rely on oral "understandings" and handshake agreements. Written documentation can be the key to preventing, resolving, or winning legal disputes.

  • Terminated Franchise? Avoid Inadvertent Trademark Infringement

    • March 21, 2012
    Authors: David G. Ross

    Unless given express written permission or direction to do so, a terminated franchisee must immediately stop using and displaying ANYTHING and EVERTYTHING that contains the franchisor's trademarks. Failure to follow this rule could lead to dire consequences.

  • Wage Payment and Collection Laws: Don't Withhold Employee Paychecks!

    • April 4, 2012
    Authors: David G. Ross

    Don't withhold employee paychecks, even if you think the employee is slacking off. Depending on your particular state's laws, you might be held liable for double or triple the amount of the check.

  • Competition's the American Way -- But "Tortious Interference" Could Get You Sued

    • April 18, 2012
    Authors: David G. Ross

    A business owner should understand the line that separates aggressive, lawful competition from conduct amounting to "tortious" or "wrongful" interference. Use of certain forbidden tactics could lead to liability.

  • "Uniform Standards" Provision of Universal Franchisee Bill of Rights is Not So Franchisee-Friendly

    • May 2, 2012
    Authors: David G. Ross

    Franchisees, be careful what you wish for.

  • Restrictive Covenants in Employment: Preventing Key Employees from Unfairly Competing

    • May 16, 2012
    Authors: David G. Ross

    Before giving employees access to sensitive information, make them sign appropriate "restrictive covenants."

  • Business About to Be Sued? Don't Destroy the "Harmful" Documents!

    • May 30, 2012
    Authors: David G. Ross

    Courts don't look kindly on "spoliation." A litigant who hides or destroys evidence is playing with fire.

  • One Man's Catchy Ad Campaign is Another Man's Consumer Protection Violation

    • June 13, 2012
    Authors: David G. Ross

    Avoid inadvertent "false advertising." This could result in liability under the Federal Trade Commission Act or a state "Consumer Protection Act."

  • The Negligent Hiring Dilemma (Part I)

    • June 9, 2013
    Authors: David G. Ross

    Be careful whom you hire. If you knew or should've known that your employee posed an unnecessary danger to coworkers or customers, you risk liability for negligent hiring.

  • The Negligent Hiring Dilemma (Part II)

    • June 24, 2013
    Authors: David G. Ross

    Those who tell you to vigilantly avoid "negligent hiring" mistakes are correct -- but they often tell you only half the story. If you're not careful, your pre-hiring safeguards might amount to inadvertent violations of anti-discrimination laws.
