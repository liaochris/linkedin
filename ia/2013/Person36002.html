David Peeler's Publications

  • A Molecular Dynamics Investigation of the Impact of Surface Chemistry on the Binding of Surrogate Human Norovirus Proteins

    • BMES Annual Meeting
    • October 26, 2012
    Authors: David Peeler, Silvina Matysiak

    Poster session and published abstract.
    Shortened abstract below:

    Pathogenic viruses can be readily transmitted via fomite surfaces and thus the adsorption and desorption of virus particles with fomites is of scientific interest. The goal of this research is to build a mechanistic understanding of the interactions between viruses and substrates with various surface chemistries. We will integrate molecular simulations with experimental research at different spatial and temporal scales to build a roadmap bridging microscopic to macroscopic properties of viruses binding to surfaces. The experimental and simulation results will be compared to assess if simulations can accurately predict the relative adsorption behavior of several virus strains on surfaces with defined chemistries. The ultimate goal of this work is to develop predictive models that describe the adsorption and desorption characteristics of non-culturable virus strains that cannot be tested in vitro.

David Peeler's Projects

  • Microscopic properties of virus-fomite interaction

    • December 2011 to Present
    Team Members: David Peeler, Dr. Krista Wigginton

    Human norovirus (HNV) is a major cause of food born illness since only a low dose is necessary for infection and it is resilient to harsh environmental conditions and disinfection methods. The protein associated with HNV does not have its structure solved for so I am working with a norovirus surrogate, the feline capsid calicivirus. I am using coarse grained molecular modeling of the protein to better understand how the protein binds to hydrophobic and hydrophilic surfaces and the human cellular membrane. This work will contribute to better methods to prevent and treat HNV infection in humans by understanding the binding behavior of the capsid protein to various surfaces and the cellular membrane.

  • DMR investigaton of CF epigenome

    • May 2011 to July 2011
    Team Members: David Peeler

    Investigated elements of differentially methylated regions identified in MZ twins with Cystic Fibrosis with a simple comparison of the USC online genome methylation database and a recent GWAS study from Dr. Gary Cutting.

  • Capstone Design Project - Mucous Sensing Tracheal Stent

    • September 2012 to Present
    Team Members: David Peeler, Sara Johnson, Kenneth Rosenberg, Matthew Black

    Apply dubbed STENTSOR (TM) Technology, this device is being developed to detect the relative amount of mucous build-up in a tracheal stent. Often, patients with tracheal stents have mucosal build-up inside the stent which leads to sudden clogging of the airway and an emergency procedure to remove the stent and replace it. The goal of the device is to detect a dangerous level of mucous build-up in the stent using capacitor plates lined along the edges and to notify the user with telemetric communication. This allows for a scheduled cleaning procedure which should not result in stent replacement.
