Lisa Lone Fight's Summary

Lisa Lone Fight (an enrolled citizen of the Mandan, Hidatsa and Arikara (Sahnish) Nation) represents the emergence of a new wave of culturally centered/connected indigenous scientists. Lisa is an experienced speaker/educator who has researched and presented extensively on the intersections of indigenous and western science. Her current research interest focuses on mapping and creating convergences among geo-spatial sciences, traditional ecological/cultural knowledge, and land change on Indigenous lands. Lisa trained in indigenous science methodology with Maybelle Good Bird and Edward Lone Fight; direct descendents of Maxi'diwiac (Buffalo Bird Woman) and studies Remote Sensing in the Department of Land Resources and Environmental Science at Montana State University in Bozeman. Recognizing Lisa’s innovative work on informal science education in indigenous communities, Lisa was part of a select group of scholars, elders and knowledge keepers featured in the Emmy award winning PBS documentary “Before There Were Parks”. Her most recent publication is a forth coming chapter in the ESRI publication “Tribal GIS”.

Specialties

INDIGENOUS SCIENCE: Board Member of The Indigenous Mapping Network. Trained at Google (Mountain View, CA) in Indigenous Mapping technology. Trained in Indigenous evaluation models.

INFORMAL SCIENCE EDUCATION: Directed, Wind River Native Science Field Center. Developed indigenous science curriculum and learning activities with tribal elders and cultural experts.

Lisa Lone Fight's Publications

  • Effective practices for creating transformative informal science education programs grounded in Native ways of knowing

    • CULTURAL STUDIES OF SCIENCE EDUCATION Volume 7, Number 1 (2012), 49-70, DOI: 10.1007/s11422-011-9374-y
    • January 12, 2012
    Authors: Lisa Lone Fight, ...Lone Fight Lisa Shibata Hi’ilani Davíd Dominique Sachatello-Sawyer Bonnie Different Cloud-Jones Li

    There are a growing number of informal science education (ISE) programs in
    Native communities that engage youth in science education and that are grounded in
    Native ways of knowing. There is also a growing body of research focusing on the
    relationship between culture, traditional knowledge, and science education. However, there
    is little research documenting how these programs are being developed and the ways in which culture and Western science are incorporated into the activities. This study outlines
    effective practices for using Native ways of knowing to strengthen ISE programs. These
    effective practices may also be used to promote change in formal education. The authors
    combine an overview of current research in informal science education with personal
    interviews with educators engaged in ISE programs offered to youth both on and off tribal
    reservations as well as experts in Indigenous education. Participating individuals and
    programs included Native communities across the United States, including Alaska and
    Hawai’i. Keeping in mind that each community is unique, ISE programs that are grounded
    in Native ways of knowing will beneﬁt by utilizing the effective practices outlined here as
    a guide for starting or strengthening existing ISE programs relevant to the needs of their
    communities

  • Tribal GIS: Supporting Native American Decision Making

    • ESRI PRESS
    • July 2012
    Authors: Lisa Lone Fight

    "In Tribal GIS: Supporting Native American Decision Making, tribal leaders tell their stories about implementing and using geographic information systems (GIS) to address their unique challenges as sovereign Nations. This book covers applications in natural resources and the environment, transportation, cultural and historical preservation, economic development, health, education, public safety, and agriculture. Showing how tribal governments responsible for the stewardship of their land and resources and the health and well-being of their People use enterprise GIS to make decisions, Tribal GIS supports tribes new to GIS and those with GIS experience."
