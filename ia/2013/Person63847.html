  • Agriculture Tax Shelter

  • Portfolio Restructure Using Treasury Bond Covered-Call Option Program

    • 1984 to 1986
    Team Members: Jim Ed Brown, Michael Dumas, Frank Tibbs, Sigrid Beck-Catanzaro

    I showed Independent American Savings (IAS) an innovative “Covered-Call Program”, which would allow them to write at-the-money Call Options against a $650 million 30-year Treasury Bond portfolio, while I would manage a $50,000,000 liquidity portfolio in order to protect negative cash-flow possibilities.

    Goal
    Use acquired deposits from a Financial Institution Merger or Acquisition to generate enough positive cash flow to pay for the cost of deposits, plus the operations of the acquired branches.

    Obstacle
    1. Using Options on Bond Futures was un-tested and un-proven.
    2. Options on Bond Futures were very new in the financial industry.
    3. The Owners and managers of IAS didn’t understand anything about the product (Options on Treasury Bond Futures); and
    4. IAS didn’t know the “ins and outs” of the Financial Futures Industry.

    Solution
    I led the team that created a Historical Performance Model by using every possible “Market” scenario. I taught IAS the “ins and outs” of the Financial Futures Industry by showing them how to use historical data to predict future results. Then, I explained our unique method of tracking a futures position, and our ability to get superior executions on our futures contract orders.

    The Result in Numbers
    The outcome was IAS gave me the order to execute an innovative "Covered-Call" program on their behalf, and the premium IAS received by writing at-the-money call options enhanced the performance of their Bond Portfolio from a yield of about 13% to over 18%.

    This was an amazing 500 basis points over the yield of the 30-year Treasury Bond. In addition to the Covered-Call Program, IAS assigned me the responsibility to manage a $50 million Treasury Note liquidity portfolio. From December 1984 through March 1986 I personally bought and sold over $5 billion in registered securities on their behalf, which netted them a $55 million profit. 

  • 3rd Source of Funds for Bank Acquisitions

    • 1984 to Present
    Team Members: Jim Ed Brown, Mark Crihfield, Bill Embry

    Goal
    Package and sell an estimated $3 billion in loans a client bank planned to acquire during a multiple-bank acquisition period, and create enough profit from the sales to pay for the acquisitions.

    Obstacle
    Indiana National Bank needed additional capital to acquire their target banks. Using a “3rd Source of Funds” for acquisition was virtually unknown in the Financial Industry.

    Controlling stock holders didn’t understand a “3rd Source of Funds”, and they didn’t know the “ins and outs” of the Private Loan Placement Industry.

    Solution
    I taught INB the ins and outs of a “3rd Source of Funds” for Acquisitions by explaining the needs and wants of investors who bought loans in the Secondary Market through Private Placements.

    We used the legal, regulatory and accounting guidelines, which required a bank to evaluate and price an acquired loan portfolio using FNMA and GNMA guidelines. Then, after the loans were acquired, we dissected the portfolio into many separate smaller portfolios, enhanced the loan packages with pledges from net worth without significantly affecting their balance sheet, and sold the smaller, enhanced packages to Private Investors. This strategy (3rd Source of Funds), created enough profit from selling the acquired loans to almost entirely pay for the acquisitions.

    The Result in Numbers
    The outcome was INB selected me and my Firm to evaluate, acquire, re-package and sell over $3 billion in acquired loans during their 1985 acquisitions. I sold the entire $3 billion in loan offerings on their behalf. My responsibilities included assisting Outside Council for my Firm, with the creation, and explanation of all Representations and Warrants for both the buyers and the sellers.

    This was the first time, (that I am aware of, when a National Bank used a "3rd Source of Funds" for acquisitions, other than the traditional, 1. Infusion of Capital, or 2. A Secondary Stock Offering.
