Bryan Feller's Summary

I completed my undergrad at a small theological school called LIFE Pacific, but I never ended up becoming a pastor. I knew I wasn't ready to be a pastor. Who is ready to be a pastor at 23 years old?

Instead, I found I had a knack for sales and marketing. I got my MBA and was blessed to be a part of some great business turnarounds and rapid growth companies. Eventually, I got tired of working for “The Man” and started a search marketing firm in 2005, and later, a full-service marketing consulting firm. As a consultant with a small team of talented consultants and creatives, I worked on projects ranging from strategic planning, to executive coaching, to marketing communications, to large sales force development initiatives. In short...

■ I’ve turned around a handful of companies, rapidly grown a few, and lost a few.

■ I was instrumental in leading one company to the make the Inc. 500 – twice.

■ I've led strategic consulting projects across industries, in firms ranging from start-ups to the Fortune 500. And not just low-level projects – senior level strategic initiatives with Fortune 500 clients.

■ I've coached executives, CEOs, entrepreneurs, pastors, and of course, my students.

■ I teach business ethics at California Baptist University. I also speak regularly on corporate strategy, sales, marketing, and prayer. And yes, those things do go together.

■ I publish a weekly video newsletter on integrating faith in one’s professional life called Monday Morning Coffee.

■ For over 25 years, I have had hands-on experience in practically every aspect of strategic marketing and sales force development. And I am always on the leading edge of what is new and working in my field.

In my business career, I never lost my heart to be a pastor. And I am still hoping to use my pastor’s heart and mind for business to change the world.

1995 – 2001 (6 years) Chino, CA

Bertolini Inc. was my first “turn-around” assignment. The company manufactures commercial seating for the hotel, church, and institutional markets. When I arrived, the entire staff was on 10% pay reduction, one of a series of measures that had been implemented to keep the company afloat. It was immediately evident that a new sales and marketing model was needed to stabilize and grow the business – immediately.

I quickly saw an opportunity to move the company from an independent sales force who sold face-to-face to an inside sales force that sold over the phone, supported by a lead generation program. With the support of the CEO, I took a big risk on the new model – and it paid off. In my first four years, I led the company from $2M to nearly $20M in annual sales.

■ I built an inside sales team that achieved $2.5M in annual sales per representative, while the industry average was $1M.

■ I executed a lead generation program that increased sales leads from 60 to 900 per month.

■ I led our sales team to improve their close rate of new leads from 10% to 25%.

■ I led a major migration to a new ERP system that integrated accounting, sales, distribution, and manufacturing into a single system.

■ I helped lead the company culture from a start-up to a professionally managed organization.

■ I was named MVP in 2000.

■ Years later, when I started my own consulting firm, I retained Bertolini Inc. as a client.

  • Death by Assumption: Why Great Strategies Fail

    • Resort Management Operations
    • May 2008
    Authors: Bryan Feller

    The absence of “assumption management” is a common cause of the death of many strategic plans. All planning is based on imperfect knowledge and involves assumptions about the future that are based on available data, combined with the experience of the planning team. Most strategic plans assume a certain future, which is a dangerous misconception. The future is always subject to change in crazy, chaotic ways no one could have ever anticipated. Add to this the competitive pace of change in your market and uncertainty increases even more. Unless the planning team has the willingness and flexibility to redefine the assumptions when more knowledge becomes available, its plan is not likely to deliver the expected results...
