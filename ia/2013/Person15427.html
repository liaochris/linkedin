Bob Lannon's Summary

My goal is to work towards new methods of automated information extraction and indexing for search. I can creatively address many problems presented by this task using my background in Linguistics and experience with programming and corpus materials.

The focus of this work, as I see it, is to connect people with information that is highly useful, but possibly "hidden" from them due to the chaotic nature of traditional methods of information storage and retrieval.

An additional, personal goal is to design and implement conduits of information that would make complex information available to non-specialists whose lives are deeply affected by its interpretation.

Undergraduate Researcher

Temple University Infant Lab

Educational Institution; 5001-10,000 employees; Higher Education industry

May 2004 – June 2005 (1 year 2 months)

Verbs are more difficult to learn than nouns. This is evidenced in young children's lexicons, which invariably contain more nouns than verbs. Under the direction of Professor Kathy Hirsh-Pasek I began researching the idea of “imageability,” a factor first measured by Paivio et al (1968). Gentner (1982) and others have suggested a connection between imageability and age-of-acquisition.

A word’s imageability is the ease by which it produces a mental image in the mind of a native language user, and therefore is an attribute that categorizes across part-of-speech.

Collaborating with Wei Yi Ma (U Delaware) and Colleen McDonough (Temple U, Post-Doc), we identified imageability as a strong predictor of age-of-acquisition. Imageability ratings by native speakers of certain Chinese verbs were found to be significantly higher than English speakers' ratings of similar English verbs. This may explain the comparative prevalence of verbs in early lexicons among Chinese children.

Bob Lannon's Publications

  • Finding needles in the right haystack: Double modals in medical consultations.

    • Penn Working Papers in Linguistics
    Authors: Bob Lannon

    (in press).

    Double modals (DMs), as in (1), are exceedingly rare in sociolinguistic interviews (Feagin 1979), despite their status as a characteristic feature of Southern US English. The frequency-related challenges presented by syntactic variables seem to be magnified for this construction (c.f. Cheshire 1999), production of which is further limited by geographic and pragmatic constraints (Mishoe & Montgomery 1994). Collecting naturally-occurring DMs is like the proverbial search for needles in a haystack.

    Our study represents the very first corpus investigation of DMs, through a search of the right ‘haystack’: the nationwide Verilogue, Inc database of recorded and transcribed physician-patient interactions (~85 million words). Prior work on DMs has largely relied on elicited data (e.g. DiPaolo 1989, Hasty 2010) and post-hoc write-up of observed speech (e.g., Coleman 1975, Mishoe & Montgomery 1994). Neither data type allows for a full examination of the social roles and interactional goals that influence DM production.

  • An image is worth a thousand words: why nouns tend to dominate verbs in early word learning

    • Developmental Science
    • March 2011
    Authors: Bob Lannon, Kathy Hirsh-Pasek, Roberta Michnick Golinkoff, Colleen McDonough, Lulu Song

    Nouns are generally easier to learn than verbs (e.g. Bornstein, 2005; Bornstein et al., 2004; Gentner, 1982; Maguire, Hirsh-Pasek & Golinkoff, 2006). Yet, verbs appear in children’s earliest vocabularies, creating a seeming paradox. This paper examines one hypothesis about the difference between noun and verb acquisition. Perhaps the advantage nouns have is not a function of grammatical form class but rather is related to a word’s imageability. Here, word imageability ratings and form class (nouns and verbs) were correlated with age of acquisition according to the MacArthur-Bates Communicative Development Inventory (CDI; Fenson et al., 1994). CDI age of acquisition was negatively correlated with words’ imageability ratings. Further, a word’s imageability contributes to the variance of the word’s age of acquisition above and beyond form class, suggesting that at the beginning of word learning, imageability might be a driving factor.
