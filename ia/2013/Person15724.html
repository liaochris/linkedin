Bonnie Zink's Summary

I am a professional member of the Professional Writers' Association of Canada (www.pwac.ca) and have worked in the communications, editorial, knowledge mobilization, and research industries for nearly twenty years.

Prior to becoming a knowledge translation and exchange (KTE) professional, I have worked as a freelance writer and editor, communications and social media strategist, policy writer and analyst, as well as an academic research officer and speech writer – in academic, corporate, and community based communities.

I work to create knowledge, mobilize knowledge (KMb), and transfer knowledge into the hands of those who can best affect positive community change and use it to build healthier communities for everyone.

With a blend of new (social media) and traditional (print) media and communications approaches, I help you tell your story and get your message out there.

  • Daylight Savings Referendum Deadline Nears

    • Verb News
    • November 12, 2010
    Authors: Bonnie Zink

    Clocks in other areas of the country have recently been turned back by an hour thanks to daylight saving time, but time is running out for the Saskatchewan government to make a decision on whether to hold referendum on the divisive issue. Premier Brad Wall promised a referendum on the time issue in conjunction with the next provincial election, currently set for November 7th, 2011, but it remains uncertain whether the government will actually proceed with the plan.
    Originally invented in 1895 by George Hudson as a way to give him more time to collect insects after work before it got dark, daylight saving time was first widely implemented by Germany in World War I to cut down on daytime coal use thereby conserving more of the fuel for the war effort.
    However, studies have shown that modern electricity usage patterns have negated the original energy-saving benefits of daylight saving time, and other studies on purported decreases in crime and increases in health resulting from the twice-yearly clock switch have also failed to find any significant correlation.
    Most of the world’s population does not follow daylight saving time, and over two dozen countries that once observed the practice have since opted out.
    With the exception of Lloydminster, Denare Beach and Creighton — who observe the same time as their Alberta or Manitoba neighbours — Saskatchewan has followed one uniform time since 1966.
    Though there have been sporadic attempts to implement daylight saving time in Saskatchewan, none have gained much traction, and the most divisive issue seems to have been which set time the province should follow.
    “The observance of one uniform time throughout the year … would work,” notes a special time committee’s report submitted to the province in 1965, “except the people of the province will never agree on whether it should be mountain or central time.”

  • Transit Extends Discount Bus Pass Program

    • Verb News
    • November 5, 2010
    Authors: Bonnie Zink

    Saskatoon employers now have one more benefit to offer their employees: cheap bus passes.
    Saskatoon Transit has announced it is now working with local businesses to bring its new Eco Pass program to their employees. The program, available as a pilot project to civic employees since July 2010, provides discounted adult bus passes to employers, who then pass the savings on to their employees.
    Any employer with at least ten employees can sign onto the program. Employers buy on-year unlimited passes at a twenty percent discount and then match it, providing employees with a forty percent discount off the cost of an adult pass. Employers can recover the cost through payroll deductions.
    “Similar Programs exist in other centres, and City Council had received several inquiries from Saskatoon’s business community as to why programs like this were not available in Saskatoon,” says Russell Dixon, Project and Marketing Manager with the City of Saskatoon.
    With employees receiving unlimited monthly travel at a discounted rate, convenient payment options and a federal tax credit for the cost of the bus pass, and employers being seen as community leaders championing environmentally policies and minimizing parking concerns, the new program offers substantial benefits for everyone involved.
    The community itself also benefits from reduced traffic and parking in congested neighbourhoods as transit ridership increases.

  • Schools Explain New Grading System Rationale

    • Verb News
    • October 8, 2010
    Authors: Bonnie Zink

    Though Saskatoon Public Schools has come under recent scrutiny over its decision to revise its student assessment process; it maintains that its decision is the correct one.
    Explaining that its pilot project is based on parent, teacher, and student feedback, published research and an eye towards developing real-world skills, SPS says its pilot project a focus on continuous student improvement and does not provide students with a free pass, as had been reported.
    Under the revised system, students will continue to face consequences for late assignments and plagiarism but their teachers are afforded better opportunities to provide descriptive feedback that will enable the student to learn from their mistakes.
    Saskatoon Public Schools Superintendent of Education John Dewar likens the revised process to that of professional performance reviews. “This revised system of assessment may be closer to the ‘real world,’ than most originally thought,” says Dewar. “Teachers now have a more effective tool to provide quality feedback to the student. Students are provided opportunities to become responsible learners.”
    SPS says the decision to implement the project was based upon the work of Robert Morzano, Anne Davies, leading researchers in the field of education and other experts who say there is an important link between student success and the type of feedback they receive.
    The public schools say placing an emphasis on teaching students to improve and learn from their experiences will go a long way in ensuring they are successful in both the short and long terms.

  • USask Fourth-Costliest: Undergrad Tuition up Five Percent for 2010-2011

    • Verb News
    • September 24, 2010
    Authors: Bonnie Zink

    Students at institutions across Canada have noticed a marked increase on their tuition bills this fall, and those from the University of Saskatchewan are no exception.
    Despite a 4.3 percent increase to their provincial operating grant, the U of S raised tuition by five percent for the 2010-2011 academic year.
    At $5,431, Saskatchewan now boasts the fourth most expensive undergraduate tuition in Canada, though the University says students still get great bang for their buck.
    “On average, the cost of tuition at the University of Saskatchewan is below what similar programs cost elsewhere in Canada,” says Brett Fairbairn, the university’s Provost and Vice-President Academic.
    “Decisions around tuition are always carefully considered,” adds Art Dumont, chair of the University’s Board of Governors. “We believe the increases are consistent with the university’s strategic goals.”
    On average, undergrads at the U of S now pay $20 more per three-credit class than they did last year, with increases in some colleges such as Commerce (6.3 percent), Pharmacy (6.1 percent), Law (9.8 percent), and Engineering (6.1 percent) being steeper than those in programs like Arts (4.4 percent) and Nursing (4.4 percent).
    Graduate students at the U of S also saw a fee increase of 4.6 percent over last year, though their tuition is still the third least expensive in the country.
    Tuition dollars represent approximately 22 percent of the university’s total operating budget, with 70 percent coming from the province.

  • City Rejects Bike License Plans

    • Verb News
    • September 17, 2010
    Authors: Bonnie Zink

    The city of Saskatoon has rejected the idea of mandatory bicycle licenses for Saskatoon riders, though one city councillor remains strongly supportive of the idea.
    After examining the issue, city administration recently recommended against a city-wide bike license program in favour of reviewing its overall strategy around cycling the streets of Saskatoon.
    However, City Councilor Myles Heidt believes bike licenses would make it simple for the public to report cyclists who ride on sidewalks or run red lights and would offer an efficient way to track stolen bicycles, maintaining cyclists currently have “free reign”.
    Several councillors have spoke out against the reimplementation of a city-wide bike license, as a similar program in the 1990s proved costly, complicated and ineffective, leading to its cancellation.
    Tom Wolf of Saskatoon Cycles says that “Councillor Heidt brings a very important issue to the fore,” but says he does not support a bike license program and instead favours the creation of separate bike lanes and the expansion of current pathways as a better alternative.

  • High Stakes Banjo Bowl: Bombers Looking to Avenge Humiliating Loss

    • Verb News
    • September 3, 2010
    Authors: Bonnie Zink

    The most anticipated Labour Day rematch in the Canadian Football League, the Manitoba Lotteries Banjo Bowl, celebrates its 7th anniversary on September 12th, when the Winnipeg Blue Bombers welcome the Roughriders to Winnipeg’s Canad Inns Stadium.
    The annual Winnipeg rematch was dubbed the Banjo Bowl in 2004 by Blue Bomber board member David Asper, after then Bombers place kicker Troy Westwood referred to Regina residents as “a bunch of banjo-pickin’ inbreds.”
    Since then, the name and the game itself have become part of prairie tradition, with fans of both teams travelling between the two cities en masse to cheer on their respective teams.
    To date Winnipeg and Saskatchewan have won three Banjo Bowls apiece, ensuring that this year’s winner will be entitled to extra bragging rights. The Bombers will likely be looking to avenge their 55–10 loss to the Riders at last year’s Banjo Bowl, a result running back Wes Cates called “a good old-fashion whipping” in a post-game interview with the Canadian Press.
    Manitoba Lotteries, the official sponsor of this year’s Banjo Bowl, promises a weekend of family-friendly activity for both hometown fans and those travelling from Saskatchewan.
    Blue Bomber players will be available for autographs in the Upperdeck Sports Bar at McPhillips Station Casino on September 10th, while Family Day at Canad Inns Stadium on the 12th includes a public BBQ, dunk tank and mascots to entertain the entire family. Doors open at 11:00 AM prior to the game’s noon kickoff.
