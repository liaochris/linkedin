Edward DeJesus' Summary

Edward DeJesus has more than 20 years of experience in the field of youth services. DeJesus brings his love for research, practice and youth culture to youth organizations and the young people they serve. DeJesus has addressed more than 300 youth service organizations and community groups, as well as over 100,000 young adults. In addition to his direct service experience, DeJesus has blended practice with policy and advocacy by working with the National Youth Employment Coalition to conduct research on effective programs that help youth acquire and maintain jobs. Using his position as Chair of the New York City Youth Employment Consortium, he fought for job training opportunities for high school dropouts. Through the Annie E. Casey Foundation, he co-authored a report of preparing juvenile offenders for labor market success. As a member of the Sar Levitan Center for Youth Policies at John Hopkins University and serving on the Task Force on Employment Opportunities for young offenders for the U.S. Office of Juvenile Justice and Delinquency Prevention, he increased awareness among policy makers on the needs of out-of-school youth.

Through advocacy, research and training, Edward educates and empowers youth, youth service organizations and policy makers on how to create options beyond the streets. Edward's is a consultant to many cities, states and national organizations on youth education and workforce issues. Some of his clients include: The National Education Association, The City of Hartford, Goodwill Industries, YWCA of Los Angeles, New Orleans Literacy Alliance, The Sand Diego Workforce Partnership, and many others.

DeJesus is particularly known for his ability to reach and capture youth and adult audiences. NPR, The Washington Post, The Baltimore Sun, The Miami Herald and Black Enterprise Magazine have all run features on DeJesus’ work.

Edward DeJesus' Experience

Executive Director

STRIVE-DC

March 2013 – Present (7 months) Washington, DC

STRIVE DC helps chronically unemployed people in the Washington, DC area transform their lives through employment. The program helps people find and keep employment through three programs. Since opening in 1999, STRIVE DC has graduated over 1,600 people from its signature job readiness program! STRIVE DC trains graduates about 150 per year from job-readiness training; places over 60% of its graduates in jobs and keeps 60% of those who find jobs, working for at least two years. In 2010, the Strive for Success GED program enrolled just over 80 students of which 70% gained at least one grade level in math and/or reading. Twenty-four (24) students so far have gone on to earn their GED. Additionally, one graduate has completed basic training and another is enrolled in a four-year university. Career Gear has clothed over 400 men thus far in 2010.

Founder and CEO

MAKiN' WorkForce Connections

Privately Held; 5001-10,000 employees; Marketing and Advertising industry

January 2013 – March 2013 (3 months) Washington. DC

MAKiN’ Workforce Connections (MWFC) LLC and the MAKIN Workforce Connections Foundation (MWFCF)was created to provide programs, training and resources to assist job seekers with the development of the #1 Job Hunting skill– Networking.

Research indicates that 65% of jobs are never advertised on-line, posted on jobs boards, or appear in the classified sections of newspapers. These "hidden" jobs are usually beyond the grasp of many “low-networked” job seekers. Despite this reality, there is not one curriculum designed to teach workforce participants this much needed skills - until now.
It’s widely known among researchers, policy makers and workforce development professionals that the number one way people find jobs is through networking. According to a national study, 47% of job seekers find jobs through networking followed by the use of internet job boards (25%) and agency/recruiters (12%) respectively. MWFC, LLC focuses not only on teaching g job seekers invaluable networking skills, it shows workforce development programs how to support them in doing so.

Through thought provoking interviews with youth who overcame many barriers to success, hard hitting facts and an experience and insight identifiable to millions of youth nationwide, youth advocate and author Ed DeJesus reveals the strategies used by many young people to get an education and achieve success.

Change is what happens when six young adults from various walks of life decide to forego the norm. Rather than flipping burgers or checking groceries, these young people light a spark for change using the culture that they love. From hip-hop to skateboarding and all points in between, these young people go to work spreading a message about life, freedom and future economic opportunity. If they can do it, you can, too.

  • Generation of Challenge

    • Sar Levitan Youth Policy Studies - Johns Hopkins University
    • March 5, 1997
    Authors: Edward DeJesus, Sar Levitan Youth Policy Network

    This report refers to a generation under challenge, meaning the 18-to-24-year-olds who have recently come of age in the United States. A significant part of this generation has fallen victim to a neglected past and may be overwhelmed by its future. The report argues for an integrated and comprehensive service delivery system that can make a difference in disconnected lives in the following chapters: (1) "Confronting the Demographic Challenge: Future Labor Market Prospects of Out-of-School Young Adults" (Andrew Sum, Neeta Fogg, and Neal Fogg); (2) "Investments in People Matter" (Stephen Mangum and Nancy Waldeck); (3) "Tales from the Bright Side: Conversations with Successful Graduates of Youth Employment Programs" (Edward deJesus); (4) "Out of School and Unemployed: Principles for More Effective Policies and Programs" (Gary Walker); (5) "Creative Resource Development: An Assessment of Potential in Selected Cities" (David Gruber); and (6) "Moving into the Mainstream: Making Connections for Disconnected Youth" (Marion Pines and Bill Spring). (Contains 17 tables and 1 chart.) (SLD)
