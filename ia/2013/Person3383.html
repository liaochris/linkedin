Albert Schram's Publications

  • Development, Environment and Culture: all bones and no meat?

    • submitted for publication
    • October 2013
    Authors: Albert Schram, Justus Kiithia, Juan Carlos Sosa Varela

    Some ideas, long discredited by mainstream academics such as the Environmental Kuznets Curve, or the theory of Stable Cultural Dimensions of national cultures continue to capture the public's imagination. Regrettably, little empirical evidence has been found supporting these hypotheses.
    Using the Environmental Performance Index Pilot Trend country data published in 2013, for the decade from 2000 to 2010 we found a significant improvement of around 20% in Environmental Health Index for all 132 countries, measured as a composite of the effect on human health of indoor air pollution, particulate matter PM2.5, access to drinking water, access to sanitation and child mortality. These variables respond to health and environmental policies and infrastructure construction, and affect an electorate's welfare directly.
    During roughly the same decade, in the World Values Survey we found a significant negative shift of around 8% in our environmental value measure for 29 countries, expressed as a diminished willingness to pay for an increase in environmental taxes between the 3rd (around 1995) and 5th (around 2005) wave of country surveys. These 29 countries account for 62% of the world's population. It seems that when Environmental Health improves and countries grow richer, the willingness to pay or environmental policies decreases.
    Although it has proven to be difficult to put any meat on the cultural dimensions or value orientation theories, we found some stimulating relationships between development, environment and culture which deserve further research. Better understanding of these relationships is essential to prevent implementation of fundamentally misguided policies.

  • The Global Expansion Of Higher Education: Are Low Income Countries Catching Up?

    • Paper Accepted for the Huon Seminar, 13-14 November 2013, Papua New Guinea University of Technology
    • August 14, 2013
    Authors: Albert Schram

    We find that the massification of higher education started in most region in the 1980s or 1990s is a universal and dynamic phenomenon. From 2000 to 2010, the Gross Enrolment Ration for tertiary education (GER) almost doubled in East Asia & Pacific region from 16% to 29%, and in South Asia from 8% to 15%. Sub-Saharan Africa also shows substantial growth from 4% to 7% but from a much lower starting point.
    We extended the analysis of GER by region, by looking at the growth of total enrolment in tertiary education per 100.000 inhabitants by country from 2000 to 2010. We found that there was no relationship between country size and enrolment growth. By contrast, we did find a statistically significant relationship between income category and enrolment growth, with low-income countries expanding enrolment quicker than middle or high income countries.
    The answer to the question in the title are low-income countries catching up is clearly affirmative. Low-income countries therefore need to devise strategies which massively increase higher-education enrolment, while not undermining the quality of teaching and research. Necessary conditions are that sufficient resources are devoted to higher-education, and that the state continues to play an effective regulatory, coordinating and steering role.
    In order to keep the sector financially sustainable, demand driven policies should be considered. Furthermore, involvement of private capital is inevitable. An excessively controlling state which keeps out private investment will not help the increase or tertiary education enrolment. A mixed system in which state and private universities both operate seems to offer the best opportunities for sustainable expansion. Regulation of universities, and policies encouraging international accreditation are required in order to warrant academic quality.

  • Future Consumers, Cellphones and Time Use in India and Papua New Guinea

    • Paper accepted by the Fourth Asia Pacific Business Research Conference, 30 September - 1 October, Singapore Bayview Hotel
    • August 26, 2013
    Authors: Albert Schram

    In India this is expected to amount to 350 million new consumers by 2025 quadrupling consumption. Today, we know little about them since they are either unborn or not participating in consumer markets.
    The adoption of cellphones in the developing world has been fast and massive, but the transformation of society and the economy through cellphones has only just begun. There is still an absence of reliable and valid data from developing countries, however, and research into the dynamics and impact of this phenomenon has been scant.
    Since the poor by definition have little money to spend, in order to find out about their future consumption patterns it may be more productive to look at their time budgets. We administered a large survey with 67 questions, and reconstructed the previous day's time diary with 158 subjects in Ahmedabad (India) in 2010 and 107 in Lae (Papua New Guinea PNG) in 2012.
    Results indicate that total household spending on cellphones use is higher in proportion to the number of individuals who own a cell phone in the household. We also calculated the income elasticity of cellphones spending for households in our samples. The median daily time savings due to avoided travel or meetings produced by the use of the cell phone reported in PNG in 2012 and in India in 2010 are respectively 30 minutes and 60 minutes per day, or between 15 hours and 30 hours per month, respectively. The PNG sample was included because India is somewhat exceptional among developing countries in the sense that public transport is relatively efficient and cheap, and cell phone rates exceptionally low.
    We explored the relationship between the reported time savings due to avoided travel and individual and household spending. Paradoxically, among low-income subjects we found a group reporting among the highest time savings from avoided travel. This is probably due to their lack of access to quick and reliable means of transport. Their opportunity cost of time is therefore high.

  • Towards Greater Social Responsibility: conclusions of EU funded research

    • European Commission
    • April 2009
    Authors: Albert Schram

    The underlying premises of CSR are that a company is a moral agent in society, and that it has some degree of responsibility beyond its shareholders to a wider circle of stakeholders. These premises are now widely accepted. The intellectual battle on CSR has been won, and “socially and environmentally responsible” is no longer seen in contradiction to a company’s “competitiveness”. Nevertheless, by defining CSR as “beyond compliance” and “voluntary”, the European Commission has created an environment where there is limited scope for binding regulation and enforcement. Although the European Parliament has repeatedly advocated for harmonization and application of CSR reporting standards, for example, the European Commission has shied away from this type of “regulated CSR”, preferring a “stimulated CSR” approach.
    The purpose of this policy review is to assess the results of four research projects on Corporate Social Responsibility (CSR) financed under 6th Framework Programme (FP6) within Socio-economic Sciences and Humanities (SSH) – CSR PLATFORM, ESTER, RARE and RESPONSE - in the light of current policy developments with the goal of both identifying possible gaps in public policy, and lessons-learned for policy makers, as well as pointing out under-researched or emerging research themes. This body of research can now be complemented by more focussed management research related to business processes on the following themes:
    • mainstreaming CSR in firm’s strategic processes including SMEs,
    • the linkage between CSR and innovation,
    • performance and impact indicators, and
    • global supply chain management.

    Research on the internal strategic processes and mainstreaming CSR in business processes, as well as research on the wider societal outcomes of CSR programmes require more research effort in the coming years.

  • Collective versus Random Fining: an experimental study on controlling ambient pollution

    • Environmental and Resource Economics (2004)Springer
    • 2004
    Authors: Albert Schram, Francisco Alpizar, Till Requate

    This paper presents an experimental study of two different pollution compliance games:collective vis-à-vis random fining as a means to regulate non-point pollution. Using samples from both Costa Rican coffee mill managers and Costa Rican students,we find that the two games perform equivalently but, although they lead to efficient outcomes through Nash play in the majority of cases, the observed frequency of Nash play is lower than theoretically predicted. Moreover, we reject the hypothesis that managers and students behave equally. Off the equilibrium, managers tend to over-abate, whereas students tend to under-abate. This result suggests the importance of considering subject pool differences in the evaluation of environmental policies by means of experiments, particularly if those policies involve certain forms of management decisions.

  • La Agricultura Centroamericana Ante Los Desafíos Climáticos, Económicos Y Tecnológicos

    • ASIES
    • June 2003
    Authors: Albert Schram, Mijail Pérez

    Los resultados obtenidos sugieren que las fincas en las que existen sistemas silvopastoriles son mucho más rentables que las fincas sin usos silvícolas o fincas tradicionales.
