Jonathan Miller's Summary

• President/CEO of Miller Samuel, a leading New York real estate appraisal and consulting firm he co-founded in 1986. Miller Samuel performs appraisals of co-ops, condos and 1-4 family properties in the New York City metro area of roughly $5 billion of property per year.

• Managing principal and co-founder of Miller Cicero, LLC, a commercial real estate advisory service firm covering the New York City region since 2002.

• State certified general real estate appraiser in New York and Connecticut and performs court testimony as an expert witness in various local, state and federal courts.

• Author of a series of market reports considered the “report of record” covering the New York City metro area for Douglas Elliman including Manhattan, Brooklyn, Queens, Long Island, Westchester, Putnam, The Hamptons and the North Fork and South Florida including Miami, Boca Raton, Fort Lauderdale and Palm Beach that are relied on by the media, financial institutions as well as government agencies including the Federal Reserve, IRS, HUD and NYC Office of Management and Budget.

• Writes a weekly “Three Cents Worth” blog column for Curbed New York and periodic columns for Curbed DC and Curbed Miami.

• Host of the Housing Helix Podcast interviewing all things housing with insights from guests including Dr. Robert Shiller, Justin Fox, Rick Sharga, Jason Bram, Daniel Gross, Guy Kawasaki, Nicolas Retsinas, Barry Ritholtz and more than 100 others.

• A well-regarded real estate expert providing insights for publications such as The New York Times, The Wall Street Journal, Barron’s, Financial Times, The New York Observer, The New York Daily News, New York Newsday, CNN/ Money, USA Today, Crain’s New York Business, New York Magazine, The Real Deal, Reuters, AP, Time Magazine, Money Magazine and others. On TV/Radio for Bloomberg, CNBC, NPR, PBS, CNN, ABC, Fox, The Daily Beast TV, Yahoo! Tech Ticker and others.

Jonathan Miller's Publications

  • The Condominium v. Cooperative Puzzle: An Empirical Analysis of Housing in New York City

    • Journal of Legal Studies/The University of Chicago Law School
    • June 1, 2007
    Authors: Jonathan Miller, Michael H. Schill, Ioan Voicu

    One of the enduring puzzles of New York City's housing market is the persistence of the housing cooperative, despite the prevailing wisdom that condominiums are more valuable than cooperatives. In this article, we examine the theoretical advantages and disadvantages of cooperatives and condominiums, and apply these theoretical insights to empirically test whether there is a price premium attributable to condominium housing. We then use our findings to speculate as to why the cooperative form remains dominant in New York City and whether its dominance is likely to continue in the future. The empirical analysis is based on hedonic models of house values and uses rich data on apartments sold in New York City between 1984 and 2002.
