Breanne Kunz's Summary

Design thought leader with over six years of experience applying user-centered design, conducting research, and guiding design strategy for a Fortune 100 technology company. Proven successes in the following:

Research and design execution:
Designed the ePrint experience for HP LaserJet printers. These new capabilities redefine the printer as a web-connected device and revolutionize the way customers interact with their content. Since 2010, 13.6 million customers have signed up for ePrint, and over 21.8 million print jobs have been sent via a mobile phone or front panel printer app.

Design strategy and planning for business outcomes:
Translated business imperatives to design efforts and prioritized over 100 design projects based on alignment with goals, customer impact, and business impact. As a result, design can focus on the highest impact projects and designers have a clear understanding of how their day-to-day work contributes to organizational success.

Linking customer experience visions to implementation realities:
Completely re-envisioned the unboxing and set up experience for LaserJet printers and developed a 5-year adoption dashboard to help the organization make it a reality. Established clear user experience objectives and measurement criteria that inspire development teams to aim for the best experience.

Design strategy, design architecture, service design, cross-channel design, experience roadmapping, customer research, ethnography, design thinking, interaction design, contextual inquiry, rapid prototyping, information architecture.

Breanne Kunz's Publications

  • Emotional experience and interaction design

    • Affect and Emotion in Human-Computer Interaction From Theory to Applications
    • 2008
    Authors: Breanne Kunz, Justin Donaldson, Youn-kyung Lim, Heekyoung Jung, David Royer, Shruti Ramalingam, Sindhia Thirumaran, Erik Stolterman

    The emotional experience of an interactive system has been the subject of a great deal of recent interest and study in the HCI community. However, many of researchers have pointed out the extreme difficulty in predicting or controlling these emotional experiences through intentional design choices. However, the user study we conducted proposes a different point-of-view than these claims. Although these emotional responses were not always tied directly to the device itself and influenced by contextual factors, we discovered that certain controllable aspects of interactive products showed clear patterns of emotion in the responses of our participants. We discuss our findings and provide implications for the design of emotional experiences in interactive devices.
