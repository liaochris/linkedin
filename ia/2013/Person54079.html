Hunter Whitney's Summary

Hunter Whitney is a senior User Experience (UX) Designer who has helped create useful and usable interface designs for clients in areas ranging from bioscience and medicine to information technology and marine biology. He is the author of "Data Insights: New Ways to Visualize and Make Sense of Data," published by Morgan Kaufmann/Elsevier. He has also written numerous articles about a range of subjects, including data visualization, for various online and print publications. His aim is to encourage conversations among people with diverse skills and perspectives about presenting data in ways that are more widely accessible and engaging. He received dual bachelor’s degrees—one in English Literature from UCLA and the other in Biology from UCSC—and has completed post-graduate neuropsychology research at UCLA. The combination of these multidisciplinary studies reflects his longstanding interest in the intersection between the humanities and the sciences.

Hunter Whitney's Projects

  • The Future of Neuroimaging: A 3D Exploration of Traumatic Brain Injury

    • April 2013 to Present
    Team Members: Hunter Whitney, Jeff Chang

    Multimodal 3D interfaces could help achieve a clearer and more comprehensive view of the brain that could be applicable to many scenarios. What’s the next shift in imaging technology and interfaces? How can we make a bigger impact on saving lives and improving quality of life? Using traumatic brain injury in the wounded veteran as an example, we will explore how 3D visualization will fundamentally affect the way we think about disease and the ways in which we care for patients.

  • HIMSS HIT X.0 Programmer Challenge

    • January 2011 to February 2011
    Team Members: Hunter Whitney, Douglas Naegele,

    I was the UX lead on a 3-person team involved in the agile development of a mobile version of an Emergency Department “Expect Form”. The purpose of the challenge was to showcase how organizations, such as hospitals, can use agile programming techniques to build focused tools which can be quickly adapted to meet a variety of changing needs. The working prototype was demonstrated at the Healthcare Information and Management Systems Society (HIMSS) HIT X.0 Programmer Challenge that was held in Orlando, FL in February 2011.

  • Health 2.0 Developer Challenge (won First Place)

    • September 2010 to October 2010
    Team Members: Hunter Whitney, Jeremy A Johnson, PhD, , Peter Vieth, Alex Kawas, Ash Damle, John Bosley, Maria DiLisio, Kathleen Sidenblad, Gary Cieradkowski, Laurie Reynolds

    I led a team that created a data visualization tool, using publicly available health data, to assess an individual’s potential risk for developing colon cancer. It was created during several hack-a-thons hosted by HealthTap and Google. The tool won first place at the Health 2.0 Developer Challenge that was held in San Francisco, CA in October 2010.

Hunter Whitney's Publications

  • Data Insights: New Ways to Visualize and Make Sense of Data

    • Morgan Kaufmann/Elsevier
    • November 27, 2012
    Authors: Hunter Whitney

    Data Insights offers multi-disciplinary perspectives and useful information about how visualizations can open your eyes to data. This thought-provoking book takes a conversational approach to presenting an overview of the subject, while also focusing on key details. It highlights the ideas and work of a variety of people who are actively contributing to this still emerging field. Case studies from business analytics, healthcare, games, security, and network monitoring, among others, portray what is going on in data visualization today. A diverse blend of original illustrations and real-world examples, both classical and cutting-edge, help fill in the picture.
    This book provides an approachable overview of important aspects of data visualization, and…

    -Demonstrates, with a variety of case studies, how visualizations can foster a clearer and more comprehensive understanding of data

    -Answers the question, "How can data visualization help me?" with discussions of how it fits into a wide array of purposes and situations

    -Makes the case that data visualization is not just about technology; it also involves a deeply human process
