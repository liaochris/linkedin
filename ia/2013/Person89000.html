Matthew King, PhD's Summary

Dr. Matthew Wilburn King is an American political geographer focused on the geo-politics of resource scarcity and environmental change. He carries out research, consulting, and writing for NGOs, think tanks, policy institutions, governments, and universities interested in governance, geo-politics of resource scarcity and environmental change, environmental security, climate change and security, commodities and conflict, natural resources, and evolutionary theories of change. Matthew holds a PhD from the University of Cambridge, England.

His research focuses on environmental security and the role of alternative environmental governance architectures in peacebuilding. He is especially interested in mapping commodity-related conflicts around the world in order to compare the nature of, sources of, and impacts of conflicts related to practices in forestry, fisheries, agriculture, mining, and energy sectors and the role of alternative governance architectures in addressing impacts.

Upon completing a Master’s degree in International Conflict Studies at Trinity College, Dublin, Matthew served as a US Presidential Management Fellow under the Clinton Administration. His tenure with government began in the US Department of Interior. He then served as the Senior Program Manager for Latin America and the Caribbean with the Office of International Activities for NOAA Research. He left government service in 2007 in order to create the Global Research, Expeditions, and Education Network (Living GREEN Network).

Matthew has lived in multiple countries and worked in over a half dozen. His prior studies included undergraduate study abroad at the University of Edinburgh, Old College of Law and Social Sciences, Scotland 1996-1997. He completed his B.A. at the University of Tulsa in Political Science and Legal Studies 1993-1998. During the spring of 2010, he returned to his undergraduate university as the Distinguished Alumnus Lecturer in Politics and Law.

Matthew King, PhD's Publications

  • Sea Grant in Latin America?: Adapting the US Sea Grant model of linked applied research, extension, and education to a Latin American Context—Is there a fit?

    • Elsevier Publishing - Marine Policy Journal
    • May 2007
    Authors: Matthew King, PhD, James Tobey, Jill Hepp, Stephen Olsen, Barry Costa-Pierce

    This paper explores the application of the US Sea Grant model of applied research, extension, and education to two case studies in Latin America: Coastal Ecuador and the Gulf of Fonseca. The analysis is based on a series of meetings and roundtables with in-country partners and leaders of the US Sea Grant program. We conclude that the Sea Grant model provides an institutional structure that Latin America lacks and the model's features would improve governance of marine and coastal resources through more effective linkages between coastal communities, universities, and policy/decision makers at local, national, and international levels.

  • Political Ecology of Mangroves in Southern Honduras: the emergence and evolution of environmental conflict in the Gulf of Fonseca 1973-2006

    • VDM Verlag
    • September 20, 2009
    Authors: Matthew King, PhD

    Dr. King recounts the history of a conflict over access to and control over the mangrove wetlands of the Gulf of Fonseca, southern Honduras between 1973 and 2006. The result of extensive research, the book affirms that this conflict emerged due to two distinct but interrelated social imaginaries. Both neoliberalism and environmentalism have increasingly become powerful ideologies over the last three decades, dominating the debates related to 'environment and development' in southern Honduras. Both have also affected the lives of local people and the mangrove ecosystem of the Gulf. Local resistance to the Honduran government, and the shrimp industry's actions, led civil society actors to reimagine the coastal landscape. Their efforts have led to nascent forms of environmental governance within the context of democratic and capitalist transformations in Honduras, reshaping rural coastal livelihoods and the environment.

  • "Environmental Governance and Peacebuilding in Post-Conflict Central America: Lessons from the Central American Commission for Environment and Development"

    • Earthscan
    • 2013
    Authors: Matthew King, PhD, Marco Antonio González Pastora, Mauricio Castro-Salazar, Carlos Manuel Rodriguez

    Governance, Natural Resources, and Post-Conflict Peacebuilding (Paperback)

    Editors: Carl Bruch, Carroll Muffett, and Sandra S. Nichols

    When the guns are silenced, people affected by conflict need food, water, shelter, and a means to earn a living. They also require a government that can meet these needs and sustain peace. While natural resources are essential to sustaining people and peace in post-conflict countries, governance failures often jeopardize these objectives.

    This book examines experiences in post-conflict governance, natural resource management, and peacebuilding from more than forty countries. It highlights the centrality of natural resource management in rebuilding governance and the rule of law, combating corruption, improving transparency and accountability, engaging disenfranchised populations, and building confidence following conflict.

    The book interweaves this analysis with an exploration of how these lessons may be applied to the formulation and implementation of effective governance interventions. It provides a concise theoretical and practical framework for policymakers, researchers, practitioners, and students.

    Governance, Natural Resources, and Post-Conflict Peacebuilding is part of a global initiative to identify and analyze lessons in post-conflict peacebuilding and natural resource management. The project has generated six volumes of case studies and analyses, with contributions by practitioners, policymakers, and researchers. Other volumes address high-value resources; land; water; livelihoods; and restoration, remediation, and reconstruction.

  • Forthcoming: Climate Change and Humanity’s Failure of Long-term Planning

    • World Watch Institute - State of the World 2014
    Authors: Matthew King, PhD

    Despite humanity’s limited ability to act on the long-term challenge of sustainability, systems of governance can be improved in their ability to address the uncertain and complex issues involved, such as population growth, resource depletion, war and terrorism, energy security, food security, climate change, and epidemiological threats. The Multilateral System is proving to lack the legitimate and committed institutions necessary to solve long-term problems at the necessary speed and scale. Humanity’s failure to address the challenges of climate change and energy security exemplifies this fact (Valaskakis, 2010a). But systems can be developed that compensate for our inherent limitation as a species to act on long-term challenges; it seems that stakeholder-based systems of governance can address this failure.

    This chapter addresses three questions: First, why does humanity make long-term plans but fail to act on them? Second, when it is clear to rational observers that the planet and humanity face major threats to their well-being, why are our systems for perceiving, confronting, and managing these threats unable to react on appropriate scales and with appropriate speed? Third, how can systems of governance compensate for the limitations of our species?

  • Forthcoming: Governance: What it is, how it works, and what happens next.

    • World Watch Institute - State of the World 2014
    Authors: D. Conor Seyle, Matthew King, PhD

    The focus on governance in this volume reflects the growing interest in the question of how human systems are organized. In the last several decades, “governance” as a concept worthy of serious study exploded in political science, public administration, and environmental studies, and many other fields. In the 1990s, Elinor Ostrom’s Nobel prize-winning work in the governance of natural resources helped policy makers and researchers take seriously the idea humans could develop sustainable, non-governmental systems for controlling access to natural resources. At the same time, in the study of globalization scholars and policy advocates retreated from the idea of global government and instead began to talk about how the global system could be structured to effectively coordinate global activity without such a system: “global governance” rather than “global government.” A simultaneous discussion developed in the development and peacebuilding communities which included a new appreciation of the critical role of good governance a mediator of conflict and economic development. This led to a new focus on good governance, and good government, by international bodies such as the World Bank and UNESCO.
