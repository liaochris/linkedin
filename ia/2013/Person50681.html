Grant Sabatier's Summary

Grant is a creative and dynamic business and marketing strategy consultant. He has more than a decade of experience in digital strategy, website design, market research, and business strategy. A majority of his experience has been in strategic consulting within the publishing, media, and higher education industries. A few of his recent clients include Kaplan Higher Education, Michigan State University, Duke University's Fuqua School of Business, Northwestern University's Kellogg School of Management, and The Wharton School at The University of Pennsylvania.

Grant is also a respected authority and speaker on the future of higher education. His publications include “The College of 2020: Students,” which was profiled on CBS and Marketplace from American Public Media.

Grant Sabatier's Publications

  • The College of 2020: Students

    • The Chronicle of Higher Education
    • June 2009
    Authors: Grant Sabatier, Martin Vanderwerf

    Is your college ready for the new student? The college student of the future will be older, more technologically savvy, and more likely to see going to college as a retail transaction.
    Students will be looking for convenience and a quick payoff from the course content, and they will be shopping for the best bargain. This report answers how you and your college should prepare. The College of 2020 Report is based on reviews of research and data on trends in higher education, interviews with experts who are shaping the future of colleges, and the results of a poll of members of a Chronicle Research Services panel of admissions officials.

  • Financial Uncertainty and the Admissions Class of Fall 2008

    • The Chronicle of Higher Education
    • February 2008
    Authors: Grant Sabatier, Martin Vanderwerf

    2008 year started out as a record-breaking one for college admissions officers, with the highest number ever of high-school graduates. About 78 percent of colleges reported that
    the number of applications increased in 2008. But it ended as one of the most challenging and puzzling. Forty-six percent of colleges surveyed by The Chronicle said the yield of admitted students, or the percentage of accepted students who matriculated, decreased this year. Of those, three-quarters said the decrease was unexpected. After being overwhelmed by piles of applications, many colleges had to dig deep into their
    waiting lists to fill their classes.
