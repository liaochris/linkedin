Matthew Grocoff's Summary

Matt is the founding principal of the THRIVE Net Zero Energy Consulting Collaborative. He was honored as a 2012 Michigan Green Leader by the Detroit Free Press, called a “proven zero energy master” and one of "Greater Detroit's most progressive personalities." He is co-founder of Mission Zero Fest and a nationally renowned advocate and thought-leader on net zero energy buildings and restorative design Matt is host of Greenovation.TV, a contributor to the Environment Report on public radio, contributor to FOX2 News EnergyTeam, writer for Home Energy Magazine and the green renovation expert for Old House Web and ImprovementCenter.com.

He is a sought-after speaker and a frequent source for journalists on the topic of net zero energy, green-building, historic preservation and energy performance.

His home was selected as one of USA Today's "Best Green Homes of 2010" and The Atlantic called it "sustainable perfection." It is America's oldest and Michigan's first net-zero energy home, meaning it annually produces more energy than his family consumes.

Matt is an adviser to the University of Michigan BLUElab team targeting Living Building Certification for his landmark home. He is also an adviser to the Lawrence Tech 2015 Solar Decathalon Team.

He has been featured in the pages and on the airwaves of hundreds of media outlets including USA TODAY, The Atlantic, Washington Post, Detroit Free Press, Fine Homebuilding, Preservation Magazine, Photon International, Solar Today, Fox Business News, This Old House and countless online magazines and blogs.

Matt is a Living Buildings Ambassador and LEED Green Associate.

Matt is a recovering attorney and producer. He's produced or directed over 175 television commercials, short form documentaries and videos.

Matt is a former adjunct lecturer at the University of Michigan-Dearborn. He received his Juris Doctor from The University of Georgia School of Law.

    An Urgent Call to Restore America’s Existing Homes

    Constructing new net-zero homes is essential going forward. But, how much would we reduce our carbon output if from now we built every new home net zero but we ignored our existing homes? The answer is ZERO. There are 130 million existing homes in the U.S. and nearly half have zero or inadequate insulation and they are responsible for 22% of our carbon emissions. Yet virtually every one of the fewer than one-hundred net-zero homes in the U.S. is either new construction or a major gut renovation.
