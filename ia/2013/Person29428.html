Daniel Burgui Iguzkiza's Summary

I am a freelance journalist and photographer focused on human rights, development and good stories.

I have published articles and images in a wide range of printed and online newspapers such as ABC, La Vanguardia, ElPais.com, Berria, El Observador or magazines such as Nuestro Tiempo, El Viajero de El País, El Mundo de los Pirineos, FronteraD.com, Tiempo de Hoy, the Italian Popoli, the Bolivian magazine Pie Izquierdo, the Argentinian El Puercoespin and regional newspapers such as Grupo Noticias and others.

I have reported from more than a dozen different countries including Algeria, Bolivia, Chile, Greenland, Iceland, Kenyan/Somali border, Kyrgyzstan, Tunisia, Uruguay or Western Sahara.

I covered the collapse of the Icelandic Government in 2009 and wrote about the crisis from Reykjavik; the famine at the Horn of Africa in 2011, the exodus of refugees fleeing from the aftermaths of the Arab Spring now trapped in Malta or massive demonstrations even in Spain or in the UK; and I have lived in Central Asia while researching features.

Between 2009 and 2013 I traveled three times to Bolivia to report on children who work inside the mines at Potosi. In 2010, this investigation was awarded with the journalism prize of the Spanish NGO ‘Manos Unidas’, given yearly to distinguish the best example of journalistic work published in Spain about developing countries. I am still working on this project tracking the lives of these children.

In 2011 I was recognized as 'Best journalist of the year in Navarre', an award given yearly by the local press association.

Daniel Burgui Iguzkiza's Projects

  • Mineritos.org

    • December 2010 to Present
    Team Members: Daniel Burgui Iguzkiza, Ander Izagirre

    Mineritos.org was born as a reportage and article about the child miners of Bolivia, but after meeting their savage life conditions and meager opportunities. We as journalist and a group of friends, readers and just people on the net, decided to do something more. We set the web site to rise funds for a project that we like so much: the Robertito School, a little school at the top of Cerro Rico in Potosi (Bolivia) which provides daily food, education and works to the families and children of the mining sector.

    Its goal is to prevent the child explotation and domestic violence building new alternatives and opportunities, not being paternalistic or creating dependencies, just providing better future. The School was build up by the miner mothers of the Cerro.

    In 2011 the School was not able to asume its budget and we got it via crowd funding.
