Patrick Hanley's Summary

I am the owner of Hanley Innovations, a small business that provides turn-key and easy-to-use software for aerodynamics conceptual design and analysis. More than 3,000 customers use our software to analyze airfoil shapes, wings/control surfaces, keel, rudders, hydrofoils, propellers, UAVs, airplanes, automobiles (in ground effect), turbomachinery, wind energy devices and other aerodynamic devices.

My areas of interest include:
-- Numerical algorithm development for steady & unsteady fluid dynamics.
-- Flight vehicle aerodynamics analysis and design.
-- Teaching in the STEM fields (aerodynamics, fluid dynamics, heat transfer, applied math).
-- HTML5 applied to simulations for education (growing interest).
-- Management of innovation and technology.

Skills:
-- 2-D & 3-D immersed boundary method (Cartesian grid) for the Euler/Naiver-Stokes equations.
-- Multi-Domain pseudospectral method for compressible Navier-Stokes Equations.
-- 2-D unsteady pseudospectral method for solving the transonic small disturbance equations.
-- Vortex panel & vortex lattice methods for aerodynamics conceptual analysis.
-- Fortran, Visual Basic, C, Open GL, Windows GDI
-- HTML 5, JavaScript, PHP, Perl

Commercial software packages that I wrote include:
-- Stallion 3D (3D aerodynamics solutions for complex geometries based on Euler/N-S equations.)
-- MultiElement Airfoil, MultiSurface Aerodynamics, 3DFoil, VisualFoil Plus, VisualFoil 5.0/NACA.
-- Science Graphs, AeroTrajectory, SmockSoft (300+ users), Perpetual Paper

Patrick Hanley's Publications

  • A Strategy for the Efficient Simulation of Viscous Compressible Flows using a Multi-domain Pseudospectral Method

    • Journal of Computational Physics
    • 1993
    Authors: Patrick Hanley

    A new multi-domain pseudospectral method is developed to simulate viscous compressible flow in a quasi-one-dimensional nozzle. The flow variables at the interface points are advanced in time using the same second-order time marching scheme as the interior points. The spatial derivatives of the inviscid flux vector are evaluated at the interface points using a spectrally accurate modification of Van Leer's flux vector splitting method. The derivatives of the viscous flux vector are evaluated alternately from neighboring sub-domains. The scheme is found to be spectrally accurate. Shock waves are resolved without oscillations for moderate Reynolds Numbers.
