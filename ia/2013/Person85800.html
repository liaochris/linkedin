Marian Schuver-van Blanken's Summary

Marian Schuver works as Senior Human Factor Consultant at Air Traffic Control the Netherlands. Her work focuses on the vision and strategy of the Human Factor department, for both training of air traffic controllers and the future air traffic controller’s role. Her work area includes innovations in competence based training in air traffic control as well as human factors in ATM system design. In this, she preserves the philosophy and principles of training, human factors and the role of the human in the ATM system. Central to all her work is the question of why air traffic control is complex, how it can be trained for and how it can reduced by procedure & system design. In this, she is able to build a bridge between science and operational practice.

Since 2012 she is working on her own research project next to her job at ATC the Netherlands focusing on the cognitive strategies that are crucial for air traffic controller performance. The results of this research will be published in (conference) papers.

Specialties:
- Cognitive complexity in air traffic control
- Cognitive strategies characterising air traffic controller expertise
- Complex cognitive skills and competence profiles
- Vision and strategy in training and human factors in air traffic control
- Building a bridge between science and operational practice

Marian Schuver-van Blanken's Publications

  • The ATC Cognitive Process & Operational Situation Model - A model for analysing cognitive complexity in ATC

    • EAAP Conference 2010
    • September 2010
    Authors: Marian Schuver-van Blanken, Hans Huisman, Mariska Roerdink

    At Air Traffic Control the Netherlands (LVNL), the ATC Cognitive Process & Operational Situation model (ACoPOS) has been developed on the basis of literature as well as practical operational insights. The model combines the competences of the LVNL ATC Performance model, used in training, with elements of the operational ATC situation. The ACoPOS model is applied to analyse cognitive complexity in handling situations in air traffic control, whereby the factors influencing and causing cognitive complexity are made visible at a glance. The model provides a clear framework for analysis in depicting how cognitive complexity is influenced by factors in the operational situation. It is recommended to use the model as a basis for setting requirements for system and training design in order to keep cognitive complexity at an acceptable level.

  • Air traffic controller strategies in operational disturbances - An exploratory study in radar control

    • 30th EAAP Conference 2012, Sardinia, Italy
    • September 25, 2012
    Authors: Marian Schuver-van Blanken, Jeroen van Merriënboer

    Within a wide range of operational disturbances, air traffic controllers are able to ensure safety whilst keeping the additional goals of efficiency and environment in optimal balance. Key elements to success in these situations are the controller strategies underlying air traffic control (ATC) task performance. Controllers continuously use strategies to adapt their task performance to the characteristics and dynamics of the situation and the operational constraints. The scope of available research has predominantly focused on strategies used by controllers in response to an increase or decrease in traffic volume or density, in experimental settings on part-tasks or on standard (canonical) ATC situations. However, research also suggests that controllers use strategies in response to the characteristics of the situation, such as the weather situation or other complexity factors. Additional research is needed to couple and expand the controller strategies found in literature to the operational ATC environment. This paper provides the initial results of an exploratory study started at ATC the Netherlands (LVNL) focusing on the air traffic controller strategies applied in response to disturbed operational ATC situations in radar control at LVNL. Based on a replay of an operational disturbance that radar controllers handled in practice, controllers are retrospectively interviewed. On the basis of these data, strategies controllers use in operational disturbances are identified based on a list of strategies from literature, supplemented with new strategies found in practice. Although the exploratory study is still ongoing, initial results are presented in the paper. Overall, all strategies from literature were found to be applied in the analysed cases with operational disturbances. In addition, we found indications for new types of strategies supplementary to the strategies available from literature, especially for perception, solving, planning and decision making.

  • User centred design from a future oriented perspective

    • EAAP conference 2012
    • September 26, 2012
    Authors: Marian Schuver-van Blanken, Mariska Roerdink

    The human is the key actor in the air traffic management (ATM) system. Although the human is often linked to occurrence of error, the human even more often has a crucial role in error prevention. The human has proven to be the most flexible element in the ATM system to manage errors. For the human to be able to fulfil this role in current and future operations, it requires user centred system design. In addition to taking into account bottlenecks from training student air traffic controllers and experiences from current practice when starting new system developments, major changes facing the ATM system in the next decades also require a look into the future.

    A methodology was developed to incorporate this future oriented perspective. The Air Traffic Controller Cognitive Process & Operational Situation (ACoPOS) model is used to depict the current and the foreseen situation in spiderweb diagrams to visualise the change. This indicates the “way-togo” for the transition to the future situation. An example is included of a major ATM change planned for LVNL, which is still in the design process and planned for implementation in the next years. The methodology is applied on this change to describe the current operation, the expected end situation and the steps that are taken to reach this end situation. A future oriented perspective as pursued by this methodology helps to view individual operational changes in the context of large-scale transitions to new operational concepts. Consequently, the ATM system can benefit fully from the human possibilities to make aviation safer and be prepared for traffic growth, now and towards the future.
