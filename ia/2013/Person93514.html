Michelle Segar, PhD, MPH's Summary

I create systems, protocols, and messages that lead to the sustainable motivation and consistent decision making that underlie fitness, health, and well-being (MichelleSegar.com).

I am a University of Michigan (UM) behavioral sustainability and motivation scientist, the Associate Director of the Sport, Health, and Activity Research and Policy Center for Women and Girls, and a Health Policy Fellow at the Center for Healthcare Research and Transformation.

My award-winning research challenges the status quo within health care by showing that logical rewards like “health” and “weight loss” do not motivate most people to sustain behavior as well as immediately-experienced and emotional rewards such as “well-being." My ideas are widely discussed in the media (The New York Times, Forbes, etc.).

My insights are informed by 3 synergistic perspectives. As a:

• Fitness motivation and behavioral sustainability researcher
• Health behavior program development and evaluation specialist
• Physical activity and lifestyle coach to women

Grounded in research on culture, motivation, decision making, and program evaluation and refined through almost 20 years of coaching people, I have created a comprehensive-but-simple motivation architecture that builds behaviors to last a lifetime.

My advanced degrees include a: 1) PhD in Psychology ( goals and motivational processes); and Master’s Degrees in 2) Health Behavior/Health Education (MPH); and in 3) Kinesiology (MS) from UM.

I advise visionary health, wellness, and HR service providers, app and gaming developers, fitness organizations, train clinicians/coaches in more straightforward, simple, and hard-hitting methods, and speak globally to audiences of C-suite executives, behavioral professionals, and consumers.

Building Behaviors to Last a Lifetime

dr. michelle segar

January 1997 – Present (16 years 10 months)

In response to 17 years of academic training, research and working directly with individuals, I designed Behavioral Blueprints that

1) build the optimal form of motivation (it's not intrinsic as most think it is);

2) permit individuals to re-prioritize their life goals so health behaviors become core values and land at the top of the "to do" list; and

3) train individuals in easy, evidence-based strategies that lead to automatic, daily decision making that favors fitness, health, well-being.

My unique and systematic behavioral approach was developed out of concepts from positive psychology, behavioral economics, dual process theories, and self-determination theory.

My method transforms a lifestyle behavior from a "chore" into a "gift."

I consult with organizations that sell health and wellness solutions, train their staff in a more efficient and systematic method to counsel and coach end users.

I license my proprietary behavioral method to leading providers of health and wellness solutions. It can be easily adapted for use in electronic media products.

I speak to to health care organizations about what's really getting in the way of optimal engagement and long term results, and to organizations/employees about how women can use physical activity as a way to move toward peace with their bodies and selves

I coach women around the world by phone and Skype using the SMART principles I developed out my research (partially funded by a grant from the National Institutes of Health). I have an upcoming book called Smart Women Don't Diet.

  • Fitting Fitness into Women’s Lives: Effects of a gender-tailored physical activity intervention

    • Women's Health Issues
    • 2002
    Authors: Michelle Segar, PhD, MPH, Toby Jayaratne, PhD, Jennifer Hanlon, MS, Caroline Richardson, MD

    I was P.I. on this National Institutes of Health grant. We conducted a follow up evaluation on a systematic behavior change method I developed to change women's mindset about physical activity so they would stay motivated over time to participate. Specifically this intervention helped participants 1) re-brand exercise by changing their reason for exercising from weight loss to well-being; 2) to re-prioritize their life goals so feeling well and good made it to the top of their daily To Do lists; and 3) train them in "high risk" situations to sustaining on-going physical activity and teach better self-regulation attitudes and skills. Data showed that participants sustained higher levels of physical activity from post-program to the study follow up, on average 10 months after the program ended. (14 months post-program for over 50% of the participants.) Since that time, I adapted this methodology to use with other health behaviors, delivered in distinct formats.

  • Rebranding Exercise: Closing the gap between values and behavior

    • International Journal of Behavioral Nutrition and Physical Activity
    • September 7, 2011
    Authors: Michelle Segar, PhD, MPH

    Behavior can only be understood by identifying the goals to which it is attached. Exercise goals related to "health" and "healthy aging" are associated with less exercise than those related to enhancing daily "quality of life," despite being equally valued. While important, pursuing distant benefits from exercise such as "health promotion," "disease prevention," and "longevity" might not be as compelling to busy individuals compared to their other daily priorities and responsibilities. By shifting our paradigm from medicine to marketing, we can glean insights into how we can better market and "sell" exercise. Because immediate payoffs motivate behavior better than distant goals, a more effective "hook" for promoting sustainable participation might be to rebrand exercise as a primary way individuals can enhance the quality of their daily lives. These findings have important implications for how we as a culture, especially those in fitness-related businesses, health promotion, health care, and public health, prescribe and market exercise on individual and population levels.

  • Physical Activity Advertisements that Feature Daily Well-being Improve Autonomy and Body Image in Overweight Women but Not Me

    • Journal of Obesity
    • June 7, 2012
    Authors: Michelle Segar, PhD, MPH

    There has been an assumption in society, especially health care, that promoting and prescribing physical activity for logical health and weight-related benefits to overweight and obese men and women is good to do – our study directly investigates this presumption. This large exercise marketing study was funded by monies from the National Institutes of Health.

  • Health Promoters Should Stop Promoting Health: New Science for Behavioral Sustainability

    • White Paper
    • August 16, 2013
    Authors: Michelle Segar, PhD, MPH

    Using interesting graphics and fresh evidence-based ideas, this white paper clearly explains why "health" is the wrong outcome for health promoters, clinicians, business, and government to market and prescribe to individuals WHEN BEHAVIORAL SUSTAINABILITY IS THE GOAL. In this paper, I also explain which outcomes better lead to the sustainable motivation and consistent decision making that underlie fitness, health, and well-being. I hope you will read this brief report.
