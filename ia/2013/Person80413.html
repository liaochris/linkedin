Leslie Pratch Pratch & Company, Inc.'s Summary

Clinical psychologist with an M.B.A. who founded a company to integrate business strategy with sophisticated clinical methods of personality assessment to predict and enhance management performance. Twofold focus on management due diligence for private equity firms vetting a prospective investment and coaching already high functioning professionals (investors, investment bankers, financial officers, accountants, lawyers, to mention a few). Expanding Pratch & Company to bring expertise in business restructuring, mergers and acquisitions, and capital formation.

Specialties

Executive assessment and development; management team building; integration of strategy and a company's most expensive human capital; succession planning; development of optimal organizational structure as the competitive environment changes or the organization grows.

Leslie Pratch Pratch & Company, Inc.'s Experience

CEO and Founder

Pratch & Company

February 1998 – Present (15 years 9 months) Chicago

Leslie Pratch, Ph.D. is a clinical psychologist who specializes in helping public and privately-held companies identify whether executives considered for crucial leadership roles possess the psychological resources and personality strengths needed to be successful in those roles.

Her evaluations are called Active Coping Assessments(SM) and are derived from research she conducted at the Booth School of Business at The University of Chicago. She makes use of this research also in coaching executives on how to improve their functioning so as to deal successfully with the crises that face CEOs and other top executives.

Leslie's dissertation research focused on what makes successful business leaders successful--do a firm’s most capable leaders possess personality traits that can be identified years before these executives are ready to assume top positions?

Leslie’s research into this question was financed by The University of Chicago. During her post-doctoral fellowship where she received additional clinical training Leslie also helped senior corporate officers of the State Farm Insurance Companies to develop a library of competencies for use in performance evaluation. As her consulting focused more and more on successful senior executives, Leslie enrolled in the M.B.A. program at the University of Chicago to receive additional training in economics and finance.

Since 1998, Leslie’s consulting has emphasized the assessment and development of executives and succession planning by officers and directors. More recently, Leslie has focused on helping private equity investors reduce the risk associated with investing in uncertain management capability.

Leslie’s clinical skills, combined with her business training, give her insight into the human dimensions of corporate strategy–-where the company’s success will be driven largely by the personality dynamics of the leader.

Leslie Pratch Pratch & Company, Inc.'s Publications

  • Why Women Leaders Need Self-Confidence

    • HBR Blog Network
    • November 28, 2011
    Authors: Leslie Pratch Pratch & Company, Inc.

    Among the relationships I examined in research I led at the University of Chicago's Business School investigating the personality predictors of leadership were gender, coping, and motivation.

    The only measure that predicted leadership for men and women alike was an active coping, an overall measure that indicates the ability to respond adaptively to stress and to grow. But this measure was far more strongly associated with perceptions of women's leadership effectiveness than it was for men's. Even stronger differences emerged when breaking down the elements of active coping.  

    Gender role stereotypes suggest that female-stereotypical forms of leadership are interpersonally oriented and collaborative, whereas male-stereotypical forms of leadership are task oriented and dominating. To the extent that women who are leaders exhibit a masculine style, they amplify their role conflict and increase the chances of receiving unfairly negative evaluations.

    Male leaders do not face a basic role conflict analogous to the conflict female leaders face because expectations about behavior that is appropriate for a leader coincide largely with beliefs about the behavior that is appropriate for men. Men are freer to carry out leadership in a variety of styles without encountering negative reactions because their leadership is ordinarily perceived as legitimate.

    For women, the ability to identify and face difficulties openly and non-defensively predicted leadership beyond any chance occurrence. The correlation between self-confidence and leadership effectiveness was also overwhelmingly statistically significant.   These findings indicate that women must have high self-esteem and high self-confidence while leading in a communal style to be perceived as effective leaders. In short, they must be stronger copers in order to transcend the constraints placed on their leadership style.

  • Integrity in Business Executives

    • The Journal of Private Equity
    • December 2009
    Authors: Leslie Pratch Pratch & Company, Inc.

    To manifest integrity in business means behaving with a clear set of values; those values balance self-interest and concern with interest in and concern with others. This balance is consistent with the values and goals of the community. Executives who have integrity think about their actions writ broadly and how they will affect others. This paper examines the development of integrity (and its lack) in business executives. The Discussion considers how investors can increase their chances of selecting executives who will act with high integrity, that is, use the independence and power that comes with their roles responsibly and constructively, "in the interests of all."

  • Why Integrity and Coping Skills Are Better Indicators of Leadereship success

    • Directorship, p. 67
    • May 24, 2012
    Authors: Leslie Pratch Pratch & Company, Inc.

    Leadership is headline news every day. What makes an effective leader? And how can you best predict and influence leadership’s outcomes? For all institutions, these are critical issues. For investors, Wall Street keeps score on this with stock price. CEOs who grace the covers of Fortune and Forbes yet their own hand-picked successors fail, raising questions regarding the ability of CEOs to make accurate predictions when succession planning. Other emerging stars also fall on their faces when they finally make it to CEO.

    All were good on paper.

    To give some specific examples: Who would have predicted from the 20-year tenure of David Pottruck at Schwab that he would fail so miserably as the hand-picked successor to founder Chuck Schwab?

    There are two main ways of finding candidates for senior management positions. Either a position is filled by an executive from within or a search firm is retained to recommend outside candidates.

    But neither process of selection is adequate to predict future performance. What is the overall rate of success in predicting executive performance over the last 20 years using either of the two selection processes? (Answer: surprisingly lower than you would think, and not at all surprising if you look at outcomes over the last 10 years!)

    Measures of past performance conflate individual capability and situational factors. Too often, hiring decisions are made without making a distinction between an executive’s success and the extent to which context may have contributed to that success.

    What do companies need to know to increase the probability that an executive will perform effectively? Two factors are essential to assess:

    • The core integrity of the executive, in the context of his or her whole personality.
    • The active coping of the executive under the widest possible range of conditions and challenges.

  • Integrative Capacity and the Evaluation of Leadership Effectiveness: A Multimethod Assessment Approach

    • Journal of Applied Behavioral Science
    • 1998
    Authors: Leslie Pratch Pratch & Company, Inc., Jordan Jacobowitz

    This is the first empirical study to examine the effects of active coping on leadership effectiveness in a population of high achieving individuals. It was conducted at The University of Chicago - Booth School of Business. This article presents a psychological framework for predicting business leadership. The Active Coping assessment framework enables a psychologist to make highly accurate predictions regarding an executive's performance in highly stressful or uncertain conditions. The predictive power of our measures is higher than any other approach presently used in Corporate America.

  • Gender, Motivation, and Coping in the Evaluation of Leadership Effectiveness

    • Consulting Psychology Journal: Practice and Research
    • 1996
    Authors: Leslie Pratch Pratch & Company, Inc., Jordan Jacobowitz

    This is the first empirical study to explore the interplay between gender, motivation, and coping style in an elite population of high achieving business leaders. It discusses how gender role stereotypes constrain the kind of leadership styles that female executives are able to use in corporate settings. Behaviors and personality traits that are tolerated in male leaders are often not tolerated in female leaders. The findings suggest that female leaders must be more task-focused, more open to complexity, and more self-confident.
