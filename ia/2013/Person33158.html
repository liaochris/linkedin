Providing direction and leadership towards the following mission, vision, and objective:

Mission: The Great Plains STEM Education Center is dedicated to the full implementation of an integrated STEM Education program in the K-12 schools.

Vision: to move North Dakota to the forefront of STEM Education.

The Center has three primary goals:

* To transform teaching and learning in the K-12 STEM fields, by influencing teacher preparation and providing in-service training and materials to those already in the classroom.

    Mission of the ND STEM Network

    The ND STEM Network connects and increases collaboration across the state, creating greater opportunities for science, technology, engineering, and mathematics education and a highly competitive workforce. The Network strengthens the education system in North Dakota, further align education with economic development and workforce needs and providing skills and opportunities for all student to be globally competitive upon graduation.

  • Agriculture Education and Outreach with the Ag Cab Lab

    • June 2012 to Present

    The purpose of the Ag Cab Lab is to engage and inform the general public through a hands-on learning center in an actual farm cab that simulates driving experiences on a farm, engages the consumer and answers consumer questions. Ultimately we would like to influence parents and children on issues around health and nutrition, teaching where their food comes from, living on the farm, and exposing them to the magic of technologies used in precision farm practices. This is accomplished through strategic placement in county and state fairs, as well as long-term educational centers such as children's museums, to maximize consumer engagement.

Project support has been realized by the ND Council on the Arts and the Fund for the Improvement of Postsecondary Education at the Department of Education.

    The Numi Offaxis Neutrino Appearance (NOvA) experiment utilizes a beam of neutrinos focused on a particle detector located at Ash River, MN to explore the fundamental properties of the neutrino. Students at UMN Crookston and I are developing a Quality Assurance and Inventory Management system on Linux, using Java EE and PostgreSQL technologies to track the installation of the NOvA detector.

  • River Watch Data System

    • May 2004 to Present

    As high school students collect water quality data on several nearby streams, tributaries, and rivers, data integrity is vital when decision makers in the basin utilize their data. Students and I have developed and maintain the River Watch site for over 25 schools in the basin, providing a straightforward but sophisticated data acquisition and interpretation system for learning and research.

  • River of Dreams Canoe Project

    • May 2009 to Present

    The River of Dreams Canoe Project captures the imagination of K-6 grade students in teaching them where their water goes. In groups, students paint small canoes then release them in a nearby water system, such as the Red River of the North, at Fargo, ND. Our web-based system allows the public to report found-canoes, ensuring privacy alongside an administration console for managing canoe release events. This project is in a partnership with the International Water Institute of Fargo, ND.

  • NCAA Golden Eagle Positive CHOICES

    • August 2009 to July 2012

    Baseline data is collected from student athletes at the University of Minnesota, Crookston campus to monitor and track attitudes and usage of alcohol and drugs that would compromise their wellness. In addition to the bi-annual survey instrument, a "Grades Matter, Performance Matters" poster campaign, senior athletes as well as residence hall leaders become Certified Peer Educators to encourage making positive choices throughout their lives. Funded for three years by the National Collegiate Athletic Association (NCAA) in a partnership with a campus ATOD program, this grant provides enhanced programming during the National Collegiate Alcohol Awareness Week in October and April, and throughout the year at athletic events.

David DeMuth, Jr.'s Publications

  • Scientific Opportunities with the Long-Baseline Neutrino Experiment

    • LBNE Collaboration (C. Adams (Yale U.) et al.)
    • July 28, 2013
    Authors: David DeMuth, Jr.

    Abstract: In this document, we describe the wealth of science opportunities and capabilities of LBNE, the Long-Baseline Neutrino Experiment. LBNE has been developed to provide a unique and compelling program for the exploration of key questions at the forefront of particle physics. Chief among the discovery opportunities are observation of CP symmetry violation in neutrino mixing, resolution of the neutrino mass hierarchy, determination of maximal or near-maximal mixing in neutrinos, searches for nucleon decay signatures, and detailed studies of neutrino bursts from galactic supernovae. To fulfill these and other goals as a world-class facility, LBNE is conceived around four central components: (1) a new, intense wide-band neutrino source at Fermilab, (2) a fine-grained `near' neutrino detector just downstream of the source, (3) the Sanford Underground Research Facility (SURF) in Lead, South Dakota at an optimal distance (~1300 km) from the neutrino source, and (4) a massive liquid argon time-projection chamber (LArTPC) deployed there as a 'far' detector. The facilities envisioned are expected to enable many other science opportunities due to the high event rates and excellent detector resolution from beam neutrinos in the near detector and atmospheric neutrinos in the far detector. This is a mature, well developed, world class experiment whose relevance, importance, and probability of unearthing critical and exciting physics has increased with time.
