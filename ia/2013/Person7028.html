Co-founder

MotionThink

Privately Held; 1-10 employees; Computer Software industry

2011 – 2012 (1 year) Palo Alto, CA

I co-founded a startup as part of the 2nd and final batch of companies in the Runway program, an incubator backed by Eric Schmidt's Innovation Endeavors that is no longer in operation.

We prototyped various product ideas around lead generation and productivity for freelance workers, and I led our efforts around user need-finding, customer development, investor relations, product prototyping, front-end engineering, legal, and finance.

We ultimately disbanded because our product interests began to diverge, but it was a great experience learning the nuts and bolts of how to start a company, how to conduct user need-finding, and how to rapidly prototype ideas to test a hypothesis.

The biggest lessons I learned were:

(1) You are unlikely to analytically derive the right answer re: what kind of product to build by using abstract entrepreneurship "frameworks"; you have to start from what you're personally passionate about, ideally solving a problem you experience firsthand, and let that guide your product development. As Paul Graham said, "it's hard to do a really good job on anything you don't think about in the shower." And that doesn't happen but for things you care about deeply.

(2) Spend most of your time doing, less time talking, and let results, not debates, drive your startup's decisions.

(3) Listen to advice, read blogs, and learn theory -- but ultimately, you have to decide what works best for your company. Everyone may have an opinion, but in the end you're the one who is held accountable. So you have to do what you believe is right in your heart, even if others disagree with you.

(4) Align 100% on roles and goals with your co-founders from Day 1.

It was an action-packed year of learning everything possible about consumer internet through owning the Y! Downloads P&L, and joining tech talks, workshops, and hack days. I left Yahoo! during a time of deep organizational change (the day after Carol Bartz was fired by the board of directors) and jumped into entrepreneurship to co-found my own startup.

Harvard Law School

I served on the Asia Law Society leadership team, where I co-chaired the largest conference on Asian business in North America in 2006 (the 1000-person Asia Business Conference), and led a 15-person delegation to Taiwan that met privately with both Taiwanese presidential candidates in 2007.

I also TF'ed for the Federal Income Tax course taught at HLS and for the undergrad class "Race, Ethnicity, and Politics in the U.S." taught at Harvard College.

I very much enjoyed the study of law, the deep policy debates it exposed me to, and the rigorous thinking and writing skills I learned from it. I am admitted to the New York Bar.

Tsinghua University

J. William Fulbright Scholar

I moved to China for 2 years after college to shoot a documentary film about rural Chinese education reform for the Zigen Fund, a 25-year-old NGO that funds and supports education and healthcare development initiatives in poor rural Chinese communities. Zigen has active projects in several provinces including Guizhou, Hebei, Shanxi, and others.

Development economics as it relates to affordable access to education and healthcare is something I am deeply interested in, and my film explored both policy issues and the human stories behind China's rural education development. Zigen has been able to use the film as an educational and fundraising tool to spread awareness and raise thousands of dollars in donations.

You can view the 35-minute film here: http://www.youtube.com/watch?v=wileqrH_4q0. It was the project I was selected for as one of ten undergraduate Fulbright Scholars to China from the United States in 2002, and I was affiliated with Tsinghua University during that time.

The University of Texas at Austin

B.A., Plan II Honors Program, History

I enrolled in the Plan II Honors interdisciplinary liberal arts program at UT (its own major), and picked up a second major in history, coming a couple classes shy of a third major in literature as well.

Studying history taught me to think about how parts of the modern world evolved. Combining that with literature showed me how writers tell stories in the context of history, and how politics and events shape the writing of fiction. I learned to think critically about historiography, or the methods by which history is interpreted, which fundamentally anchors and frames how debates are carried out in the present. History and literature were also great teachers of how to analyze and write thesis-driven essays compellingly.

In my junior spring, I externed as a U.S. Congressman Bill Archer Fellow in Washington, D.C., as a Labor Affairs Intern at the U.S. Department of State, Bureau of Democracy, Human Rights, and Labor. I graduated a proud longhorn Phi Beta Kappa and summa cum laude.
