Charles Euchner's Summary

Charles Euchner is case writer and editor at Yale School of Management.

Euchner is a writer and teacher with over 20 years of experience in the classroom and nine books in print. Through his company, The Writing Code, Euchner offers one- and two-day "boot camp" seminars to corporate professionals, book authors, and students at all levels.

Euchner's book "Nobody Turn Me Around: A People's History of the 1963 March on Washington" was published by Beacon in 2010 and is under development for a major documentary. A major new edition of his book "The Big Book of Writing" is available in ebook and paperback formats. A number of "minis" -- short books on various writing and communications topics, like Truman Capote, Mad Men, email, documentary writing, business writing, and more -- are nearing completion.

Euchner's previous books explore civil rights ("Nobody Turn Me Around"), baseball ("The Last Nine Innings" and "Little League, Big Dreams"), cities and planning ("Urban Policy Reconsidered," "Playing the Field," "Governing Greater Boston"), and grassroots social movements ("Extraordinary Politics").

Charles Euchner has delivered his writing seminars -- dynamic one- and two-day sessions that involve roleplaying, team story-writing, videos, and more -- in 12 states and the District of Columbia. He has worked with major corporations, schools, teachers unions, and universities.

    • Sourcebooks
    • February 2006
    Authors: Charles Euchner

    A dramatic narrative of the seventh game of the 2001 World Series, when the Arizona Diamondbacks defeated the three-time defending champion New York Yankees. The book uses the game to analyze the game's recent changes -- globalization, sabermetrics, and biomechanics, among other things. "The last word on the inside game of baseball," says Frank Deford. "Rich in thought-provoking detail and context, in the manner of Malcolm Gladwell," says Sports Illustrated's Tom Verducci.

    • Sourcebooks
    • August 2006
    Authors: Charles Euchner

    A clear-eyed view of the modern world of youth sports, as viewed through the 2005 Little League World Series. "The book and its unsparing look at the harsh reality of youth sports just might pique the interest of parents whose kids play in the more than 8,000 officially sanctioned League team," says Publishers Weekly.
