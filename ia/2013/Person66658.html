John Armstrong's Summary

My professional teaching career began as a Ph.D. student when I taught philosophy at the University of Arizona and, during a summer, at Brigham Young University. Since joining the Southern Virginia University faculty, I have taught numerous courses in philosophy and the humanities, including one as a visiting professor at Washington and Lee University. I love philosophy for the importance of the questions it addresses and the habits of thought it imparts.

My experience in higher education administration has included a regular three-year term as chair of the Division of Humanities and four years as the associate provost at Southern Virginia. While helping to administer the academic affairs of the university, I taught two courses per semester.

Among colleges and universities, I am partial to those with core curricula centered on great works of literature, philosophy, history, art and science from western and other traditions. I also prefer colleges that create common intellectual experiences for their students and encourage their spiritual growth.

Specialties

As an academic administrator, I have strengths in working with accreditation and state agencies, assessing student learning outcomes in general education and major programs, institutional effectiveness, academic planning, student registration and advisement, and fundraising for faculty development.

As a professor, my teaching and scholarship focus on the history of philosophy, especially ancient Greek and Roman philosophy, and on moral and political philosophy.

John Armstrong's Projects

  • Accreditation with the Southern Association of Colleges and Schools Commission on Colleges

    • 2006 to 2012
    Team Members: John Armstrong, Madison Sowell, Rodney Smith, Paul Edwards, richard whitehead, Scott Doxey, Bob Huch, Brett Garcia, Burke Olsen, Gertrud L. Kraut, Craig Lawhorne

    Southern Virginia University sought membership with the Southern Association of Colleges and Schools Commission on Colleges. The Commission granted initial candidacy to the university in 2010 and then initial accreditation (membership) in 2012.

    In September 2011, the university sent a 256-page, single-spaced compliance certification with over 400 supporting documents to the Commission to demonstrate compliance with all the Commission's standards, including those on mission, governance, institutional effectiveness, curriculum, faculty, library, student life, finances, and facilities. A nine-member accreditation committee visited the university in October 2011. The committee's post-visit report contained "no recommendations," i.e., no areas of noncompliance. On June 21, 2012, Commission's board of trustees granted initial accreditation to the Southern Virginia University, effective January 1, 2012.

    This was the university's second institutional accreditation since Southern Virginia began anew in 1996. The first was granted by a national accrediting agency, the American Academy for Liberal Education, in 2003 after a three-year candidacy period.

John Armstrong's Publications

  • "After the Ascent: Plato on Becoming Like God"

    • Oxford Studies in Ancient Philosophy
    • 2004
    Authors: John Armstrong

    Plato is associated with the idea that the body holds us back from knowing ultimate reality and so we should try to distance ourselves from its influence. This sentiment appears is several of his dialogues including Theaetetus where the flight from the physical world is compared to becoming like God. In some major dialogues of Plato's later career such as Philebus and Laws, however, the idea of becoming like God takes a different turn. God is an intelligent force that tries to create order in the physical world. I argue that likeness to God in these dialogues involves imitating God's effort by trying to order our bodies, souls, and societies as intelligence directs. Becoming like Plato's God is not to abandon the world. It is to improve it.

  • "Aristotle on the Philosophical Nature of Poetry"

    • The Classical Quarterly
    • 1998
    Authors: John Armstrong

    In Poetics chapter 9, Aristotle famously claims that poetry is more philosophical than history. What does this mean? I argue that he is talking about the metaphysics of events. Poets seek causal coherence among the events in their stories. Historians must report what happened whether or not the events of history exhibit causal coherence. This makes the poet's job more philosophical than the historian's, for the poet is seeking a unified plot — an action-type — that serves as the backbone of the story he is trying to tell. The poet's final script is a token of an Aristotelian universal, the universal being an action-type that could have been fleshed out in many different ways, although with similar characters. Hence, the poet shows us what he thinks it is likely or necessary for agents of different types to do in the circumstances that confront them. Aristotelian mimesis, on this account, is the poetic representation of "one action," that is, of one action-type.

  • "Epicurean Justice"

    • Phronesis
    • 1997
    Authors: John Armstrong

    Epicurus (341–270 B.C.) is one of the first social contract theorists, holding that justice is an agreement neither to harm nor be harmed. He also says that living justly is necessary and sufficient for living pleasantly, which is the Epicurean goal. Some say that there are two accounts of justice in Epicurus — one as a personal virtue, the other as a virtue of institutions. I argue that the personal virtue derives from compliance with just social institutions, and so we need to attribute only one account of justice to Epicurus. I show how this interpretation makes sense of claims about justice by Epicurus and his followers, including Hermarchus, Lucretius, and Diogenes of Oinoanda.
