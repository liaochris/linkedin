  • Workplaces of today, with a debt to '60s

    • The Boston Globe
    • December 6, 2010
    Authors: Jeffrey Mulliken AIA LEED AP

    Recently the Boston Globe published an editorial, "Noisy offices: Less distraction for Dilbert," roughly stating that when privacy and concentration are required, build walls and doors. I responded with a little historical background on the open office itself (how we got here) and how we design spaces around how work actually gets done, we can accommodate both collaboration and privacy.

    Full Text:

    The transition to open-plan work environments began in 1968, with the introduction of Action Office by the Herman Miller Co., inspired by the Bürolandschaft, or office landscape, concept developed in Germany in the late 1950s. The ideologies that supported these concepts included egalitarian management, free flow of information, opportunities for collaboration, and a healthier worker (with features such as stand-up desks to increase blood flow) — all in the quest for more productive, satisfied employees.
    Office landscape devolved to a Dilbert world, where space planning efficiency means rows of identical cubes. Sound masking can become a necessity in these environments. “Dilbertville’’ is really Bürolandschaft taken to an illogical extreme. But we shouldn’t blame the cube, and put everyone behind a wall and a door.
    Some forward-thinking companies today are implementing work spaces that do include individual cubes with low panels to encourage interaction, but also include flexible open and closed collaboration spaces (not just mind-deadening conference rooms), informal socialization areas, and yes, privacy rooms with a wall and a door.
    Designing work environments that support the many ways work actually gets done, including collaboration, interaction, and privacy when needed, can help bring peace and satisfaction to tormented workers.
