Daniel Klass' Projects

  • Intel® Data Segmentation

    • January 2013 to April 2013
    Team Members: Daniel Klass, Josh Copeland, Matt Royer, Kevin Williams, Tim Duket, Juan Carlos Sansón, Sergio Arguelles

    My senior design team was charged with finding a method by which our customer, Intel, could separate data on user’s devices in terms of whether it was for corporate purposes or personal purposes. This would alleviate the need to store all user data and thus reduce the storage space required and cost. Also, the method by which this is accomplished ideally would require a minimum of user interaction and handle itself in a mostly automated fashion.

    The method that was found to have the most flexibility and produce good quality results was that of a multi-variable linear regression. The user’s file metadata would be properly coded by an algorithm, and then fed into a table so a regression could be run on it in a periodic fashion. The data would be subject to heuristics determined by IT as well as general checks on how good the regression equation really is.

    The main decisions based on the linear regression in the logical decision tree happen simultaneously as the confidence checks of the equation. Due to the way the regression has been tailored, the equation outputs a value typically between zero and one, where close to zero implies being “personal” and close to one implies being “corporate.” When the heuristics are applied during the file saving process, they prompt the user when saving with the proper personal or corporate folder based on the regression's output. The end decision, should IT policies not have any interference, is up to the user. Most often, the cutoff(s) between zero and one will handle the majority of the data.

    The results we have found have been very good. At a minimum we have found our method matches data following the 80/20 rule quite well; a worst case of 79% of our data was correctly classified by the equation with all variables included. With this in mind, the cutoff values between zero and one can be tailored to be more or less accurate and may depend on the situation and scale of implementation (personal, departmental, or company-wide).

  • Omni-Rupter® Production Optimization

    • May 2012 to August 2012
    Team Members: Daniel Klass, Kurt Lowes

    Following a Kaizen and data gathering, a lean transformation of the Omni-Rupter® assembly line was put in motion. This included drastic inventory reduction, staffing reductions (32%), footprint reduction (1175 square feet) and lead time reduction (96%). I oversaw and managed the processes of parts being put onto a Kanban inventory control system, and improving visual controls for the assembly line part delivery racks. I wrote standard work for the final assembly line, and created a layout of the re-engineered manufacturing area. Overall savings were $421,000 with $315,000 recurring yearly.

  • Circuit Switcher Production Optimization

    • February 2011 to August 2011
    Team Members: Daniel Klass, Bob Dempsey, Catrina Miksis, Balaji Rajaramachandran, Bruno Castañon Quirarte, Steve Brick

    Circuit switcher production optimization project covered three product lines: Series 2000 ®, Mark V ®, and Trans-Rupter II ®. The project was spurred by EPA regulations restricting the use of greenhouse gases, specifically Sulphur Hexafluoride (SF6). New evacuation and pressurization equipment was needed, however the current layout of the production area was not conducive to their use. A new layout was designed, implemented, and stabilized. In addition to improving the layout, the process of making circuit switchers was documented and standardized. Ergonomics was improved to exceed OSHA requirements for the benefit of assemblers as well as to increase output.

  • Project Management Tool

    • May 2010 to August 2010
    Team Members: Daniel Klass, Alec Gil, Michael Lubin

    A new tool was needed to keep track of project progress and milestones. It needed allow personal progress input and the ability to publish reports on an internal server. The tool was made using Microsoft Excel and incorporated VBA macros to handle the logic behind what locations the reports should be published to (based on project progress). Information was input by the user and was then tabulated and graphically represented both broken down by milestone and as a weighted composition.
