Chas Klivans' Summary

“Today’s businesses succeed by adapting to where the market is going. Better adaptation leads companies to increased sales and profits,” Chas Klivans says. “My experiences in an Afghan prison, the Amazon jungle, and billion-dollar boardrooms make it clear to me that the business environment is like the real jungle, tough but neutral, containing everything a company needs to succeed and traps that can lead to failure. Companies must stop blaming outside forces, accept full responsibility, and go into jungle survival mode just like I did years ago.

“In my experience,” he says, “companies uncover superior products and management tools from similar businesses two to three times their size, not from inside the company. They keep their team, don’t need to go on vision retreats, and just use these proven tactics to successfully execute the plan with minimal risk.”

★ As Chas followed this path, the successes piled up. ★

“Premier, Inc., a $7 billion medical product company, recruited me to manage supply chain purchasing,” Chas says. “In two years, we added $200 million in new revenue to a base of $250 million.”

After 28 years, Chas left the corporate world to start a trusted advisor service, The CEO’s Navigator, in 2001. “With my private clients,” he says, “first, like a surgeon cutting out a cancer we cut away years of accumulated habits, people who don’t fit their positions, and stagnant thinking. Second, like a tree trimmer who prunes away excess foliage, we allow its healthy parts to blossom.”

Chas is a businessperson who speaks, advises, and writes. He is currently completing his first book, which reveals his proven, easily customizable system to build sales and profits while creating a strong corporate brand.

Chas Klivans' Experience

President, Turnaround Specialist, Business Consultant, CEO Coach, Keynote Speaker, Author

The CEO's Navigator - Trusted Advisor Services

2001 – Present (12 years) Tustin, CA

“I’ve been turning companies around for over 12 years,” Chas Klivans says. “No matter how good or bad the economy is, these underperforming companies share many of the same problems and blind spots.”

He believes that today’s businesses succeed by adapting to where the market is going, not where it has been. “Remember Tarzan?” he asks. “He listened to the animals around him to escape danger and find sustenance. Companies master their business jungle when they listen to the ‘animals’ in their environment, who point them to superior innovation, which they import into their departments to improve results.”

One reinvention was a subsidiary of the luxury consumer products company Waterford Wedgwood. “Everyone blamed the sales department for falling sales and weak profits,” he says. “The more we dug, the more we found that the sales department was not to blame.”

In the other 5 departments, he uncovered dominating egos, ineffective marketing practices, and sub-standard manufacturing, which combined to make a competent sales department’s job impossible.

As soon as the company cleaned up the departmental problems and created a new growth plan, the subsidiary was on track to improve sales from $37 million to $60 million, gross margins from 24% to 51%, and after-tax profit from 1% to 18%. He used his unique Kitchen Cabinet approach to recruit top industry operating executives like the former CEO of Laura Ashley to provide external input and verification.

“In another engagement,” he says. “Three elderly brothers invited me into their machine tool business. They were scared of losing their company and living a penniless retirement.” Chas found problems in 4 departments and wrote a plan that grew sales from $3 million to $10 million and after-tax profit from 3%-30%. He kept his promise to keep the family together and when they sold the company three years later, the brothers joyfully retired with significant wealth and gave the employees $1 million in bonuses.

Vice President, Global E-Business, E-Commerce Executive

Edwards Lifesciences

Public Company; 5001-10,000 employees; EW; Medical Devices industry

2001 – 2001 (less than a year) Irvine, California

“Edwards, then a $1.5 billion public medical device company operating in 30 countries, recruited me to build a 30-person global team to develop a plan to transition operations to e-commerce,” Chas says. “I had to make the project pay for itself by increasing revenue and profits and decreasing cost of goods sold and operating expenses.”

He led the global team that created the work plan based on outside business benchmarking. Then, instead of relying exclusively on insiders like most companies do, he implemented one of his signature tools to validate the plan: recruiting an outside Kitchen Cabinet of the world’s most renowned medical product experts from Accenture, IBM Global, SAIC, and Cap Gemini, who acted as mentors to the corporate executive management.

Vice President, Relationship Management

Premier, Inc.

1999 – 2000 (1 year) San Diego, CA

“Premier was huge,” Chas says, “a $7 billion medical product, device, equipment, and supply Group Purchasing Organization.” While there, he managed $250 million in supply chain purchasing for five complex hospital organizations in this business alliance with revenues ranging from $1.5 billion to $4 billion.

“My first challenge,” Chas says, “was to increase that $250 million in sales by 25% or $62.5 million. That’s a lot.” He accomplished that task so well that one customer’s CEO invited him to participate in monthly C-level planning meetings.

“My second challenge was to integrate a new customer, a 48-hospital system, Catholic Healthcare West, into the purchasing coop in 90 days,” he says. “The leaders of that system said it couldn’t be done.” Chas made it happen by creating an envelope of trust between the two organizations so they could work together for mutual reward. As a result, CHW pulled the Premier purchasing team into every level of the company and generated $100 million in sales for Premier in the first year.

Co-Founder, Vice President

MedFirst Healthcare, Inc.

1996 – 1998 (2 years) Culver City, CA

“We created a whole new business model with this startup company,” Chas says, “I remember putting the team together and then approaching Goldman Sachs for funding.”

The company wanted only $20 million but Chas and his team knew that Goldman almost never looked at companies needing less than $150 million. “We sent them a proposal anyway,” Chas says. “After all, you never know what might happen. Goldman surprised us all by writing a check for the $20 million.”

Chas asked one of the partners from Goldman why they dropped their funding floor to $20 million for us. The lead Goldman partner said, “Because all of you are an ‘A’ team.” I replied, “Sir, with all due respect, that sounds nice, but what does that mean?” He said, “We know that you cannot anticipate all the problems you will have nor can you know what changes in the business you will need to make. But you have the management and effective leadership qualities it takes to walk through that ‘wall of fire’ to make it happen.”

The executive was right. Chas as the cofounder created the strategic planning process, the business plan for sales strategy and implementation, did market research to isolate industry trends and define product development, and led the operational roll-out in two national markets. “It took the team just two years, “Chas says, “to take the company from nothing to $70 million in sales annually.”

President, Turnaround CEO, Mergers and Acquisitions Specialist, Speaker

Practice Management Institute, Inc.

1989 – 1996 (7 years) Irvine, CA

“When two multi-million dollar medical practice family businesses, being torn apart by family feuding and employee malfeasance hired me as interim CEO, all the strategies I had learned in graduate school and corporate America failed miserably,” Chas says. “The survival skills I learned in Afghanistan and Peru came to my rescue. They made me realize that I had to change the thinking of the top owners before I could hope to change their tactics. The owners loved and adopted the mantra I used in Afghanistan, ‘Do you want to survive or do you want to be right?’ After the top leadership changed its thinking, we created a corporate turnaround strategy that transformed their business from a hobby lifestyle to a business that produced real wealth and an exit strategy that led to a comfortable retirement.”

While at PMI, Chas represented both small private companies that wanted to sell and large public companies that wanted to buy. “As I facilitated 30 of these mergers, I did all the financial due diligence, profiled target company leaders, did competitive intelligence, constructed sales expansion plans, made pro formas with back-up schedules, created budgets, and valuated business and the people to facilitate the sale,” he says. “That gave us a tremendous ability to look deep into company financials when we had to determine where problems originated.”

He added, “When I later helped another company, a $50 million multi-state franchising company build their first digital dashboard for use by corporate leaders down to the level of field staff, we uncovered hidden root causes of low sales growth and low profitability and under-performing leaders in several under-performing departments.” To grade department key performance indicators (KPI), Chas developed the PMI Index, a unique weekly digital dashboard management tool he still uses today.

Regional Manager Development, Regional Sales Director, Business Development

Republic Health Corporation

1987 – 1989 (2 years) Santa Ana, CA

“This assignment was a real challenge,” Chas says, “one guy, me, with no specific training, in a new office and a new territory, in charge of building sales from nothing in the 12 states west of the Rocky Mountains.”

Chas created the marketing to generate leads, set up prospect funnel management, wrote and delivered proposals, developed company and customer return on investment income statements, built trusting relationships with customer C-level executives (including CEO, COO, CIO, and CFO), negotiated multi-million dollar contracts, and managed post-close customer service.

“In the first six months, I sold nothing,” he says. “Then I remembered the Amazon jungle and Afghanistan and thought to myself, ‘I’ve been through much worse and I can handle this.’ So I went into jungle survival mode and dug deep, found out how to adapt to this new territory, and turned failure into a raving success. In the first two years, I initiated and closed over $14 million in new sales.”
