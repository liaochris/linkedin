Joshua Saint's Summary

I've worked in a number of different industries (commercial refrigeration, automotive manufacturing, mortgage, office supplies, higher education, food & beverage, etc.), and one of the things I've learned is that data is data. Face it, all data is input, manipulated, stored, and output. Those common operations explain why every organization can store its data in a SQL database whether that data concerns students, loans, service calls, or widget sales. Sure, the tables and transactions vary in form and complexity, but the same language is used to build, maintain, and query all the different databases.

Not coincidentally, the same skills are used by the developers and analysts who slice and dice that data and present it in a way that is not just appetizing, but is also useful. In other words, that mass of data has been turned into meaningful, insightful, and actionable information, and is served in just the right quantities, in just the right format, and to just the right consumers.

Virtually every position I've held has started out as either a developer or a data analyst, and each has ended up with me doing both in some fashion. The combination is natural - my focus has always been on user interface design and the visual presentation of data, and you can't do either successfully without a thorough understanding of the data that underlies a system and the needs of the people who enter, manipulate, and review that data.

My current emphasis is on the extraction and analysis of data - commonly referred to as business intelligence - and on the development of interactive, web-based dashboards that give managers the ability to make decisions based on timely, accurate, and focused information. A common need of all businesses that requires an uncommon mix of skills, experience, and ability.
