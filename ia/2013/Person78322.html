Dina LaPolt's Experience

UCLA Extension, Entertainment Studies Dept

UCLA Extension

Educational Institution; 201-500 employees; Education Management industry

January 2002 – Present (11 years 9 months)

Whether you are an artist, manager, lawyer, accountant, music industry executive, producer, songwriter, or music publisher, this course is essential to your understanding of the various ways in which rights are exploited and monetized and how revenue is generated in the music business. One of the most important topics covered by the course is the critical importance of building your artist’s brand as a business. The necessity of ancillary revenue streams outside of music – such as sponsorships and endorsements, opportunities in film and television, book publishing, and creative forms of merchandising – cannot be overstated. The music industry began as a product-based business but has quickly evolved into a service-based business over the past several years despite the fact that the U.S. Copyright Act and other laws do not currently support these transformations. Much of this course focuses on the legal and business issues related to these drastic changes.

Also examined are arrangements between record companies and artists; production deals, producer agreements and other legal and business issues that arise in the recording studio; trademark and rights of publicity; agreements relating to the artist’s executive team and how those job descriptions are evolving with the industry; topics and agreements relating to music publishing rights; monetizing downloads and subscription services; and discussions pertaining to fans, brands, social networking sites, and cultural communities as they affect music, copyrights, and merchandising in a global music market.

Throughout the course, Dina also discusses current events relating to the ever-changing tides of the music industry and their impact on the future of the business. Class discussions are encouraged as many of the attendees in the class are already working in the music business and can bring their own unique perspective and experiences to the table.

    • International Association of Entertainment Lawyers www.IAEL.org
    • January 15, 2012
    Authors: Dina LaPolt

    Building Your Artist’s Brand as a Business focuses on expanding the artist’s career beyond traditional revenue streams into new territory while still respecting their artistic integrity. The concept of branding, what used to be viewed by an artist as “selling out” has now become the norm and quite possibly the necessity for maintaining a successful music career. Too often, artists prematurely present their vision to uninterested business people and audiences due to lack of analysis as to who their fans actually are.
    The music industry is a global business and representatives must understand the legal and practical aspects of other countries, not just the United States.
