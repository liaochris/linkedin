John H. Shrawder's Summary

Educator, Faculty Developer, Exec. Director, Teaching For Success®

Founded, Teaching For Success® (TFS) in 1989. Originated and now manage the TFS Adjunct Faculty e-Mentor Program. This program is all about helping colleges and universities connect with their adjunct faculty for the purpose of improving teaching clarity, confidence, and competence.

Executive Director Teaching For Success, Manager, Leader: Faculty Developer and Trainer, Community College Aviation Dept. Chair, Full-time Aviation Instructor, USAF Acft Maintenance Officer, Aircraft Mechanic.

Specialties: Creating and delivering adjunct faculty instructional improvement and professional success training and development e-resources and face-to-face learning experiences.

  • Indentify and Organize The Critical Success Factors of Good Teaching

    • September 2002 to Present
    Team Members: John H. Shrawder, Bill Geibel, Penny Shrawder, Dave Bequette

    Every job, like teaching consists of a set of Critical Success Factors. (CSFs). A CSF is a task is so crucial to the successful outcome of the job that the failure to get acceptable results in this area will doom the entire job effort to failure. I have been involved in an ongoing project to identify the CSF of good teaching and to organize and define them for the purpose of improving efforts to train and develop the skills and capabilities of Adjunct and Part-time Higher education faculty. The posted chart is a current summary of our thinking at Teaching For Success Faculty Success Center.

  • A TFS Quick Course: How to Build Positive Class Discipline

    • August 2012 to Present
    Team Members: John H. Shrawder, Elizabeth Butts, Michael Griffith

Using the advice, and following the steps found in this excellent TFS QuickCourse, an instructor will be able to determine what behavior(s) is (are) unacceptable and outline a strategy for dealing positively with this behavior in a systematic way.

  • A TFS Quick Course: How to Ensure Integrity in Academic Writing

    • February 2010 to Present
    Team Members: John H. Shrawder, Thomas M. Powers

    First, writing integrity should be identified as a learning outcome. Making the effort to ensure that all students complete all writing assignments honestly is a goal that benefits everyone. Only when integrity is maintained can you feel confident in the efficacy of your teaching. Only when you are sure your students are legitimately generating their own writing can you be convinced that your students have gained invaluable academic skills and an awareness of the professional values necessary for success.

  • A TFS Quick Course: How to Test and Evaluate Learning

    • April 2008 to Present
    Team Members: John H. Shrawder, Kay Steffen, Brian Shmaefsky, Michael Griffith

    Student arguments, complaints and disappointments over testing are all a possibility during any course, unless you have developed a sound working knowledge of the evaluation and testing skills required to be a successful instructor. Conflict occurs because students care deeply about tests and test results, since they so profoundly affect their academic careers. When I talk with students, I find poorly written, tricky and ambiguous or irrelevant test questions are at the top of the list of students’ complaints about their instructors. Students deserve good, fair tests, and this Quick Course can help you create them for your students, it will give you the fundamental knowledge you need to be comfortable and competent in the area of testing and evaluation.

  • A TFS Quick Course: How to Make Small-Group Learning Work

    • September 2009 to Present
    Team Members: John H. Shrawder, Francine Armenth-Brothers

    Have you ever used small-group learning in your classes? Were you happy with the results? Have you considered making this instructional design an integral part of your courses, but aren’t sure how to go about it? Do you have reservations about incorporating small groups into your curriculum, perhaps believing other methods are more successful? If so, you share concerns common throughout higher education. This mini-course is designed to address these concerns and help you achieve successful results with small-group learning. It teaches you the benefits and major principles of small-group work, as well as how to properly assess it. It also addresses instructors’ most common concerns, and concludes with several small-group structures that you can implement immediately.

  • A TFS Quick Course: How to Prevent Harassment in the Academic Setting

    • July 2007 to Present
    Team Members: John H. Shrawder, Brian Shmaefsky

    This QuickStudy is NOT intended to dispense legal advice nor set policy for any institution. Its sole purpose is to increase awareness among instructors, staff, and administrators of harassment issues and to help foster professional teaching behaviors and practices. This material has been written from the instructor’s point of view. The way an instructor behaves sets the tone of the learning environment. Student evaluations of faculty competence reflect that faculty behavior plays an integral role in developing a learning environment for the students. So, even with the best instruction, student comprehension can be compromised and diminished when faculty are perceived to act in inappropriate ways.

  • TFS Faculty Development Workshop: How to Have Your Best Class Ever

    • July 2011 to Present
    Team Members: John H. Shrawder, Penny Shrawder

    A new TFS Workshop to connect faculty to better teaching through increased clarity, confidence and competence. To teach their best class ever, each attendee to this workshop is challenged to create and takeaway a personalized teaching plan that includes selecting a major-focus goal and formulating a set of practical action steps necessary to bring this goal to fruition.

    In order to successfully select a major-focus goal and formulate the required action steps, faculty will explore the characteristics of the growth mindset and learn how to acquire the expertise necessary to reach desired goals and outcomes. Faculty will learn how to apply the concept of Critical Success Factors to their teaching efforts. Faculty will rate their expertise in each of the six Critical Success Factors of good teaching and work out a personal development path to strengthen skills in less-developed factors.
