    Conflict Resolution; Dysfunctional Teams

    I was contacted by a client to provide help to two cross-functional project teams comprised of key personnel from information technology and information services.

    A serious lack of trust existed between the project leaders and between the teams. Within and between the teams, there was a hostile whispering campaign, poor cooperation, selective withholding of valuable information, and other forms of overt and covert sabotage. Many people were behaving in passive-aggressive ways. Meetings and discussions were tense and often unproductive. Outside of meetings, rumors and intrigue filled the company. The resulting consequences included poor individual and organizational morale.

    After conducting one-on-one interviews with all project team members, we decided to spend a day with each project team off-site.

    We structured an off-site day where all participants were to suspend their assumptions about the teams, team membership, and their own expectations of success. We focused on getting group members to consider four key questions related to the project team's mission, resources, obstacles and opportunities. Where previously there had been a climate of censure and dysfunction, we cleared the air and created an everyday organizational climate where people could speak and be heard.

    During both sessions, we all were able to agree on ground rules for project teamwork that our clients in retrospect say marked the turning point for success. Once ground rules had been established, the project teams began to change the ways they conducted business. Notably, they expanded opportunities for participation and worked harder to communicate during critical decision points.

    Symptom Resolution VS Problem Resolution

    Three departments needed to work closely with each other, but the stress, conflicts, and turf wars between them always took center stage.

    Initially, I listened and observed. Through a series of meetings I continued to hear one name come up: Katherine. Katherine had been the director of these three departments thirteen years earlier. To the intense frustration of some and the outward delight of others, she had been terminated those dozen-plus years ago, but the dissention over Katherine's termination still lingered.

    Discovering the "Katherine Legacy" was a profound one for this case. In that moment it became crystal clear that, from the moment of Katherine's termination, this organization had been coping - however feebly - with a deep split. The "us versus them" culture was pervasive.

    Once found, this covert hot spot was dealt with by bringing it out into the open. The people of the three departments and I discussed the issues, rather than run from them. This issue had sucked the creative life from their talented staffs and had held back the productivity of their organization for more than a decade. To say this realization was a profound "Aha!" for the director would not be an understatement.

    Based on solid, mutually agreed upon, and effective communication habits, the strength of this organization was restored.
    • Departments were back to work.
    • Attitudes and behaviors were noticeably improved.
    • People within and between departments were getting along.
    • Individual and organizational objectives were achieved.
    • Productivity was measurably increased.

    Refining Employee Performance Evaluation Skills


    "Sophia" had been a successful business owner for over 17 years. She wanted to refine her employee performance evaluation skills.

    Sophia self-identified that she "wanted to be liked" and had a tendency to avoid problems. We established objectives, decided on realistic measures for success, agreed on timelines, and got to work on her two goals:
    • create performance evaluations that were more specific and less anxiety provoking for her employees and herself, and
    • directly connect her performance evaluations to her organizational goals

    We looked carefully at how decisions were made in her business. The need for attention to detail (specificity) and keeping on track (relevance to the vision). Sophia wanted to make the evaluation process specific to the individual, but relevant to the organizational goals. She created clear, measurable performance standards and procedural guidelines for all staff.

    On Sophia's side of the evaluation process, she realized establishing trust and using different questioning techniques encouraged the employee to bring up his/her possible areas of need and performance improvement, which improved buy-in. We utilized a conversational approach, which left both the employee and the supervisor more at ease and improved the level of trust.

    This performance evaluation process became one of professional development and coaching. It was conducive to open ended conversations that continued outside of Sophia's office. It allowed both Sophia and her employees to be open to create solutions, rather than be limited to HR forms, structures and legalese. Sophia had created for her business "win-win" performance evaluations.

    Successful Mergers

    During this six month experience, thirty out of the ninety employees and managers were slated to lose their jobs due to a merger/acquisition.

    My consulting duties included acting as a confidential sounding board for the general manager. He asked me to help him chart the course of this major upheaval. He needed to troubleshoot current hot spots and anticipate potential issues. He sought to successfully choreograph the timing and delivery of difficult messages and to do so in the most respectful way possible, but also in a way that would be the least disruptive to the company's productivity. Balancing these concerns promised to be a challenge. He needed a trusted, confidential, yet objective professional to whom he could turn.

    I set up measurable objectives to evaluate success of the merger and the transition at each step of the six-month process. These were clearly laid out. Our organization and goal setting made some of the toughest decisions more palatable.

    In addition, I was asked to provide a series of workshops to assist all employees to recognize the inevitable emotional challenges that were inherent with this significant life changing event. We talked about natural human reactions to change, including depression, frustration, anger, etc.

    Our work together showed the employees that the general manager (and implicitly, the company) respected them, even though the company was moving in this new direction. Although top management only comprehended part of what he did to support this merger, it was clear to many that it was his skillful planning that diverted many potential disasters, including potential lawsuits, and internal sabotage.
