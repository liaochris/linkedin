Mark Crowley Ph.D.'s Summary

I am a researching scalable methods for planning in large scale, spatial problems under uncertainty. My research falls under the growing field of Computational Sustainability. In particular I am looking at planning in forest management and invasive species control. I am an expert in methods for stochastic planning using reinforcement learning, especially with the use of policy gradients. I have extensive experience using probabilistic graphical models such as Bayesian networks and Markov random fields as well as solution methods for these such as various exact inference methods as well as Monte-Carlo Markov chain approximation methods.

I have a great deal of experience teaching computer science at UBC especially focussed on general concepts of programming, design and software development as well as artificial intelligence methods.

I also have a great interest in new social media technologies and their implications for communication and interaction in the future. I believe there is a lot of automated reasoning that could be done with social network data using concepts from AI and game theory.

If you have ideas or want to discuss Computational Sustainability, artificial intelligence research or computer science education please feel free to contact me to chat.

Specialties: planning, spatial modelling, computational sustainability, probabilistic modeling, Bayesian network modeling, teaching computer science, web development, social media technoligies, game theory

See my website http://markcrowley.ca for more recent research interests and information.

  • Seeing the forest despite the trees: large scale spatial-temporal decision making

    • In Proceedings of the Twenty-Fifth Conference on Uncertainty in Artificial Intelligence (2009), pp. 126-134. Key: crowley2009
    • 2009
    Authors: Mark Crowley Ph.D., David Poole, John Nelson

    We introduce a challenging real-world planning problem where actions must be taken at each location in a spatial area at each point in time. We use forestry planning as the motivating application. In Large Scale Spatial-Temporal (LSST) planning problems, the state and action spaces are defined as the cross-products of many local state and action spaces spread over a large spatial area such as a city or forest. These problems possess state uncertainty, have complex utility functions involving spatial constraints and we generally must rely on simulations rather than an explicit transition model. We define LSST problems as reinforcement learning problems and present a solution using policy gradients. We compare two different policy formulations: an explicit policy that identifies each location in space and the action to take there; and an abstract policy that defines the proportion of actions to take across all locations in space. We show that the abstract policy is more robust and achieves higher rewards with far fewer parameters than the elementary policy. This abstract policy is also a better fit to the properties that practitioners in LSST problem domains require for such methods to be widely useful.
