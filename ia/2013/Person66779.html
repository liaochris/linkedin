John Nwosu, Esq.'s Summary

I am a general legal practitioner in Dallas. Texas with emphasis in Criminal, Family, Immigration and Personal Injury Law.

I spent my formative years in Nigeria. After arriving in the United States alone at the age of sixteen without a high school diploma, I first acquired a GED, and then enrolled in college. I generally worked a minimum of 50 hours per week while pursuing my degree to help defray educational expenses and support family members in Nigeria. Despite my work schedule, I graduated summa cum laude in three and one half years. After working for two and one half years as an application programmer, I felt quite unfulfilled, and a career in law offered the kind of fulfillment that I sought.

As a person of diverse cultural and work experiences, I have been sensitized to some of the inequities present in our legal system. And my desire to play a role in seeking solutions to some of these problems drew me to the legal profession. I have had four unique legal internships at a state supreme court, a criminal defense and civil rights law firm, a large corporate firm and a foreign government financial crimes prosecution agency.

In addition to my legal internships, while in Law school, I participated in the Criminal Advocacy Clinic where I worked in three-person team representing individuals that have been accused of committing crimes. We represented them from arraignment to the final disposition of the case. I served as the lead student attorney in one of the case. Also, in spring of 2008, I worked at my law school’s Prisoners Rights’ Clinic as a litigation assistant and successfully secured the release of a client who received favorable parole but was still languishing in prison two years later.

Specialties

Criminal Law, Family Law, Immigration Law, Personal Injury, Debt Collection Defense & Traffic Ticket Defense.

Economic and Financial Crimes Commission, Abuja, Nigeria

December 2008 – February 2009 (3 months)

The Commission is charged with investigating and prosecuting corrupt government officials. My work there centered on admissibility of electronic evidence. Because the Nigerian Evidence Act, enacted in late 1950's does not have provision for electronic evidence and one of the major task of the Commission is the prosecution of financial crimes. So, I helped them to create some kind of uniformity in presenting electronic evidence. I did this by looking at United States' case laws, treatises, law review articles and ALRs from the 1960s and 1970s when electronic evidence (especially video recording) was a hot button issue in the United States. I looked at where it worked and how it was presented, where it failed and why, and how the attorneys at that time tried to educate the judges that were unfamiliar with electronic/video recording before presenting such. Using all these materials, I was able to put together some kind of manual they could use as a guide when trying to get electronic evidence into trial.
