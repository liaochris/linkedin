Brandon Mogan, PE, LEED AP's Summary

Brandon Mogan has built his career around providing a unique synthesis of technical and regulatory expertise to utility and industrial clients across the United States, ranging from small industrial facilities to Fortune 500 companies.

Developing unique solutions to complex regulatory issues is Mr. Mogan’s modus operandi. With an in-depth understanding of policy and regulation, and a commitment to understanding key client drivers, he is relied upon by his clients to develop efficient and effective compliance solutions. His career has been distinguished by successful regulatory negotiations that minimize the compliance burden for his clients and often effect change in policy and regulation.

Brandon is committed to excellence in project management, team leadership, and client satisfaction. This commitment manifests in on-time, on-budget project execution, earning him the accolade of “go-to” consultant from his clients.

Mr. Mogan’s primary practice area is air quality. In addition to his federal air regulatory experience, he has experience with state and local air regulations in: Alabama, Arizona, Connecticut, Georgia, Louisiana, Maryland, Missouri, Nevada, North Carolina, Ohio, Pennsylvania, Puerto Rico, South Carolina, Texas, Virginia, Washington, and Wyoming.

Specialties: BACT Analyses; Greenhouse Gas (GHG) Reporting Rule, Subpart W - Natural Gas Systems Compliance Assistance, FERC ER Development, Air Quality Regulatory Permitting and Compliance; Air Dispersion Modeling; Landfill Design and Permitting; Stormwater (NPDES) Permitting; Wastewater Pretreatment System Design and Permitting; Floodplain Development Permitting, Zoning, and Land Use Planning; and, Litigation Support.

Brandon Mogan, PE, LEED AP's Projects

  • Environmental Services in Support of Compressor Station Construction

    • October 2011 to January 2012
    Team Members: Brandon Mogan, PE, LEED AP

    Mr. Mogan was the project manager and air quality technical lead for a natural gas compressor station construction project for a confidential client. Geosyntec's scope of services for this project included the preparation of the abbreviated Environmental Report required for authorization from FERC; preparation of an air quality construction permit application, including air dispersion modeling; and providing an environmental inspector during the construction phase of the project.

  • Regulatory Negotiations: Title V and MACT Applicability

    • October 2011 to March 2012
    Team Members: Brandon Mogan, PE, LEED AP

    An industrial client in Hart County, Georgia was a major source of hazardous air pollutants (HAP) on the first published compliance date in the Remediation MACT (40 CFR Part 63, Subpart GGGGG). Therefore, Georgia EPD determined that the facility would always be subject to Title V permitting requirements due to the once in always in (OIAI) policy despite the subsequent removal of several significant sources of air pollution, which decreased potential to emit to below major source thresholds. When the facility needed to replace two outdated scrubbers with larger units they were faced with an unmanageable SIP Construction Permit Application and Title V Modification processing timeframe. Geosyntec performed an in-depth analysis of the regulatory basis for Georgia EPD’s application of the OIAI policy and submitted the findings of this analysis to Georgia EPD in a letter requesting that they rescind their application of the OIAI policy. Georgia EPD agreed with the request and this unique regulatory solution resulted in: 1) the facility being allowed to operate as a non-Title V source and 2) the permit application for the new scrubbers being processed in a manageable timeframe.

  • BACT Analysis for CO, PM, and NOx from Wood-Fired Boiler - Somers, CT

    • June 2008 to October 2008
    Team Members: Brandon Mogan, PE, LEED AP

    Potential emissions of carbon monoxide (CO), particulate matter (PM), and nitrous oxides (NOx) from the proposed 6-megawatt wood-fired boiler at the facility were estimated to exceed the best available control technology (BACT) requirement threshold levels as defined in RSCA Section 22a-174-3a. The facility had already purchased an electrostatic precipitator (ESP) to control PM emissions. Mr. Mogan conducted a BACT analysis, which demonstrated that the facility’s ESP would be considered the BACT for PM emissions from the proposed boiler. A BACT analysis was also conducted for CO and NOx, which demonstrated that “good combustion practices” would be considered BACT for these pollutants and that add-on controls for CO and NOx would be economically infeasible. Thus, the facility was permitted to install the proposed boiler without having to purchase additional air pollution control equipment, other than the ESP they already owned.

  • Mandatory GHG Reporting Rule, Subpart W Compliance

    • April 2011 to December 2011
    Team Members: Brandon Mogan, PE, LEED AP

    Mr. Mogan served as the project manager and technical lead for a 40 CFR Part 98, Subpart W applicability study for ten (10) natural gas facilities, including natural gas transmission compression, liquefied natural gas (LNG) storage, and natural gas distribution. This project involved the preparation of GHG monitoring plans, conducting required GHG monitoring, and preparing GHG emissions calculations.

  • Air Construction Permitting Support for a Flexographic Printing Operation

    • January 2009 to February 2009
    Team Members: Brandon Mogan, PE, LEED AP

    Mr. Mogan was the technical lead providing air permitting support to an industrial client in Spartanburg, SC for the construction of their new flexographic printing operation. Initial screening showed that post-construction toxic air pollutants (TAP) emissions from the facility would cause offsite impacts exceeding SCDHEC R. 61-62.5, Standard 8 allowable thresholds. Refined air dispersion modeling was performed using AERMOD that incorporated two operating scenarios: the water-based scenario, where pollutants discharge out of multiple stacks; and the solvent-based scenario, where pollutants are all routed through a common control device before being emitted through a common stack. The results of the refined modeling verified that offsite concentrations of the TAP ethanolamine would exceed SCDHEC R. 61-62.5, Standard 8 allowable thresholds. This impact was mitigated by redesigning the facility’s stacks, which allowed the facility to expand without requiring additional air pollution control equipment.

  • Regulatory Negotiations: Vertical Dispersion Requirements

    • June 2009 to August 2009
    Team Members: Brandon Mogan, PE, LEED AP

    An air regulatory audit of an industrial client’s facility in Vancouver, WA indicated that the facility was subject to local air permitting requirements. The Southwest Clean Air Agency (SWCAA), the local regulatory body, required that all exhaust stacks have no impedance to vertical flow (i.e., no rain cap); including those associated with shop comfort heaters. Mr. Mogan conducted research into local building and fire codes, UL standards, and similar regulations in other states. The results of this research indicated that modification of the UL approved shop comfort heater stacks was technically infeasible. Correspondence with the SWCAA on this matter resulted in the facility being exempted from this requirement, and it prompted SWCAA to incorporate a similar exemption into their regulations.

  • Floodplain Permitting and Zoning Ordinance Variance Negotiations

    • August 2010 to September 2010
    Team Members: Brandon Mogan, PE, LEED AP

    A large indoor agricultural client purchased an adjacent parcel of land to accommodate a proposed 30 acre expansion. The subject parcel is situated at the confluence of two waterways which collect water from a basin greater than 700 acres. As such, nearly 75% of the parcel is located within the FEMA floodplain. Additionally, Mecklenburg County has established Surface Water Improvement & Management (SWIM) buffers which extend to the FEMA floodplain in areas draining more than 600 acres. Local ordinances restrict construction within the SWIM buffer. Without a variance to the SWIM buffer ordinance, this property would have been unusable for the purposes of greenhouse construction. Mr. Mogan led the technical effort of obtaining a floodplain development permit and crafting a successful ordinance variance request, which included a zero-discharge stormwater retention basin and a deeded tree-save area.
