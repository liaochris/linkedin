J. Edward Bivens' Projects

  • Still in Play: A Performance of Getting Ready

    • August 2010 to September 2011
    Team Members: J. Edward Bivens

    An examination and homage to sustained attention, Still in Play: A Performance of Getting Ready is the story of a troupe that is really a tribe struggling to be an ensemble. A group of actors come together to prepare for a show, and in their attentions to themselves and each other, they weave the fabric of their determination to make something happen on the stage. Taking place in the 60 minutes before the performance, the actors inhabit the space in ways that become evidently theatrical, moving from individual actions to group actions, and revealing the conflicted eros of collaboration.
    Some actors are born ready; they come in with all the energy and focus they are ever going to have. Others have to slowly put on their decided bodies, a step-by-step transformation from person in the world to person on the stage. In Still in Play: A Performance of Getting Ready, whether they play ductball, mutter into a camera, rehearse a monologue, enact a song, groan, or simply sit and wait, the actors all seek the moments of heightened awareness that emerge when someone participates in their chosen art form.

  • Places Please: The Waiting

    • August 2010 to October 2010
    Team Members: J. Edward Bivens

    Gallery showing at the Museum of Contemporary Art Chicago.

    We had a lot of cameras running during Curious’s residency workshop at the MCA, but one camera in particular captured by far the most interesting material. It was set up toward the back of the stage, a director’s chair placed in front of it, and the actors would occasionally deliver impromptu “testimonials” straight into the lens, or they might do nothing at all, or they might leave the camera running while going off to do something else on another part of the stage. None of this being rehearsed in any way.

    When we decided to create a video installation comprising footage from the workshop, these were the scenes I was immediately drawn to: those long periods of absence, followed by bursts of presence, sometimes intense presence, always with the sound of something else going on beyond the frame. But there was way too much footage to simply string it all together end to end, so to halve the length I began placing long clips together in a 2.35:1 splitscreen format—frames speaking and listening to each other across the tiny divide, different trajectories of time running side by side, colliding with each other, overlapping. Also the fact that having two eyes—the doubleness—is what allows us to perceive depth in the space around us. This has yielded some interesting results, especially about the way attention shifts from one side to the other and the anticipation from one “empty” frame layers an additional meaning on top of the “non-empty” frame beside it. The waiting, the doing, the speaking, the looking. It’s when the most mundane activities take on a special charm and significance that my interest is most piqued, when difference and repetition begin to synthesize into something… New. Unseen. Different.
