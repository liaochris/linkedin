Laurie E. Miller, PhD's Summary

Most of my engineering experience centers around something needing to be done with no one available who had any idea how. Need some computational electromagetics FDTD done, and students keep getting scared off by the math? Get Laurie to do it. Need a library of analysis on commercially available semiconductor lasers and their potential to be used in ways they were never designed for? Get Laurie to do it. Need statistical process control applied to a multi-robot-and-human installation? Get Laurie to do it. Need better, faster, cheaper high performance computing for the emerging modern grid? Drop me a line.

Specialties

R&D of new computational techniques for power systems analysis. Consulting and field engineering, computational science on clusters and supercomputers, smart grid modeling, practical photovoltaics.

Laurie E. Miller, PhD's Experience

Doctoral Researcher

University of Minnesota

Educational Institution; 10,001+ employees; Higher Education industry

June 2005 – June 2013 (8 years 1 month)

Dissertation title: Fast Power Systems Risk Assessment. My focus is on new methods for load flow analysis of very large systems using inexpensive commodity hardware; recent developments in hardware technology are making possible levels of computation -- that would previously required an off-site supercomputer -- on commodity hardware that can be kept on-site to ensure data security. The methods I develop can be expanded well beyond load flow analysis, which I hope to pursue in the future.

Research Assistant, Computational Electromagnetics

University of Minnesota

Educational Institution; 10,001+ employees; Higher Education industry

September 1998 – January 2001 (2 years 5 months)

I wrote my senior honors thesis on implementations of boundary conditions in three dimensions in FDTD (the Finite-Difference, Time-Domain method for computational electromagnetics). I also wrote the first ToyFDTD tutorial codes and built and published the ToyFDTD website, which garnered tens of thousands of hits from 60 countries in the first month and grew from there; the codes are still in use today. I went on to model advanced structures including waveguide mirrors and exit faces for VCSELs (Vertical-Cavity Surface-Emitting Lasers), performing large numerical runs at various supercomputer installations and creating animated graphical renderings of the three-dimensional data sets as they stepped forward in time.

Project Manager, Electrical and Array Engineer

Solar Vehicle Research Project, University of Minnesota

December 1995 – August 1998 (2 years 9 months)

World Solar Rallye University Class Champions Akita, Japan, 1998

• Managed 25 engineers from six departments working in four design teams
• Raised $250,000 in cash and in-kind donations
• Designed electrical layout of solar array and primary power integration
• Constructed solar array unparalleled for reliability and performance in 1996-1998 university class teams
• Redesigned and rebuilt solar array for 1998 Japanese regulations

Halfway through my undergraduate degree, I realized that my greatest strength at the moment was my calculus ability, to the point that that math department asked me to run a special seminar for the first-years. Realizing I could easily become a "textbook engineer" who is brilliant at problems on paper but can't build a thing, I transferred to the University of Minnesota, told all my relatives not to expect to see me for two years, and immersed myself in designing and building a solar-powered race vehicle out of other people's garbage that could win a major race.

While my main focus was on the solar array, battery pack, and associated power electronics, I got my hands dirty in pretty much every capacity I could, including wet layups of sandwich composites and associated mold construction, experiments one really should not do with air compressors and battery cycling, destructive photovoltaic testing, and experiments in ad-hoc repairs to anything and everything that might possible occur in racetime.

Semiconductor Laser Research

TSI

Privately Held; 501-1000 employees; Mechanical or Industrial Engineering industry

December 1997 – March 1998 (4 months)

• Developed detailed laser testing and characterization progress
• Searched global semiconductor laser industry for lasers for commercial product line
• Researched, tested, and documented laser capabilities and weaknesses
• Initial design of control panel and microcontroller code for handheld measurement device

It's hard not to love a job where they pay you to play with lasers for 40 hours a week. My division used semiconductor lasers in measurement devices and required specific and unusual capabilities in the lasers. Since the available laser product lines were continually changing and my division continually invented new products, it was my job to test every commercially-produced laser I could get my hands on that might suit our purposes, and to build a library of results that could be used as our needs changed. For years after I left, I would run into engineers from my division saying they were still using my lab notebooks as a primary reference.
