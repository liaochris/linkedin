Transitioned to general management in healthcare after several years of clinical practice.

Previous clinical experience includes practising as a clinical pharmacist for the Kuala Lumpur General Hospital's Neurology and Neurosurgery departments, working closely with doctors to treat patients with neurological diseases.

Qualifications include an MPharm and an MBA from the University of Strathclyde, Glasgow as well as board-certification as a clinical pharmacotherapy specialist by the Board of Pharmacy Specialties, USA.

Currently pursuing a doctorate in public health at the University of Lancaster.

Research interests include how customer service techniques can improve patient outcomes and how market research can improve implementation of public health campaigns.

  • Effect of pharmacist-managed medication therapy adherence clinic on patients using interferon-beta in multiple sclerosis: a non-experimental, observational study

    • 12th Asian Conference of Clinical Pharmacy
    • July 9, 2012
    Authors: Mark Cheong, Jenny Thong

    Objective: The purpose of this study is to observe the effects of a pharmacist-managed medication therapy adherence clinic (MTAC) on patients using interferon-beta in relapsing-remitting multiple sclerosis

    Subjects and methods: 27 patients starting on interferon-beta were enrolled in the MTAC and monitored the pharmacist where they were educated on MS and interferon therapy, symptom and ADR management and the importance of adherence. On each visit, the patients’ knowledge of multiple sclerosis (MS) and interferon were assessed using a 40-point questionnaire. Symptoms were also identified and the patients were asked to self-rate their health using a 10-point visual analog scale (VAS) based on the EQ-VAS scale.

    Results: At the end of the monitoring period, only 1 out of the 27 patients (3.7%) was found to be non-adherent to the interferon therapy. The patients’ knowledge on MS and interferon therapy improved from a mean score of 20.15 to 31.67 (p < 0.01) while the patients’ self-rated health improved from a mean score of 5.9 to 6.5 on the 10-point VAS scale (p = 0.037). The improvement in patient knowledge was noted to be correlated with the improvement in self-rated health (r = 0.38). The patients also reported a reduction in the number of symptoms suffered from an average of 11.14 to 7.92 symptoms (p = 0.003). Only 12 of the patients suffered from fever due to interferon therapy while only 2 patients suffered from injection site reactions.

    Conclusion: The findings suggest that regular monitoring by a pharmacist helped to maintain patient adherence to interferon therapy while improving patient knowledge may have contributed to the increase in self-rated perception of health. The monitoring and interventions made by the pharmacist may have also contributed to the reduction of symptoms observed in the patients.
