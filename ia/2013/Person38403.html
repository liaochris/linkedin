Trevy McDonald's Publications

  • Time Will Tell

    • Reyomi
    • April 10, 1999
    Authors: Trevy McDonald

    What has time told you about the power of friendship in your life? Embark on a ten-year journey with Thomasine, Rachel, and Hope as they experience life, love, and major life decisions during the years following their high school graduation. Thomasine Mintor, more affectionately known as Sina, is an anthropologist and feminist scholar at the prestigious Steeplechase University in the Research Triangle Park. Rachel Curtis Brown is a single mother of two and a special education teacher in Nashville who loves to country-line dance. Hope Jones-McCoy is an accountant turned healthcare administrator in Kalamazoo who is entirely devoted to her high school sweetheart Jason.

    Sina left her North Carolina home for Chicago and her ten year reunion where she would see close friends Rachel and Hope. She spent the first half of her ten-year journey into everything and everybody but herself. After she reached her threshold of tragic experiences, a spiritual awakening led her to make a change in her life. Rachel, loyal and trusting was the bridge that held them together. Hope Jones-McCoy went through life masquerading self-righteousness for saintliness until the world she spent ten years building came crashing down around her.

    A bridge that spans ten years and counting. Hope, on the surface, like the girder. Sina, after her awakening, like the banister that keeps you from going over the edge. Rachel--the arch who brings the two together. A bridge that's sometimes as shaky as the Lake Pontchartrain Causeway, sometimes as unexpected as the Chesapeake Bay Bridge-Tunnel, and sometimes as strong and beautiful as an old Roman arch bridge. Will their friendship endure? Time Will Tell.

    ...a timeless novel that will entertain readers for years to come...a for sure must have in your library. -- Yolanda Joe, Author of My Fine Lady

  • Nature of a Sistuh

    • Carolina Academic Press
    • November 18, 1998
    Authors: Trevy McDonald, Tee Ford-Ahmed

    The twentieth century has been an era of perpetual growth and development for Black women in American society. Black women today are continuing to move from being predefined objects to becoming self-defining subjects. Books by cultural critics bell hooks, Patricia Hill Collins, and Paula Giddings are the order of discussion in academic settings across the U.S. At the same time, Black women's fiction by authors including Alice Walker, Toni Morrison, and Terry McMillan are the focus of paper presentations, articles, and feature films which explore self-definition.

    Compiling some of the best scholarship and qualitative methodologies since Black Women in America in 1990, Nature of a Sistuh reconsiders some of the constructions made of Black women by "others" in higher education, corporate offices, organized religions, television and other mass media. Written by and about women of color, this book examines and chronicles their struggle to become subjects who chart their own successes and futures. This collection of seventeen essays is edited by McDonald and Ford-Ahmed and is arranged into four parts providing stirring personal testimony, theoretical frameworks, and an array of methods for resisting the oppression of limited and one-dimensional representations of themselves by others.

  • Building Diverse Communities

    • Hampton Press
    • September 2003
    Authors: Trevy McDonald, Tee Ford-Ahmed, Mark Orbe

    A need exists for a book that explores how various methodological approaches from a humanistic perspective can promote a sense of community within and outside the academy. This volume combines, in one collection, a variety of methodologies and applications, as well as a mosaic of diverse communities. The opening chapters focus on successful strategies used in academia to build community through curriculum development and instruction and collegial relationships. The chapters in the second section not only explore the theoretical basis of scholarship, but how researchers can put theory into practice. Part III offers chapters that highlight the intricate ways in which community can be built via a variety of public channels. In the final section authors explore the significance of narrative and its role in empowering communities
