Andy Volk's Summary

Andy Volk is the VP of Apps & Developer Relations for Mxit, the Stellenbosch, South Africa-based mobile social network.

His early startup experience includes inventing and launching Live365.com in 1999, which grew exponentially to become the Internet's second-largest broadcaster with over 2.7 million annual tuning hours. More recently, Andy was the VP of Product at Kongregate (acquired by GameStop, NASDAQ: GME), a social gaming destination and community site for gamers and game developers, powered by a strong advertising network and virtual currency platform.

Andy has led a range of pioneering web products over the last 13 years, including products at Yahoo! (music, video-on-demand, video player platform, and video search), LOYAL3 (direct-to-consumer company stock purchasing via Facebook), and Scour.com (Internet broadcasting and peer-to-peer technology).

Andy is also the founder of Downtempo, an Internet product development & strategy firm located in San Francisco, whose client projects have included work for MTV, NASA, Craigslist Foundation, SpotXchange, LACMA, and GLOBE.gov. In addition to their client work, Downtempo incubated and launched its own in-house projects, including "Is It Safe To Visit?", the world's first travel advisory search engine.

Andy is a U.S. Citizen and has Permanent Residency in South Africa.

Andy Volk's Projects

  • Grrwl

    • May 2013 to Present
    Team Members: Andy Volk, Marshall Yount

    Grrwl: On-demand information to *any* mobile phone.

    Grrwl allows anyone in South Africa to retrieve information for free by giving a "missed call" to a specific phone number, and receiving an SMS with the requested information.

    By provisioning thousands of phone numbers in a country, Grrwl can provide information services such as breaking news, transport schedules, sports scores, etc. Sending requested information over SMS means that we can reach 100% of the mobile phones in South Africa, as opposed to requiring a data-enabled phone.

    In future, we can provision "Grrwl lines" for brands and agencies, and create platforms for surveys and quizzes. By using the phone number as a user ID, we can build up user profiles from usage, and target advertising accordingly, and by avoiding spam / unsolicited messages, we can build user trust in a low-trust environment.

    A proof-of-concept has been built using Twilio and Heroku, and we're currently validating the business model.

  • Phone Genome

    • November 2010 to Present
    Team Members: Andy Volk

    Phone Genome is a platform with three goals: to help people stay in better touch with their friends through calls and text messaging, to provide social sharing features around phone calls and text messages, and to help people understand their phone history and costs. Think "Foursquare for your phone calls and texts".

    The project began as a web-side experience, where users would link their phone company account to Phone Genome, and we would proceed to download their phone call/text history and analyze it for them. We would then show the user an activity feed of all their call/text history, along with a more detailed analysis of their calling patterns, and provide suggestions on ways to save money.

    We also designed an accompanying iOS app, Friend Shaker, which provided a new form of social phone dialer. Instead of users being asked to choose who they want to call, they would choose why they wanted to call someone, and we would suggest appropriate contacts from their phone book based on how contacts were tagged. (For example, you could choose "get lunch near work", and it would suggest a friend nearby work that you hadn't caught up with in a while.)

    The initial Phone Genome website was created, along with the first scrapers to load a user's phone history data, but was never launched to the public.

  • NiceTips

    • August 2009 to Present
    Team Members: Andy Volk

    Built during an exceptionally sunny week in Budapest, NiceTips was a product designed to help people discover and share tiny (SMS-sized) pieces of useful travel advice ("tips").

    Tips were associated with cities, and then cross-referenced by categories, with a simple vote/up down model, commenting, and the ability to send 'fanmail' to the tip's author. Future features were slated to allow tips to be targeted to a given neighborhood with in a city, for users to retrieve tip suggestions based on location, to provide an SMS query interface, and for users to assemble and print or download a guidebook of tips in advance of a visit.

    By keeping tips short, we were focused on enabling travelers to arrive in new cities without a guidebook, and to pull out their cellphone and send a single SMS to receive immediately useful travel advice (ie. "taxi cost from Mumbai Airport to Colaba?").

  • Is It Safe To Visit?

    • November 2008 to Present
    Team Members: Andy Volk, Brent Fitzgerald

    The world's first (as far as we know!) travel advisory search engine.

    Is It Safe to Visit? (IISTV) was built to answer a simple (and often asked) question: "Is it 'safe' to visit a specific country?"

    IISTV aggregates daily safety ratings per country from multiple sources: the U.S. Department of State, Canadian Foreign Affairs Department, Australia Smart Traveler, and New Zealand Safe Travel, and averages these into the IISTV Advisory Level that appears over each country's page on the site.

    The product was designed with an elegantly minimal interface and RESTful URLs, along with RSS rating updates and embedded microdata, and a popular "Just show me wherever!" feature.

    IISTV is not being actively maintained, so not all rating sources are currently displayed.

  • Showscout

    • February 2006 to Present
    Team Members: Andy Volk, Michael Grafton, Noah Lang

    Showscout was designed to allow people to subscribe to each other's "taste" in video, primarily movies and TV shows. We enabled direct delivery of content to TiVo subscribers by pre-programming their TiVo via our ability to programmatically access TiVo's web interface (pre-TiVo API) and map content choices against local programming grid information for each subscriber.

    The net effect was that a tastemaker could create a "channel" of movies, TV shows (either specific episodes or all of them), and other television content. When a user subscribed to that channel, Showscout would map all the channel items against what was available on their TiVo's program grid (either TV or Cable), and then schedule recordings, and mark them as 'watched' after they were recorded on a user's TiVo.

    The roots of the product are described in the WIRED article "The Super Network" from September 2005 (http://www.wired.com/wired/archive/13.09/yahoo.html). After they realized the power of having people with excellent taste pre-programming their TiVos for them, he and Noah Lang (head of community) brought in Mike Grafton as the lead developer to build Showscout.

    Showscout was kept in private beta and never released to the general public.
