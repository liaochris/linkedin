Chris Bingham's Summary

I’m a systems engineer and solution architect with broad experience across both Windows and Linux, having worked with customers in both Europe and North America in industries as diverse as defence, automotive, and pharmaceuticals.

I have both certifications and experience with Windows Server and Red Hat Enterprise Linux, having undertaken projects such as architecting HPC clusters, Active Directory, and group policies for multi-national customers. I’m also practiced in meeting regulatory compliance requirements, in particular GxP (21CFR Part 11) and military/governmental security.

In many of my projects I make heavy use of scripting to automate processes and reduce on-going maintenance costs for my customers.

I believe my biggest strength is my ability to “hit the ground running” and pick up new technologies very quickly.
I also invest heavily, both in time and money, in training and am currently studying for a degree with the Open University. As part of this degree, I intend to study web services and software development using Java over the next couple of years.

Specialties: Amazon Web Services, OpenStack, High Performance Computing (HPC), Linux, Red Hat Enterprise Linux (RHEL), Windows Server, Active Directory, Scripting, Automation, Group Policy, GxP, Perl, Solution Architecture, Systems Engineering, Process Engineering, Vertec, Exchange Server

  • BizCloud AWS Deployment

    • November 2012 to Present

    As part of a partnership between Datalynx and BizCloud, my colleague Cristian and I were asked to arrange for the BizCloud application to be deployed on top of Amazon Web Services.

    For this, I designed an infrastructure making use of EC2 instances to run the application, with both auto-scaling with CloudWatch alarms and an Elastic Load Balancer with SSL, S3 buckets for storage, ElastiCache for memcached, and Route 53 for DNS. I selected the HerokuPostgres service to host the application’s database.

    Once the design was agreed I implemented it as a CloudFormation template. By basing the EC2 instances on Amazon Linux and making extensive use of metadata and cfn-init, I was able to fully automate the setup of the instances. This both removed the need to maintain custom AMIs and allowed us to make use of cfn-hup to initiate on-the-fly reconfiguration of live EC2 instances simply by updating the original CloudFormation template.

    All elements of this infrastructure were also replication across at least two AWS availability zones for fault tolerance and high availability. I also made use of the “Mappings” feature to make the CloudFormation template highly portable between AWS regions.
