Phil Burgess' Summary

Phil Burgess is an educator, writer, businessman, advisor to leaders of business and government, media commentator, social entrepreneur, adventurer and story-teller who has served in senior positions in the public service and the private sector.

Faculty appointments include The Ohio State University (where he was also director of the university-wide Behavioral Sciences Laboratory), University of Colorado and Colorado School of Mines, where he taught public policy, management, and resource economics. He also served as visiting professor at UCLA’s School for Public Policy and Social Research, teaching in the graduate program on communications and culture; and visiting professor at the Business School of the Royal Melbourne Institute of Technology (RMIT) in Australia.

Public service posts include president & chief executive of the National Academy of Public Administration (NAPA), chartered by the US Congress to provide trusted advice to Federal institutions, including the White House and Congress; chief executive of two think tanks (The Annapolis Institute and the Center for the New West), and two economic development organizations (the Federation of Rocky Mountain States and the Western Coal Export Council); president of the Western Governors' Policy Office (WESTPO), where he served 43 governors during West’s energy boom years (1975-82), and member of America’s first privacy commission.

International service includes appointments to the U.S. Department of State’s European Advisory Council; the US committee of the Pacific Economic Cooperation Council (PECC) on the committee for transportation, trade and tourism; and the Victoria (Australia) Small Business Advisory Group on Business Ethics.

From 1990-2000, Phil wrote a weekly column on political, business and cultural issues for the Rocky Mountain News/Scripps Howard News Service. He was named "Best Corporate Communications Officer" at the 2008 Australia Telecoms Awards ceremony.

Specialties

Broadcast and print media commentator; technology & society (Internet, social media; telecommunications policy and technology); entrepreneurship; jobs, growth & economic development; non-profit management; tourism, natural resources development; higher education; health care policy and administration; retirement, successful aging; international relations/national security policy; Amercian government, Constitutional law, political theory,federalism; quantitative appoaches to policy analysis.

The Annapolis Institute (TAI) is a private, nonpartisan think tank, established in 1993. TAI has two purposes: To help leadership at all levels to anticipate the forces shaping the lives of people, communities and institutions in the 21st century -- including technology, demography, politics, the economy and culture; and to advance principled leadership in the management of business enterprises, government agencies and non-profit organizations.

Mission
To help leaders anticipate change and set directions in the context of principled decision-making.

Approach
To connect leaders with evidence and insights that are timely, actionable and thematic; and to help leaders set directions and make things work by establishing a culture of principled leadership.

As Senior Vice President for Corporate Affairs and Public Relations it was my job to develop and execute the company’s strategic communications plan and to provide leadership and oversight to the management of media relations, product and brand publicity, employee communications, executive communications and speechwriting, financial communications for investor relations, creative services (for advertising, web hosting, event management, etc.), Olympic programs and the U S West Foundation, a corporate philanthropic organization that gives more than $20 million annually to worthy causes in the 14-state region and around the nation. In addition, I served as a member of the senior management team, which developed and reviewed the company’s strategy and policy. I also served as the company’s spokesman on matters of strategic importance (such as the merger with Qwest) or in crisis situations.

As founding chief executive officer of the Center, a $3m-$4m per year non-profit think tank, I provided leadership and oversight of all policy, program and resource management functions. The Center was established to assemble civic leaders along with leaders of business, government, the research community and the media to address and clarify Western issues — e.g., public land reform, gentrification, water policy, arid lands, mined land reclamation, nuclear waste disposal; national and global issues that have a particular impact on the West, such as tax reform, technology policy (including telecommunications, transportation, energy) and development, recreation and tourism policy, environmental policy (air quality, reclamation, wilderness, global warming, sprawl, etc.), small business job creation, trade policy, growth management, health care reform and the like; and public management or decision process issues such as federalism, states’ rights, capacity building, the role of the media, electronic lobbying, inter-regional equity, and political reform – including sunshine and sunset laws, term limits, and primary elections.
