Jason Forcier's Publications

  • Has Skinner Killed the Katz? Are Society's Expectations of Privacy Reasonable in Today's Techological World?

    • Unpublished
    • May 2, 2013
    Authors: Jason Forcier

    Is the right to privacy unreasonable when we carry our cell phones as we move about? Should cell phone tracking require a probable cause and a search warrant, or is the lower standard of reasonable suspicion and no search warrant good enough? Should police be able to trick your cell phone into disclosing its location through the use of "Stingray" devices?

    This article analyzes recent 2012 court decisions from United States v. Jones (GPS tracking) and United States v. Skinner (cell phone tracking). In particular, this paper delves into why the Supreme Court is extremely receptive to the idea of extending the right to privacy to one's geolocation information given off by one's cell phone. More specifically, the opinions from Justices Alito and Sotomayor, in U.S. v. Jones, provide strong support that the Supreme Court will likely hear U.S. v. Skinner in 2013 and could realistically change how we look at privacy laws in America.

  • Taking the bar early: Making law students ‘practice ready’

    • Maricopa Lawyer
    • March 2013
    Authors: Jason Forcier

    The Arizona Supreme Court amended its Rule 34, Admission to the Bar, to allow law students in their final semester to sit for the bar exam, within 120 days prior to graduation. This short opinion shows how students can make the streamline the transition from student to lawyer in a timely fashion.

  • Making “Practice Ready” Practice Ready: Arizona’s Attempt to Streamline the Final Process for Admission to the Bar

    • Accord, the electronic journal at Phoenix Law Review
    • February 6, 2013
    Authors: Jason Forcier

    The Arizona Supreme Court amended its Rule 34, Admission to the Bar, to allow law students in their final semester to sit for the bar exam, within 120 days prior to graduation. This comment looks at the reasons why this pilot program will help law students make the transition from student, to lawyer, and streamline the lengthy process to sit the bar in Arizona.
