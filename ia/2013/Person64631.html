  • HOW SMART LEADERS BRING OUT THE BEST

    • Blog @ JMCohenAssoc.com
    • February 25, 2013
    Authors: Jeffrey Cohen

    THE TRANSITION FROM GENIUS TO GENIUS MAKER: HOW SMART LEADERS BRING OUT THE BEST IN THEIR STAFF

    When clients describe the leader they want me to coach, they invariable use the word “smart” or “genius.” Do your leaders also bring out the best in others? If your employees were intellectually gifted and effective managers, your employer should be outmaneuvering competitors. Is that happening? Covey succinctly put his finger on the conundrum:

    Some corporations have made hiring the most intelligent individuals a core strategy on the basis that smarter people can solve problems more quickly than the competition. But that only works if the organizations can access that intelligence.

    According to surveys on engagement, most workers have greater capabilities, creativity, talent, initiative and resourcefulness than their jobs allow—or even require. Other surveys reveal that many workers feel stressed to produce more with less.

    These results are paradoxical: People are underutilized and overworked at the same time.

    Some leaders understand how to create genius: They’re “genius-makers.” Many bosses, however, seem to excel at draining people of their intelligence and abilities.

    For the most part, leaders are highly intelligent and capable professionals—traits that facilitate their promotion. How does one successfully make the shift from genius to genius-maker? It’s during this transition that I’m often called in. Among my many resources is Wiseman and McKeown's book, Multipliers: How the Best Leaders Make Everyone Smarter. They conclude that some bosses make us better and smarter by eliciting and revitalizing our intelligence. Others seem to stifle capability, wanting to be seen as the smartest person (reminds me of Enron). They suck the energy out of the team, whose members end up looking or feeling dumb. IQs seem to drop, creativity is squelched and meeting times double.

    I’d love to hear your experiences!
