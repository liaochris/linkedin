Mike Adams' Summary

For parts of five decades now I've been in the business of putting ink on media, often in the most demanding of environments. And what's interesting to me is that even though the processes used to get the image to the media have changed, once the ink hits the media, the result and how to control it--even in this digital age--are still much more the same than they're different.

These days, when it comes to reproducing color, we live in a world of fractured knowledge. There are the old-school guys who began in a before-digital world, who think of color only as color, and then there are the digital guys, who really prefer to think of color only as numbers.

However, what the old-school guys overlook is that whether they like it or not, the numbers exist, they're not going away, and they have to be accounted for. I can't imagine an instance of a color image making it into printed form today without being digitized somewhere in the process. And once digitized it is, by definition, numbers.

But what the digital whiz-kids haven't caught onto is that the numbers only get you to the point at which you're finally going to have to use a mechanical process to print. Controlling that mechanical process is and always will be more of an art than a science, and in the end, what matters is what you see, not the delta E.

What I bring to the table, and what I think makes Correct Color unique, is an intimate understanding of the world of color as numbers, and of mechanical color reproduction.

Specialties

Device calibration and ICC profiling, color management consultation, color management training, color management seminars, color workflow implementation and optimization, digital imaging consulting, RIP setup, printer optimization.

Mike Adams' Experience

Owner

Correct Color

Privately Held; Myself Only; Information Technology and Services industry

2006 – Present (7 years)

Correct Color is a complete, comprehensive, color management solutions provider.

If you're in the business of digitally reproducing color images, you know how frustrating color can be. You may love the idea of color management or you may hate the idea of color management; but if you want to succeed at whatever your color craft may be, you can't ignore color management and expect to get consistent results or spectacular results.

However, if you implement complete, comprehensive, inception-to-completion color management, not only will you get consistent results, you'll get consistently spectacular results.

At Correct Color, we've implemented:

• Color management for photographers

• Color management for fine art inkjet printers

• Color management for grand format printers

• Color management for sign shops

• Color management for traditional printers

• Color management for graphic designers

• Color management for ad agencies, and even...

• Color management for architects

Disparate fields, no doubt, but all of them work with digital color images, and need their finished color to be correct, consistent, and if spectacular is in the bargain, it sure doesn't hurt.

Large-format media sales

CAD Supplies Specialty

Privately Held; 11-50 employees; Wholesale industry

2005 – 2006 (1 year)

At this company I attempted to sell large format media and supplies to the graphics market in Austin.

They had no real presence in that market and no real knowledge of it, and as a result, their position in it was extremely weak.

I conceived the idea of offering color management services for free as a way of getting clients to try their products, and the idea was so successful that I discovered there was a market for color management services for a fee.

CAD Supplies was disinterested in selling color management stand alone, however, so I left and formed Correct Color.

Closed Circuit Television Sales

Security and Surveillance

2001 – 2005 (4 years) Jacksonville, Florida Area

After the dotcom crash and the demise of Memories On Demand, I got out of graphics and imaging for awhile. Mainly because this job came along, and it paid quite a bit of money.

Not that I didn't have to work for it. The term "coffee is for closers" just about sums this place up. Except the actual slogan was "two a day and you can stay." Meaning they set you three appointments a day, and you'd better close two of them if you wanted to keep your job.

The sales force was scattered around the country, and it was some group. I look back on it now as playing for the sales Yankees for awhile. It was the best group of pure sales guys I have ever seen.

Founder

Memories On Demand

1999 – 2001 (2 years)

Memories On Demand was an idea that came to me standing in line at the Margaritaville store in Key West, Florida. The idea was to sell posters and other large-format artwork printed on demand from "image galleries" we'd set up at heavily-trafficked entertainment industry websites.

Customizable too, no less. (Good idea, wrong venue, as it turned out. Just check out Buildasign and eSigns.)

We had three clients at MOD: The Tragically Hip; Megadeth; and Diamond Rio. But in the end MOD wound up as just another casuality of the great dotcom debacle of '00.

What MOD was, however, was one of the first fully ICC-profiled large-format digital printers in the world.

Owner

Adams Trade Associates

1990 – 1999 (9 years)

In this venture I bought and sold printing equipment of any type and size and style, just about anywhere in the world. It wasn't unknown for me to sell table-top AB Dicks, and one time I sold a Planeta that took seven 18-wheel big rigs to get from LA to Maine.

I got to see a lot during those years. I got to see printing as it was done when I was in the trenches at Harp Press get shipped to Mexico a press and a platemaker and a camera at a time, and I had a front row seat as the digital era took its place.

Minister of Propaganda

Harp Press

1979 – 1989 (10 years)

I built the foundation of my color knowledge at Harp Press, learning from some of the best craftsmen who ever worked at the art of printing.

There was money in Dallas in those days. There were a lot of young graphic designers eager to make their mark, and there were a lot of printers eager to push the edges of the envelope to help them do it.

I've said it all my life and I'll say it here because you might roll your eyes but you can't argue, but for maybe a year or so around 1983, Harp Press just might have been the best printing company in the world--at least in terms of quality of jobs produced.

At Harp, I developed color-separation and -printing procedures that were the standard for quality printing in a time when color separations were first moving from the process camera to the laser scanner. I also developed an ink formula and technique for printing double-pass solids that became the absolute standard for quality and is in use to this day around the world.
