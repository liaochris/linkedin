Ioana Urma's Summary

I am very passionate about using my artistic skills to provide beautiful and meaningful things to as many people as possible. To make art that is accessible to all.

I became an architect exactly for that reason, for both the artistic and the social aspect. On larger commercial projects and in urban design projects I've had more opportunity to achieve this, because they are spaces for the larger public to use and enjoy.

On a smaller scale, I realized that this can also be achieved through designing things than can be reproduced. So everyone can, for a relatively low price, own something beautiful, either as a print, a booklet or a t-shirt.

As often as possible, I try to throw on top of that an educational or informative aspect, to give greater depth to the work.

Why? For one, I really like explaining things I know to others, to share knowledge, and, I am a very curious person, so I like to keep on learning new things... and as I learn more and more, I need to share more and more! And if it is possible to add a meaningful layer to something, why not... it can make an artistic piece more "useful."

I love what I do and I spend a long time on it, on all the details, from the thickness of a line in a drawing, to the amount of space a person needs to sit comfortably and talk to another, to how it feels to walk barefoot on a warm floor, to coordinating a deep psychological experience of colors, to ensuring that everything works together as a whole and is clear and simple, enjoyable as an experience and fun!

Specialties

Interior Designer of Cornell University HE Admissions Office

Ioana Urma

Sole Proprietorship; Myself Only; Design industry

May 1993 – August 1993 (4 months) Ithaca, NY

This is a fun fact for Cornell alumni, mostly! As a junior studying interior design, I redesigned the admissions office of the College of Human Ecology at Cornell University.

Initially, I was hired to order new furnishings, but after I saw the space I provided them with an analysis that demonstrated that furnishings would not solve their spatial requirements nor improve the image of the college admissions office.

So I convinced them to move the admissions office to the front of the school, designed the entry/waiting area to feel open and have a direct view onto the college courtyard, chose green carpet to make it look like the courtyard was extending into the space, and reorganized the personal offices to be of more flexible use/size and with better overall circulation and facilities.

Years later, everyone is still very happy... If you don't believe me, talk to the director of admissions there!

    • July 2012 to Present
    Team Members: Ioana Urma

    Located within Boston's historic (brick) core, this monument to the city's founding makes tangible and spatial the experience of unfiltered, intangible information found on the internet.
