  • Why Were Biological Analogies in Economics “a Bad Thing”? Edith Penrose’s battles against social Darwinism and McCarthyism

    • Science in Context
    • 2011
    Authors: Clement Levallois

    The heuristic value of evolutionary biology for economics is still much under debate. We suggest that in addition to analytical considerations, socio-cultural values can well be at stake in this issue. To demonstrate it, we use a historical case and focus on the criticism of biological analogies in the theory of the firm formulated by economist Edith Penrose in postwar United States. We find that in addition to the analytical arguments developed in her paper, she perceived that biological analogies were suspect of a conservative bias – as in social Darwinism. Using original FBI archives, we explain this perception by documenting the broader context of Edith Penrose’s personal and professional evolution, from her student days at Berkeley to her defense of Owen Lattimore against McCarthyism. We conclude that in the case under study at least, science and values were certainly intertwined in accounting for her skepticism towards biological analogies – insight we develop in the conclusion about today’s relationships between biology and economics.

  • Translating upwards: linking the neural and social sciences via neuroeconomics

    • Nature Reviews Neuroscience (forthcoming)
    • October 2012
    Authors: Clement Levallois, John A. Clithero, Paul Wouters, Ale Smidts, Scott Huettel

    A look at neuroeconomics through social network analysis and bibliometrics: do neuroscientists, psychologists and economists really work together in this emerging field? Is new integrated knowledge produced? While we provide positive answers to these questions, we suggest steps for continued and improved translation of neuroscientific knowledge in the social sciences.

  • Virtual Knowledge - Sloppy Data Floods or Precise Social Science Methodologies

    • MIT Press
    • 2012
    Authors: Clement Levallois, Stephanie Steinmetz, Paul Wouters

    Looking at the disciplines of sociology and economics in a historical and contemporary perspective, we critically examine the claim (of Chris Anderson' fame) that data intensive research is challenging theory-based research in these 2 disciplines.
