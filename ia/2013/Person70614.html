Julian Randall's Publications

  • 'I Don't Know What I'm Doing, How About You?': Discourse and Identity in Practitioners Dealing with the Survivors of Childhood Sexual Abuse.

    • Organization
    • November 2007
    Authors: Julian Randall, Iain Munro

    This research is based on interviews conducted with a voluntary
    group of health practitioners who care for the adult survivors of childhood
    sexual abuse in one area of Scotland. This project takes a broadly
    interpretive approach to the interviews, and examines the processes of
    sense-making apparent in the scripts of the doctors, community nurse
    and counsellors who comprise this voluntary Forum. Those interviewed
    were highly sceptical of traditional medical approaches to dealing with
    survivors of such abuse, and they all questioned the effectiveness of
    expert professional knowledge. The research highlights the role of patient
    disclosure as a key mechanism in the process of their treatment, which is
    akin to the confessional technology discussed in detail in the work of Michel
    Foucault. Combined with other medical technologies patient disclosure
    is revealed as a technique of normalization. In this particular case the
    experts themselves were engaged in unravelling this process in search of
    alternative approaches to caring for their patients, which were based on
    a relationship of equal partnership rather than of expert authority. This
    research thus begins to illustrate the processes of sense-making and identity
    formation which exist between professional health care workers and the
    victims of abuse for whom they care.

  • Ambiguity and Ambivalence: Senior Managers' Accounts of Organizational Change in a Restructured Government Department.

    • Journal of Organizational Change Management
    • November 2008
    Authors: Julian Randall, Stephen Procter

    The way in which workers and managers interpret change at work has been an important focus of interest for researchers. These interpretations may indicate that managers are assimilating change as they listen to accounts of other workers with previous experience in the unfolding of such events. On the other hand, there may be a divergence among workers concerning the value and meaning to be ascribed to the change events. If this is the case, a culture of ambiguity may be said to exist, where the nature, degree and value of the cultural change are highly contested and remarkably unclear (McLoughlin et al; 2005). Following Piderit (2000), this paper suggests such perceived ambiguity may explain the disparity between an individual’s expectancy of change and their response to it, and also that, individuals’ ambivalence may influence whether they accept change, adapt to it, or reject it out-of-hand, . Using the findings of 20 senior managers in a Civil Service Department we show how different dimensions of ambivalence in different individuals can lead not only to different responses to imposed change at work, but can also illustrate how individuals come to terms with the demands of change. We also show how different groups express both etic and emic accounts in their narratives and antenarratives to make sense of their unfolding experience.

  • Institutional Logics and Contradictions: Competing and Collaborating Logics in a Forum of Medical and Voluntary Practitioners

    • Journal of Change Management
    • April 2010
    Authors: Julian Randall, Iain Munro

    Early contributions to neo-institutional theory emphasise how patterns of action and meaning are framed through professional norms and the techniques and operations that construct and sustain them. The empirical evidence is less clear about how such institutional logics come to be changed. This paper explores the translation of meaning and practice in members of a forum comprising medical practitioners and voluntary counsellors whose patients are the adult survivors of childhood sexual abuse. The case study illustrates the translation of meaning and practice which can takes place when practitioners share a social space providing an opportunity for exchange of experience based on shared proximal knowledge derived from the care of their patients

  • Foucault’s Care of the Self: a Case from Mental health Work

    • Organization Studies
    • November 2010
    Authors: Julian Randall, Iain Munro

    This research concerns mental health workers who treat the victims of sexual abuse. The health workers were all part of a forum composed of doctors, counsellors and a community
    psychiatric nurse, who met to discuss issues of best practice in their everyday work. The research examined how these workers make sense of their work, particularly in terms of how they understand the concept of care and the kinds of knowledge that they use in their work, professional and otherwise.
