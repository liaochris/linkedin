Ian Douglas' Summary

I have taught politics at universities in the UK, the US, Egypt and Palestine. My research concerns centre on the State, population and the history of systems of power. My main influences include Nietzsche, Heidegger and Michel Foucault, among others. From 2005-09 I devoted myself almost entirely to political activism focused on Iraq and the geopolitics of the Arab world. Increasingly I am fascinated by astrophysics, quantum mechanics and particle physics, on the one hand, and primordial animal life on the other.

Specialties: My doctorate was entitled, "On the genealogy of globalism," aiming to trace — via practical logistics and as a conceptual horizon — the reduction of the world as field, and the emergence of systems of political conditioning (governmentalities) based on exposure (panopticism) and acceleration (telepresence). Other research concerns include: madness, discipline, prisons, the institutional matrix of the modern State, ecological wars, animality, ancient life, and oblivion. As an activist, I have been and remain concerned with the politics of the Arab world, and the international politics of Western imperialism.
