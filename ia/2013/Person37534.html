Andrew Ray's Publications

  • Disruption of the neuroanatomy of the anterior cingulate cortex in the rat maternal infection model of autism.

    • Submitted; Nature Neuroscience
    Authors: Andrew Ray

    Data collected showed that serotonergic fibers from the raphe which normally project to the anterior cingulate cortex were lost by as much as 90%. The data also demostrated that the numbers of specific subsets of GABAergic interneurons which contained parvalbumin or calretinin were significantly higher or lower (respectively) than normal in the anterior cingulate. The changes were limited to the anterior cingulate, suggesting that the timing of the maternal infection will affect different regions of cortical development, ultimately causing different disorders (schizophrenia, autism, ADHD, etc.) depending on the timing (and probably duration) of the event.

Andrew Ray's Projects

  • Development of a semi-high-throughput autism model

    • July 2010 to 2012
    Team Members: Andrew Ray

    Using the maternal infection protocol first developed by Dr. Paul Patterson at Cal Tech, I am currently looking for anatomical markers consistent with changes in the human brain related to autism spectrum disorders. One promising marker is the loss of serotonergic fibers in the anterior cingulate cortex. If this alteration is consistent, it could be used as a semi-high throughput assay to screen for compounds which could be given to the mother for the prevention of autism. I also intend to test several other autism-related models in the hope that this anatomical marker would also have better penetrance than the genetic markers currently available (which typically only relate to 1-2% of any given cases).

    ASD is too complex for narrow or single-minded perspectives. The notion that there is “1 right answer” or a “single best approach” is limiting and potentially harmful when trying to address it. None-the-less, there is tremendous fragmentation in the field. This is a reality that exacerbates and compounds the many challenges people in the Autism Community already face. The WesternU FACE Group (Faculty for Autism Collaboration and Education) promotes an integrated perspective in which diverse viewpoints are not only valuable, but critical to being able to understand the bigger picture and any given individual’s constellation of symptoms. The more broad-based the overall perspective, the greater the opportunity to identify potential solutions for patients. The broader the framework, the more likely better research questions can be asked. This program brings together various, and sometimes seemingly incompatible, viewpoints from our diverse Faculty so that knowledge and understanding of Autism can be enriched, and thus so can the ability to help. It’s imperative that we stop taking an “Autistic” approach to Autism. Furthermore, a positive frame must accompany perspective on any given patient, one that identifies what a patient CAN do, not just what they can’t. Treatment approaches should be based on building from current levels of functionality. ALL progress, no matter how small, is a success and should always be celebrated. For more information about this upcoming program, please visit The Autism Channel at http://theautismchannel.tv/.
