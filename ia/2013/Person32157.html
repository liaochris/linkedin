David Deutsch • 杜大卫's Summary

Connection. Communication. Curiosity.

These core values drive my business and life. I would call them my three C's but that would be corny. These values have driven my career and life with great overall success.

Connection means leveraging my networks for the benefit of others and not just myself. I freely make connections for others with no expectation of reciprocity (including you, dear reader.) Call it whatever you want, but I call it enlightened self-interest.

At least 50% of business challenges could probably be solved with better communications. Beyond learning to speak and write well, excellent communications starts with listening: to customers, employees, partners and the public with a spirit of openness and a desire to improve.

Finally, curiosity requires almost a childlike desire to experiment and grow without fear of failure. Indeed I believe failure is your friend and should be embraced rather than shunned. Acknowledging mistakes and learning from them can take a struggling business to the next level.

These are the values that I live every day, whether using Facebook, Twitter, LinkedIn or attending a networking event with colleagues. Billion dollar deals will always be done on golf courses over drinks; and closing those deals requires that you listen well, incorporate what others say and offering to help. These core values and represent the value I bring to my clients every day.

David Deutsch • 杜大卫's Education

University of Southern California

Masters, Communication Management

2006 – 2007

Masters Thesis: Managing change in a multicultural organization. Change management is one of the most stressful, and therefore least productive, times in an organization. Clear and consistent communication is key during corporate transitions. But employees do not process information in the same way, especially those from different countries. Thus my Masters focus was on discovering the best practices for how to manage change in such an intercultural organization.

I discovered that the best way to manage change, especially among people from high-context cultures such as India and China, requires listening closely to their concerns by first building personal relationships with them. It is particularly critical that direct supervisors create this relationship to ensure that messages are delivered clearly, since such employees will probably not respond well to people they do not have relationships with.
