  • Next Generation Wireless Network Deployment Architecture/Process:

    • Joseph (David) Patt
    • May 29, 2007
    Authors: Joseph (David) Pratt

    The next generation or packet network approach, which is generating the most interest, within the wireless corridor, is technology based on soft-switching. In this approach, functionalities formerly executed via a circuit switch are divided between a soft-switch, or media gateway controller, and a media gateway. All routing, service selection, call control, and signaling functions are performed per the soft-switch. The Media Gateway switches voice processing, echo canceling, coding, etc. The soft-switch architecture utilizes a standard gateway control protocol, such as MGCP, or Megaco/H.248, to manage one or more media gateways.

    • Consideration needs to exercised, towards the migration of a soft-switch platform, from the conventional circuit switched application, due to new competitive pressures, making it ever more critical for mobile-network service providers to manage their respective assets more stringently, and next generation systems can promote significant cost-savings, and economies-of-scale.

    • Flexibility is one of the paramount benefits of the next generation of soft-switch technologies. This evolution is accelerating at an extremely fast pace, so carriers must adapt quickly, to address competitive pressures, and gain an immediate advantage. All networking confines of circuit switch architecture are bundled under a proprietary domain, thus, all hardware, and software must be obtained from the original switch vendor. This type of scenario minimizes a carriers ability to control their destiny, whereas, a vendor exhibits total control of the respective network service provider, wherein, costs are controlled via the equipment manufacturer. Hence, the carrier cannot replace a part of the existing network solution, without the induction of a new and innovative costly approach.

    • A next-generation soft-switch application is based on the premise of industry standards, and is implemented as multiple/modular components to update or replace a network.

  • LTE Next Generation Test Challenges

    • Joseph (David) Pratt
    • October 1, 2009
    Authors: Joseph (David) Pratt

    To match such demanding requirements, it’s necessary to break design down into sub-systems and build a test plan that allows each part of the design to be characterized. Thorough testing at each integration stage is essential before testing the complete device. Without it, the diagnosis of problems can occur so late in the program that it becomes difficult to manage the final release stages, including field trials and compliance testing.

    How the tasks are broken down will depend to some extent on how the device has been developed. Device manufacturers currently buy in at least some of the hardware or software. Thus, the RF section, baseband processor, system software, or application software may have been procured externally, so it can be expected to have been tested to some extent. Alternatively, an existing platform may be extended to add support for LTE, so the embedded operating system and legacy radio access technologies will be reused. Finally, some designs will be completely new, leaving behind all the associated compromises, though all of aspects of the design will be unproven.

    Measurement Needs:

    Regardless of how the design has come about, several key performance measurements need to be made. Some of these are familiar from previous technologies, like maximum output power, power control, and receiver sensitivity. But due to the transmission schemes used (OFDMA in the downlink, SC-FDMA in the uplink), new measurement equipment will be needed to support these tests.

    Other measurements are specific to LTE. With its OFDMA transmission scheme, for example, error vector magnitude (EVM) per sub-carrier becomes an essential test of modulator performance. As the modulation bandwidth becomes a higher percentage of the center frequency, this can pose a challenge with some modulator architectures.

    As a result, the EVM can be seen to rise at the band edges, so it needs special attention at the design stage.

  • Wireless Telecom Emergency Restoration Plan

    • Joseph (David) Pratt
    • June 24, 2010
    Authors: Joseph (David) Pratt

    The purpose of this plan is to ensure that a wireless service provider has the capability of responding and recovering from a network-affecting emergency. An emergency, as it applies to said plan is an event or crisis, which meets the following criteria:

    1. The event or crises causes or could cause damage to the integrity of the operation of the network.
    2. The condition could or is causing a severe strain on company resources.
    3. This condition could be for a prolonged period of time.

    It is to be noted that an emergency can be an event or crisis, which either has or may occur. Emergencies should not be confused with abnormal conditions, whereby, some component of the network is in a degraded state and may not meet the above criteria. If all three of the referenced criteria are met the event then constitutes an emergency condition. The magnitude and impact of an emergency will determine the response category. For the purpose of this document, emergencies shall be categorized, as follows:

    Level 1: The event or crisis has not yet occurred but is anticipated.
    Level 2: The event or crisis has occurred and the magnitude and impact are limited in scope, so that only parts of the Emergency
    Recovery Plan, require implementation.
    Level 3: A major crisis has occurred and the deployment of major
    resources is required.

    The objectives of this plan are as follows:

    • To provide a plan-of-action, for the quick and efficient restoration of services, during an emergency.
    • To provide pre-emergency contingencies, in order to mitigate the effects of potentially service affecting conditions.

    Scope:

    This plan encompasses the Network Switching Subsystem (NSS), Base Station Subsystem (BSS), the Operations & Maintenance Center (OMC), and the Network Management Subsystem (NMS). The current emphasis of the aforementioned plan is on the BSS infrastructure, which is the most vulnerable aspect of the network and vulnerable to natural disasters.

  • Femtocell Network Deployment Synopsis

    • December 2009
    Authors: Joseph (David) Pratt

    Femtocells Will Become an Integral Part of LTE Deployments:

    Femtocells are just starting to hit the wireless market but considerable momentum is building for the technology to play a key role when carriers start to deploy LTE networks.
    More than eighteen (18) wireless providers indicate that they are planning to deploy LTE technology within the next two years. Verizon Wireless has said that it will test the technology this year and is hopeful to offer commercial LTE by next year. Plans by the carriers to roll out LTE coincide with 3G femtocells being introduced to the market and LTE femtocells are currently being developed. The femtocell will play a key role in LTE network rollout plans.

    The 3G femtocell market is beginning to take shape, as Sprint Nextel Corp. and Verizon Wireless offer femtocell products to customers to boost wireless coverage in homes, where location or structures may interfere with wireless signals. AT&T Mobility is expected to offer a similar product, dubbed 3G MicroCell, later this year, and T-Mobile USA Inc. offers an in-home Wi-Fi router that allows customers to place calls using its broadband network with select devices. Mass commercial deployments for femtocells are forecast this year. When standards were being established for 3G technologies, femtocells were not included in the initial work but updates have been added to the standard to address the technology.

    The industry is taking a smarter approach in the standards process for LTE. The femtocell is part of the process in developing the standard, which is expected to be completed next month, and manufacturers are developing LTE femtocells. It makes sense that femtocells are being included in LTE because the 4G technology is expected to deliver higher speeds through a broadband connection. The femtocells on the market today route network calls through a customer’s broadband Internet connection with the utilization of a small base station.
