Gregg Bernstein's Summary

Gregg Bernstein is a UX Design Researcher for MailChimp. He spends his days listening, thinking, collaborating, writing, and then listening some more. Since 1996, he's designed for clients and taught for university students.

Gregg earned his MFA in Graphic Design from the Savannah College of Art & Design, where - for some reason - he became fascinated with the presentation of tax return forms and digital legal agreements. He's been hooked on UX research ever since.

He resides with his family in Athens, Georgia. In his spare time, he runs early and often.

  • The Fine Print: Redesigning Legal Contracts for the Digital Environment

    • Birmingham (UK) Institute of Art & Design Typographic Hub
    • May 15, 2011
    Authors: Gregg Bernstein

    The application of the principles of graphic and information design to the presentation of legal agreements in the digital environment could promote usability, comprehension, and communication. This paper dissects the Click-Through Agreement (CTA), a component of the typical software installation process wherein a user is bound to an agreement with a vendor by assenting to the terms via a “click.” The CTA often takes the form of a small box containing many lines of legal language, which the user may scroll through before clicking to agree to the terms of the agreement.

    With the advisement of a legal scholar and using principles from various design disciplines—foundations, web, information, usability, accessibility, and experience—this paper explores the current state of the CTA and how it might be improved for the sake of both the user and the vendor. The design findings are reconciled with research into human behavior, the decision-making process, and how the human eye receives information displayed on screen.

    From this study a proposed model for the CTA is presented, which divides the contractual terms into a series of screens. Each screen presents a summary, or chunk, of the agreement, which must be assented to via initialing before moving on to the next screen. The full agreement, as well as the opportunity to download or print each page, is always available via hyperlinked text and icons.

Gregg Bernstein's Projects

  • I agree to ____.

    • June 2010 to Present
    Team Members: Gregg Bernstein

    Nearly every software vendor uses some form of the click-through agreement [CTA] as part of the installation process. Yet this important and contractually binding information is presented in an unreadable, unusable, and unintuitive format.

    Within this site, I explore the state of the CTA today, the information it contains, and how it may be presented in a readable, comprehensible manner through the application of graphic design. This project began as my MFA Graphic Design thesis at the Savannah College of Art and Design [SCAD], and continues today.

    Dry? Yes. Exciting? No. Useful? Hopefully.

    At best, perhaps software vendors and their legal teams might review and revise their CTAs for the sake of their customers. At worst, you've just wasted two minutes. At the very least, you might find out what you're agreeing to when you click "I Agree."

Gregg Bernstein's Patents

  • Portable combination precision ruler, t-square, and angle

    • United States Patent Application 61/316,785
    • Filed March 23, 2010
    Inventors: Gregg Bernstein

    This invention relates to ruler-type measuring devices. The present invention
    relates particularly to precision measurements that would otherwise require a tsquare,
    framing square, or right angle.

    At present, such precision measurements require devices that are lacking in
    portability. Measurement squares and angles, by nature of their shapes, are
    difficult to pack for travel or commuting purposes.

    The present invention combines a 12-to-18 inch metallic ruler (or similar
    equivalent measuring device with markings for centimeters, picas, points, etc.)
    with its packaging to create a portable precision measuring device.

    The ruler is rounded at one end and squared off on the other. The rounded end
    features a hole for peg hanging.

    The packaging features two die cut channels into which the ruler may be
    inserted. Inside the channel are locking mechanisms which work in tandem with
    the hole in the ruler end to hold the ruler in place.

    The unique property of this particular invention is that the box becomes a
    makeshift t-square or right angle when the ruler is inserted into one of two die
    cut channels in the box. The locking mechanism, which uses the peg hole of the
    ruler, allows for quick, portable sketches and renderings by functioning as a tsquare
    or angle.
