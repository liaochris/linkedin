Eric Klein's Summary

As R&D Director at C3, I oversee research and analysis on what's happening, why is it happening, what should be happening, who it should be happening to and what technology we need to make it happen - in addition to the implementation of new technologies, processes, and best practices. Our focus is large music and food festivals all over the world (Austin City Limits Music Festival, Lollapalooza (Chicago, Chile, Brazil), Orion Music + More (Detroit), Austin Food & Wine Festival, Music City Eats Festival (Nashville), Big Day Out (Australia), and more); artist management (Bassnectar, Delta Spirit, and many more); concert promotion (see a few at c3concerts.com); and various other special mass gathering events.

My path leading to this current role was made up of 10+ years in IT and network management in higher education, healthcare, and manufacturing, followed by 12 in consulting and data management for SAS Institute in telecom (AT&T) and retail (Wal-Mart), followed by 3 years of trying to start and run my own record label and other creative businesses which were massive failures that drained my 401k that I would do over again in a heartbeat just for the fun and experience. I have also spent the last several years falling in love with Jesus. And I have a passion for counseling - or really just listening - and have a special place in my heart for anyone struggling with or considering suicide. There are way too many of you (e.g., a research paper recently revealed that over 5,000 students at UT Austin are considering suicide and over 3,000 of them are seriously considering it. If it's you, I'd gladly drop what I'm doing to come meet you and talk more about it and find you some help.)

The worst part of the job was having to work long weekends for special analyses only to find out that the analyses was never even read. I was told one day, "Eric, look at these 4 corner offices. He was a great organizer.

Assistant Director of Telecommunications

Systems Programmer

Managed international satellite network connecting all manufacturing plants. Performed global network capacity planning and analysis. But the real fun was my first week on the job. Since it was a manufacturing plant with dangerous chemicals and since we were on the third floor, we were trained to throw our desk chairs through the window and jump by hanging down from the window ledge by our hands to reduce the distance and trying to land on our feet so our legs would break and not our backs. If an alarm were to go off, we only had seconds to live if we didn't escape. Luckily, we never had to try it out. I left because I didn't like driving 30 minutes down IH-20 to Arlington every day in the traffic in my Jeep Wrangler - not to mention that they were always telling us to save up vacation in case we all got laid off due to Japan's rise in the industry.

Senior Systems Engineer

Texas Christian University

Educational Institution; 1001-5000 employees; Higher Education industry

May 1982 – December 1986 (4 years 8 months) Fort Worth, Texas

I started out tearing printouts off of the huge printers and filing them in professor's mailboxes. But over my 4.5 years at TCU, I became responsible for installation and maintenance of campus data network infrastructure and computer center operations support. I worked part-time 30 to 40 hours per week during college. We used some of the first Macs ever made, discovered this new concept called a "hyperlink", developed software on Commodore 64 and Atari, made our VAX 11-780, Xerox Sigma-9, and IBM Series-1 systems sing, and labeled the crap out of everything. I loved labeling things. I loved reading hex dumps to IBM support. I loved assembly language. Walls of systems manuals was our status symbol before electronic documentation ever existed. Staying up all night in the lab to play Moria on old VT-100 terminals was second only to staying up all night at honky-tonks in Ft. Worth's Stock Yards on the north side of town. Debugging the mainframe with Ken Shulte (RIP) meant getting out his long wood stick and killing cockroaches behind the giant (yet tiny) disk drives. Things were a bit easier back then. Busting student hackers who stole tests from professors online kinda sucked since they all likely went on to make millions for NASA and Microsoft even though they were expelled for some of the first trojan horse viruses ever written.

Head Chef / Chef / Salad Chef / Dishwasher / Bus Boy

The Hayloft Restaurant

December 1979 – August 1980 (9 months) Fort Worth, Texas - Forest Park Blvd.

During my sophomore and junior years in high school, I worked at a steak restaurant called The Hayloft. I worked my way up from bus boy all the way to Head Chef. There wasn't much chef to it. We just threw steaks on the grill and covered them in butter. I was pretty good at making 100 chicken friend steaks at a time with cream gravy. The real Head Chef there, Charlie, taught me everything I know about cooking a good steak. Since it was located on the Trinity River, rats used to come in pretty easily. At night after closing, we would put left over chicken on the floor and shoot them with rat shot. Every now and again, one would crawl up on a chair and make a customer scream. The other bus boys taught me how to buy cheap wine at 7-11 and add powdered cool-aid to it to make it into expensive wine. We also spent a lot of nights playing poker with our paychecks. Country music was usually cranked pretty loud after the doors closed and I still remember most of the words - Alabama and the Gatlin Brothers seemed to be the most popular.

Cook / Dishwasher / Ice Cream Server

Dairy Queen

Privately Held; 201-500 employees; Food & Beverages industry

September 1979 – November 1979 (3 months) Fort Worth, Texas - Bluebonnet Circle

During my sophomore year in high school, I cooked hamburgers, washed dishes, and served ice cream at Dairy Queen. I wasn't 16 yet so I had to get Senator Moncrief to sign a paper giving me permission to work while being under age. At the same time, I got a special driver's permit so I could drive my dad's red Ford F-150 back and forth to work. Somehow the JV football games were on the way most of the time. Draining and cleaning the ice cream machines and re-using the mixture the next day is enough to make you never eat them again. I can't remember why I left. I think it was the orange polyester tunic we had to wear. Our boss, Mrs. Mann, was awesome.

Shoe Shine Boy

Westcliff Barbershop

September 1978 – August 1979 (1 year) Forth Worth, Texas - Westcliff Shopping Center

I started getting my hair cut at Westcliff Barbershop sometime around 5 years old. My grandmother would take me. So many years later, during my freshman year in high school, while still getting my hair cut there every month, I decided it would be an awesome career to be a shoe shine boy. Alton Finney, a former Navy officer, ran the place and the other barber was Dale Marsh. Alton gave me $1 per shine and supplied all of my equipment - and I got free hair cuts and they bought me lunch on Saturdays. But there was one exception. I had to ask every person who walked in the door if I could shine or clean their shoes. That was Alton's way of making sure I wouldn't hide from school mates. One of my best customer's was Vernon Baird from Mrs. Baird's bread. He would drop of a whole bag of shoes for me to shine each week. Another was the local funeral parlor director. He got there every Wednesday around 3pm and I had to run from school to get there in time. He always tipped $5 which was about 25% of my weekly income.

Lawn Boy

Cye and Eric's Lawn Service

March 1977 – August 1977 (6 months) Fort Worth, Texas

My middle school buddy and I started our own lawn mowing service. We got rich. How incredible was it that someone would just hand us $40 for pushing a mower around for a few hours? Our parents would drop us off and pick us up at each lawn. I opened a checking account and learned how to keep it balanced. Unfortunately, I didn't learn how to save any of it. Other friends who mowed lawns went on to become millionaire entrepreneurs, but I bailed out because I was allergic to grass and would scratch my neck until it bled. Bummer. I wish neighborhood kids still mowed lawns today. I guess its easier now and more profitable to start their own Internet companies.
