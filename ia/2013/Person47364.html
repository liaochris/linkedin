Gawain Weaver's Projects

  • Willard E. Worden Gelatin Silver Print Treatment

    • May 2011 to August 2011
    Team Members: Gawain Weaver, Jennifer Olsen

    Willard Worden (1868-1946) was a San Francisco based photographer who trained as a painter in Philadelphia before serving as a photographer in the Phillipines during the Spanish-American War. He established a photography studio in San Francisco in 1902, first at the Cliff House and later on Stockton Street.

    This sulfur-toned gelatin silver print of a ship was most likely taken in the San Francisco Bay. It came to the studio with a large loss in the lower left corner, numerous losses and tears along the edges, and ferrotyping in the bottom half of the print where the surface had stuck to glass.

    The photograph was washed and light bleached to reduce staining and brittleness. The ferrotyping was reduced through washing. The loss in the lower left corner was filled and retouched to match the surrounding sea imagery. Tears and cracks in the prints were mended and retouched to reduce their visibility.

  • Eadweard Muybridge, Panorama of San Francisco, 1878

    • September 2010 to January 2011
    Team Members: Gawain Weaver, Jennifer DiJoseph

    Conservation of an 18 feet long albumen print panorama of San Francisco by famed photographer Eadward Muybridge. The treatment was performed at Gawain Weaver Art Conservation in 2010.

  • The AIC Guide to Digital Photography and Conservation Documentation

    • American Institute for Conservation of Historic and Artistic Works
    • 2008
    Authors: Gawain Weaver, Dawn Heller, Jeffrey Warda, Editor, Dan Kushel, Timothy Vitale, Franziska Frey

    A comprehensive guide on all aspects of digital photography of works of art and cultural heritage written specifically for conservators and collections care custodians. Topics include information on photography equipment (including different types of cameras, lenses, lights, printers, and software options); image processing guidelines (including practical recommendations on the use of color management, image file formats, file naming protocols, and metadata); and advice on best practices for storage and backup of electronic records with examples of different types of backup strategies. The second half of the book is devoted to explaining and illustrating photographic tools and techniques that are unique to conservation documentation photography, including visible light, ultraviolet, and infrared photography. A color appendix includes step-by-step screenshots of current software applications to illustrate key aspects of digital photographic documentation. This includes the photographic capture process with a camera tethered to a computer and use of Adobe® Photoshop® and Adobe Photoshop Lightroom® software; color-managed printing to an inkjet printer; and detailed instructions on creating and applying metadata and keyword templates for photographic records in Lightroom and Adobe Bridge® software. With over 120 color figures, this expanded second edition is more than twice the size of the first edition and incorporates an internal spiral binding to allow the book to lay flat. Awarded the Preservation Publication Award from the Society of American Archivists in 2009, this book is an essential reference for the conservator's library.

  • Tese de Mestrado "Characterization and Identification of Printed Objects"

    • FCT-UNL
    • October 22, 2008
    Authors: Gawain Weaver, Lénia Fernandes, Ryan Boatright, José Sanches Ramos

    A study about the physical appearance of pre-photographic, photomechanical, photographic and digital positive reflective prints was made, relating the obtained images with the history, materials and technology used to create them. The studied samples are from the Image Permanence Institute (IPI) study collection. The digital images were obtained using a digital SLR on a copystand and a compound light microscope, with different lighting angles (0º, 45ºand 90º) and magnifications from overall views on the copystand down to a 20x objective lens on the microscope. Most of these images were originally created by IPI for www.digitalsamplebook.org, a web tool for teaching print identification, and will be used on the www.graphicsatlas.org website, along with textual information on identification, technology and history information about these reproduction processes.

  • Cool And Dry: The Care And Preservation Of Photographs

    • Chubb Collectors Newsletter
    • May 11, 2012
    Authors: Gawain Weaver

    Fine art photographs are valued both as a source of aesthetic enjoyment and as a financial investment. In considering the care of photographic print collections it is the care of silver images and color dyes that provide the two primary challenges and cover the majority of the photographs in most collections. While each photographic process has its own particular forms of deterioration, temperature, relative humidity (RH), light, pollutants, handling and enclosures are elements that affect any collection of photographs.

  • Chromogenic Characterization: A Study of Kodak Color Prints, 1942-2008

    • Topics in Photographic Preservation 13
    • 2010
    Authors: Gawain Weaver, Zach Long

    The Eastman Kodak Co. and their coupler-incorporated chromogenic print process, were nearly synonymous with the 20th century color snapshot. Introduced in 1942 and still manufactured today, samples of these prints from intervals across the manufacturing history were studied in detail in order to gain a fuller understanding of the material characteristics of this photographic process. The following aspects of the prints were examined: support, dye cloud structure, layer order, backprinting and stamps, dye and coupler stability, and fluorescence. The prints were documented overall and in cross-section under both visible and UV radiation, using an Olympus AX-70 compound microscope, and a Canon EOS 5D digital SLR. Changes in the print characteristics over time were documented and when possible, correlated to known technological developments. The documentation of these changes over time made it possible to date nearly any print to within a few years. Subsequent findings from this inquiry significantly add to the knowledge about these ubiquitous yet rarely studied photographic prints.

  • A Guide to Fiber-Base Gelatin Silver Print Condition and Deterioration

    • Notes on Photographs
    • 2008
    Authors: Gawain Weaver

    The condition of a gelatin silver print can be described in many different ways. Prints are often said to be warm-toned and to have silver-mirroring or a patina. But what do we mean when we use these words? What kind of aging process, physical change, or deterioration are we really describing? What is their cause? What do they say about the history of a print? And, perhaps more importantly, what do they tell us about the future of a print? The purpose of this guide is to answer these questions, and to make the examination of a black-and-white print a more informative and rewarding activity. Understanding how a print deteriorates and the visual clues that indicate the various causes of deterioration can be very useful in the evaluation of a print’s condition, authenticity, and material history.

  • Early Color Photography

    • Bloom! Experiments in Color Photography by Edward Steichen. Luxembourg: Musee d'Art Moderne Grand-Duc Jean.
    • 2007
    Authors: Gawain Weaver

    As soon as the daguerreotype was announced in 1839, and for the first 100 years of photography, there was a great desire to record the colors of nature with the camera. Hand-applied pigments and watercolors were used from the beginning, but these were a far cry from the direct capture of the colors of nature. Nineteenth-century experimenters pursued many attempts to directly imprint color upon the photographic plate. The French physicist Gabriel Lippman came the closest when he used the interference of light to recreate the colors of nature optically, an invention that won him the Nobel Prize in 1908. Real success in color photography, however, would ultimately come from the use of dyes and pigments in combination with traditional black and white photography. In the 1860s, a French inventor named Louis Ducos du Hauron laid out the foundations for the future of color photography. He outlined methods for the use of additive colors --- red, green, and blue --- to create color photographs to be viewed in transmitted light or by projection. He also explained the use of subtractive primary colors --- cyan, magenta, and yellow as we now know them—to produce color photographs. Before any color photography could be fully successful, the discovery of dyes to make black and white emulsions sensitive to the entire visible spectrum would be necessary. This began in the 1860s and 70s, and by the first few years of the 20th century, black and white negatives could be made that were sensitive to all the visible colors from violet to red.

  • Commercial Photograph Coatings in North America, 1950 to the Present

    • In Constance McCabe (Ed.), Coatings on Photographs: Materials, Techniques, and Conservation (pp. 200-217). Washington, DC: American Institute for Conservation.
    • 2005
    Authors: Gawain Weaver

    Recognizing our limited knowledge of the many proprietary formulas and resins currently in use, this chapter outlines the types of commercial photograph coatings that have been used as well as their methods of application, and traces their technical and industrial development from 1950 to the present. The discussion is limited to liquid coatings applied to photographic
    prints after processing. The geographic focus of the research is North America.

  • Capital Portraits: Conservation of the Topley Studio Index

    • ANAGPIC
    • 2005
    Authors: Gawain Weaver

    Photographic albums have been discussed in the conservation literature at some length in the past two decades. Treatments have varied widely in their approach to the photographic album, though the trend appears to be towards more conservative treatments. Discussions about these treatments have often focused on the dilemma of treating a single object—a book, that has many components—binding, pages, handwriting, and of course photographs. The dilemmas involved in the treatment of composite objects are as old as conservation, and the discussions of the past two decades, have, not surprisingly, not provided any definitive answers for the treatment of photograph albums, but have provided a resource for the consideration of current treatments. I will be presenting today the details of my own encounter with these issues, my assistance in the treatment of a single album in the Topley Collection of the Library and Archives Canada, and the conservation survey of the 66 albums which make up the Topley Index, a pictorial history of William James Topley’s Ottawa portrait studio.

  • Looking Back: How Digital Screens Mimic the Autochrome

    • IMAGE, 45(2), 26-29.
    • 2007
    Authors: Gawain Weaver

    Color science has its own mathematics, with additive and subtractive colors being two possible ways of mixing colors to create a full spectrum. Many early color photographic processes revolved around additive colors, while more recent color photography used subtractive colors. The recent transition to digital has brought photography full circle, since the digital screens found on computer monitors, digital cameras, cell phones, and televisions employ additive color technology.
