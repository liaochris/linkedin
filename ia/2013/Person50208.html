Senior Industrial Engineer

Ethyl Corporation

Public Company; 1001-5000 employees; EY; Chemicals industry

July 1984 – October 1987 (3 years 4 months) Harrisburg, Pennsylvania Area

The Capitol Products Division of Ethyl manufactured aluminum ingot, bars, tubes, and extrusions. My work started with finishing processes across three facilities (PA, GA, IN) including paint, anodizing, and various mechanically distressed finishes. The company also manufactured aluminum windows and doors, and the competitive landscape left the company challenged for growth.

While implementing an early Manufacturing Resource Planning enterprise software solutions (before ERP) it became clear that this would simply automate planning for processes that were not ideal. Researched potential systemic solutions and developed (on my own time) a plan to re-engineer the company using the Toyota Production System with liberal application of the Theory of Contraints.

Presented plan to manager, up several levels to the CEO, who allocated the entire Industrial Engineering team and others to implement recommendations.

Slashed order cycles from 5 weeks to 1 week, reduced inventory by 80%+, reduced defects by over 80%, increased productivity by 100%, etc. Classic JIT / Lean benefits.

To the best of my knowledge, our team delivered one of the first "lean" projects in the US (1985).

Industrial Engineer

Allied Tube & Conduit

Public Company; 1001-5000 employees; Mining & Metals industry

1981 – 1982 (1 year) Greater Chicago Area

Allied Tube & Conduit is a pioneer in the tubing industry. The company invented the process of in-line galvanizing, developed numerous tube and pipe products, originated the process of in-line steel tube galvanizing, and invented IMC (intermediate metal conduit). The company was acquired by Tyco International in 1987.

At the time the company operated 7 high speed rolling mills that produced over 50% of US demand for galvanized and nickel-plated tubing for electrical, sprinkler, and mechanical purposes. At the time several of these mills operated at up to 600' per minute, 24 hours per day. My job was to drive downtime toward zero. Process tooling design, single minute exchange of dies (SMED) and capital equipment specification, selection, and implementation.
