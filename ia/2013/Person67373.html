Jon Aleckson, PhD's Summary

Web Courseworks, the company I run, creates sophisticated programs for electronic education. Our programs typically incorporate a high level of interactivity; they are designed to (a) allow users to learn by doing, and (b) help users sort rapidly through large sets of information or complex processes. We are able to improve the reach and efficiency of advanced academic curricula, K–12 courses, professional training tracks, and human resource departments.

The culture of Web Courseworks is influenced by my passion for understanding how people learn. This manifests in two ways:

1. My attention to the unprecedented pedagogical opportunities inherent in new technology (e.g., how we can turn people from novices into experts in less time, for less money).

2. My commitment to project management methods that enhance collaboration. When eLearning developers both teach one another and learn from one another, they create extremely high-value products.

The second point is of great importance to the eLearning industry. I conducted my doctoral research in the area of how eLearning development teams work together—specifically, how certain corporate cultures and certain methods of project management can lead to dramatic increases in the quality of deliverables. This is the topic of a book I recently co-wrote with instructional designer Penny Ralston-Berg, titled MindMeld: Micro-Collaboration between eLearning Designers and Instructor Experts (Atwood Publishing 2011). Based largely on my PhD dissertation, MindMeld outlines the five factors, both theoretical and practical, that go into creating exemplary eLearning tools.

At Web Courseworks, I guide project teams in implementing the combined lessons of my research and my decades of practical experience. All Web Courseworks deliverables are grounded in cutting-edge development processes, thanks to which we regularly exceed industry standards.

Jon Aleckson, PhD's Publications

  • MindMeld: Micro-Collaboration between eLearning Designers and Instructor Experts

    • Atwood Publishing
    • June 14, 2011
    Authors: Jon Aleckson, PhD, Penny Ralston-Berg

    MindMeld draws heavily on my doctoral dissertation, but is much more practical than academic. It outlines the five major factors that influence eLearning development teams: power relationships, project management, communication, evaluation, and momentum. It places online education at the intersection of video game theory and active learning theory, and provides concrete strategies for taking advantage of both fields.
