Carnegie Mellon University, Pekkan Cardiovascular Biofluids Group

Educational Institution; 5001-10,000 employees; Higher Education industry

August 2010 – December 2010 (5 months)

A myocardial infarction can damage myocardial tissue, which may lead to myocardial scarring or ventricular aneurysms, including ventricular fibrillation and ventricular tachycardia. Current methods of determining the extent of myocardium damage include electrocardiogram (ECG), echocardiography, and various blood tests. While these are good indicators of tissue damage, they are highly qualitative. This research seeks to quantitatively determine the damage in the myocardium following an infarction. This is done by injecting a cardiac perfusion contrast agent intravenously in the left arm of a myocardial infarction patient, using convolution filters to remove artifacts from MRI images, and then measuring the brightness of voxels to track diffusion of dye into the myocardium. Measurement of the difference in brightness of the voxels in MRI images quantitatively indicates how well the dye is being diffused into the myocardium, which is then quantitatively interpreted to yield precise indications of the state of health of the myocardium.

The world is experiencing a demographic transition, and as the population is growing older, non-communicable diseases (NCDs) are becoming the main causes of ill health. Today, NCDs are the major cause of death in lower-middle, upper-middle, and high-income countries, and by 2015, they will also be the leading cause of death in low-income countries as well. Medical devices are extensively used for diagnosis, prevention, monitoring, treatment and alleviation of NCDs, and efficient disease management requires patients to be able to access medical expertise quickly, frequently and with minimum travel.
