  • Making Renewable Energy FiT: How an Independent Feed-in-Tariff Certifying Organization Could Help Accelerate Renewable Energy Deployment in the U.S.

    • NATURAL RESOURCES JOURNAL
    • September 2012
    Authors: Ian Boisvert, Ivan Leiben

    Of all countries Germany has the world’s highest amount of installed solar power. That is a direct result of implementing Feed-In-Tariffs (“FiTs”). FiTs are government-mandated, long-term purchase contracts offered to renewable energy generators at wholesale rates that return to them the cost of installation plus a reasonable profit. Among all competing policies FiTs are the most effective and efficient at deploying renewable energy. Unlike Germany and, to a growing extent, Spain, FiTs are not widely used in the United States notwithstanding a desire to promote renewable energy. Where states have tried something like FiTs the results are discouraging because their FiTs lack necessary components. Moreover, the federal structure and complex electricity laws lessen the possibility of widespread FiT adoption in the US. To resolve these difficulties we propose creating a FiT Certification Board (FCB) to certify FiT programs as meeting minimum elements necessary for an effective FiT. We draw from the German and Spanish experiences to determine the minimum elements and necessary components to guide certification. In turn, the FCB could help unleash renewable energy deployment in the U.S.

  • Lifting the Looking Glass: Tradable Occupation Could Facilitate Ocean Renewable Energy in New Zealand

    • New Zealand Journal of Environmental Law
    • December 1, 2011
    Authors: Ian Boisvert

    With abundant ocean renewable energy resources New Zealand has the possibility of being a global leader in this emerging clean technology. However, its framework for allocating coastal space for renewable energy development, the Resource Management Act, suffers from paradoxes, inconsistencies, and too much discretion for local authorities. Coupling these issues with incentives for existing users to stymie new coastal applicants through drawn out litigation is arguably discouraging ocean renewable energy development and encouraging coastal conflict.

    To tackle these challenges this paper recommends that ocean renewable energy developers, as a group, build a nationwide rapport, that local authorities strengthen their processes and accountability, and that the national government allow commercial and cultural coastal users the ability to privately trade occupation of coastal space among themselves. The last recommendation will encourage cooperation, build a new market, and generate new sources of public revenue to strengthen oceans governance.

  • Mountains of “Blue Tape”: Barriers to United States and New Zealand Marine Renewable Energy Projects

    • Annual Report of Executive Committee of Ocean Energy Systems
