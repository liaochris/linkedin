Teaching Fellow

UCLA - Department of Ethnomusicology

Educational Institution; 10,001+ employees; Higher Education industry

September 2003 – June 2004 (10 months)

One of the best experiences of my time in graduate school at UCLA was as a Teaching Fellow. After returning from fieldwork in Morocco, I wrote a proposal for an undergraduate honors seminar, "Nationalism, Identity and Resistance in African Popular Music", that was submitted by the Department of Ethnomusicology for consideration for a Collegium of University Teaching Fellows fellowship. My proposal was one of fourteen accepted university-wide. The opportunity to develop and teach my own class was a great learning experience. A great perk of the fellowship was participation in a seminar on pedagogy & teaching technology led by Professor Peter Kollock.

In August 2004, my friend poet and teacher Neeli Cherkovski and I discussed my developing a course on ethnographic research for a new MFA in Creative Inquiry program at New College of California (San Francisco). I taught that course during the Fall semester and also worked as a thesis advisor to students in New College's MA in Humanities & Leadership program.

In March 2005, I joined the Board of Directors of the Purple Silk Music Education Foundation (a music education program based in Oakland, California that teaches musical skills to a diverse population of middle school and high school students using traditional Chinese instruments). I served as a Board Member until September 2006 and gained valuable experience in the challenges of operating an arts non-profit.

Fulbright Fellow

Fulbright-Hayes Doctoral Dissertation Research Abroad Fellowship

February 2002 – December 2002 (11 months)

In 2001, I decided that my research for my dissertation would examine how local, national and transnational influences and connections affected the development of a regional variety of Moroccan popular music. I gained the approval of my doctoral committee and applied for funding. I was awarded a Fulbright-Hayes Doctoral Dissertation Research Abroad Fellowship and my wife and I left for Morocco in February 2002.

After a couple of months in Fez, I was introduced to Moroccan alternative music (fusion, hip-hop, electronica, rock) and shifted the subject of my research. In March 2006, I filed my dissertation, "French Fries in the Tagine: Re-imagining Moroccan Popular Music", which describes what I found.
