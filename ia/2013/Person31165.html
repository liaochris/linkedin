David Kusek's Summary

I am a digital change agent that creates products and services that people crave and help grow businesses that change the world. I embrace tech and know how to leverage it to get amazing things done. I have developed numerous strategies for major brands in education and digital media. I quickly bring results to the table.

Pioneered the path for the multibillion-dollar digital music market that exists today. I am a co-developer of the MIDI standard that opened up electronic music to millions. I positioned Berklee College of Music as Online Music Education World Leader in 170 Countries.

-Built a successful online education and training division generating tens of millions in new annual revenue serving over 15,000 students/year.

-Significantly increased the visibility, awareness and market share of the “Berklee” brand in the global marketplace and enhanced its image, all while increasing enrollment, revenue and surplus.

-Developed over 200 Online Courses and Programs optimized to coordinate feedback between instructor and student. The system is self-serve allowing registration, payment, and scheduling, a unique offering at any online school. Won the Best Online Course in the Country Award for 8 years in a row.

-Founded digital media consultancy focused on technology, marketing, and business development. Clients include AOL, IBM, Nokia, Island, A&M Records, BMG, Polygram, Roland, Yamaha, Boston Acoustics, Brown University and others.

- Co-authored the best selling music business book The Future of Music: Manifesto for the Digital Music Revolution in 2005 in which we predicted the transformation that has occurred in the marketplace.

David Kusek's Publications

  • The Future of Music: Manifesto for the Digital Music Revolution

    • Berklee Press, 2005
    • 2005
    Authors: David Kusek, Gerd Leonhard

    In what could be one of the most provocative music books published, two innovators in music technology take a fascinating look at the impact of the digital revolution on the music business and predict "a future in which music will be like water: ubiquitous and free-flowing." Kusek and Leonhard foresee the disappearance of CDs and record stores as we know them; consumers will have access to more products than ever, though, through a vast range of digital radio channels, person-to-person Internet file sharing and a host of subscription services. The authors are especially good at describing how the way record companies operate - as both owners and distributors of music, with artists making less than executives - will also drastically change: individual CD sales, for example, will be replaced by "a very potent 'liquid' pricing system that incorporates subscriptions, bundles of various media types, multi-access deals, and added-value services." While the authors often shift from analysts into cheerleaders for the über-wired future they predict - "Let's replace inefficient content-protection schemes with effective means of sharing-control and superdistribution!" - their clearly written and groundbreaking book is the first major statement of what may be "the new digital reality" of the music business in the future. - Publisher's Weekly
