Cira Louise Brown's Summary

I'm passionate about visual communication, experiential design and informal learning and teaching. I love the craft of interpreting and presenting information for wide audiences, and I feel strongly about the tremendous potential of museums and makerspaces within the community. Presently, I am enrolled in the Museum Studies graduate program at Tufts University where I have gained a holistic understanding of museum operations and educational strategies. Excellence in this field requires cross-disciplinary skills, and my strategy has been to prepare accordingly -- I have a diverse background in print and digital media design, curriculum development and teaching, and I am currently transitioning my design business to focus on museum exhibition development. My base is at Artisan's Asylum, a community-run craft studio and fabrication space in Somerville, MA.

I deeply value the public understanding of science. My historical research focuses on the influence of science in society during the early-to-mid 20th Century - particularly the eras of the Jet Age, Radio Age, Atomic Age and the Space Age. My work has examined the cultural impact of these technological accelerations and the resultant changes to media climates and public attitudes about science.

Specialties: Graphic Design, HTML/CSS, PHP/MySQL, Omeka, WordPress, iBook/ebook development, SEO, various web content platforms.

Cira Louise Brown's Projects

  • The History of Early Modern Physics: A Digital Exhibit

    • May 2010 to Present
    Team Members: Cira Louise Brown

    Project Demonstration
    Humanities+Digital: Visual Interpretations Conference 2010, MIT, Cambridge, MA

    Abstract:
    From a social and scientific standpoint, the start of the 20th Century is a busy time. Nations were being shaped by imperialism, industrialization and political revolution – forces which later set the conditions for both World Wars. The study of physics had transitioned from being primarily motivated by applied technology to a field of rapidly expanding abstract research. These two simultaneous paradigm shifts eventually merged together, and the seemingly benign studies of physicists culminated in the development of the atomic bomb. Questions about the social responsibility of the scientist inevitably became apparent – questions that remain pertinent today.

    This historical context has often been marginalized in traditional physics curricula. Similarly, science is often neglected from standard history courses. This project serves as a supplemental resource to bridge these fields in a visually engaging and accessible way. Our collective human history has been characterized by both conflict and inquiry, and the narrative of early modern physics is an exemplary case study of these human endeavors in tandem.

    The online exhibit consists of four modules:
    1. An expandable timeline that juxtaposes world events and milestones in physics (utilizes open-source software created by the MIT SIMILE Project).
    2. An interactive map showing the geographical migration of scientists during the rise of the Third Reich in Germany (also utilizes SIMILE code).
    3. A visual glossary that dynamically displays related ideas, people and associated time periods (custom-coded for this project).
    4. A chronology of the evolution of the atomic model (an illustrated schematic).

  • Big Science/Big Media: The Early US Space Race and Mass Communication

    • May 2011 to Present
    Team Members: Cira Louise Brown

    Paper Presentation
    Media In Transition 7: The Promise and Peril of Transition, MIT, Cambridge, MA

    Abstract:
    The media frenzy surrounding the Space Race was not merely an exercise in grandiose heroification, nor a manifestation of the Military-Industrial complexities of the Cold War environment; it was also a transformative process that created new paradigms of large-scale science and technological communication to the masses. As the American public was confronted with (and persuaded to sponsor) the lofty endeavor of landing on the moon, the crafts of using mass media to blend entertainment with news, sustain a sense of urgency, and promote a highly risky agenda – facets of the news that we are very familiar with today – were being sharpened. By drawing on James L. Kauffman’s Selling Outer Space: Kennedy, the Media, and Funding for Project Apollo, 1961-1963 and media archives from the era, this case study examines the formation of seminal strategies in Big Science publicization, conveying immediacy and a collective national call to action, and the vagueness of shared notions of security and insecurity.
