Arthur O Anderson MD's Summary

Art Anderson is a Physician-Scientist and board certified Pathologist who has been engaged in Cellular Immunology Research since 1972. He is also an Applied Ethicist who has been involved in Human Research Subject Protections since 1975 when he was appointed chair of the USAMRIID IRB, called the Human Use Committee [HUC]. He wrote the first operational guidelines for this committee based on Nuremberg Code Principles in 1975. He has maintained these policies and procedures and updated them as new DOD, Federal and State guidelines, directives and regulations became available. Presently he serves as Director, Office of Human Use and Ethics, is a member of the HUC and is the Research Integrity Officer for the institute. He actively participates in local and national meetings of organizations whose focus is protecting the rights, safety and welfare of human research volunteer subjects. He is presently retired from his clinical and bench research positions, but participates with other scientists in collaborations which benefit from his >30 years experience in immunology, virology and pathology research.

Specialties: Regulatory Affairs, Medical Research Oversight, International Medical Ethics Development, Research Integrity, Basic Immunology Research related to vaccines, Viral Pathogenesis

Chief, Department of Respiratory and Mucosal Immunity

USAMRIID , US Army Medical Research Institute of Infectious Diseases

Government Agency; 501-1000 employees; Research industry

July 1983 – July 1996 (13 years 1 month) Fort Detrick MD 21702

The Department of Respiratory and Mucosal Immunity developed and tested candidate vaccines for protection against aerosol disseminated pathogens, and studied adjuvants and recombinant antigens to continue to improve protection by vaccines prior to advanced development.

With Kouji Matsushima & Chris Larsen, identified the first chemokine IL-8 (1989 Science 243 1464-1466) and & the 2nd MCP-1 (1990 J.Exp.Med. v171 p2177) which heralded an entirely new field of immunology, inflammation and infectious diseases.

The fibroblastic reticular cell conduit FRCC which I briefly described in 1975 (Amer. J. Path. 80:387-418) proved to be a novel anatomical structure for distribution of soluble antigens and/or chemokines to HEV and DC in lymph nodes. The role of the FRCC in immunity was further elaborated by collaboration with J Elizabeth Gretz and Stephen Shaw at NCI where its function in transporting chemokines and <70KDa antigenic solutes was confirmed and shown to be relevant to in vivo immune and inflammatory reactions in lymph nodes.

Principal Investigator Experimental Pathology

USAMRIID , US Army Medical Research Institute of Infectious Diseases

Government Agency; 501-1000 employees; Research industry

July 1974 – June 1980 (6 years) Fort Detrick MD 21702

Created a research program aimed at discovery and development of molecularly-defined adjuvants for enhancing vaccine immunogenicity and effectiveness based on the hypothesis that materials that increase the rate of lymphocyte recirculation through lymph nodes would stochastically increase an immune response to vaccine by exposing more migrating cells to antigen presenting cells. In vitro lymphocyte chemotaxis was also studied as a correlate to the above.

This work helped identify: a. Molecular Mechanisms of lymphocyte homing and recirculation; b. Importance of chemotaxis in lymphocyte homing and recirculation; c. Angiogenesis of high endothelial venules in immune and inflammatory responses; d. Lymph Node Fibroblastic Reticular Cell Conduit and its function while studying permeability of LN microvasculature in 1975.

The program also identified a family of molecules in leukocyte dialysates (molecules that were less than 10 KDa) as being a source of chemotactic factors that regulated traffic of lymphocytes in lymph nodes. Although unable to purify them in the 1970s these small chemotactic molecules were identified as Chemokines by 1989.

Pathology Resident

Johns Hopkins Hospital

Nonprofit; 10,001+ employees; Hospital & Health Care industry

1970 – 1974 (4 years) Baltimore MD

Residency training in anatomic pathology, surgical pathology and cytology. My concentration was in Medical Biopsies, especially Renal, Gut and Liver. I performed elective research in transplantation immunology under Dr. Norman D. Anderson, that explored effects of allografts on regional lymph nodes. I used histology, electron microscopy, cytochemistry and immunohistochemistry to characterize changes of cells, structures and compartments of lymph nodes sequentially after application of skin grafts. This was part of a program project grant that explored the role of microvasculature in immune responses initiated by alloantigens released by transplanted tissues. These studies began my quest to understand lymphatic tissue microenvironments and the mechanisms that control lymphocyte recirculation and cell interactions within lymph nodes, spleen and mucosal lymphatic tissue.

Arthur O Anderson MD's Publications

  • Scientific and Ethical Importance of Animal Models in Biodefense Research

    • in Biodefense Research Methodology and Animal Models, Second Edition
    • November 15, 2011
    Authors: Arthur O Anderson MD, James R. Swearengen and Arthur O. Anderson

    The chapter describes the need for a new method of FDA approval of drugs and vaccines for treatment and prophylaxis of highly hazardous diseases where testing for substantial evidence of human clinical efficacy would be immoral because many people would die in a controlled study. Use of animal surrogates in well designed animal models was first allowed by FDA for approval upon publication of the Animal Efficacy Rule on 31 May 2002. The Chapter describes the historical process leading up to this decision and projects ways this method of approval may be appropriately used moving forward.

  • Ultrastructural localization of extracellular matrix proteins of the lymph node cortex: evidence supporting the reticular network as a pathway for lymphocyte migration.

    • BMC Immunology 2010, 11:42
    • 2010
    Authors: Arthur O Anderson MD, Gregg P Sobocinski Katherine Toy Walter Bobrowski Stephen Shaw Arthur O Anderson and Eric P Kaldjian

    This article provides ultrastructural evidence for molecules involved in formation and structure of the fibroblastic reticular cell conduit [FRCC]. It adds fine detail and additional molecular moieties to the extracellular and cellular matrix molecules known to be associated with the FRCC

  • Fibroblastic reticular cells and their role in viral hemorrhagic fevers

    • Expert Rev. Anti Infect. Ther. 7(4), 423-435
    • 2009
    Authors: Arthur O Anderson MD, Keith E Steele Arthur O Anderson and Mansour Mohamadzadeh

    Beginning with Ebola virus where we found that the fibroblastic reticular cell conduit was involved in amplifying and disseminating infection, we subsequently found that other hemorrhagic disease viruses appeared to use the FRCC in similar ways to avoid the innate immune system as they invaded the host and subsequently diminished the ability of the acquired immune system to fight them.

  • Reduced Levels of Protein Tyrosine Phosphatase CD45 Protect Mice from the Lethal Effects of Ebola Virus Infection

    • Cell Host & Microbe 6, 162–173
    • 2009
    Authors: Arthur O Anderson MD, R Panchal, S Bradfute, B Peyser, K Warfield, G Ruthel, D Lane, T Kenny, A Anderson, W Raschke & S Bavari

    The title says it all. While CD45 has been described as the Common Leukocyte Antigen, it also has a function linked to the fact that it is a tyrosine kinase. Reduction of expression of CD45 appears to alter Immune Homeostasis in a way that enables the host to eliminate this dangerous pathogen more quickly than if CD45 levels were normal.

  • Ethical and Legal Dilemmas in Biodefense Research (Chapter 24)

    • Textbook of Military Medicine: Medical Aspects of Biological Warfare, Borden Institute
    • 2007
    Authors: Arthur O Anderson MD, JEFFREY E STEPHENSON and ARTHUR O ANDERSON

    The need to protect American soldiers from natural or unusually disseminated infectious diseases has existed from the earliest years of this nation. With progress in regulations aimed at protecting patients and consumers, the ability to provide these protections began to disappear because the process of testing for marketing approval required involving humans in very dangerous tests of clinical efficacy. This chapter discusses the various avenues that may be taken to provide vaccines and drugs to protect soldiers from hazardous diseases and makes moral assessments of the various alternatives.

Arthur O Anderson MD's Education

The Johns Hopkins University

postdoctoral fellow, Pathology, Transplant Immunology

1970 – 1974

I participated in a transplantation research program with Dr. Norman Anderson while a pathology resident, supported by a Training Grant at Johns Hopkins University. Our project was aimed at understanding how vascular endothelium may be involved in immune responses to alloantigens. I used light and electron microscopy, cytochemistry and immunohistochemistry to explore effects of alloantigenic stimulation on microvasculature and lymphocyte recirculation in rat lymph nodes. My electron microscopic and cytochemical analyses of lymph node structures like the fibroblastic reticular cell stroma, microvasculature, and high endothelial venules resulted in 4 seminal publications appearing between 1975 and 1976 which jump started my career as interest in lymphocyte homing and dendritic cells was becoming important in the new field of cellular immunology.

I served as a Master's Thesis Instructor in the Department of Art As Applied to Medicine in my last year of Pathology training (1973-1974)

Activities and Societies: International Academy of Pathology, American Association of Immunologists, American Society of Investigative Pathologists

University of Maryland School of Medicine

MD, Medicine

1966 – 1970

The medical curriculum integrated the disciplines in the preclinical didactic education. Biochemistry, Biophysics and Physiology was completely integrated. Microbiology, Pharmacology and Pathology also contained some integrated sections. Students conducted a year long research project and submitted a manuscript on the results.The decision about what project to conduct was by consensus of the four assigned lab partners. Our project dealt with the effects of chemically variable forms of barbiturates on evoked cortical responses in cat brains using encephisole perfusion techniques. I was challenged to "invent" a perfusion system that oxygenated and warmed the fluids. I also did the microvascular procedure of inserting a glass microcannula into a pial arteriole and ligation of anastomotic branches. This experience was very useful in the research I later performed.

The clinical rotations were as expected for a patient care oriented medical school.

Activities and Societies: Student Curriculum Review Committee, Nu Sigma Nu medical student fraternity, Student American Medical Association, Yearbook Art & Photography Editor.
