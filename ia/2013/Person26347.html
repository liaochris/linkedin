Colin Robbins' Summary

I am a technologist that can speak business language, comfortable operating at a board level.

As a member of board of Nexor, I am responsible for the long term technology vision and strategy of the business. A key success has been the re-introduction of a research and innovation programme, leading to funded research projects and new product lines that enable business efficiency through the secure exchange of data.

As a technologist I have been researching internet technology before it was called the Internet; my primary research was in the area of highly distributed directory systems as a foundation for secure messaging, expertise that was globally recognised and lead to the invention of LDAP. This opened doors into a wide ranging career that has encompassed a broad range of technologies and concepts such as smart cards, secure networking, secure data exchange and information governance.

I am committed to the development of the cyber security industry, supporting a number of industry associations, to do my bit to increase the security awareness and skills of UK industry.

Outside of work, I am member of the Institute of Swimming, and coach the East Midlands regional junior Water Polo teams.


Specialties:
• Secure Information Exchange to enable interconnectivity of segregated networks;
• Data Guards to ensure security policy is enforced between data connections;
• Data Diodes to provide a 100% guarantee of one way data flows;
• Secure Software Development to ensure the integrity of software developed;
• Email Gateways to enable interconnectivity between different secure email systems;
• Deep Content Checking to find hidden content in data exchanges.

  • Preventing Document Based Malware from Devastating your Business

    Authors: Colin Robbins

    Viruses used to be the province of hackers whose aim was to demonstrate their technical prowess by defacing web sites.   • The Role of Virtualisation in Cross-Domain Solutions

    • Nexor
    • March 2011
    Authors: Colin Robbins

    Virtualisation is an increasing trend in IT, enabling major reductions in the cost of computing. However, the adoption of virtualisation in specialist environments requires careful consideration. Cross-Domain Solutions (CDS), in which systems are deployed to enable secure and reliable transfer of information between classified domains, require a high degree of assurance that the data will not be compromised. The security of these systems is therefore paramount and the use of virtualisation must be assessed carefully.

    To understand the level of assurance required in a CDS it is necessary to analyse both the impact level and the threat scenarios. There will typically be guidance available to the appropriate impact level associated with various types of incident, including a data compromise. Combined with a view of the type and likelihood of a potential attack, it is possible to assess the risk and, therefore, determine whether you can trust a solution to provide an appropriate level of protection. Trust is typically measured by the level of assurance afforded by virtue of an industry standard or national scheme. In relation to CDS, network separation and policy enforcement are the two primary perspectives to check for assurance and these must be considered in any assessment of virtualisation in a CDS context.

  • Preventing Data Leakage to a Public Cloud

    • Nexor
    • May 2011
    Authors: Colin Robbins

    Public sector organisations across the world are looking to embrace cloud services with the objective of reducing overall IT spend through use of shared services.

    Within the UK public sector, cloud technology is considered "good" for Impact Level1 0 (IL0) and IL1 level data (80% of data), but not suitable for the higher impact levels (20% of data). To facilitate adoption of the cloud, the risk that higher impact level data may leak needs to be managed.

    Nexor, experts in connecting, transforming and protecting sensitive information in cyberspace, has developed a proof-of-concept to show how its solutions that have been tried and tested in cross domain environments can be suitably adapted to meet the needs of the cloud.

    The proof-of-concept shows how a content-filtering proxy can intercept typical web application data, in this case Microsoft SharePoint data, and make a release decision. The concept applies equally well to other cloud applications and platforms.

  • Network Admission Control and Identity Management

    • Siemens Insight Consulting
    • December 2007
    Authors: Colin Robbins, Simon Khan

    Most organisations have complex network infrastructures that have developed over time to serve different needs. Most systems now use the ubiquitous IP protocol to transfer information, which means that they can share a common network infrastructure. This brings advantages: a more coherent and converged network is easier to maintain and can provide a user experience that is both richer and simpler.

    Taking advantage of this convergence allows additional facilities to be provided across the network. Previously separate services, such as voice, video conferencing and security surveillance, can now use the same network infrastructure, reducing costs and allowing a greater range of users to benefit than ever before.

    Allied to these changes has been a trend towards flexible working. Providing your users with portable computers, allowing them to use their own computing equipment, use of wireless networks and secure remote access solutions allows them to work more flexibly and efficiently, as well as saving costs on office infrastructure. As a consequence of this, and of increased use of Internet-based resources, the traditional network perimeter has all but disappeared. The business benefits for this, summarised in a white paper from the Jericho Forum, include wider connectivity with external organisations, electronic interaction with customers, closer alignment of the network with business boundaries and flexible working.

  • Successfully deploying single sign-on within an outsourced environment

    • Siemens Insight Consulting
    • 2006
    Authors: Colin Robbins, Edward Hamilton

    If implemented correctly SSO can be a rare breed of technology: making a user’s life simpler while also improving system security and the business processes. SSO does not have the usual tradeoff between ease of use and higher security – it both increases security by ensuring you know who is accessing your systems and makes a user’s life easier with less passwords to remember. Thus, SSO is a vital technology for an organisation to consider as part of an overall Identity Management system. This white paper explores the main techniques used to achieve SSO and then considers the merits of different implementation approaches.

    • October 1999

    Computer security is an increasing problem for today’s businesses.   • TSB Feasibility Studies for Digital Services: Providing trust and control over sensitive data while using cloud services

    • May 2011 to September 2011
    Team Members: Colin Robbins

    Cloud technologies and services are becoming increasingly available and are attractive propositions for public and private organisations due to the potential for reduced cost as a result of economies of scale from sharing resources. However, the adoption of cloud services implies the passing of trust and responsibility for the protection of organisational data to the cloud service provider. All organisations have sensitive information which they will not wish to have stored within a cloud service. This project developed a guarding service which can be deployed within an organisations boundardy to monitor and guard against sensitive information identified by the organisatoin passing from its control. This additional level of control and trust in what information be shared with cloud service providers will provide the necessary confidance to allow organisations to sucessful adopt cloud services.
