Greg Barrett's Summary

Professional journalist for newspapers and wire for twenty years covering beats and stories that ranged from sports, religion, education, medicine and local government to war, Washington, Wall Street and foreign affairs.

Author of two social-justice non-fiction books, The Gospel of Father Joe: Revolutions & Revelations in the Slums of Bangkok (Wiley, 2008), winner of a Nautilus Book Awards Silver Medal for conscious media, journalism and investigative reporting; and The Gospel of Rutba: War, Peace and the Good Samaritan Story in Iraq (Orbis Books, June 2012; paperback, March 2013), the recipient of a 2013 Catholic Press Book Awards.

Specialties: Features. Investigations. Narrative Nonfiction.

Greg Barrett's Publications

  • The Gospel of Rutba: War, Peace, and the Good Samaritan Story in Iraq

    • Orbis Books
    • June 1, 2012
    Authors: Greg Barrett

    In 2003, three U.S. Christian peacemakers weathered the first horrifying days of Shock and Awe in Baghdad only to be nearly killed in a car accident as they rescued by Iraqi Muslims who took them to a clinic in the bombed-out town of Rutba, where they received protection and care. In sending the Americas on their way, their hosts had only one request: God and tell the world of Rutba. In fulfillment of that pledge, the peacemakers returned to Rutba in 2010 to thank the doctors and all who saved them and to contribute to an ongoing process of peace, friendship, and reconciliation.

  • The Gospel of Father Joe: Revolutions & Revelations in the Slums of Bangkok

    • Wiley Books
    • April 1, 2008
    Authors: Greg Barrett

    Three decades ago in a cordoned-off corner of the developing world an angry Catholic priest armed only with pencil, paper, and crayons, declared a revolution. From a shanty school shared with Buddhists and Muslims in Bangkok's squatter slums, Father Joe Maier began his advance on abject poverty. Today, his Human Development Foundation and Mercy Centre charity is responsible for thirty-two preschools that have taught more than twenty thousand children how to read and write. Despite the crippling neglect found in impoverishment, he is raising international scholars and injecting a sense of purpose into shantytowns and squatter camps that used to have neither.
