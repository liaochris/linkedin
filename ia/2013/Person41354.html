  • Neurochemical, Anatomical and Genetic Etiology of Panic Disorder and a Review of Current Available Pharmaceutical Treatments

    • January 2012 to Present
    Team Members: Emi Ying Wang

    Various biological theories attempt to address the etiology of panic disorder. This paper
    will attempt to provide an in-depth analysis on possible neurochemical, anatomical and genetic
    factors contributing to panic disorder. Neurochemical theories suspect: a faulty autonomic
    nervous system (ANS) (Wilkinson et al., 1998), irregulation of cholecystokinin (CCK) (Jerabek
    et al, 1999), decreased gamma-Aminobutyric acid (GABA) levels (Ham et al, 2006),
    dysregulation of Serotonin (5-HT) system (Maron et al, 2004), and sensitivity to other substances
    (Siegel et al, 1999). Examples of anatomical factors include: decreased regional gray mater
    volume (Lai, C.H., 2011) and increased brainstem volume (Tuescher et al, 2010). Genetic
    factors are observed in its heritability (Hettema et al, 2001) and specific genes such as PDE4B
    which also contributes to Panic Disorder (Otowa et al, 2010).

  • Maslow's Self-Actualization in Relation to Lao Tzu's Tao Te Ching

    • June 2011 to Present
    Team Members: Emi Ying Wang

    This theory is named ‘self-actualization’. It proposes that everyone has the potential to be the best they can ever be; or be self-actualized; however it is a rare phenomenon. It can only be achieved by: obtaining freedom from pathological thought and illness; progression through the hierarchy of needs; meaningful embodiment of Maslow’s B-values; and the development to one’s full potential (Maslow, 1998).
    An understanding of this concept can be clearly represented in the following statement, “what a
    man can be, he must be. This need we may call self-actualization” (Maslow, 1943, p. 382).
    Sharing similarities with Lao Tzu’s Taoism, Maslow’s theory transcends culture and
    demonstrates excellent versatility; largely benefitting therapists to gain a diverse view of the
    development of human potential.
