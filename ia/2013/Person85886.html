Marie Duggan's Summary

* I have been blessed with 6 beautiful children. My third son was diagnosed severely autistic and mentally retarded when he was 18 months old.

*At that time there were no resources available to me, no books, no Googling the www, no family support groups and no other mom that I knew that had a child like mine. I was completely isolated

*I refused to accept what all the "professionals" had told me "you can love him and keep him at home until you can no longer manage him and then you can institutionalize him. He will never learn...

*Jump ahead I have learned a lot about autism on this fierce journey. The mantra that runs through my head 24/7 is "HOPE"

*I am thrilled to be able to share with others what I have learned. My son is continent, can tie his shoes, is a happy big guy with a smile that brings joy to your face. By focusing in on his visual channel, symbols, icons, drawings digital images, his behaviors decreased dramatically. He had a way to communicate. Speech is power, I give mom a symbol she gives me what I want, jumps through hoops, hugs and tickles me...I don't have to scream, kick, bite, punch and tantrum because she doesn't understand me.

*I created visual routines, schedules, transitions, behavioral rules and began programming these supports into his computer. His siblings recorded voice was behind every image. He would create a visual sentence, tap a button and his siblings voice would speak for him acting as his expressive speech. At the age of 12 my son had emerging speech!!!

*As the technology started evolving at a rapid pace I decided to start Technology For Autism Now, Inc. 501c3. TAN's suite of autism apps and web based platform will be life changing for children/people with autism. If you are interested in helping TAN - contact me! These children don't have time to wait, the new statistics are 1in88 :(

"Improving the lives of children with autism through innovative technology solutions."

Executive Director/Founder

Technology For Autism Now, Inc

December 2009 – Present (4 years 1 month) Greater Boston Area

Founded by Marie Duggan – a mother of a child severely affected with autism, and now internationally recognized advocate for all children on the autism spectrum – Technology for Autism Now is a 501(c)(3) non-profit organization seeks to increase the availability, quality and use of assistive technology communications solutions for the autism community.
TAN is on a mission to help children with autism have a voice and to understand the world they live in so that they can be active participants in their communities and lead into functional young adults and beyond. As Marie Duggan noted after recounting the struggles of advocating for her own child, “The most profound words ever spoken to me by a professional were, ‘Your son has the goods, but we need to provide him with the tools.’ If we keep that in mind. I know we can move mountains for these kids.”

To that end, TAN aims to address the many unmet service needs that children face today with a goal of helping challenged children lead more functional lives in their homes, schools and communities. Equally important, TAN’s mission includes providing critical support & tools for parents, teachers and caregivers who are often poorly equipped to deal with the sometimes debilitating demands that come with raising a child with autism.
TAN Digital Toolset: Providing Practical Point-of-Need Apps
TAN plans to develop applications and software solutions for various mobile and in-home devices, such as iPods, iPads and personal computers, as well as a robust web portal (“online digital toolkit”) to share best practices and practical, easy-to-use tools for families, teachers, professionals and caregivers dealing with children and autism.

Family Technology Liaison

Children's Hospital Boston

Nonprofit; 5001-10,000 employees; Hospital & Health Care industry

September 2004 – November 2009 (5 years 3 months)

I formed a group of parents all of which had varying degrees of children/young adults with autism. We met monthly at the hospital. It was very highly received. I shared my experiences with my child and resources I knew of that many didn't as their children were younger, they shared stories, some happy some sad but it was a safe place for parents to feel like they could speak without being judged. Some were quiet and just listened but soon we became a nice safe circle of friends. We talked about everything! We had sandwiches and desserts, went way past our mtg times and were asked to leave at one mtg so they could lock the doors! Autism is my passion and my experiences with my son, the good the bad and the very bad seemed to be okay. I wanted other parents to know that there were other kiddos out there doing the same types of things. My son had emerging speech at the age of 12! I could offer HOPE! We also talked a lot about communication, behaviors and you name it... as well as assistive technology with our non verbal kids and the many ways we could address that from low tech all the way up to high tech. It was devastating to me to tell the members of the group that we had to stop, it was out of my control.

With my non profit we will be supporting parents who need each other and those who often deal with the debilitating demands of raising a child on the spectrum. I was very saddened the group stopped. It was hope, a little respite, a laugh, a cry, a bite to eat and go home with some new info, It was great for networking. I felt really good that in some small way I helped a mom or dad one nite a month.
