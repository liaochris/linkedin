Nick Brooks' Summary

I'm an independent consultant specialising in climate change and international development, and Director of Garama 3C Ltd (www.garama.co.uk), a climate change consultancy company. My work encompasses adaptation, climate change mainstreaming, climate risk assessment, evaluation and tracking adaptation, and climate change training for development professionals (Garama offers training in adaptation and mainstreaming, and other climate change topics). I have a special interest in climate change and Africa.

My recent clients include UNDP, the African Development Bank, UK DFID, the European Commission, IIED, the World Bank, the OECD and Tractebel Engineering. I also work closely with the School of International Development at the University of East Anglia, delivering training and other consultancy services.

In addition to my consultancy work, I also conduct research into past climatic changes, their impacts on human societies, and adaptation to severe and abrupt climatic and environmental change. This work involves collaboration with colleagues from a wide range of fields, including palaeoclimatology, geomorphology, archaeology and anthropology. Since 2002 I have been Co-Director of the Western Sahara Project, under which geoarchaeological fieldwork is conducted in the disputed, non-self governing territory of Western Sahara. Previously I participated in similar work in southwestern Libya, on the Fezzan Project.

Specialties: Climate change and international development, climate change mainstreaming/integration, adaptation, climate risk assessment, adaptation evaluation, climate change training for development professionals, climate change and Africa, past climates of the Sahara, past climate change and adaptation.

For more information see our website at www.garama.co.uk.

Director

Western Sahara Project

October 2002 – Present (11 years 2 months) Western Sahara

The Western Sahara Project is a field-based project in the disputed non-self governing territory of Western Sahara, the aim of which is to understand links between past environmental and cultural changes through archaeological and palaeo-environmental research. Fieldwork takes place in the areas of Western Sahara controlled by the Polisario independence movement, ther remainder of the territory being occupied by Morocco. The project is a non-profit undertaking funded by research grants, private sponsorship, and the participation of paying volunteers. Travel to the field is via Algeria and Mauritania, depending on the study area.
