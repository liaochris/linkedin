Master's Thesis

Rethinking Downtown Highways

October 2009 – December 2010 (1 year 3 months) Georgia Institute of Technology

Many of the mid-century freeways, once championed by planners as tools for urban renewal, have created swaths of blight through city neighborhoods. Their negative impacts on the larger urban framework require new ideas for healthier alternatives to aid in preserving and building sustainable cities. Urban design and transportation planning have an emerging new set of values. Cities have begun to understand that connectivity is not served by highways but by urban street networks. An important role for urban design is to shape the way interactions are made to benefit citizens, urban spaces, and the economy.

The selected case studies in this thesis reflect a diversity of approaches – suggesting no single strategy exists for addressing downtown highway issues. This presents the fact that multiple alternatives must be considered in every situation because each approach varies in costs and opportunities. A typology of highway alternations derived from the case studies includes seven different techniques: burying, demolishing, taming, capping or bridging, elevating, retaining, and relocating. The final chapter applies the conclusions from the case studies to the Downtown Connector– Interstate 75/85– in Downtown Atlanta, Georgia.
