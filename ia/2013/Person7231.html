Andrew Stopford, Ph.D.'s Summary

I am an Instrumentation Specialist with over 10 years of research experience gained in both academic and industrial laboratories in Europe and North America. I have contributed to fundamental, instrument development, petroleomics, proteomics, metabolomics and structural biology research programs and have studied many different compound classes, ranging from VOCs to very large multimeric protein complexes. I am skilled in the operation, maintenance, troubleshooting and repair of a broad range of analytical instrumentation, and would be interested in applying this knowledge to any exciting, unusual or novel research projects which address challenging scientific problems.

Specialties: Analytical Chemistry, Fundamental Studies, Instrument Development & Modification, Petroleomics, Proteomics, Mass Spectrometry, Metabolomics and Structural Biology.

Andrew Stopford, Ph.D.'s Experience

Post-doctoral Research Fellow: Instrumentation

University of Calgary

Educational Institution; 1001-5000 employees; Higher Education industry

April 2011 – Present (2 years 4 months)

I use a variety of analytical techniques to characterise extremely complex fossil fuel-derived samples with the ultimate aim of predicting the bulk physicochemical properties of the fuel source. I am also responsible for the development of instrumentation that partitions analytes between gas-charged oil and brine over a range of temperature and pressure conditions. Data from this device will be used to model the dispersion of oil from the 2010 Deep Water Horizon event in the Gulf of Mexico as well as providing the initial conditions needed to predict the placement of remediative actions should a marine oil spill reoccur.

Lead Scientist: Metabolomic Mass Spectrometry

University of Calgary

Educational Institution; 1001-5000 employees; Higher Education industry

March 2010 – December 2010 (10 months)

I was employed as a Lead Scientist in Metabolomic Mass Spectrometry at the University of Calgary where I managed and ran a mass spectrometry facility. My work supported the analytical needs of both a nationwide metabolomic research project funded by Genome Canada and the local activities of multiple research groups at the University of Calgary. I was responsible for the metabolic profiling of 75 different plant species as part of a coordinated effort to delineate the biosynthetic pathways of medically significant natural products. Metabolomic profiling data, in conjunction with high-throughput gene sequencing data, will inform the development of bioreactors that can synthesise high value natural products in a regulated environment.

Post-doctoral Scholar: Structural Biology

McGill University

Educational Institution; 10,001+ employees; Higher Education industry

January 2009 – January 2010 (1 year 1 month)

I was responsible for the analysis of the structural defect resulting from a F508 deletion mutation in the cystic fibrosis transmembrane conductance regulator (CFTR) protein. Data obtained using proteolytic digestion, isotopic labelling, thermodynamic analysis and mass spectrometry was combined with data from molecular biological studies in an effort to understand the influence of this structural defect at the molecular, metabolic and cellular levels. This information will be used to inform the development of therapeutics for the treatment of Cystic Fibrosis.

Senior Mass Spectrometrist: Contract Research

LGC Ltd.

Privately Held; 1001-5000 employees; Biotechnology industry

May 2008 – January 2009 (9 months)

I was responsible for the proteomic analysis of extracellular domains associated with cancer therapeutics expressed in cell-based vaccines along with investigations into the relationship between proteolytic digestion and cellular integrity. The aim of the project was to identify cell surface stress biomarkers that would allow the industrial scale-up of commercial cancer vaccine production. I retained a continuing consultancy role for several months following my departure from LGC Ltd.

Post-graduate Research Student: Physical Chemistry

University of Edinburgh

Educational Institution; 5001-10,000 employees; Research industry

October 2003 – November 2008 (5 years 2 months)

My post-graduate research examined the influence of solution composition, sequence truncation, point mutation and functional group substitution on the gas phase structural properties of Melittin, beta-Defensin derivatives, Tryptophan Cage and Phenolic Oxime Copper extractants. I designed and developed several analytical methods/technologies including a gas inlet system for gas phase ion/molecule reactions within a quadrupole ion trap and a series of energy resolved experimental measurement techniques. This work was performed using Q, QIT, Q-TOF and FT-ICR mass spectrometers and was supplemented by extensive electrostatic modelling using both SIMION 3D and CPO3D.

Research Assistant: Tandem Mass Spectrometry

University of Edinburgh

Educational Institution; 5001-10,000 employees; Research industry

June 2003 – October 2003 (5 months)

I was responsible for the optimisation of peptide and protein ECD fragmentation efficiency using 3 and 9.4 Tesla FT-ICR mass spectrometers. Retarding field analyses were used to characterise the kinetic energy distribution of electrons emitted from filament, high surface area and hollow dispenser cathodes. This information was used to optimise the kinetic energy difference between electrons within the electron beam and ions contained within the Penning trap and led to a significant improvement in analyte fragmentation efficiency.

Research Assistant: Metabolomic Mass Spectrometry

University of Stirling

Educational Institution; 1001-5000 employees; Higher Education industry

June 2001 – June 2003 (2 years 1 month)

I was responsible for the headspace SPE and SPME GC/MS analysis of floral scent emitted from Bluebell, Freesia, Sweetpea and Snapdragon species. Correlations were drawn between scent metabolite profiles and histological studies following visualisation of glandular trichomes using light and scanning electron microscopy. The main components of floral scent were identified as terpenes, terpenoids and ionones and emission was localised to specific structures within the flower. The influence of ethylene, 1-methylcyclopropene, silver thiosulphate and sucrose upon scent emission was also examined.
