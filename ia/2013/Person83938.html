  • Project Figo

    • June 2013 to Present
    Team Members: Macharia Waruingi, MD, DHA, Susan Peiffer, Dr.Janine White Cummings,DHA,RN,MBA/MHM,BA,BS,CLNC, Dr. Tina Nixon, Genifer Johnson DHA MSN RN, Dean Armitage, DHA, MBA, OPA-C, LSA, ATC/LAT, John R. Harton, Shelia Brewer, Jonathan Mwangi, Carol Enuenwosu-Aki, Tolu Ademodi, segun agunbiade, Fredrick Oluga

    The purpose of Figo is to create a network of franchised world-class renal replacement therapy centers (RRT) centers in Kenya. In this project we will refer to RRT centers as dialysis centers. Kenya is a rapidly growing nation located in East Africa. The general problem is that millions of people with kidney disease die each year in Kenya because of lack of appropriate treatment.

    In general, health service for kidney patients are not available in the nation. People have to travel many miles to seek treatment that is hardly available in large medical centers. Kidney patients and healthcare consumers in general are willing to pay for better care, of high value with improved supply of drugs, better technical quality, better maintained health facilities, and shorter waiting times.

    The specific problem is the lack of systematic effort to create an organization that can provide comprehensive kidney care in the country. Such an organization can promote prevention of occurrence of kidney diseases and provide health services of the best quality possible, at the lowest cost, affording the greatest value to consumers of care.

  • Emergecing

    • Ustawi International, Inc.
    • October 12, 2010
    Authors: Macharia Waruingi, MD, DHA

    This book is an attempt to explain how we can be effective in our work in global health. I used the emerging grounded theory design to investigate the paradoxical increase in the incidence of disease and poverty that exist at a time when there is increasing investment for fighting the diseases and poverty. I learned that failure to acknowledge tacit knowledge within local communities in developing countries leads to ultimate failure of many global health projects, creating a relevance paradox.

    We can overcome these problems in global health, and indeed other social problems by developing a skill for emergencing the more that people can know, but cannot tell. Emergencing is a theoretical model of knowledge emergence for effectiveness in our actions. Although the substantive area of study was global health, emergencing as a process of creating, and innovating, applies to all types of settings. Emergencing helps us to go beyond thinking outside the box, to thinking where there is no box.

  • Blue Wall of Silence: Perceptions of the Influence of Training on Law Enforcement Suicide

    • Lulu
    • March 2010
    Authors: Macharia Waruingi, MD, DHA, Dr. Olivia Johnson

    Law enforcement is a dangerous profession but the truth remains more officers kill themselves than are killed in the line-of-duty. A failure to acknowledge this silent killer causes valuable governmental funding to be lost in the form of training and education, and numerous officers to take their own lives before help is available. Study results hope to produce a mindset in which shame and anger no longer produce a shield for this deadly topic, but rather, a platform to begin feeling comfortable talking about the issues they face, without seeing suicide as a viable option.

Macharia Waruingi, MD, DHA's Summary

I am trained in medicine and health administration. I hold doctorate degrees in both fields. My skills derive from training, and work in higher education, health administration, and medicine.

I currently facilitate doctoral courses to health care leaders attending the School of Advanced Studies (SAS). I facilitate two types of courses: (a) regular doctoral course work in the Doctor of Health Administration program, and (b) doctoral dissertation seminars and dissertation-mentoring program.

In November 2008, the SAS approved me to facilitate nine doctoral courses to doctoral students enrolled in the Doctor of Health Administration program. I have taught many doctoral learners diverse subjects including healthcare economics, administration of complex healthcare systems, executive information systems, managing resources in healthcare organizations, to name just a few.

In February 2009, I fulfilled the requirement for doctoral dissertation mentoring embodied in the doctoral mentor training. Upon completion of this training, I acquired the certificate of a doctoral dissertation mentor. I gained approval to teach doctoral seminar courses. I have chaired several dissertation committees as a mentor. I have also served several dissertation committees as a member. Serving as a dissertation mentor affords me much opportunity to practice transformational leadership, master the qualitative and quantitative approaches to research and comprehend the ARB/IRB processes.

I mentor doctoral research to doctoral learners across all disciplines in the School of Advanced Studies including learners in Doctor of Health Administration, Doctor of Business Administration, Doctor of Management, Doctor of Management in Organizational Behavior, and Doctor of Education programs. The fundamental research process is the same in all disciplines of doctoral studies; however, the depth and breadth of knowledge differs substantially across the doctoral disciplines.

Specialties

I have advised many health care leaders in strategy and operations, as a consultant and mentor across the world. I deliver health administration mentorship and training through the Global Health Care Systems Research Institute (GHCS RI). Clients are leaders in diverse health care organizations around the world.
