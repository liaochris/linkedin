Albert S. Richardson's Summary

Albert S. Richardson, is an expert in the fields of vibration, galloping, structural loading and aerodynamics. Albert is the President and Founder of AR Products, LLC • Research Consulting Associates, research and development/manufacturer of products related to vibration and galloping motions control of overhead power lines and guy cables of TV/RADIO broadcast towers.

Other qualifications include manufacturing of patented vibration control devices, writing and presentation of technical papers for IEEE, ASME, ASCE, CIGRE, and other professional organizations. Mr. Richardson is a life member of IEEE and ASME, and a member of CIGRE. He has published more than 80 papers, and has been issued more than 16 patents, three patents pending, in the US and other countries.

Products include twenty models of vibration control devices, and seven models of galloping control devices. Over 50,000 devices sold. Value of installed products over $25 million. Value of protected equipment exceeds $5 billion.

Technology Utilization and Collision Avoidance System for Commercial Aircraft

NASA Electronics Research Center

Government Agency; 51-200 employees; Government Administration industry

January 1964 – December 1970 (7 years) Cambridge, MA

I joined NASA in Cambridge as a Consultant in 1964, and after one year I was hired full-time. For the first two years I was engaged in " Technology Utilization", which was to show how NASA technology was converted to commercial use. Then I became involved with the development of a Collision Avoidance System for Commercial aircraft. I became a team member of the study group and traveled to meetings in different cities. After a year of that, I joined with Dr. Charlie Leigh, a brilliant physicist, who was a pilot who had a concept for a pilot warning indicator, or PWI. It would detect the strobe lights from other aircraft and display to the pilot the azimuth from his plane the direction from which the strobe signal was coming. I spent the rest of my career at NASA on this project until the NASA center was closed by Nixon in 1970. (Revenge for the Kennedy dynasty).

Military Service | World War II | Torpedoman 3/C

US Navy

Government Agency; 10,001+ employees; Military industry

March 1943 – March 1946 (3 years 1 month) Boston, South Carolina, Casablanca

I was on the DD 155, USS COLE (not the one that was bombed in Yemen). In World War II the ship received three battle stars plus the Presidential Unit Citation for its action in the invasion of North Africa on November 8, 1942. It also was involved in the invasion of Sicily and Italy.

I joined the ship in Charleston, SC in November, 1943, and went on convoy to Casablanca where I saw the battleship Jean Bart sunk in the mud on its side, with its deck guns pointing to the sky. The ship that sank the Jean Bart (Vichey French Battle Ship), confiscated by the German's, was none other than the USS Massachusetts. It lay back twelve miles away and fired from its 14in. and 16in. guns. It literally blew it out of the water with over 100 shells fired. The sinking occurred during the invasion of North Africa which was commanded by ol' blood and guts himself, General George S. Patton. The invasion was a great success. Even though I was not involved in it I could wear the Presidential Unit Citation ribbon because I was on the ship. My "battle action" consisted of manning the depth charge rack on the fantail while we chased Nazi submarines while on convoy duty. Going over and coming back we dropped over 100 depth charges. I never saw so much as an oil slick for our efforts.

I returned from Casablanca to Boston and learned that one enlisted man on the ship would be sent to the Navy's V-12 program to become an officer in the navy. I applied for it and was awarded that honor. I attended MIT IN 1944, during the war. When the war ended in 1945 I was discharged in March 1946 and immediately applied to go back to MIT, under the GI Bill. I graduated in October 1947 with a SB Aeronautical Engineering Degree. Later, when I was working at MIT in the Aeroelastic Research Lab, I obtained an SM Degree, also in Aeronautical Engineering. I was on the Dean's honor list six out of eight semesters as an undergraduate.

Albert S. Richardson's Projects

  • Consultant | American Electric Power Corporation

    • January 1960 to December 1960
    Team Members: Albert S. Richardson

    American Electric Power Corporation approached Mr. Richardson at MIT when he was the Executive Officer of the Aero-Elastic and Structures Research Laboratory. He was informed about a major problem that faced the utility industry, known as is Galloping Conductors. It was a condition that existed in winter months when ice formations on overhead transmission lines formed airfoils. In strong winds this caused the lines to move up and down creating flashover between phases and consequent power outages.

    Technical Approach: Mr. Richardson set up a research project at MIT consisting of (i) analysis, (ii) wind tunnel testing, (iii) model testing, (vi) field-testing on transmission lines. The project continued for three years under the sponsorship of the Edison Electric Institute. There were eight progress reports over the time period, three papers written and presented to the IEEE in 1963, and one paper presented by Mr. Richardson at the First International Conference on Buildings and Structures at Teddington, England.

  • Consultant | Metcalf and Eddy

    • January 1962 to December 1962
    Team Members: Albert S. Richardson

    RCA was approached by Metcalf and Eddy to help determine the correct wind loads on new over-horizon radar to be built in Thule, Greenland. The radar reflector was a structure that measured approximately 100ft. height by 300ft. breadth. It had a back-up structure that was a wire screen mesh for reflecting the radar signal from the antenna and from the target. It was to be used as the early warning system for missile targets from the east. Another similar system was to be installed in Alaska.

    Technical Approach: A series of wind tunnel tests were conducted on a scale model of the structure at MIT. The measurements included 250 pressure measurements on the surface of the model, which assumed to be iced. From these measurements, made at incremental angles of yaw, calculations were made for the overturning moments and forces on the foundations. These data allowed Metcalf and Eddy to design the foundations and the structures.

  • Consultant | Hydro Quebec Research

    • January 1972 to December 1972
    Team Members: Albert S. Richardson

    Research Consulting Associates (RCA) was approached by Hydro Quebec Research to study a new transmission line structure comprised of two angled pylons guyed to the ground on one side and a system of catenary cables between the two pylons.

    Technical Approach: A scale model of the proposed structure was designed and fabricated. It was transported to the laboratory outside Montreal, Quebec, and set up to simulate several spans of the three-phase line. Strain gages were provided on the model to measure dynamic loading of the structure due to broken conductor and other types of loading.

  • Consultant | Madison Gas and Electric Company

    • January 1975 to December 1975
    Team Members: Albert S. Richardson

    Madison Gas and Electric Company approached Research Consulting Associates (RCA) to become an expert witness in a lawsuit against several defendants deemed to be responsible for the collapse of a new 345kV transmission in Wisconsin. The line stretched from the city of Madison to Fond du Lac, a distance of 65 Miles. The collapse occurred at night during a windstorm of 75MPH from the North.

    Technical Approach: A study was undertaken that included the weather conditions along the line at the time of the failure. It also included a study of broken Stockbridge dampers found along the path of the transmission line. The defendants had previously stated that the cause of the disaster was Galloping Conductors. RCA hired two additional experts, (i) a former US Government Meteorologist, who studied the weather reports to find out if there could be any ice anywhere along the transmission line. He found no possibility of ice. (ii) An MIT professor who was a recognized expert in field of fatigue of metals. He found that the failures of the Stockbridge dampers could not have been caused by Galloping Conductors. The RCA report concluded that the failure could not have been caused by galloping. The defendants settled the case.

  • Consultant | Superior Continental, Corp.

    • January 1979 to December 1979
    Team Members: Albert S. Richardson

    Research Consulting Associates (RCA) was approached by Superior Continental, Corp., a manufacturer of communication cable in North Carolina. They had been awarded a contract from the South African Railroad to install a new cable in South Africa running parallel to the railroad, along a North-South direction for a distance of 1,000km. The cable was under construction and a problem of Galloping was observed on the spans. There was no ice in the area, although the cable shape was a figure eight design, having a small diameter at the top and a larger diameter at the bottom. The overall dimensions of the cable were one x two inches.

    Technical Approach: RCA recommended wind tunnel tests be carried out at the Aeronautics Department at N.C. State University in Raleigh, N.C. The test results indicated that galloping instability could occur in high winds when the wind angle of attack was in a critical range. The remedy consisted of having the cable manufacturer fabricate ten feet long circular cylinders with a longitudinal slit. A special tool was also designed to install the cylinders in the field. Only 20% of the total distance of 1,000km needed to be covered by the cylinders. A paper was presented to IEEE in late 1979.

  • Consultant | US Department of Energy

    • January 1983 to December 1983
    Team Members: Albert S. Richardson

    Research Consulting Associates (RCA) made a proposal to the US Department of Energy concerning the WINDAMPER® device, and certain wind tunnel testing for lift and drag of a full-scale device at the MIT wind tunnel. The proposal came under the DOE item, Energy-Related Inventions.

    Technical Approach: RCA carried out the wind tunnel tests at MIT and made further analyses of the device, which were later presented in a paper before the IEEE, at Vancouver, BC. The testing and analysis made certain improvements possible in the WINDAMPER® device.

  • Consultant | Raytheon Company

    • January 1984 to December 1984
    Team Members: Albert S. Richardson

    Research Consulting Associates (RCA) was approached by Raytheon Company to study a problem that occurred in the Aleutian Islands with wind loading of a recently installed phased array system, over-the-horizon radar, sold to the US Navy to detect ballistic missiles from the west. It consisted of a row of two steel poles, 100ft. high and 22in. diameter. Altogether there are 750 such poles in the system. A winter storm caused almost half of the poles to fail.

    Technical Approach: An analysis was performed to obtain the first five natural frequencies and mode shapes of the pole structure. The analysis included calculations of bending stress at the base of the pole where it joins the base plate. The first and second modes developed high dynamic stress, exceeding the fatigue allowable stress of the material. Further analysis was performed to develop three possible concepts for damping the vibration. One of these was chosen by Raytheon and put into practice..

  • Consultant

    • January 1985 to December 1985
    Team Members: Albert S. Richardson

    Research Consulting Associates (RCA) was approached by a steel pole manufacturer and described what appeared to be a fatigue problem on steel poles delivered to a utility company in the state of Washington. Another consultant, hired by the utility had concluded that it was indeed a fatigue problem. The manufacturer asked that RCA review the report, and to make any other tests or analyses required.

    Technical approach: The pole design was an H-frame consisting of two identical steel-galvanized poles attached to a horizontal frame and cantilever arms at the top. Various heights were used along the line. The three phases of conductor attached to the arms and frame by vertical insulators. An initial analysis was made to obtain the first five natural frequencies of the structure and their mode shapes. These were then used in dynamic analyses of bending moments and stresses at the pole junctions.

    It was concluded that all stresses calculated were below the allowable fatigue stress of steel. This led to further examination. Since samples of material were cut from the pole joints at the weld line, some of these samples were obtained, and given to a consultant at MIT who is a recognized expert in fatigue problems. He looked at the samples and analyzed the chemical content of each sample on a spectrograph. He found a high fraction of zinc in the samples. He also found no signs of beach marks in the samples, which are characteristic of fatigue damage. This led to the conclusion that fatigue was not the problem. The cracks in the joints were filled by zinc at the time of the hot-dip galvanize process. This information was presented in a report to the client.

  • Consultant | New England Electric

    • January 1985 to December 1985
    Team Members: Albert S. Richardson

    A new transmission line was to be built in northern New England to connect to a sub-station in Quebec. The line was to be 400kV operating at DC voltage. The configuration of the bi-polar line was to be triple-bundled conductor and there was concern for dynamic loads and vibration. Research Consulting Associates (RCA) was hired by New England Electric to conduct dynamic tests on a scale model at MIT, and to conduct field tests of Aeolian vibration on a section of the line in North New Hampshire, already built, but not energized.

    Technical Approach: RCA and a technician spent two weeks in northern New Hampshire with a device known as Fast Fourier Transform Analyzer. This device was attached to a cable that in turn was attached to an accelerometer, mounted on the transmission conductor. Certain measurements were made of the wind-induced vibration of the bundled conductor. Other measurements were made of the motion caused by impacting the conductor. The test results were put into a final report for the client, and also published as a technical paper in Electric Power Systems Research, in Switzerland. In addition to the field research, a research project was set up at MIT for the testing of a scale model of the transmission line. Conditions tested included, (i) broken sub-conductor, (ii) broken insulator, (iii) load measurements for these and other tests. The results were submitted to the client in a technical report.

  • Consultant | CE Maguire

    • January 1992 to December 1992
    Team Members: Albert S. Richardson

    CE Maguire approached Research Consulting Associates (RCA) to study a new transmission line to be constructed in Key West, FL. The voltage was to be 138kV, and was to be on concrete poles and horizontal line posts. The objective was to help design the line with regard to vibration and to find out why certain insulator line posts had failed on an existing similar transmission line.

    Technical Approach: By using the same technician and the same FFT Analyzer tests were carried out over two weeks time, and an analysis of the test results were made. It was concluded that insufficient damping was the cause of the failures. RCA recommended that vibration dampers be installed on both the existing line and the new line. RCA helped the client select a supplier of the vibration dampers.

  • Consultant | US Department of Energy

    • January 1993 to December 1993
    Team Members: Albert S. Richardson

    Research Consulting Associates (RCA) made two proposals to US Department of Energy under their joint program with the US Department of Commerce, titled "Energy-Related Inventions Program". Prior to this time, RCA had successfully proposed a project dealing with the WINDAMPER® invention. Now, two new concepts were being introduced. The AR®TWISTER device was developed to control galloping conductors by inertia, where the WINDAMPER® controlled galloping conductors by using the wind. Under galloping conditions, the AR®TWISTER device would cause the conductor to twist, thereby altering the angle of attack of the wind against the conductor and the ice shape frozen to it.

    The second proposal was an invention called, a low-cost galloping indicator. For many years such a device had been desired to replace the costly method of electronic instruments, and /or video cameras. The galloping indicator was designed to trigger mechanically when galloping occurred. It was designed to be observed from the ground, or from the air after a galloping event.

    Technical approach: As a part of the original proposal, RCA had obtained an agreement from PSI Energy, in Indiana to test the two devices on a 230kV transmission line in their territory. The line was twenty miles long, and ran north- to-south in central Indiana. As a part of the project, PSI Energy purchased twenty miles worth of WINDAMPER® devices to be tested on the same line. All required devices were installed at PSI Energy expense. In the first winter after the installation, an ice storm swept through the area causing galloping of the transmission line. A lineman patrolling the transmission line was able to take video records of the installed devices. Both WINDAMPER® and AR®TWISTER devices did perform well in comparison to conductors not equipped by any anti-gallop devices. A final report was submitted to DOE and a paper was written and published in Electric Power Systems Research, in Switzerland.
