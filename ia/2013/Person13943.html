Brian Galvin's Summary

After enjoying two long careers in tech—first as a nuclear engineer in the Navy (where I ultimately was Chief Engineer of nuclear warship), and then as a many-hatted technology executive in the enterprise software space (where I ultimately was CTO at Genesys and Nuance, and CEO at Aria Solutions), I now independently practice patent law full time, representing solo inventors, startups, and growing technology companies around the world.

I am a US Patent Agent, rather than a Patent Attorney, so I practice in all geographies in matters before the US PTO, but not in the courts. I am also a serial inventor with 12 US patents and many pending applications.

I provide Patents++™ service: patent and IP strategy counseling, patent prosecution, patent litigation (in the PTO directly, and as a litigation consultant in cases before the Federal courts). While many of my clients are, not unsurprisingly, in the software field, I have also have clients in mechanical and electrical areas, as well as medical devices and biotech.

I have been actively leveraging recent developments in US patent law to provide my clients a fully-rounded approach to patents, in alignment with their business objectives. Specifically, I proactively monitor and interdict competitive patent portfolios, and I use newly-available techniques to rapidly build strong patent portfolios for my clients—and at a fraction of the cost they used to pay.

Specialties: Registered United States Patent Agent. Inventor (14 issued and two dozen pending patents). Experienced technology executive, particularly in energy and software/web industries. Litigation consultant specializing in patent litigation, particularly involving contact center and software patents. Litigator in US PTO cases.

Brian Galvin's Publications

  • XML-Based Multimodal Interaction Framework for Contact Center Applications

  • “The Myth of Technocracy”

    • Proceedings of the US Naval Institute
    • 1988
    Authors: Brian Galvin

    Won an Third Prize in US Naval Institute's Leadership Essay contest with article discussing certain myths about leaderships and "technocrats" that persistently pop up in the military (and elsewhere…). Basically, it is difficult to be a leader in a technical field if you don't have technical knowledge as well as the traditional elements of leadership.

  • “The Generalist Navy Must Go”

    • Proceedings of the US Naval Institute
    • 1993
    Authors: Brian Galvin

    Discussed the historical background of when the US Navy became the world's only major navy with generalist line officers (rather than Engineers, Weapons Officers, and Navigators, each with its own career track and expertise). It was actually a classic case of a special interest (Teddy Roosevelt when he was looking for something to achieve as Assistant Secretary of the Navy) getting a change made because no one else really cared (one Congressman actually said, "let's try it; we can always change back if it doesn't work"). Ten years later, several fatal accidents had occurred, but the Navy leadership says "We have come this far, we don't want to change again". Then, in the late 1990s, when this issue was discussed, people would say, "People obviously had a good reason to make it this way, and it's the Navy way, so no reason to consider changing it".

    Really a classic case of how changes really happen, and while not a total disaster in this case, we would be better off in an age of extreme technological complexity in naval ships to have officers who actually understand what it is they are commanding.

  • “Punching Combat’s Equations”

    • Proceedings of the US Naval Institute
    • 1989
    Authors: Brian Galvin

    Discussed the era from 1890 to 1914, when naval officers led a wave of innovation that ultimately prepared our Navy for modern sea warfare. Today, the Navy relies mostly on academics and defense contractors for innovation in how to respond to the world's very complex challenges, and my argument was that the feeling among many officers that they didn't have what it takes to provide solutions to current problems was wrong. I gave a simple example of a real-world naval tactical problem that, with a little bit of mathematical reasoning, could easily be solved by line officers.

    This is actually an important concept. When I deployed in the late 1980s into the areas near Iran, there were no "official" publications describing how to respond to an attack by the Silkworm missile (should it occur). There was no such publication, because the defense analyst community had not finished their work and gotten it approved by all the bureaucracy. Not wanting to go into harm's way without knowing how to respond to a known threat, I figured out an appropriate countermeasure using scattered data that was available, and we trained our crew.

    Never wait around for the authorities to tell you how to think…

  • “Numerical Studies of Standing Solitons in a Nonlinear Lattice”

    • Journal of the Acoustical Society of America
    • 1990
    Authors: Brian Galvin, Andrés Larraza, Bruce Denardo

    For my masters thesis, I created a numerical simulation of a particular kind of physical system that involved linearly coupled, nonlinear oscillators. We used this simulator to connect experimental work by Denardo and theoretical work by Larraza, and we discovered some very interesting new phenomena.
