Adam Pope's Summary

I believe in digital workplaces that are collaborative and build on collective knowledge; where digital libraries contain quality content that is easy to find and edit; where rich skills can be effectively transferred. A place that focuses us on the tasks that matter and brings people together to foster shared values.

Having managed the digital intranet environment for 80 communities of practice at Arup since 2007, I have developed my librarianship skills to encompass online community management, website design (CSS and HTML), and agile project management to readily enable a strong vision for effective digital workplaces.

Adam Pope's Publications

  • Telephone survey research for library managers

    • Library Management
    • 2003
    Authors: Adam Pope, Philip Calvert

    Purpose – To explore and evaluate the evidence about the value of using telephone surveys, especially in market research for a library.

    Design/methodology/approach – A critical summary and review of the literature in this field.

    Findings – This paper demonstrates that there are five major reasons for using this method of surveying customer preferences: response rates are higher; data can be analysed sooner; the cost of surveys is lower than alternative methods; calls can be monitored for quality; and the telephone offers the benefits of spontaneity.

    Practical implications – The paper contains extensive information on “best practice” of telephone surveying including designing the questionnaire and conducting the interview. There is information on getting a representative sample, plus coping with “no answers”, unlisted numbers and answering machines.

    Originality/value – Library managers with a commitment to using innovative techniques for market research will find telephone surveys offer a useful and cheap alternative to other survey methods. No previous paper has examined the use of, and the value of, telephone surveys in libraries.

  • The impacts of the Internet on public library use: An analysis of the consumer market for library and Internet services in New Zealand

    • New Zealand Libraries 49(8):271
    • March 2003
    Authors: Adam Pope

    In 2000 a survey of 3097 US adults investigated the market for Internet and library services. Results indicated a complementary relationship between the two information providers which would nevertheless require monitoring. A telephone survey of 829 randomly-selected over-18-year-olds was conducted in the Waikato between February and April 2002. It partially replicated the one conducted in the US, and revealed that 73% of Internet users in New Zealand in 2002 were users of libraries, that 21 services offered by libraries were patronised 10.7% more heavily on average by users of the Internet than by library users who did not use the Internet, and that Internet only use, and Internet and library use, rose as the demographic grew younger, whilst library-only users declined, as did users of neither. The exception of a decrease in Internet & library users amongst 18-30 year-olds indicated the very real prospect of library use diminishing over time. This finding was strengthened by cross-country comparison. Using an average weighted by library popularity, of Internet & library users who chose to use only one provider for quick reference needs, 72% only used the Internet. For work-related needs 81% used only the Internet, whilst for leisure 59% used only the library.
