Kirsten Broadfoot's Summary

I am particularly interested in the interprofessional ‘re-organizing’ of medicine and clinical interaction, especially as it is impacted by cultures, technology and relationship centered care, and the consequences of same on medical education and socialization. A dialogic scholar, I hold two values as paramount – dialogue and diversity, and am committed to the expression of diverse ways of being, thinking, speaking and acting and their potential to reshape our social, personal and professional worlds.

Much of my work is conducted under the rubric of “good work”, or the discovery and support of organizing, communicative and cultured practices that allow for health, humanity, creativity, innovation and justice to reside at work. In this work, I examine how practices come to be, their origins and the values that are inculcated therein and I ask how they can be different or whose voices, thoughts, values are suppressed. Diversity brings tolerance and compassion into what I teach and research. It is a core commitment as an ethnographic researcher.

My long fascination with technology – not just the tool, but how the tool and its accompanying values and philosophy engage us as individuals and groups - finds its expression in the CAPE where I work with simulation as a methodology and pedagogical practice to augment clinical contexts and educate future healthcare professionals. In this part of my work, the simulator is not just a mannequin. It is an object that orients working and interprofessional practice. It can be modified to meet its environment and as a result, it modifies those who respond to it. I find this work exciting as it opens up so many opportunities for extending and transforming clinical practice.
