Deirdre Danahar's Summary

Helping professionals make a life, not just a living and do their best work now.

I work with people with complex, busy lives who want to master the art of doing their best work, without the guilt, strain or emptiness of compromising for their careers.

My particular point of view centers on people knowing their signatures strengths the pluses and minus of these and then applying these along with a healthy doses of compassion, forthright exploration and action so you can do your best work without sacrificing your family, your health, or the interests and activities outside your profession that bring you joy.

In my 11 years as coach, I realized I almost always work with people who are looking for 4 highly specific kinds of change. And so I developed 4 coaching paths that are tailored to each client's professional, personal and work relationships and environments.

1. Expanding Possibility - for Successful Professionals: Best for people who have achieved a high level of professional success and now find their work uninspired and stale.

2. Entrepreneurs: Best for business owners who want to master the complexity of both owning the business and being the business.

3. Lead to Inspire: Best for organizational leaders who understand the importance of leading by example but are often overextended and concerned about the qualities they model.

4. Creating a Work-Life Flow: Best for successful mid-career professionals who love their work and feel engaged but are struggling to find a life that feels good to them because they need bring neglected parts of their lives back into the picture.

I earned a B.A. in Psychology from the University of Massachusetts at Amherst and an M.S.W. and M.P.H. from Tulane University. I am a member of the International Coach Federation with an ACC, the National Association of Social Workers, the American Public Health Association and am a Licensed Independent Clinical Social Worker.

Specialties

life coaching, executive coaching, strategic planning, sustainability planning, systems change, extensive HIV experience, development and delivery of training/technical assistance, contract oversight

  • Two Key Things Smart Busy Professionals Do to Get More of the Right Things Done

    • May 2012 to Present
    Team Members: Deirdre Danahar

  • Facilitating the Research to Practice Process: A Review of the Literature

    • January 2009 to September 2009
    Team Members: Deirdre Danahar, Carol Hays, DG Mawn, Lourdes Vazquez

    The wide ranging recognition across public health and human service fields of the need to implement evidence based practices has grown increasingly strong in recent years. Outcome measures in many fields now place particular emphasis on the extent to which evidence based programs, policies and practices are implemented with federal resources, Yet research about the utilization of evidence based practices in the public health, social services, and medical fields suggests that as few as 25 percent of public health and human service practitioners utilize evidence based approaches or rely on research evidence more generally to guide their decision making and practice (Nutley et al., 2008). A growing body of research literature spanning a variety of public health, social service and medical fields is stimulating a dialogue among researchers, funders and service professionals to identify methods for expediting the uptake of research and evidence based approaches into field practice. This research is
    establishing theories of how the science to practice bridge can best be built and operationalized to better encourage and strengthen the use of research and evidence based practices. This literature review considers the elements in the research to practice process and the characteristics and dynamics that work to support and inhibit the process. Policy makers as well as those who work as change agents and providers of technical support to promote utilization of research to inform and strengthen practice may find this review useful as they consider how further encourage evidence based practice within their systems.
