Extreme Blue MBA Intern

IBM

Public Company; 10,001+ employees; IBM; Information Technology and Services industry

June 2005 – August 2005 (3 months)

IBM Extreme Blue is the top internship program at IBM, where one MBA and three technical interns work independently in a startup-like environment to evaluate horizon 3 technologies. I was accepted onto a project sponsored by IBM’s Linux Technology Center.

Our team evaluated the technical and commercial feasibility of dynamic kernel-level tracing in Linux. We created a technique for dynamically inserting trace points into a running Linux kernel, with the goal of determining root causes of performance issues with function-level granularity.

Detailed responsibilities:
• Evaluate the potential market for and competitive impact of dynamic kernel-level tracing.
• Evangelize the project within IBM to ensure development is funded after the internship ended.

At the end of the program, we presented our results to IBM senior leadership. The proof of concept designed by our team received funding to be continued by a team of IBM engineers as an internal project.

Brian Warner's Publications

  • Understanding the Open Source Development Model

    • The Linux Foundation
    • November 2011
    Authors: Brian Warner, Ibrahim Haddad, Ph.D.

    This paper presents an overview of the open source development model. It discusses the typical progression from an idea to an implemented feature, and highlights some of the key characteristics of open source development.

  • Overview of the MeeGo Compliance Program

    • The Linux Foundation
    • June 2011
    Authors: Brian Warner, Ibrahim Haddad, Ph.D., Mats Wichmann

    While MeeGo is an open source project, all products that will include MeeGo branding must successfully demonstrate compliance with the MeeGo specification. This whitepaper provides an overview of the compliance process for products that ship with MeeGo.

  • Establishing an Open Source Software Strategy: Key Considerations and Tactical Recommendations

    • The Linux Foundation
    • November 2011
    Authors: Brian Warner, Ibrahim Haddad, Ph.D.

    In this article, we explore three basic strategies for organizations that utilize and integrate open source software in their products: consumer, contributor and leader.

  • Upstreaming: Strengthening Open Source Development - Process, Benefits and Practical Guidance

    • The Linux Foundation
    • January 2012
    Authors: Brian Warner, Ibrahim Haddad, Ph.D.

    Upstreaming is a term used to describe the process of contributing in-house source code modifications back to an open source project, with the goal of having them accepted and distributed in future project releases. This paper discusses the process of upstreaming, the benefits to all parties involved (companies, projects, open source ecosystem), and highlights some best practices to follow.

  • Open Sourcing Proprietary Technology Made Simple: A High Level Roadmap for Open Sourcing Your Code

    • The Linux Foundation
    • February 2012
    Authors: Brian Warner, Ibrahim Haddad, Ph.D.

    As corporate participation in open source grows, many companies are discovering the advantages of releasing formerly proprietary technologies as open source. This article introduces a basic process and checklist that may be followed when creating a new open source project from proprietary code, to ensure a successful release.

  • Managing Open Innovation Technologies - Chapter 3, "Organizational Participation in Open Innovation Communities."

    • Springer
    • December 2012
    Authors: Brian Warner, Matt Germonprez

    Open innovation increases the profit of companies and organizations via the input and the adoption of new ideas that are transformed into new processes, products, and services. Yet, how do we ensure that adopters of such innovations focus on relevant problems and use appropriate methods? How should we manage open innovation technologies? How can we exploit distributed knowledge and inventions? And how can we promote them successfully on the market?
