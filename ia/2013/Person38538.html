David Rotenstein's Summary

I specialize in consultation services to land use attorneys and developers working through historic preservation regulatory reviews, especially efforts to designate properties historic by local historic preservation commissions and historical societies.

Historical research, litigation research, land-use history, oral history, and historic preservation.

* Designed and completed complex research projects throughout the United States.

* Advanced problem solving skills.

* Effective oral, written, and electronic communications skills.

* Proven track record meeting budgets and schedules.

* Provided historical research services to the production company behind the PBS series, "The History Detectives." The three segments were on a nuclear attack early warning system; Amelia Earhart; and, Duke Ellington.

* Appointed by Montgomery County Executive in 2004 to the Historic Preservation Commission. Three years as a commissioner; two as vice-chair; and, one year as chairman.

* Appointed by the Montgomery County Planning Board in 2009 as a historic preservation expert to the Zoning Advisory Panel working on rewriting the county zoning code.

* Experience in local and federal public policy (regulation, historic preservation, land use).

* Published the results of research in peer-reviewed academic journals and popular media.

* Created a successful public history blog in 2010 that has been cited by mainstream media outlets and by national historic preservation organizations.

David Rotenstein's Publications

  • Separate and unequal: Preserving Jim Crow

    • History Sidebar / Like the Dew
    • July 8, 2013
    Authors: David Rotenstein

    Decatur’s Beacon Elementary and Trinity High schools were among the hundreds of equalization schools built in Georgia after World War II. They were constructed in 1955 and 1956 on the site where the city had maintained its African American school, the Herring Street School, since the early twentieth century. In early 2013, three years after receiving a $10,000 federal historic preservation grant that should have led to the property’s protection, the City of Decatur began demolishing parts of the two schools to build a new police headquarters and civic plaza.

  • Gentrification Hits Small Cities Too: Unsustainable Policies in Decatur, Georgia

    • Tikkun Daily
    • February 26, 2013
    Authors: David Rotenstein

    Decatur is a hipster haven of a little less than 20,000 residents six miles east of downtown Atlanta, Ga. Its downtown is a revitalization success story: brewpubs and trendy restaurants jockey for space among locally owned boutiques, coffee shops, and frozen yogurt joints. The city's Oakhurst neighborhood, however, is where super-gentrification is impacting aging African American homeowners. This article explores those impacts.

  • Tearing Down Buildings and People in a Small Southern City

    • Like the Dew
    • May 4, 2013
    Authors: David Rotenstein

    In 2012 several Decatur residents began a concerted effort to get me to stop writing about gentrification, teardowns, and class and racial bias in the city's Oakhurst neighborhood. Some of the folks turned to unlawful means, including cyberstalking. This article documents how we identified a Decatur mommy blogger as one of the cyberstalkers.
