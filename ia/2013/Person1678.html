Adam Gordon's Summary

I lead Executive Education (Open Enrollment, Management Advancement, and Leadership Development) at the Wits Business School, Johannesburg.
I have been in the tertiary and post-tertiary education industry since 2002, based in the UK and working around the world primarily as a designer and teacher of graduate-executive-level programmes in strategy & planning areas, and as an facilitator-entrepreneur of executive education offerings spanning the developed world and emerging markets.
In addition to this, I was a specialist analyst and facilitator in the field of industry & policy foresight, running workshops in executive forums and working with company/government leaders to render and implement innovative and robust responses to systemic change. Prior to this I was in a boutique futures consulting firm in Washington D.C., and prior to this I was a business journalist.
In addition to the usual journal articles, I have a book out: “Future Savvy: Quality in Foresight” (Amacom Press, NY). I also write "Management By Looking Ahead" at Forbes.com. (blogs.forbes.com/adamgordon/)

Adam Gordon's Publications

  • Future Savvy: Quality in Foresight

    • Amacom Press
    • January 15, 2009
    Authors: Adam Gordon

    There's no shortage of predictions available to organizations looking to anticipate and profit from future trends. Apparently helpful forecasts are ubiquitous in everyday communications such as newspapers and business magazines, and in specialized sources such as government and think-tank forecasts, consultant reports, and stock-market guides. These resources are crucial but they are also of very mixed quality. While everyone knows a future-focus is crucial for strategic vision and organizational readiness, what information from the endless sea of sources is valid? How do you know which predictions to take seriously, which to be wary of, and which to throw out entirely? Which ones do you let guide your decisions? Future Savvy provides a hands-on approach to judging predictive material of all types, including providing a battery of critical tests to apply to any forecast to assess its validity, and judge how to fit it into everyday management thinking.
