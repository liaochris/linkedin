Medical Imaging and Informatics Lab

June 2012 – Present (1 year 4 months) David Geffen School of Medicine at UCLA

My work at the UCLA Medical Imaging and Informatics Lab (MII) is currently focused on the transformation of clinical documentation on patients in screening or treatment for lung cancer at UCLA into a format accessible by patients. There are several key elements in this process. First, relevant information from the patient's full-text record must be extracted using natural language processing to identify key concepts and learning systems to determine relevance and context. This information must then be represented in a format accessible by patients by translating medical jargon into lay terms using a combination of concept matching and contextualization using online patient-oriented reference materials (just as those in the Mayo portal and similar sites). Finally, the information must be presented in a well-organized high security portal that patients can use comfortably and, if desired, share with family and friends.

Student Researcher

Winfree Lab

April 2009 – Present (4 years 6 months) California Institute of Technology

My research in the Winfree lab is focused on the development of tools to help design useful molecular systems out of nucleic acids. Nucleic acid systems are interesting because the programmability of nucleic acid sequences and the predictability of their interactions make them an excellent substrate for molecular-level computing. Molecular computing has many potential applications in medicine, chemistry, bioengineering, and many other fields. For example, a group at Caltech recently demonstrated a nucleic acid system that activates an apoptotic pathway in only cells that are from a specific cancer. My first project in the lab was the investigation of a possible extension to a previously developed nucleic acid system for the transduction and amplification of a nucleic acid signal in vitro. This project was my first major experience in experimental molecular biology, and I learned many techniques and best practices for experimental work.

My current project is the development of an automated verifier for nucleic acid systems. One major challenge in the design of these systems is that they grow in complexity very quickly. These errors can generally be caught experimentally, but at a high cost. By using techniques from computer science, potential problems can be identified at the design stage. The design and implementation of this verifier is substantively complete and a manuscript is in preparation. The verifier is complete and has been tested, and a paper is currently in preparation.

Student Researcher

Chandy Lab

September 2009 – April 2012 (2 years 8 months) California Institute of Technology

My research in the Chandy lab focused on the development of low-cost screening techniques for the detection of cardiac pathologies. We developed a low-cost stethoscope $30 and EKG $5 that can be recorded from on a cellphone or laptop, and worked with collaborators at UCI Medical School to collect data on the clinical effectiveness of these devices through field testing. We also started development an online database of auscultatory recordings of both normal and pathological heart sounds with our collaborators at UCI as well as physicians in India. This database can be used both for training of medical students as well as for the development of automated systems for classifying cardiac pathologies.

We also worked in parallel to develop an automated system to detect arrythmias and heart murmurs using either synchronized auscultatory and electrophysiological readings or auscultatory readings alone. This system is intended to run on a cellphone in order to allow it to be deployed in the developing world. The system can be used with little to no training so that individuals in areas not served by medical personnel can still receive some screening in order to make health decisions.

Student Researcher

Koch Lab

April 2008 – March 2009 (1 year) California Institute of Technology

My research in the Koch lab involved the computational investigation of a hypothesis about neuronal communications. We were interested in determining what effect the electrical fields that arise from neuronal transmembrane currents (‘ephaptic effects’) have on neighboring neurons. I first developed a simulation by adapting the existing neuronal simulation software to take into account the mechanism that we were investigating. I then worked to extend this software to allow parallel computation in order to reduce the computation time required from weeks to hours.

Concurrently, I developed software for the visualization of perturbations of the transmembrane voltage and membrane electric field over time in order to facilitate the analysis of our results from the simulations above. This software and the simulations were used subsequently for a paper published in the Journal of Neuroscience.
