Evan Rapoport's Experience

Product Manager

Google

Public Company; 10,001+ employees; GOOG; Internet industry

August 2011 – Present (2 years 2 months) Mountain View, CA

We live in a beautiful world with places that inspire us, from the wonders of national parks to the depths of the oceans to the dynamics of our biggest cities. Imagine a map with incredible photographs in all these places. Everywhere you ever dream of going, you can virtually explore before you go. You can take this map anywhere, and discover new places through photos shared by fellow travelers and residents.

Of course, I'm describing Google Maps, and you can do this all today. As a Product Manager on Google Maps, I help lead a number of efforts to create the most inspiring collection of geolocated photos possible to help people explore the world.

I currently work on:
Views, a new community that allows you to contribute 360 degree photo spheres to Maps
Photo Sphere, which enables Android users to add their panoramas to Maps (and G+)
Panoramio, a large, active photo community of very talented geo photographers
Street View, which has published over 5 million miles of panoramas in over 50 countries
Google Business Photos, which gives trusted photographers the ability to create Street View experiences for small and medium businesses

Application Specialist

Archinoetics

Privately Held; 11-50 employees; Research industry

March 2005 – July 2011 (6 years 5 months) Honolulu, HI

Designed, developed, and launched Internet applications that helped world-famous adventurers, National Geographic correspondents, extreme athletes, travelers, and research scientists share geolocated stories on a map.

Co-created K-12 science and technology programs that, through NOAA grants deployed GPS-tracked ocean drifter buoys to provide students and teachers with hands-on, project-based curricula for remotely monitoring the ocean and the environmental impact of marine debris.

Raised over $1M in competitive grant funding through the National Science Foundation, and worked on similar grants through DARPA, to develop software, hardware, and user studies for brain-computer interfaces (BCI) for assistive technologies.
