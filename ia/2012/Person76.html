Al Macintyre's Summary

I swim in a sea of multiple programming languages and tools that interact with each other. I am a pretty good swimmer both in this sea and in the real world.

In the real world, I can fall asleep floating in a swimming pool (being overweight might have something to do with that). I haven't found the time to do much real world swimming recently.

In the computer world I have managed to do some things that amazed some peers.
However, I am never satisfied with my performance, always striving to do better.

I have been working with BPCS since approx 1989.
Before that I worked with MAPICS since 1984.
Before that, it was home brew, with very little packaged software.

I first got into computer career when I flunked out of UC in the early 1960's. At the time it was punched cards, paper tape, tabulating equipment wiring boards, advancing into what was known at the time as mini-computers.

I first began to explore the world of micro computers in the early 1970's but there I never gained the expertise that I had in my professional work.

Specialties

I am fluent in several computer languages,somewhere between what are sometimes called legacy and bleeding edge. In recent years, most of my "programming" work has been SQL/400 embedded in RPG, and *OUTFILEs from query/400.

Computer Support

Global Wire Technologies

2001 – August 2006 (5 years)

While IBM has moved from AS/400 to iSeries to System i to other names, we have one of the last AS/400'ds ever made.

Continuing ERP support duties on IBM Midrange that had migrated from S/34 then S/36 then AS/400 (model 170), my job responsibilities evolved to be dominated by Computer Janitor and Data Mining and Accounting Guide.

As Computer Janitor, I locate and clean up Data Glitches, so my co-workers can focus on their jobs, with computer activities being largely transparent to them.

I love the word "glitch" which means "no fault errors", we can focus on fixing the data base, fixing processes that lead to errors, and avoid the destructive politics of finger pointing.

I called it Data Mining, my boss called it "Heavy Lifting", basically it was locating & extracting data into management-friendly structures, to help their analysis and decision-making. This included "Excel-Friendly programs" to compensate for differences in data handling IBM vs. Microsoft.

Accounting Guide is a mixture of Data Glitch Forensics and Data Flow Microscope. We have professionals in all manner of accounting and auditing, whose understanding of our ERP has its weak points. I help guide them to solve their data challenges.

Examples: How come this variance spikes different ways each month? Audit tooling really needed? Are we paying vendors for more than we really owe? New metrics to better manage production. Proving all sorts of negatives. Where did this data come from?

With any ERP, some updates must run when other users off the system, so I work swing shift, implementing growth in time consuming tasks managing data that co-workers will need first thing next morning.

Over time, the company got new top managers, new owners. In August 2006, the company was sold to Kauffman Engineering, HQ in Lebanon In, and once again company name got changed to become one of their divisions.

Computer Support

Central Industries of Indiana Inc.

1984 – 2000 (16 years)

Think of all the different kinds of tasks that might be expected of IT staff working on an IBM Midrange System that supports a manufacturing operation of maybe 100 workers, most of the factory workers, but over a score of clerical: Operations; Programming; Security; Performance; Administration; Help Desk. Well that's me, a Jack of all IT trades, Master of Some.

When I started, the company ERP was in midst of conversion from Home Brew to MAPICS I, a conversion that had been going since long before my arrival, and before completion, we switched to BPCS on System/36, which at the time was NOT Y2K compliant, like MAPICS I was. I darn near got fired for being rude & hostile about converting from a Y2K compliant to non-compliant system.

Then in 1998 the company spent a small fortune converting to allegedly Y2K compliant BPCS 405 CD version. I had predicted 10 years earlier, that the cost to accomplish this, had the potential to bankrupt the company. Fortunately the job got done for approx $ 1/4 million.

In 1999 I had 3 months to install several thousand patches to fix Y2K and other oversights in 405 CD, after which it really was Y2K compliant, except for a few bugs.

In 2001 new owners changed company name to Global Wire Technologies to better reflect the evolving nature of the business.

IBM Computer Staff

Willis Music Company

June 1966 – February 1984 (17 years 9 months)

I was originally hired as gopher of Computer Administrator, in an era of punched cards, paper tape, wiring boards of tabulating equipment.

I advanced into his position, did programming, troubleshooting, systems analysis, help desk.

I was responsible for integrity of millions of lines of code and data. My programs included data validation, and detection and repair of dirty data.

I learned a lot about conversions. I think the best were those that separated hardware and software.

In a hardware conversion, implement it over a weekend, where on Monday morning, the user has exactly the same interfaces and appearances as they had Friday nite.

In a software conversion, offer the user optional enhancements piecemeal, so they can continue to do their old job while learning the new features, at their own pace.

I left due to personal aggravation with repetition of internally created problems, and false accusations by senior management.
