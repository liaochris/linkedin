Lexia B. Krown's Summary

Over the last ten years, I have accumulated substantial experience in international trade regulations‚ particularly those relating to U.S. export controls, i.e., ITAR‚ EAR‚ FACR‚ FTR, and to import regulations of U.S. Customs, ATF (USMIL), EPA, NRC, FCC, FDA, etc. I have experience applying U.S. export and import laws and regulations to a wide range of business transactions.

My practice focuses on legal and enforcement issues arising under U.S. export controls‚ Customs regulations, trade and economic sanctions‚ antiboycott controls‚ the Foreign Corrupt Practices Act‚ trade agreements, supply chain management and security, and the like. My services, among others, include C-TPAT; valuation for import and export; classifications (HTSUS, CCL, USML, USMiL); TSA Certifications; export licensing; country of origin and CO Marking; Trade Agreements; internal independent investigations. My firm and I regularly provide compliance consulting service in addition to dealing with purely legal matters.

Lexia B. Krown's Publications

  • Clarity as the Last Resort? Why Federal Rule of Appellate Procedure 4 Should and Could Stipulate Which Judgments Are ‘Final’

    • The Ohio State Law Journal
    • December 2009
    Authors: Lexia B. Krown

    Rule 4 and FirsTier, which interpreted the rule, have not been able to standardize the circuits’ treatment of prematurity. As this article shows, the circuits have not changed their approach to finality. Some courts have held that a premature notice of appeal from a non-final order may ripen into a valid notice of appeal if a final judgment has been entered by the time the appeal is heard and the appellee suffers no prejudice. Other courts have strictly construed the statutory language and have held that FRAP 4(a)(2) and (b)(2) saves a premature notice of appeal only when a district court announces a decision that would be appealable if immediately followed by the entry of judgment. Some others would find jurisdiction where a premature notice of appeal is filed after a final judgment is announced but before it is entered. Yet, some courts have even interpreted FirsTier as a narrow exception to the pragmatic approach.

    At the same time, a number of precedents have formed into recognizable categories allowing for a partial solution. This article proposes addition to Rule 4; with that, it addresses a fair share of issues with premature appeals, some of which courts dismiss without a possibility of cure. It is a necessary warning to any practitioner to dot all i’s and cross all t’s in this context by re-filing the notice of appeal. The reality is such that when a court enters a real final judgment, a practitioner can easily miss the event or even deem refilling unnecessary by relying on Rule 4. While only a partial solution, the proposed addition to Rule 4 can assure protection of litigants’ rights of appeal in numerous circumstances and thus fewer headaches to lawyers and their clients, as well as to judges.

    Keywords: Prematurity of Appeals, Proposed Amendment to FRAP 4, Fatality, Final Judgment Rule, Relation Forward, Finality Doctrine, Pragmatic Approach to Finality, Technical Approach to Finality, FirsTier, Rule 4 of FRAP, History of the Final Judgment Rule

  • United States v. UPS: The Status of Remedies under the Customs Broker Penalty Statute

    • 19 Tulane Journal of International and Comparative Law 487 (ISSN 1069-4455)
    Authors: Lexia B. Krown, Kevin Williams

    Although an important question to the customs brokerage community, the U.S. Court of Appeals for the Federal Circuit did not reach it in United States v. UPS Customhouse Brokerage, Inc. The unanswered question was: what is the maximum penalty Customs can assess against a Customs broker for failing to exercise responsible supervision and control as required by the Broker Penalty Statute? With that, the goal of the article is two-fold: to provide the details concerning this controversy and to discuss whether the CIT reasonably if not correctly interpreted the Broker Penalty Statute when it agreed with Customs position.

    R. Kevin Williams & Lexia B. Krown, United States v. UPS: The Status of Remedies under the Customs Broker Penalty Statute, 19 Tul. J. Int'l & Comp. L. 487 (Spring 2011)
