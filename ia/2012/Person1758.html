Thomas Retterbush's Summary

After a long, hard walk on the wild side, I have changed from bad boy to good guy, I have embarked on a road of redemption.

Although diagnosed with bi-polar depression, as a recovering heroin addict, I have been clean for years as a successful entrepreneur, an investigator and activist of human and civil rights, the environment, hunger, poverty, disease and addiction, exposing conspiracies and cover-ups, while fighting corruption, censorship and government control.

As a recovering drug addict diagnosed with clinical depression, after founding the Disabled Veterans Association and AssEtEbooks.com, I became involved with several worthwhile causes such as A Real Plan To End Our Oil Addiction, Wounded Warriors, Stand Up for Veterans, End Violence Against Girls and Make Our Food Safe projects, not to mention the humanitarian blogs I have launched to inform, teach and help others, such as ActionActivist.com, Addicts Not Anonymous.com, CensorshipWatch.net, ConspiracyWatch.net, AlQaedawatch.com, Heath-be.com, as well as several others dedicated to good of mankind, doing what I can to help make the world a better place the best way I know how.

Founder and President

Disabled Veterans Association

July 2005 – Present (7 years)

As the son of a veteran and a veteran myself, having been "officially" homeless (I was staying with friends), as well as diagnosed bi-polar and addicted to drugs, I have felt the plight of the forgotten vet. No longer being able to stand by and watch yesterday's heroes receive substandard health care, inadequate social assistance and atrocious financial compensation I was compelled to do something. After failing my attempts to tackle the problems by means of the institutions already in place, I knew I would need to take matters into my own hands; thus the Disabled Veteran Association was born.

With almost no financial reserves of my own, and no government, corporate or charitable backing I was forced to take small, little steps; one at a time.

At first this is exactly what I did; help one vet in need at a time. Usually finding homeless, sick, alcoholic or drug addicted vets right of the street and doing whatever I could. More often then not, I could do little more than make a small financial, contribution, offer him some close, shelter, contacts and advice.

I am currently busy with construction of the Veterans Voice website, which will soon be found at at http://veteransvoice.info, scheduled to launch later on this year.

I am also working on the founding Veterans Anonymous, a 12 step program for vets, to serve as a fellowship, enabling members to help themselves by helping each other.
