Peggy Schoen’s Summary

(pschoen329@gmail.com) With 25 years of communications experience in several industries on two continents, I've learned that communications strategy is about changing behavior. My strength is that I can sit down with a team -- managers, marketers, researchers or engineers -- and help them to identify what they want to accomplish and then to zero in on what behaviors must be changed to get there.

Once behaviors are defined, I keep the creative team focused on three other "Bs" -- believability, brevity and beauty. Having worked with many multi-national and multi-cultural audiences, I always strive to keep messages truthful and simple, as well as to let great photos and illustrations replace words whenever possible.

Whether it's a web site, a blog, a publication, a podcast or a video -- I believe that communication vehicles are a lot like our friends' houses -- we're likely to spend more time in the ones that are clean, uncomplicated, comfortable and friendly.

  • Employee Communications Manager

    (Privately Held; 501-1000 employees; Telecommunications industry)

    April 2000 — October 2001 (1 year 7 months)

    (Washington, D.C.)
    In 2000, Intelsat faced the challenge of transitioning an international organization into a private U.S. company. Over the course of 18 months, the organization revamped its business strategy and financial structure; launched a new brand, mission & values; retrained managers in U.S. employment law compliance and goals-based performance management; and conducted a reduction in force.

  • Manager, Promotion & Sponsorship

    Furuset Ice Hockey Club

    (Sports industry)

    1989 — January 1991 (2 years)

    (Oslo, Norway)
    Of all the jobs I might have envisioned doing as a young journalism student, press and promotion for a Norwegian hockey team was definitely not one. I took the job because a friend needed my help and I was game for a challenge. As far as many of the club's members were concerned, I had four strikes from the start - I was young, I was a woman, I wasn't Norwegian and I knew next to nothing about ice hockey.

    But thanks to the support of the players, coaches, supporters and a couple of great mentors, I negotiated some of the club's largest sponsorship and television deals, and learned many valuable life lessons from my players, such as "today's bad press is tomorrow's fish wrapping." Several years later, when I was asked to do some consulting for the NHL on the Norwegian ice hockey market, I realized that striking out at the plate is a part of striking out of one's comfort zone.
