Robert Farrow’s Summary

I have been using a Model Driven Architecture (MDA) approach to software for 20 years. Automating code creation has always been a substantial part of my approach. I was using UML since its inception and I have recently become a user of the NetBeans approach of MDA using UML.

My 20+ years experience at writing software, has been about a commitment to clarity. Software quality is the result of clarity. MDA provides the ability to automate the details of code creation and that means much higher quality.

My favorite job is "the hard problem." One that has the additional challenge of having never been done before. Taking a research idea and figuring out how to organize it into a balanced solution; flexible, scalable and efficient.

If necessary, I can be the team leader and take the role of communication. I have had very high-profile positions where I was reporting to steering teams that had two Motorola VPs. Requirements gathering is a well developed skill and I love making sense of a users problem. But my best value is staying close to the code.

  • EDA Software Manager

    Texas Instruments

    (Public Company; 10,001 or more employees; TXN; Semiconductors industry)

    February 1996 — April 2004 (8 years 3 months)

    Returned to TI to manage a team tasked with creating a suite of tools to support a new design style called Dynamic Logic. Lead a small team of experts in several aspects of EDA to create a study and a plan for an entire flow.

    I left this group when an opportunity to design a new kind chip configuration capability. The approach was a declarative language that described the design blocks and produced a hierarchical netlist. This is like MDA for chip design.

This too is a form of MDA, for chip design.

  • Project Manager

    Motorola Semiconductor

    (Public Company; 10,001 or more employees; MOT; Semiconductors industry)

    July 1994 — January 1996 (1 year 7 months)

    Lead a small team of scheduling experts to create an implementation of a wafer fab scheduling tool. This was based on Motorola patented technology. The steering team for this project consisted of three Motorola VPs, including the one who was the patent holder.

    The project was done using Common Lisp on both the Mac and Unix platforms. We successfully implemented the technology into our initial target fab.     I was offered the job of leading the process improvement effort, but felt that the scheduling experts were better suited to that work. It would have been very much a non-software position.

  • Software Developer

    Texas Instruments

    (Public Company; 10,001 or more employees; TXN; Semiconductors industry)

    August 1988 — June 1994 (5 years 11 months)

    Joined TI to work on an integrated set of chip design (EDA) tools. Using object-oriented Lisp (Flavors) we developed a complete chip design tool suite. Initially I developed the timing analysis tool. Having no prior background in EE, I took all the requirements from a senior chip designer.

    After completing the Timing tool, I worked with a senior member of the team to over haul the tool integration component. I developed all the common components of the system. Based on a mechanism I created for lazy data creation (to manage space) I integrated an OODB, which was being developed by TI R&D.

    I moved to TI R&D after the integrated tool set was scrapped in favor of commercial EDA tools. There I developed mpeg4 algorithms, neural network and fuzzy logic chip simulators. I implemented a complete MPEG2 transcoder.

  • Knowledge Engineer

    Navistar

    (Public Company; 10,001 or more employees; NAV; Automotive industry)

    January 1988 — July 1988 (7 months)

    Went to work on a 30 person effort to develop an expert system for manufacturing trucks. While everyone else was more educated than I was, I took an automated approach to rule creation. Instead of entering the rules from my expert, I created an interface that would allow me to write a spec that could generate the rules.

    Initially this unique approach raised some concerns until I demonstrated that the results. I produced my entire rule set in 20% of the time allowed in the schedule. And that time included writing the rule generator.

    This demonstrated that the automated approach might be more broadly applied. With the help of one other developer, we wrote a program that generated the entire rule set overnight. This was based on an existing conventional database of manufacturing constraints that was already in use.

    The result of my work caused the entire project to be redirected into small teams to implement AI solutions for each area of the manufacturing process.
