David Hebert’s Summary

Professor Hebert specializes in international-comparative studies of music education, specifically musical creativity, wind bands, online learning, popular music, competition, indigenous traditions, and educational policy. He is a music scholar, conductor, jazz trumpeter, vocalist, and songwriter. Grant recipient of Simpson Humanities Center, National Band Association, Japan Ministry of Education, Arts Council of New Zealand, Nordplus, and US Department of Education. Author, Wind Bands and Cultural Identity in Japanese Schools (Springer, forthcoming) and contributor to various books, including Sociology and Music Education (forthcoming), Music Education for a Changing Society (in press), Multicultural Perspectives in Music Education (Vol. II & III, 2009), Music Education in the Asia-Pacific Region, Music of Japan Today, and the best-selling Making Music school textbooks (Silver Burdett). Editorial work for International Journal of Education and the Arts, Research and Issues in Music Education, Research in New Zealand Performing Arts, Finnish Journal of Music Education, and external reviews for Arts and Learning Research Journal and Oxford University Press. Keynote Lecturer for the Nordic Network for Music Education's 2008 and 2009 international conferences (Finland and Sweden). Band Director for All-State Honor Band of Connecticut Independent Schools (2008) and Expert Reviewer for US Department of Education, adjudicating applications to its 2008 Professional Development for Arts Educators major grants program. Born in the 1970s, he is among the most widely published and cited music scholars of his generation, with research published (or in press) within 15 different peer-reviewed journals and 10 books, and cited in 40 or more publications and professional presentations by other scholars.

David Hebert’s Specialties:

Music research (music education and ethnomusicology); jazz performance; band conducting; music lecturing, mentoring and academic management.

My scholarly work examines issues of pluralism, identity, and cultural relevance in music education, as well as the process by which new musical traditions emerge and change – both sonically and socially – as they are adopted into institutional settings.
