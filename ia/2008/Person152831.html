Matthew Pope’s Summary

I am currently working as part of an international consortium on developing a generic, high throughput method that will allow validation of biomarkers as a prelude to development of clinical diagnostic tests using human blood plasma. My research takes place at the University of Victoria in British Columbia under Dr. Terry Pearson.

Before my current position I worked in Nairobi, Kenya under Dr. Steve Kemp at the International Livestock Research Institute. My research involved congenic phenotyping of trypanosome-resistant and susceptible murine strains and bovine breeds.

Prior to my career in science I worked as a professional guide for 8 years in various regions throughout the world: Antarctica, New Zealand, Argentina, Siberia, the Falkland Islands, South Georgia, Arctic Canada, and Greenland.

Matthew Pope’s Specialties:

Proteomics, surface plasmon resonance, antibody peptide interactions, mass spectrometry, web development

Matthew Pope’s Experience

  • Research Scientist

    University of Victoria

    (Educational Institution; Biotechnology industry)

    April 2007 — Present (1 year 2 months)

    Victoria, Canada
    Currently working as the only Canadian team which is part of the NCI-funded CPTAC (Clinical Proteomic Technology Assessment for Cancer) group. Other members include the Broad institute of MIT and Harvard, the Fred Hutchinson Cancer Research Center, the Plasma Proteome Institute, and Harvard University and it's affiliated hospitals.

    The primary focus of CPTAC is to develop a generic, high throughput method that will allow validation of biomarkers as a prelude to development of clinical diagnostic tests using human blood plasma. This is accomplished by combining protein and peptide enrichment techniques with highly sensitive and sophisticated mass spectrometers designed to detect and identify extremely low abundance molecules.

  • Visiting Scientist

    ILRI (International Livestock Research Institute)

    (Biotechnology industry)

    October 2006 — April 2007 (7 months)

    Nairobi, Kenya
    This position at ILRI was part of an international consortium funded by the Wellcome Trust which combines advanced linkage mapping in trypanosome-resistant and susceptible murine strains and cattle breeds. Research performed was directly aimed at ILRI’s mandate to help alleviate poverty and increase food security while protecting the natural resource base. Trypanosomiasis is endemic in 7 million km2 of Africa and is one of the major constraints to livestock productivity, with 60 million cattle at constant risk of infection, and an estimated US$ 1.3 billion annual cost per annum. Research involved several investigations under Dr. S. Kemp involving congenic phenotyping of trypanosomiasis-resistant and susceptible murine strains and bovine breeds.

  • Skipper/Naturalist

    Ocean Explorations

    (Leisure, Travel & Tourism industry)

    June 2000 — September 2006 (6 years 4 months)

    Victoria, Canada
    Lead guide and zodiac driver on more than 1000 marine eco-trips in search of whales and other marine mammals off the coast of Vancouver Island over the past six years. Aside from extensive knowledge of the surrounding waters, fauna, and boat mechanics, this job demands high-quality communication skills since guests are present with one guide who functions as driver and naturalist for the entire duration of the excursion.

  • Lecturer/Zodiac-Driver

    Quark Expeditions

    (Privately Held; Leisure, Travel & Tourism industry)

    December 2000 — September 2004 (3 years 10 months)

    Antarctica, Falkland Islands, Siberia, Arctic Canada, Greenland
    As a key member of a small team responsible for transporting and educating up to 100 tourists to some of the most inaccessible regions of the world, a comprehensive knowledge of the terrain, weather patterns, and fauna of these regions was essential. The dynamic and dangerous nature of these environments mandated that good communication and logistical skills were vital for a total of 23 safe and successful expeditions over the course of 4 years. Educational duties included presenting numerous 45 minute lectures on varied topics such as: Whales of the Southern Ocean, Antarctic Penguins, Southern Ocean Seabirds, Seals of the Antarctic and sub-Antarctic, and the Antarctic Foodweb to multinational audiences of a varied knowledge base. Logistical duties included transporting passengers from the mother ship to shore via Mark-5 Zodiacs in often-dangerous waters; with no incidents recorded for the entire four years.
