Troy Messina’s Summary

For almost 10 years, I have worked in both academic and industry research. My experiences span from state-of-the-art semiconductors to fundamental solid-state and biophysical systems. My most recent experiences studying protein dynamics have made me interested in protein-protein interactions specifically for bacterial chemotaxis systems to try and understand conformational dependence for signaling in cells. I am transitioning into a liberal arts teaching college where I will work on physics and biophysics curricula and perform solid-state and biophysics research with undergraduate students.

Troy Messina’s Experience

  • Assistant Professor of Physics

    Centenary College of Louisiana

    (Educational Institution; 51-200 employees; Motion Pictures and Film industry)

    July 2007 — Present (7 months)

    I am an assistant professor of physics at a liberal arts college in Shreveport, LA. The college maintains a high level of research goals involving undergraduates in addition to traditional liberal arts education, with a student to teacher ratio of twelve across the college.   • NIH NRSA Post-Doctoral Research Fellow

    Rutgers University

    (Educational Institution; 1-10 employees; Research industry)

    September 2003 — July 2007 (3 years 11 months)

    I have been using time-correlated single photon counting (TCSPC) for biophysical kinetics experiments. Femto- and picosecond Ti-Al2O3 laser is used for single molecule confocal microscopy. I analyze time-dependent fluorescence, rotational anisotropy, and Stokes shift. I have been primarily interested in the chemosensory protein galactose/glucose binding protein for ligand-binding studies and ß-lactoglobulin (an amyloid-forming protein). Using unique fluorescent labeling schemes, the conformational, structural, and functional kinetics can be studied. In addition, the use of micro- and nanofluidics helps confine molecules to the microscope confocal volume without hindering natural behavior. I am implementing for the first time hidden Markov models (HMM) to analyze photon streams, which make maximum use of the information provided by these experiments.

  • Semiconductor Industry Intern

    International Sematech/I300I

    (Non-Profit; 501-1000 employees; Semiconductors industry)

    June 1997 — April 2002 (4 years 11 months)

    From June 1997 to May 2002 I conducted research as an intern for the International 300mm Initiative (I300I) and International Sematech (ISMT) in parallel with my dissertation studies. These companies are consortia from the semiconductor industry set forth to eliminate cost-bearing and technological obstacles within the industry. My research included analysis of production capability of 300 mm silicon wafers and metrology tools as compared to state-of-the-art 200 mm technology, developing mathematical models for the International Technology Roadmap for Semiconductors (ITRS) Starting Materials division, as well as thermal stability and wet etch capability of potential high-k dielectric materials alternatives ZrO2 and HfO2 to replace SiOx. The wet etch studies enabled ISMT to file for a patent on the film removal process that we developed.

  • Contract Engineer

    Xidex Corporation

    (Privately Held; 1-10 employees; Nanotechnology industry)

    July 2000 — December 2000 (6 months)

    As part of a collaboration with my graduate research advisor, I was contracted to design, construct, and use a chemical vapor deposition (CVD) system for growth of carbon nanotubes (CNTs). The CNTs were grown using a precursor catalyst method, and analyzed with scanning electron microscopy (SEM). The growth conditions were tailored for growing a single, straight CNT on an ultrasenstive crystal silicon cantilever for scanning force microscopy. The nanometer diameter of the CNT provides higher resolution force microscopy than standard commercial cantilevers.
