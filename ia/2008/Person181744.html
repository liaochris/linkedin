Steven Ganz’s Summary

My field of interest is the semantics of programming languages. It allows me to skirt the border between research and practice, as I have done throughout my career. My passion is and has been applying the rigor of sound theory to obtain new perspectives and insights into the solution of relevant problems.

My undergraduate education in Penn's Management and Technology program balanced these concerns. In my consulting work for American Management Systems, I helped to develop, customize, and install large software systems, but also to develop an innovative product for interview management. I returned to research with a vengeance, however, to complete my doctoral thesis, "Encapsulation of State with Monad Transformers". Monads are a mathematical construct that have proven useful in representing effects that occur during program execution. Monad transformers make this process modular, so that a language can be described one feature at a time. My doctoral work applied monad transformers to the problem of representing the division of effects into regions and their encapsulation, or containment, within portions of a program. This allows programs to be analyzed more efficiently, by ignoring effects that are not relevant. That in turn allows resources to be made available when they needed so that code runs more reliably. My work for Elo has allowed me to apply software modeling techniques to the development of analysis tools. These tools model the ways in which configurations of components may be formed and reformed.

I have found that my dual motivations effectively reinforce each other. Experience in building large systems gives an appreciation for the sorts of abstraction that are most valuable in generating economies of scale. And an understanding of issues of language design enables one to design products that are intuitive to use, robust in their application, and efficient in their implementation.
