James Morgan’s Summary

After closing the door on my 23 years in magazines, during which time I edited many of the best-known writers in America, I’ve spent the past dozen years authoring or collaborating on six books. Now I’m combining those two aspects of my career into a service that can help editors, agents, and other writers in several important ways:

•BOOK DOCTORING—When you or your writer have a manuscript that needs anything from general touchup to time-intensive overall therapy

•BOOK EDITING—For swamped in-house editors, I can provide turnkey supervision of a project from concept to copy-edit-ready manuscript; or I can take an existing draft off your hands and work with the writer to produce the book you want to publish

•COLLABORATING AND GHOSTWRITING—By a proven author who’s written compellingly on a wide range of subjects, from Bill Clinton’s mother’s makeup routine, to the development of the interstate highway system, to Henri Matisse’s depiction of light

•CONCEPT POSITIONING, PROPOSAL WRITING—Taking the rough idea and helping you shape it into a marketable commodity

James Morgan’s Experience

  • Editor and Author

    Self-employed

    (Publishing industry)

    March 1994 — Present (14 years 8 months)

    Author, co-author, or collaborator/ghostwriter on six books (see linked website), including one bestseller and two New York Times Notable Books of the Year.

    My articles and essays have appeared in THE ATLANTIC, THE NEW YORKER, THE WASHINGTON POST MAGAZINE, AMERICAN HERITAGE, MEN'S JOURNAL, GQ, PLAYBOY, OUTSIDE, PRESERVATION, and NATIONAL GEOGRAPHIC TRAVELER, to which I'm a Contributing Editor. I've also written a screenplay, "Hot Springs," for Paramount Pictures.

    Since expanding my scope to include book editing, I've edited books for:

    *SCRIBNER (Simon & Schuster)

    *BASIC BOOKS (Perseus Group)

    *UNION SQUARE PRESS (Sterling Publishing)

    and for several independent authors.

  • Consulting Editor

    Whittle Communications

    (Publishing industry)

    September 1989 — March 1994 (4 years 7 months)

    I was brought in to help reposition SPECIAL REPORT Magazine. I also consulted on television projects and edited and wrote for Whittle publications.

  • Editorial Director

    Southern Magazine

    (Privately Held; Publishing industry)

    March 1986 — September 1989 (3 years 7 months)

    Returning to my native South to participate in the excitement of this magazine start-up, I played a pivotal role in defining the magazine's personality and in directing the small, tight-knit staff. I brought in such writers as Barry Hannah, Walker Percy, Beverly Lowry, and Harry Crews. SOUTHERN MAGAZINE was nominated for a National Magazine Award and won many editorial and design awards presented by the City and Regional Magazine Association. In 1989, the magazine was sold to Southern Progress Corporation, an arm of Time, Inc.

  • Articles Editor

    Playboy Magazine

    (Public Company; Publishing industry)

    September 1978 — March 1986 (7 years 7 months)

    Recruited for the top nonfiction job of Articles Editor, I directed a staff of nine editors in producing articles, essays, and humor for the magazine. I personally edited such authors as Truman Capote, Norman Mailer, E.L. Doctorow, Kurt Vonnegut, William F. Buckley Jr., Willie Morris, Barbara Grizzuti Harrison, Richard Rhodes, Thomas McGuane, Roy Blount Jr., Irwin Shaw, Jim Harrison, Stephen King, Erica Jong, Roger Kahn, Bruce Jay Friedman, and many more.

    I also lectured widely during these years.

  • Editor in Chief

    TWA Ambassador Magazine

    (Public Company; Publishing industry)

    October 1973 — August 1978 (4 years 11 months)

    When I took over this publication--the inflight magazine for Trans World Airlines--it was essentially a 40-page pamphlet; when I left nearly five years later, it was 130 pages, making money and winning awards.

    The key was deep soul-searching and subsequent repositioning. Once we realized what we weren't--a "travel magazine"--and began expressing what we were--a "lifestyle magazine for business travelers"--the whole picture changed.

    This was one of the great jobs of my life, largely because of some wonderful mentors--Tom Benson and Bob Pool--who encouraged me to "go for it." Because of the impressive changes we made, I had my first opportunity to work with major writers--William F. Buckley, Jr., Calvin Trillin, and Leon Uris, among them. I also was able to persuade the longtime editor of THE SATURDAY REVIEW, Norman Cousins, to make the long trek to St. Paul, Minnesota, to speak to our entire company.

  • Editor in Chief

    Kansas City Magazine

    (Non-Profit; Publishing industry)

    October 1971 — October 1973 (2 years 1 month)

    Having never worked on a magazine before, I talked my way into the top job at this publication of the Chamber of Commerce of Greater Kansas City. Good thing they were desperate.

    Presiding over a staff of five, I learned on the job as I supervised the redesign and modernization of the magazine. Our efforts were rewarded with many awards from the City and Regional Magazine Association. I also headed up the Chamber's Communications Department, producing brochures and booklets.

    None of this would've been possible without the constant involvement of my great mentor William S. Johnson, who was Don Hall's right-hand man at Hallmark and was charged with hiring the editor of the Chamber magazine. Bill saw something in me--something that made him believe if he threw me into the deep water I would somehow manage to swim!

  • Editor/Writer

    Hallmark Cards, Inc.

    (Privately Held; Consumer Goods industry)

    September 1968 — October 1971 (3 years 2 months)

    Recruited out of grad school, I became a writer/editor of greeting cards. Working at Hallmark was terrific fun--and very funny. One of my first assignments was to be locked in a room with a fellow writer composing a personal greeting card for Hallmark founder J.C. Hall to send to his friend Richard Nixon upon the latter's expected election as President that November. I haven't laughed so hard since.

    Returning from my military service in 1970, I transferred to Hallmark's Book Department, where I soon was involved in the development of a line of large gift books called Hallmark Crown Editions. It was my introduction to the kind of "graphic narrative" that would eventually lead me to magazines.

    Another incipient magazine influence at Hallmark was my boss in the book department, Richard Rhodes. He was then beginning to write for HARPER'S, and would eventually go on to win the Pulitzer Prize for his book THE MAKING OF THE ATOMIC BOMB. Dick encouraged me to chase my magazine dreams.

  • Graduate Instructor in English Composition

    University of Mississippi

    (Higher Education industry)

    September 1966 — May 1968 (1 year 9 months)

    While working on my Master's Degree in English, I taught two freshman classes in English composition, covering everything from basic grammar to the nuances of narrative structure. It was a grounding that I've built on ever since.
