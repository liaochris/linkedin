Ricard Giner’s Summary

The web facilitates - like no other medium in history - three things: access to information, transaction of money and goods, and communication and community.

I love what the web can do for people and what it can do for business. I have led a small team of web publishers, marketers and salespersons in a large company to grow web sales by 560% in four years, applying robust traffic-to-enquiry-to-conversion-and-back-to-traffic principles. These principles are universal and transferable, and much older than the web - they're actually as old as commerce itself.

I'm now embarking on making these principles work for a completely different company.

Ricard Giner’s Education

  • King's College London, U. of London

    MPhil, Philosophy, 1992 — 1993

    Not completed - did one year's research at MPhil level in Philosophy of Mind, Ethics and Aesthetics and then chose to pursue another career path. This was scholarship-funded study. It was a tough, but ultimately liberating decision - I had to reject a place in a world-class department as well as a scholarship for which hundreds of candidates had competed. But these decisions make you stronger and more capable.

  • The University of Edinburgh

    MSc, Philosophy, 1990 — 1991

    The most rewarding study decision I ever made, and the best years of my life (I stayed in Edinburgh as a resident for another year). The academic and overall university experience was fantastic - challenging, rewarding, illuminating. I met some of the most interesting and enlightened people there I've ever met before or since. And the city is one of the finest in the world. I go back about once a year, just for pleasure.

  • University of Kent

    BA, Philosophy & Social Anthropology, 1986 — 1989

    Not a great experience. I made a poor and not very well informed choice when I was 17 or so and went to a university that was trying - and failing - to reconcile the collegiate model with the departmental model. I was also historically the first person to do the combined Philosophy & Social Anthropology degree, and there was little understanding or interest in its potential between the academics. So neither side took me seriously or helped me to develop. There was one anthropology course which was quite philosophical and therefore made an effort to find shared ground. Overall a disappointing experience, not very demanding academically, student motivation and academic ability was also quite low, but the party scene was legendary! Thankfully I took courage and moved on.
