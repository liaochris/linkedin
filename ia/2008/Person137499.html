Thomas "myerman" Myer’s Summary

One day a ten-year-old boy reached into his box of cereal and discovered a ring of power. He didn't know it was a ring of power until he put it on, and then he knew because he just felt different.

So the boy decided to go out and see what powers he had. First he tried to sneak up on a bunch of girls but they saw him right away. Nein on the invisibility.

Next he tried seeing through a brick wall. Next he tried to lift a big truck. Next, teleportation. Next, mind reading. Next, he raced a motorcycle on foot. Nothing!

Disgusted by the ring of power that wasn't, he tried to take the ring off, but it wouldn't come off his finger.

He heard a distinct voice in his head that said, "You've tried all the feats of power that are obvious, but not the one that will benefit you most."

What do you think the feat of power is? What will the ring bestow on the boy?

Okay, enough with the story time, let me tell you what we do. But before I do that, let me tell you what we used to do.

We used to build really big web sites. During the course of our work, though, we realized something important:

Most of our customers couldn't create good content to save their lives.

Their landing pages were lame. Their white papers were atrocious. Their ebooks sucked. None of their web pages had any sense of flow or chutzpah. Everyone was writing me-too content.

This pained me a lot. I've written two books (and am working on a third) as well as hundreds of white papers, articles, case studies, landing pages, and catalog entries. I take content seriously! In fact, I'm a bit obsessive, and have been known to optimize my own landing pages until they're cash-spitting machines.

Hence, the switch in focus. Yes, we'll build your web site for you, but we'll act as producers. Why? Because we personally don't want to be distracted from the most important task at hand, and that is: GREAT CONTENT.

See http://www.tripledogs.com for more detail.

  • Information Architect

    Vignette

    (Public Company; 501-1000 employees; vign; Internet industry)

    October 1998 — May 2001 (2 years 8 months)

    Ah, what can I say about my time at Vignette? I joined as a technical writer and was assigned to support the AST. Great group of guys, learned a lot from them.

    Later on, we were sucked into the VGM Partner Portal effort, along with 60 or 70 other souls. I became an information architect. Basically, for a 14 months my job was "air traffic controller", presenting requirements from content development to the web developers and business stakeholders, then explaining to the content developers why I was being largely ignored.

    I also did a lot of content usability and traditional information architecture tasks (card sorting, heat maps, chunking, etc). Helped a good deal with content strategy, working closely with the KM and XML guys.

    In January 2001, the layoffs started. Lots of great people set adrift in the big, bad world.

    By the time May 2001 rolled around, we'd had quite a few layoffs, and I was ready to go. Time to start my own thing.

  • Technical Writer

    Cisco Systems

    (Public Company; 10,001 or more employees; csco; Computer Networking industry)

    March 1998 — October 1999 (1 year 8 months)

    Maintained NetRanger documentation as my official day job. One day I mentioned to someone that I had been reading up on XML a lot and got sucked into the SGML/XML initiative, and from there, I met a whole other group of people and got sucked into being my local group's liaison for CCIM, the Documentum-based content management tool.

    Needless to say, I was busy at Cisco. I liked the people, the work wasn't too difficult, and I was making good money. Why leave? I got bored, frankly. Needed new challenges. So I looked around and found myelf one of those high-flying dot-coms (see Vignette).

  • Technical Writer (and Webmaster, for a while)

    WheelGroup

    (Privately Held; 51-200 employees; Computer Software industry)

    October 1996 — March 1998 (1 year 6 months)

    My first real job in high-tech! Unbelievable!

    I was hired to be a technical writer, and that's what I did initially, until somebody in marketing realized that I'd been playing around with HTML since grad school. That made me interim web master until I could find someone to fill in that role full time.

    I worked closely with the best bunch of engineers anyone could ever gather. They patiently sat there while I interviewed them. They set up test environments for me to play with. They allowed me to sit in on security calls in the NOC. They gave me tons of books to read--on UNIX, perl, and computer security.

    I ate it up, naturally. I found that I was good not only at the whole writing thing (of which I had no doubt, really, I just had concerns about the technical stuff) but that I also really dug the geeky stuff.

    The romance begins.

  • Assistant Editor

    Harcourt Assessment

    (Public Company; 1001-5000 employees; Publishing industry)

    1995 — 1996 (1 year)

    My first real job after college and graduate work. Started out as a callow temp, moved on to high-powered executive-type assistant editor in charge of ...wait, no, that wasn't me.

    Learned a lot here. How to pay attention to detail. How to play well with others (particularly design, manufacturing, and marketing). How painful some authors can be for a publisher (and have sworn never to be that person in my own writing career).

    Met a technical writer who was moving on to this company called WheelGroup. It was her last day at Harcourt. About six months later, I get an interview with WheelGroup and then a job! Things started happening fast.

    I'll never forget the fantastic people at Harcourt, though. On the day I gave my two week notice, they all said nice things about me and then put little stickies on my desk, chair, and office supplies to mark their territory.

    Good times.
