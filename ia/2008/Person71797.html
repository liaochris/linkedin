Graeme Davis’s Summary

Writer and/or designer on 21 shipped computer and videogame titles, across most platforms.

Award-winning developer of intellectual properties. I helped develop Vampire: the Masquerade and Warhammer FRP, among others.

Published novelist and writer of popular nonfiction, specializing in history and archaeology.

Copy editor with book, newspaper, magazine, and e-zine experience: can use Quark, InDesign, Acrobat, and basic HTML.

Occasional stage and voice actor.

Graeme Davis’s Specialties:

Writing, editing, desktop and web publishing, historical research, IP development, story creation.

  • Senior Game Designer

    VR1 Entertainment

    (Privately Held; 51-200 employees; Computer Games industry)

    August 1996 — April 2002 (5 years 9 months)

    I worked as a writer and designer on several massively-multiplayer online games for publishers such as Microsoft, AOL, and So-Net Japan, as well as proposals and prototypes for various consoles. Team size varied from just me to 50+ people, including writers and designers whom I mentored and supervised.

    I also established documentation standards for the company, organizing and conducting necessary training; represented the company at E3, GDC and other events, speaking at GDC in 1999 and 2000; prepared and presented demos and pitch documents to potential publishers and partners; and advised senior management on matters of game design, IP development, and story design.

    My personal favourite was "Lost Continents," a pulp-fantasy MMORPG set in the 30s and best described as "Indiana Jones Sees The Mummy Return to the World of Tomorrow." Unfortunately this was never finished, but a few screen shots can be found on the web.

  • Senior Designer

    Magnet Interactive Studios

    (Privately Held; 201-500 employees; Computer Games industry)

    January 1995 — July 1996 (1 year 7 months)

    I worked on PC/Mac games and multimedia products for clients including Microsoft, IBM, and Time-Life Publications, and acted as design adviser and guru across the edutainment division before being moved to the games division. I trained and supervised two junior designers, and advised senior management on design and story issues while acting as lead designer on multiple projects.

    "One Small Square: Backyard," a CD-ROM adaptation of a children's nature guide, won gold in the 1995 New York Film Festival (interactive category) and was nominated for a Codie (Best home learning program for adolescents, 1996).

  • Lead Game Designer

    Microprose UK Ltd

    (Public Company; 201-500 employees; Computer Games industry)

    April 1992 — August 1993 (1 year 5 months)

    While acting as lead designer on a game that was subsequently canceled, I helped out on a number of projects, including "B-17 Flying Fortress," "Harrier Jump Jet," "The Legacy," and "Fields of Glory." My work included design, research, writing, and voice talent. I supervised one junior designer/researcher, and provided design and story direction to a development team of around a dozen people.

    For "Fields of Glory", a Napoleonic wargame, I wrote and performed battlefield commands in French, German and English - people were hanging out of windows listening to me scream "Charge!" "Fire!" and "Vive l'Empereur!" at the top of my lungs into a microphone right outside the office! I understand it was important to get that outdoor ambience . . .

  • Staff Writer/Editor

    Games Workshop

    (Privately Held; 501-1000 employees; Computer Games industry)

    April 1986 — October 1990 (4 years 7 months)

    Not really computer games, but I couldn't find a category for tabletop games.

    I was part of the development team for Warhammer Fantasy Roleplay, and went on to become the line editor, completing 20-odd WFRP titles and handling all things WFRP for Games Workshop. As well as writing when the opportunity arose, I built and supervised a top-class stable of freelancers, many of whom are still regarded as industry leaders.

    In addition, I had some design input into everything GW produced during that time, including 3rd edition Warhammer Fantasy Battle and 1st edition Warhammer 40K.

    I wrote and edited numerous articles for White Dwarf magazine, usually supporting new game or miniature releases, as well as handling submissions, reviews, a Q&A column, and occasional stints as guest editor of the letters column. I also wrote various ad text, box back text, and so on. This is where I learned the basics of copywriting, copy editing, proofreading, and layout.

  • University of Durham

    I was working on a Ph.D. project in archaeology, designing and building a computer database of prehistoric burial sites in the north-east of England. At the time this involved Fortran 77 and NUMAC, the mainframe shared between Durham and Newcastle Universities. Today it could probably all be done in Access.

    My thesis remains incomplete, but I keep promising myself I'll get back to it some day.
