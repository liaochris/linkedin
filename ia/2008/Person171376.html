Scott Silver’s Summary

I've worked at startups like i-drive.com and big companies like Google, Amazon and Netscape.

  • Sr. Manager, Ordering Experience and Post Order Processing

    Amazon.com

    (Public Company; 10,001 or more employees; AMZN; Computer Software industry)

    May 2005 — July 2006 (1 year 3 months)

    I managed about 75 folks on the ordering system. My teams built, operated and improved the front-end systems that allow customers to add to cart, checkout, modify an order and the backend systems that turned those orders into boxes at your door step by coordinating inventory management, payment execution, fraud prevention, and physical fulfillment.

  • Sr. Manager, Ordering Experience

    Amazon.com

    (Public Company; 10,001 or more employees; AMZN; Computer Software industry)

    October 2004 — May 2005 (8 months)

    I managed about 40 people operating and building the consumer-facing ordering systems. In this new role, I got involved with the teams that not only took orders, but also managed the shopping cart (before you checkout) and Your Account (order care after you've placed your order). If you've put something into your cart, checked out, or changed your orders after the fact then you're using the software my group builds and operates.   • Manager, Consumer Ordering

    Amazon.com

    (Public Company; 10,001 or more employees; AMZN; Internet industry)

    June 2003 — September 2004 (1 year 4 months)

    I managed a team of 7 software developers building and operating the checkout portion of Amazon's consumer facing ordering systems. We took a big monolithic binary and broke up into a bunch of different services, turning Amazon's ordering system into a services oriented architecture. We had to do all this while never dropping an order on the floor and without customer's noticing. The system is built in C++ with a Perl (Mason, a Perl template language) frontend.

    We introduced the first high volume, high write (as opposed to read) service-based transaction system at Amazon.

  • VP Engineering

    i-drive.com

    (Internet industry)

    May 1999 — January 2003 (3 years 9 months)

    I built the software team of about 35 engineers and testers. With two others, I built the initial version of the product. Eventually we built an engineering team around our highly scalable Internet storage platform. We scaled the system to 9MM users, 27TB of storage and almost 1GB of sustained transfers. The system was fairly innovative for its time - we glued together a bunch of NFS storage into a single logical volume and then partitioned it out using a relational database as our File Allocation Table on steroids. We did clever things like single sourcing via MD5 hashing - so we only ever had one copy of a file. Also we let folks share files without making copies. We had almost 1PB of logical storage. We built it with Java, C++, Oracle, J2EE and lots of love. I built the persistence mechanism and designed the partitioned data model. I hired the bulk of the team and managed 5 managers. The company was acquired for peanuts by EMC, unfortunately the product died.

  • Lead Engineer

    Connectix Corporation

    (Privately Held; 51-200 employees; Computer Software industry)

    May 1998 — May 1999 (1 year 1 month)

    In my second attempt too build a Java Virtual Machine, I joined some colleagues from Apple and Netscape to build a VM for Windows and Macintosh. We used an SSA intermediate representation (IR). I built the component that transformed the IR graph into a linear stream of instructions. The trick here is to avoid extraneous branches and make sure no code that is invariant in loops ends up outside of the loop, I used Tarjan's dominator algorithm to figure out where to put nodes. I built a persistent cache for the natively translated code. This is tough because Java is so dynamic there is substantial complexity in figuring out when you can pull from cache.

    Long after I left, Microsoft bought Connectix for its VirtualPC x86 emulator, that is now part of Microsoft's virtualization stack.

  • Member of Technical Staff

    Netscape Communications Corp.

    (Public Company; 1001-5000 employees; NSCP; Computer Software industry)

    June 1996 — January 1998 (1 year 8 months)

    In my first few months I worked with Dan Clifford to to build MacCVS, a GUI for CVS for MacOS 8. Before this Mac developers had no good way of pulling a tree to their desktop. I spent a lot of time debugging PowerPlant, a framework by Metrowerks for the Mac. The product is still available, though I have no idea why you'd use it. You can get it at http://www.maccvspro.org.

    Next, I shipped a couple versions of Sun's JVM that we built into Navigator. I fixed a bunch of nasty low-level bugs in the Mac version.

    Next, I built a Java Virtual Machine working with Waldemar Horwat and five other engineers. I built a retargettable backend using a "BURS" data description of the target processor. We targeted PowerPC, SPARC, x86, HPPA and Alpha. I did bazillions of interviews and helped hire a large part of the team. This is still marginally alive in the mozilla project "Electrical Fire."
