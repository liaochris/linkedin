Dr. Samuel Doughty’s Summary

Kinematics & dynamics of machines, vibrations, mechanical and electromechanical system dynamics and simulation, machine design. I am a retired Mechanical Engineering Professor with a number of years of industrial experience as well. I specialize in the mathematical analysis and simulation of physical systems for design and problem correction.

Dr. Samuel Doughty’s Specialties:

Dynamic analysis of mechanism trains, cams, gears, electric motor driven systems, and advanced strength of materials. Torsional vibration of Internal Combustion engine driven machine trains is one very important specialty.

Dr. Samuel Doughty’s Experience

  • Mechanical Engineer

    NSWCCD

    (Government Agency; 1001-5000 employees; Mechanical or Industrial Engineering industry)

    August 1999 — June 2006 (6 years 11 months)

    Research in mechanical and electromechanical systems for the US Navy for the future all electric warship. There are many major problems to be solved in converting all actuation on a warship to purely electromechanical actuation, the most obvious being things like how do you launch an aircraft from a carrier with no steam for the catapult and no hydraulic system for the arresting gear? I also worked on some very interesting problems related to pulsed alternators providing power for rail guns and pulsed laser weapons.

  • Analytical Engineer

    MPC

    (Privately Held; 201-500 employees; Mechanical or Industrial Engineering industry)

    October 1996 — August 1998 (1 year 11 months)

    MPC is a manufacturer of small electromechanical devices (motors, gearmotors, gear trains, brakes, etc.) and flight deck controls for the aerospace industry. I was the in-house consultant for all technical problems throughout the company. In this position I received a wide variety of problems, some of which took 15 minutes to solve while others took 15 days. They involved cams, gears, motors, random vibrations, and general dynamic response of systems, as well as a number of basic stress analysis questions.

  • Prof. of ME

    MSOE

    (Educational Institution; 201-500 employees; Higher Education industry)

    August 1989 — May 1992 (2 years 10 months)

    MSOE (the Milwaukee School of Engineering) is a private engineering college in Milwaukee, WI. I went there as Prof. of ME where I taught theory of machines, vibrations, and machine design, and a couple of computer related courses. I also taught some courses in advanced dynamics and mechanical analysis in the graduate program in the evenings.

  • Prof. of ME

    University of Wisconsin - Platteville

    (Educational Institution; 501-1000 employees; Higher Education industry)

    1983 — 1989 (6 years)

    UW-Platteville is a small (5000 students) campus of the Univ. of Wisconsin in the small town of Platteville, WI. I started there as Assoc Prof and later became Prof of ME. I taught a variety of mechanics based courses, but the course that I was most known for was theory of machines. I developed this course into one of the most advanced in the department, one of the first in the department to make full use of microcomputers. I wrote a textbook for this course, titled Mechanics of Machines, (Wiley, 1988). The department head often referred to this course as the "gate" to the upper division courses, and to me as the "gate keeper."

  • Analytical Engineer

    Stewart & Stevenson Services

    (Privately Held; 201-500 employees; Mechanical or Industrial Engineering industry)

    June 1974 — August 1976 (2 years 3 months)

    S & S was (at that time, at least) the largest distributor of Detroit Diesel engines in the world. They sold engines and packaged them with other machines to make complete engine driven machine packages, quite often engine-generator sets. I was head of the Analysis Engineering group, charged with two areas (1) torsional response calculations for every machine that was built, and (2) seismic analysis for all the stand-by power gen sets that were being built to go into nuclear power plants.

  • Senior Staff Physicist

    Hamilton Watch Co

    (Public Company; 1001-5000 employees; HMW; Mechanical or Industrial Engineering industry)

    January 1970 — August 1971 (1 year 8 months)

    Hamilton Watch was in the business of making mechanical time fuzes for ordinance. They were embroiled in litigation against the US Army regarding an inadequate design specification that had come from Frankford Arsenal in Philadelphia which was costing the company a fortune to build. I was chiefly employed to support this legal action with theoretical analysis in preparation for a trial and to make the presentations at that trial. I spent a year and a half analyzing the kinematics of the fuze, with the variations allowed by the tolerances in the spec, and the effect that this would have on the timing function of the fuze. I then presented this before the Armed Forces Board of Contract Appeals (the court).
