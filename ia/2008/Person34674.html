    (Public Company; 201-500 employees; NMSS; Telecommunications industry)

    June 2005 — December 2007 (2 years 7 months)

  • Senior Software Designer

    Verint

    (Public Company; 51-200 employees; vrnt; Computer Networking industry)

    July 2004 — June 2005 (1 year)

    After the demise of Hyperchip, I found a job at Verint aka Smartsight. I lead a new project on linux. The project is a video recorder for the banking industry, so the next time you do some operation on an ATM machine, smile!!
    Again I did the board bringup (uboot) and integrating linux kernel to a powerpc board with a bunch of DSP (TI DM642 and Philips Trimedia) for the video part, it is always interesting having your board boot the first time.
    I introduced a CMS to the cie, I used Plone as an intranet for my project, others groups of Verint liked the idea and adopted Plone for their project. By the way, Plone is FREE, even an embedded designer can mount a web site 8-))
    Anyway when I left Verint, the board is in live trial and ready to be build in mass production.

  • Team Lead - Fast Path

    Hyperchip

    (Privately Held; 51-200 employees; Telecommunications industry)

    October 2001 — May 2004 (2 years 8 months)

    A proud member of the LLSW (LowLevelSoftwareGroup), I was part of the platform group (board bring-up, internal router communications, Network Processor API).
    Two third of my years at Hyperchip I team lead the FastFath group that provided the network processor API for routing protocol people, our team took care the IP/MPLS packet from the ingress interface port up to the egress of the packet from the router.
    I implemented PGM protocol (IPproto=113), a multicast reliable protocol into the IP stack of NetBSD and VxWorks, this protocol is used to support the distributed nature of routing protocol table.
    I then moved to the IP Protocol team, a sub-group of routing protocol, our group provide a distributed IP stack to the upper layer protocols (MPLS, OSPF, ISIS, BGP).
    Before Hyperchip closed door, I started to work on the Ethernet Link Aggregation (802.11ad) and began to look at IPv6 stuff.
    Hyperchip was my 2nd startup cie.

  • Team Lead

    NMS (previously InnoMediaLogic)

    (Telecommunications industry)

    April 1998 — October 2001 (3 years 7 months)

    In 1998 I joined IML, later acquired by NMS in 2000.
    IML was my first "startup" experience, I saw the grow of the cie from 15 people to 140.
    Working for a startup is tough, I gained lots of maturity which serve me well when I later joined Hyperchip (another startup adventure I enjoyed).
    At IML, I learned all one needs to know about ATM networks, i.e. not in the comfort of the lab but at customer sites, most of them are in field trials or already in production.
    I was the only engineer who visited on site the top 3 customers of IML (Jetstream, VSN, Cellit), I'm still proud of it!
    When I worked closely with Jetstream Communications (San Jose), I got the taste of Silicon Valley startup environment that everybody is talking about.
    I left NMS to join Hyperchip because working on a core router is a once in a lifetime opportunity.

  • Senior Software Designer

    Eicon

    (Computer Networking industry)

    October 1995 — April 1998 (2 years 7 months)

    At Eicon, I was part of the BaseTech Group, aka the CoreTechnology (board group and WAN protocols).
    While at Eicon, I learned the ropes of communication WAN protocols, I got the chance to play and change the code of X.25, FrameRelay, PPP, ISDN.
    During this era, ISDN 128kbps was considered as "highspeed" 8-).
    I was among the few at Eicon who has the opportunity to work on the host part and the WAN card, making me a suitable candidate to "fire-fight", I earned my stripes through several successful customer support trips in France (FranceTelecom), Germany (Siemens Nixdorf), USA (Telxon).

  • Software Designer

    Securiplex

    (Public Company; 51-200 employees; Computer Software industry)

    September 1994 — October 1995 (1 year 2 months)

    Securiplex was a division of SNC-Lavallin, I was part of the embedded software group that implemented the control software for the fire protection system at the Brussel Aiport (Belgium) and the CEE parliament.
    It was my "bapteme de l'air" doing work on a real project after graduation.
    The work was very stressful since the system was "live", one cannot allow a snafu for a firmware upgrade.
    Any bug introduced can possibly "close" the airport and our company could receive hefty fine $$$.
    During this project that I had the luxury of using the HP Emulator for the 68000, a cadillac for a software guy.
    It was at this job that I fully understood the meaning of "hard real-time" versus "soft real-time".
    I got the first exposure of a high availability distributed system while working on the fire protection system.
