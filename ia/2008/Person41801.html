Curt Monash’s Summary

I have been an industry, product, and/or stock analyst since 1981. My work has probably been most influential in the areas of database management, application development tools, online services, and analytic technologies, but I've touched on quite a few other areas as well. Most recently I've written a great deal about specialty database management systems, especially data warehouse appliances; business intelligence and other analytic technologies; and text mining in particular. Much of this is on the blogs www.dbms2.com and www.texttechnologies.com. A single starting point for all my research is www.monash.com/research.html

Since the mid-1990s, where I've really earned my keep has been as a strategy and marketing consultant to software companies (and those in related industries), from the very largest (Oracle, SAP, Microsoft, AOL) all the way down to pre-revenue startups (Forte', Vertica, ParAccel).

Curt Monash’s Specialties:

Guiding my clients to positioning and messaging strategies that are both **credible** and **competitively viable**.

The endorsements below result from work with Oracle, SAP, Computer Associates, Sybase, and Forte', among other companies.

Curt Monash’s Experience

  • President

    Monash Research

    (Privately Held; 1-10 employees; Computer Software industry)

    1987 — Present (21 years)

    Analyst, strategic consultant, marketing consultant, and entrepreneur since going independent in 1987. Clients for strategic consulting have included a broad range of important software and internet services companies, including Oracle, SAP, Microsoft, CA, AOL, Peoplesoft, Business Objects, Teradata, and Netezza. Many startups have also been clients, including DATAllegro, Vertica, Mark Logic, Tivoli, Forte', and EnterpriseDB. Our flagship offering for vendors of all sizes is the Monash Advantage executive program.

  • Writer

    Blogs, white papers, Network World, and more

    (Privately Held; Myself Only; Information Technology and Services industry)

    August 1981 — Present (26 years 6 months)

    I write for an increasingly popular network of blog, including www.texttechnologies.com (search, text mining, anti-spam, other word-related technologies, and general internet stuff), www.dbms2.com (database management, analytics and related technologies), and www.monashreport.com (almost everything else, ranging from analytics to appliances to public policy). The best single starting point for these is www.monash.com/blogs.html. I also write fiercely independent sponsored white papers, and an occasional Network World column called "The Soft Touch".

  • Editor and Publisher

    Monash Software Letter

    (Privately Held; 1-10 employees; Computer Software industry)

    1991 — 1998 (7 years)

    The Monash Software Letter was one of the most insightful and influential publications addressing the software industry. From the rise of Oracle to the failure of Sybase System 10 to a whole range of technology platform shifts (UNIX, GUIs, object-oriented programming, application-serving middleware, etc.), it called major trends well before the mainstream press and analyst community picked up on them.

  • Founder

    Evernet Systems

    (Privately Held; 201-500 employees; Computer Networking industry)

    1987 — 1988 (1 year)

    Founder of roll-up systems integrator in the local area network (LAN) arena. Raised $7 million first-round funding from VCs including Kleiner Perkins. The company achieved >$50 million revenue run rate before it was sold.

    Evernet was the "SuperVAR" (a term I coined) referred to approvingly in early editions of Geoffrey Moore's Crossing the Chasm.

  • First Vice President

    PaineWebber, Inc.

    (Public Company; 10,001 or more employees; Capital Markets industry)

    1981 — 1987 (6 years)

    Highly-regarded analyst of the Computer Software and Services industries. #1 in the Institutional Investor All-Star Team 1984, #2 or #3 1983, 1985, and 1986. #2 on Greenwich Research poll 1984-6. Distinguished Service Award from ADAPSO. Generally credited with having brought understanding of actual technology and its competitive implications to the financial analysis of software companies, and to have recognized the potential of microcomputer software companies during the mid-1980s drought.
