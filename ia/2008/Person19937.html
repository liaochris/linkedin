Barry Spencer’s Summary

  • Founder and CTO

    Festoon

    (Privately Held; 11-50 employees; Computer Software industry)

    April 2005 — September 2006 (1 year 6 months)

    Festoon Inc. is the company behind the product Festoon; the insanely awesome video conferencing plug-in for Skype. Festoon has over 1M users.

    Originally called "vSkype", Festoon allows groups of people to communicate using audio, video and document sharing. It uses a client/server architecture to extend far beyond Skype's normal capacity of 5 people -- and allows up to 200 people to meet on the Skype platform.

    Festoon also has a fun EyeCandy feature that lets you customize your video in wacky ways while meeting with other people.

    In addition to working with Skype, Festoon also works with GoogleTalk and allows users on both platforms to communicate with each other.

    I was the CTO and lead developer at Festoon.

  • CTO and Founder

    Santa Cruz Networks

    (Privately Held; 11-50 employees; Computer Software industry)

    February 2003 — April 2005 (2 years 3 months)

    Santa Cruz Networks (SCN) was the brainchild of myself and CEO Keith Teare.

    At SCN, I led the development of the company's product line, including the super-cool RTCX conferencing server. In case it's not immediately apparent, RTCX stands for "Real Time Communications Exchange". This server features an integrated platform for instant messaging, multimedia conferencing, application sharing. The architecture allows multiple RTCXs to be associated -- so that users hosted on different domains can communicate with each other.

    SCN also hosted it's own on-line service based on the RTCX architecture called VidiTel.

    An example of an RTCX based service is Cloud Meeting (www.cloudmeeting.com).

  • CTO and Founder

    Reality Fusion

    (Privately Held; 11-50 employees; Computer Software industry)

    April 1997 — February 2003 (5 years 11 months)

    Guy Kawasaki described it as the "Best demo he had ever seen". Chris Shipley described it as a "Labor of Love". Fortune Magazine featured the company as one of the 10 coolest in 1998.

    Reality Fusion (RFI) was the passion of myself and a small group of developers who diligently worked through many nights in my thank-god-at-least-it-has-a-bathroom garage.

    The original video interaction technology was bundled on an estimated 6M video cameras. This technology was spun off by David Warthen and can be seen at www.eyegames.com.

    RFI also produced the first serious contender in the video chat space with SeeSaw. SeeSaw signed up over 100K users in it's first 6 months. Later in it's life it became a pay-for service under the name of CameraCafe.

    RFI also created an award-winning ASP video conferencing and collaboration service called TeamView. CNET ranked TeamView an Excellent 8.0 -- far ahead of other contenders by big companies like Microsoft and First Virtual.

  • CEO and Founder

    Cool Software Technology

    (Privately Held; 11-50 employees; Computer Software industry)

    October 1993 — November 1996 (3 years 2 months)

    In 1993 I founded Cool Software in lovely downtown Santa Cruz, California.

    While we aspired to far greater things, the company was "at the end of the day", a software development outsourcer. We liked to think of ourselves as software ghost writers.

    Our clients included Starfish, Borland, General Magic, Lotus, Oracle, Corel, Novell, Seagate, and several others. Projects were focused on "core/engine technology". Developed Magic Cap for Windows, Quattro Pro Formula Composer (US Patent 5,603,021), Starfish Web Publisher.

    THE VISA AD: Phone system? $8K, DEC Priorus Server? $15K, Mega-Huge UPS? $27K, Green and Blue Neon Sign by local artist? $800, Writing software at 3AM in a former brothel? Priceless.

  • Software Architect

    Borland

    (Public Company; 201-500 employees; Computer Software industry)

    July 1990 — October 1993 (3 years 4 months)

    Philippe Kahn pulled out all the stops when he assembled the amazing team for Quattro Pro for Windows. The team consisted of enthusiastic developers from the US, Hungary, Denmark, England, Taiwan, Canada, Hong Kong and China.

    My job was to help design and develop key components of the product including the cell engine, the formula compiler, interpreter, underlying financial functions, compatibility with 1-2-3 and Excel, coprocessor support.

    Quattro Pro received PC Magazine's Technical Excellence Award -- with proven performance far beyond that of Microsoft's Excel.

    I was also the coinventor of Borland's 3D spreadsheet. (US Patents 6,104,325, 5,572,644)

  • Software Architect

    Lotus Development Corporation

    (Public Company; 1001-5000 employees; was LOTS; Computer Software industry)

    January 1983 — July 1989 (6 years 7 months)

    What a cool time, what amazing people! You would have a hard time finding a more dynamic startup story than that of Lotus Development Corporation.

    I was the project leader and architect for the products including 1-2-3 and Symphony. Of course, I didn't actually write 1-2-3. My friend and mentor Jon Sachs did that. My job was to take the product a few steps important beyond the initial vision.

    I had the privilege of working with great people; in addition to Jon, I worked Ray Ozzie for a year or two. Ray is now the Chief Software Architect at Microsoft.

    I loved working at Lotus. It was all software (8080 assembler, in fact), all the time. I worked, and worked, and worked. If I worked too late -- I'd sleep at the hotel next door.

    Sample of geeky accomplishments: I was the father of the LIM spec (eliminated the pesky 512K boundary), I shipped the 1st product to support the 8087 coprocessor, and I developed the 1st 3D spreadsheet -- later became 1-2-3 r3.

  • Senior Software Engineer

    Commercial Union Insurance

    (Public Company; 1001-5000 employees; Computer Software industry)

    April 1979 — January 1983 (3 years 10 months)

    Could only be described as "the most exciting job at an insurance company ever".

    Imagine this; two floors of a Boston high-rise filled-to-the-gills with the very latest state-of-the art computer hardware from DEC, Wang, IBM and others. Then, add to it a couple of managers with far-out ideas about agency automation, a few brilliant role models, and my very own VT-100 connected to it all.

    When I wasn't having fun hacking in this hacker's paradise, I was developing important insurance software.

    Most notably, I created a real-time platform for serial communications targeting a variety of different computers and operating systems including VMS, CP/M, CP/M 86, MS-DOS, Unix, RSTS, RT-11, PDP-11s. Also developed various internal agency automation tools.

    Thanks Paul and Kip!
