Hong Gu’s Summary

At Risø, I was involved in developing polymer particles which could be loaded with fluorescent indicators and bombarded into cells. Since indicators for Pi were not existing, these polymer particles were loaded with pH sensitive indicator combinations as model system for ion measurements with nanoparticles. Two papers resulted from this phase of the Ph D project, which appeared in Polymers for Advanced Technologies and Chemistry of Materials, respectively.

In the group of Wolf Frommer, I designed a nanosensor based on proteins from jelly fish (GFP variants), and periplasmic binding proteins (PBP) from gram-negative bacteria. Based on a PBP from Synechococcos I was able to develop a number of nanosensor prototypes with different Pi affinities that changed there FRET efficiency when binding to the ligand. Fluorescence resonance energy transfer allows happens when two fluorescent molecules with overlapping emission and excitation ranges are close together. The sensors worked well in vitro and in vivo. While it was difficult to get nanosensor expression in plants, a transfection protocol allowed to transfect the nansosensor constructs in animal CHO cells, where it was possible to follow the change in intracellular Pi when applying high concentrations of Pi externally.

In Denmark at Copenhagen University (former the KVL), I worked on improving the working range of the phosphate nanosensors and on a method to incorporate the sensors into plant cells without transformation. By using a transduction domain from the HIV virus and adding it to the amino terminus of the nanosensor, it was possible to incorporate the sensor in onion root cells. This part of the work was submitted to the Journal of Experimental Botany, again with me as the first author.
