Jon Willis’s Summary

My main area of expertise and experience as a community development specialist and medical anthropologist is in the area of interactions between cultures. My early research experience was with colonial impacts on colonised people, including the development of separate cultural groups by Eurasians in Singapore and Malaysia, then later the impact of colonial law and bureaucracy on the lives of Pitjantjatjara people in Central Australia. Since moving into medical anthropology, I have become increasingly interested in the inroads made by complex medical and technological cultures of practice on the lived health cultures of various Australian subgroups, including Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander people and HIV positive people (as well as the overlap between these groups). My interest started with environmental health, and the kinds of impacts that houses as technological objects had on health, but lately it has been how drugs, medical procedures, health policy and living-as-a-sick-person affect and are affected by other aspects of lived culture.

Jon Willis’s Specialties:

Indigenous Australians, social aspects of HIV/AIDS, communicable disease policy, health beliefs and practices, health promotion

Jon Willis’s Education

  • University of Queensland

    PhD, Tropical Health, Medical Anthropology, February 1992 — March 1997

    My PhD was called Romance, Ritual and Risk: Pitjantjatjara masculinity in the era of AIDS. While studying I completed two major journal articles: one on renal disease in Aboriginal people published in the Australian Journal of Public Health, and one on palliative care in Aboriginal settings published in Anthrology and Medicine.

  • University of New England

    M Litt, Sociology of Law, February 1988 — March 1989

    My masters thesis was called Engine of Oppression and was about the colonial impact of land tenure law prior to the Mabo Native Title judgement in the High Court of Australia.

  • University of NSW

    BA Hons, Anthropology, Sociology, Psychology, February 1980 — November 1983

    My honours thesis was called The Serani and the Upper Ten: Eurasian Ehtnicity in Singapore, and remains a key reference on Eurasian History at the University of Singapore. I did field work at Siglap and Katong for the thesis, as well as visiting Malacca. I remain indebted to the Rodriguez family of Siglap for their help with my work.

    While at UNSW, I was a resident of Goldstein College, 1981-2.
