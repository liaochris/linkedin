Justin Calen Chiang’s Summary

THE WAY OF SNOW: A Review by the UK Bradford Film Festival

For some filmmakers, the will to create is so compelling; almost nothing can prevent them from completing their movie. When newcomer Justin Calen Chiang found that his small crew were unavailable for work on his debut feature The Way of Snow, he resolved to continue with just two people. Justin ended up writing, directing, starring in, producing, shooting, editing, scoring, post-producing and sound-mixing the film single-handedly, and perhaps unsurprisingly found the work taking a toll on his health: “(I suffered) a severe vomit inducing neck injury as well as a skin infection and a knee and hip injury. I could barely walk at times.”

The true indie spirit shines on then, and The Way of Snow is crafted with an integrity that never waivers. It’s fitting that this is a very personal story: 20-something Jay (Chiang) is trying hesitantly to get over a tough time, and it’s apparent that he has a history of self- harm. Other than domestic conversations with his sister and working at his new job, Jay’s life is limited but thankfully stable for the first time in a while. Getting back out there’s the thing, and Jay’s steps back into his aloof social circle start to remind him of the hypocrisy and casual cruelty that gnawed at his self-esteem in the first place.

Moving back and forth between his public and his home life, Jay is almost never off screen. The camera watches impassively and with candid calm, whether Jay’s alone or in company. Through this even-handed access, we see fully the gap that’s opened up between the public version of Jay, and his damaged private self. It’s this gap that’s most affecting, and which creates a delicate and unresolved sadness. Around his own controlled performance, Chiang constructs his movie with a minimal but deft economy, and the end result is a real artisan’s achievement, quietly vital in its intent and execution.

Tom Vincent
