Michelle Mooney’s Summary

Michelle is a Crain's Business "40 Under 40" alumna and has been profiled in the New York Times for her work improving commercial corridors in New York City neighborhoods. Her career in community and economic development includes community development finance, workforce development, residential and commercial real estate development and public and nonprofit management. She is known for her strategic planning and creative problem solving.

Michelle Mooney’s Specialties:

Community and economic development finance, brownfield redevelopment, New Markets Tax Credits, workforce development, urban planning, commercial revitalization, historic preservation, policy and new program development, strategic planning, airport & sea port development, etc.

Michelle Mooney’s Experience

  • Executive Director of Retail Development

    Department of Small Business Services, City of New York

    (Government Agency; 201-500 employees; Government Administration industry)

    January 2006 — June 2007 (1 year 6 months)

    Managed professional staff and provided technical assistance and training to nonprofit organizations in the areas of business attraction, district marketing and community revitalization.

    Developed a storefront and façade incentive program as part of a $38 million revitalization plan for the historic Fulton-Nassau Corridor in Lower Manhattan.

    Restructured the City’s storefront program and improved construction completion rates.

    Developed a GIS-based, online real estate tool designed to provide location analysis data to small businesses and to market vacant, neighborhood commercial properties.

  • Leasing Officer and Special Projects

    Port of Long Beach

    (Government Agency; 201-500 employees; International Trade and Development industry)

    December 2004 — October 2005 (11 months)

    Negotiated property leases and pipeline licenses with foreign and domestic companies for a 3,200-acre international seaport.

    Led the planning and development of a ten-year Strategic Plan for the second busiest seaport in the country that addressed port operations, land use, security, trade relations, fiscal policy, intergovernmental relations, environmental protection, transportation, community relations and an overarching commitment to sustainability.

    Developed the Port’s first sustainability program and designed its inaugural projects.

  • Deputy Director of Development

    Department of Development, Cuyahoga County, Ohio

    (Government Agency; 51-200 employees; Government Administration industry)

    September 2000 — December 2004 (4 years 4 months)

    In partnership with the Director, oversaw a $55 million budget and supervised 160 staff employed in the divisions of Community and Economic Development, Housing, Workforce, Sanitary Engineering, Administration and the Cuyahoga County Airport; other duties included attracting grant funding, media coordination, new program development, strategic planning and intergovernmental and community relations.

    Negotiated public/private partnerships with residential and commercial developers.

    Reviewed and approved a variety of grant, loan and tax-exempt bond financing requests from residents, businesses, developers, nonprofits and local governments.

    Served as the Interim Director of the Cuyahoga County Workforce Development Department for six months and oversaw a budget of $11.5 million.

  • Section Manager, Business Development

    Department of Economic Development, City of Cleveland

    (Government Agency; 51-200 employees; Financial Services industry)

    May 1996 — September 2000 (4 years 5 months)

    Managed professional staff; oversaw Cleveland’s tax abatement program; planned and marketed new industrial parks; presented development projects to City Council, City Planning and other local, state and federal government entities; reviewed and executed grant/loan agreements and lien instruments and managed development legislation.

    Negotiated, underwrote and secured multi-source incentive/financing packages often including municipal land sales, foreign trade zone benefits, tax-exempt bond financing, tax credits, infrastructure and streetscape grants, tax abatement or tax increment financing, brownfield remediation incentives and low-interest loans.

    Leveraged more than a half-billion dollars in private investment and the retention/creation of more than 10,000 jobs.

  • Executive Director

    Westown Community Development Corporation

    (Non-Profit; 1-10 employees; Civic & Social Organization industry)

    April 1990 — May 1996 (6 years 2 months)

    Oversaw an annual agency budget of $400,000 and professional staff serving residents and businesses in a service area of approximately six square miles; direct and supervisory involvement in community planning, crime prevention, district marketing, residential rehabilitation and new construction and storefront renovation.
