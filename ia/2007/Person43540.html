Jonathan Rothberg’s Summary

Dr. Rothberg invented the technology and initiated projects to sequence the first human (Jim Watson), the Neanderthal Genome, and crack the disappearance of the honey bees (CCD). Dr. Rothberg was born in 1963 in New Haven, Connecticut. He earned a B.S. in chemical engineering from Carnegie Mellon University and Ph.D. in biology from Yale University. Dr. Rothberg is the inventor and founding Chairman of 454 Life Sciences, Ion Torrent Systems, The Rothberg Institute for Childhood Diseases, and the co-founder and Chairman of RainDance Technologies and Clarifi Corporation, and founder of CuraGen Corporation, and was its CEO and Chairman from inception to 2005, responsible for all financing, developing the Drug Pipeline, licensing, and bringing drugs into the clinic. Dr. Rothberg was named an Ernst and Young Entrepreneur of the Year and is the receipt of The Wall Street Journal’s Gold Medal for Innovation for his invention of 454 sequencing, the first next generation sequencing technology, and the first sequencing on a chip. Dr. Rothberg’s scientific work has been featured on the covers of Cell, Science, and Nature. Most recently Dr. Rothberg's technology solved the mystery behind the disapearance of the honey bee, initiated the Neanderthal Genome project with Svante Paabo, and completed the first sequence of an individual human genome (James Watson). Dr. Rothbergs breaktrough in metagenomics allowed for deciphering of the world's oceans and understanding the mystery behind the disappearance of the honey bee. Dr. Rothberg has twice been selected as a World Economic Forum Technology Pioneer. Dr. Rothberg is a member of the National Academy of Engineering, The Connecticut Academy of Science and Engineering, and serves on the board of trustees of Carnegie Mellon University.

Jonathan Rothberg’s Specialties:

Founding Companies, sequencing human genomes, solving mysteries from honey bees to Neanderthal, inventing new technologies, financing and bringing novel innovations to the market. Able to integrate teams from diverse fields including microelectronics, nanotechnology, chemistry, physics, chemical engineering, material science, computer science and molecular biology.
