Byrne Hobart’s Summary

My experience is pretty scattered, since I'm currently in the process of turning value investing and financial writing from a full-time hobby to a full-time job. Briefly, I've been investing for years, I've worked at one financial job and a few other administrative jobs, and I've read a ridiculous number of financial statements and business books. I write a financial weblog (http://marketview.blogspot.com), and alternate between looking for work in the hedge fund industry and looking for interesting stocks and trends.

I'm pretty far along the Graham/Fisher/Buffet/John Burr Williams/KKR/Carl Icahn school of in-depth fundamental analysis and (when possible) controlling positions in undervalued companies. Unfortunately, I don't precisely have funding to match my ambitions, so the best success I can point to is someone else making a killing with an idea I had first: In late 2003, I wrote a report on Blair Corp (Amex: BL), noting that the company was underperforming and undervalued, and suggesting that this was because management wasn't sufficiently terrified of shareholders. My suggestion was to buy 5-10% of the stock, and force management to 1) Dump their consumer credit division, 2) Stop hoarding cash, 3) Buy back a huge chunk of stock, and 4) Get rid of their Internet division (á la Wired Online -- same content, different owner). I saw a pile of cash and mismanaged assets; the market saw a low-end mail-order retailer with a few unused and unuseful appendages. I passed my report on to a financially-savvy and financially-connected people; they passed on the plan but commended the effort.

A few months later, a hedge fund I've never heard of bought a 13-D-worthy stake, and convinced management to put plans 1 through 3 into effect (4 was, in retrospect, a bad idea -- delusions of 90's grandeur, I suppose). I'm not sure how much they made, but it looked like about a 30% return over two or three months, which is not exactly paltry.
