Creed O'Hanlon’s Summary

Creed was infamous as the founder and former CEO of Australia’s most successful – and controversial – web development company, Spike Networks. In July 1999, he led Spike to an IPO raising $35 million on the Australian Stock Exchange.

Resigning from Spike in 2000, Creed joined Mazda Motor Corporation in 2001 as its ‘Creative Spark’ for Global Brand Marketing in Hiroshima, Japan. He held this position until his retirement from business in late 2003.

Qualified as a British RYA/DTI Yachtmaster in 1976, he helps build and operate sailing multihulls for charter and expeditions around S.E. Asia.

Occasionally, he publishes accounts of this erratic life in words and images.

Creed O'Hanlon’s Interests:

He has been a member of the Governor of California’s advisory committee on arts and technology, a council member of The Museum of Australia, Canberra, a member of Austrade’s Telecommunications Industry Advisory Panel, and part of the advisory board of the Australian edition of The Industry Standard magazine. He was also a founding judge for the Australian Financial Review/Telstra Internet Awards, and the IdN International Design Awards. Represented by Celebrity Speakers, Sydney, he has addressed gatherings at Herring In Hollywood, Promax, Webnoize, The American Film Institute, The Australian Film Commission, The Screen Producers of Australia Association, UCLA, The University of NSW, The College of Fine Art and XMediaLab, Sydney, to name but a few. His journalism has appeared in The New York Times, Variety, The Industry Standard, The Bulletin, Griffith Review, Australian Financial Review, and The Sydney Morning Herald. His short fiction has appeared in the Griffith Review.

Creed O'Hanlon’s Honors:

In 2007, his memoir included in A Revealed Life, an anthology by ABC Books

In 2006, wrote the preface for Living Brands by Raymond Nadeau, a best-selling book published by McGraw-Hill

In 2005, his memoir included in Best Australian Essays 2005, edited by Robert Dessaix for Black Inc.

In 2005 he was a finalist in the Sun-Microsystems IT Journalism Awards (Columnist).

In 2004 his short fiction included in Best Australian Stories 2004, edited by Frank Moorhouse for Black Inc.

Since 1997, has been the subject of major profiles published in The Bulletin, The Australian, The Sydney Morning Herald, The Financial Review, Nikkei, The Hollywood Reporter, Variety, The Industry Standard, Advertising Age, Asian Economic Review and others. He has appeared on various TV programmes, including CNBC, Bloomberg, Four Corners (ABC), The Today Show, and A Current Affair.

Australia's national rock radio station, Triple J, has described him as 'the Malcolm McLaren of the Net'.
