Dr. Lauchlan A. K. Mackinnon’s Summary

I am focused around organizational creativity and innovation, with a particular interest in Idea Management Systems that enable your organization to capture, develop, select and implement ideas to turn them into business outcomes.

I am also interested in related organizational and personal transformation, particularly through methdologies such as change management and performance management.

I have interests in:

* Organisational Creativity and Innovation (creativity tools and techniques; innovation management)
* Idea Management Systems (tap and put into operation ideas from employees, customers, and partners to deliver improved business value)
* Balanced Scorecard and Strategy Mapping (define and implement metrics to realise and monitor the effectiveness of your strategy)
* Organisational Learning and Knowledge Management
* Change Management
* Organisational Culture (culture assessment, definition, and development)
* Scenario Planning (identify your primary business drivers, work with your management and staff to develop flexible mental frameworks to respond intelligently to changes in your operating environment)
* Blue Ocean Strategy ('value innovation' to shape markets you own and where your competition is irrelevant)

  • Doctoral Researcher

    The University of Queensland

    (Educational Institution; 1001-5000 employees; Research industry)

    February 1998 — March 2006 (8 years 2 months)

    My doctoral thesis:

    * drew on the psychology of creativity literature to examine the genesis of economic ideas in the creative work processes of economists, and
    * drew on sociological theory to help understand generally how ideas are located in social systems and specifically how how economic ideas become institutionalised into economic institutions.

    I argued that once economists have ideas about the economy, and those ideas are then disseminated within the economy, those ideas help change the nature and dynamics of the economic system and economists are not 'objectively' modelling economic systems being studied, like classical 19th century physicists, but are instead active participants in shaping the economic system they are studying - more like quantum physicists in the 20th century.

    My thesis (http://eprint.uq.edu.au/archive/00004455/) was very interdisciplinary, drawing on literature in economics, psychology, sociology and philosophy.
