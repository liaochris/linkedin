Jonathan Parry MEng (Oxon) MBA MIMechE SPE's Summary

An expert in global natural gas productive capacity, corporate strategy, and complex well production and development. My recent work includes IHS CERA's Global Unconventional Gas Suite. Before joining IHS CERA in 2008, I held technical posts with Shell International and Chevron.

As an Engineering Advisor I have participated in major capital projects in North and South America, Europe, Asia, and the Middle East. I specialized in the production and completion of complex wells, for example, deepwater oil, high-pressure high-temperature gas, intelligent well, and high rate natural gas wells. I have led technology developments to enhance these wells, and instructed at the BP/Chevron training alliance in deepwater artificial lift. I have lived in the United Kingdom, Netherlands, and the United States.

Based in Houston, Texas, I am a professional engineer and a member of the Institution of Mechanical Engineers, the American Society of Mechanical Engineers, and the Society of Petroleum Engineers. I hold a Master’s degree in Engineering Science, Economics and Management from Christ Church, Oxford University where I was an ICI engineering scholar, and a Master of Business Administration for Executives from the Jesse H. Jones Graduate School of Management at Rice University where I was a Jones Scholar.

Specialties

• Complex well production and development, including deepwater, high rate gas and arctic completions
• Global unconventional resource potential and its impact on gas markets, business development and corporate strategy

  • Gas from the Gulf: North American Supply Overhang Reduces Impact of Deepwater Drilling Moratorium

    • IHS CERA
    • June 2010
    Authors: Jonathan Parry MEng (Oxon) MBA MIMechE SPE, Mary Barcella

    This Strategic report concludes that the six-month US deepwater drilling moratorium in response to the Gulf of Mexico (GOM) oil spill is unlikely to eradicate the current North American natural gas supply overhang. Unlike domestic oil production, natural gas does not depend heavily on GOM development. GOM gas production overall is in a long-term decline while shale gas production prospects have soared.
    ■ The drilling moratorium will reduce GOM production by 0.2 billion cubic feet (Bcf) per day in 2010, 0.5 Bcf per day in 2011, and 0.4 Bcf per day in 2012, assuming that activity resumes when the moratorium is lifted.
    ■ The GOM is increasingly less important for North American gas production.
    ■ The shale gale oversupply will persist until the rig count falls.
    ■ Uncertainty, not market fundamentals, may lead to higher prices.

  • Something's Gotta Give

    • IHS CERA
    • December 2009
    Authors: Jonathan Parry MEng (Oxon) MBA MIMechE SPE, Marcela Rosas

    This Strategic report answers the strategic question of the year for gas markets in 2010 - How can the conundrum of increasing gas production and increasing levels of gas supplies in storage be resolved? In recent years the North American natural gas market has proved to be very resilient and adaptive under an array of stressful situations, and we expect the market to respond accordingly in this instance as well. Given our expectations for demand, IHS CERA believes that the current path of increasing gas production is unsustainable; therefore something has to give way. For consumers, the following adjustments would be a welcome extension of the recent low price environment. However, for producers, they represent an uncomfortable set of alternatives. The anticipated changes are:
    ■ A sharp downward adjustment in the gas-directed rig count to a point at which production declines. This will likely require a capitulation in the still-robust gas futures market.
    ■ A diversion of liquefied natural gas imports away from North America caused by a further decline in spot gas prices.
    ■ An increase in consumption as a result of lower-than-expected spot gas prices.

  • The Mighty Marcellus

    • IHS CERA
    • December 2009
    Authors: Jonathan Parry MEng (Oxon) MBA MIMechE SPE, Teddy Muhlfelder

    This Strategic report states that the shale revolution, in combination with the global recession, has shifted the North American natural gas market from supply constrained to demand constrained. The Marcellus, with its massive areal coverage and 262 trillion cubic feet of technically recoverable resource potential, is the next game changer for the North American gas market. IHS CERA has increased its Marcellus supply outlook threefold to just over 6 billion cubic feet per day by 2018, which would make it North America’s third most productive play.
    ■ The Marcellus Shale carries significant advantages. The Marcellus will likely become a game changing, local supply source for the Northeast market, covering a huge area from West Virginia to New York. Our analysis shows that the play is economically viable. Drilling conditions in Pennsylvania are favorable, and near-by significant long-haul pipelines and storage allow large-scale access to market.
    ■ Market maker and volume displacement. The Marcellus Shale can displace imported Gulf Coast and to a lesser degree western Canadian gas, allowing shippers to shed upstream pipeline capacity in favor of closer supply sources.
    ■ The "Continental Reunion" will be cemented. The growth in production from the Marcellus will put further downward pressure on eastern gas prices, reducing the spread between western and eastern gas prices.
    ■ Impact on Henry Hub. Displacement of volumes back into the Gulf Coast will further saturate the Louisiana and Gulf Coast area, the home of Henry Hub, providing increased gas-on-gas competition. This is a key factor in IHS CERA’s Henry Hub price outlook.

  • Friction over Fraccing: Hydraulic Fracturing and Safe Drinking Water Regulation

    • IHS CERA
    • October 2009
    Authors: Jonathan Parry MEng (Oxon) MBA MIMechE SPE, Samantha Gross, Mary Barcella

    This Strategic report reviews issue related to Hydrualic Fracturing.
    ■ Gas production is considerably less water intensive than other types of energy production.
    ■ Hydraulic fracturing of gas shales is unlikely to contaminate drinking water, but contamination from surface handling of wastes is possible.
    ■ Both state and federal authorities are involved in regulating waste disposal from oil and gas production. In most cases the US Environmental Protection Agency has delegated to the states primary enforcement authority for underground waste disposal. If the SDWA were to regulate hydraulic fracturing, states would likely gain primary enforcement authority for this as well.
    ■ A proposed requirement that gas producers disclose the chemical constituents of fracturing fluids raises concerns about trade secrets. The greatest challenge in drafting legislation and regulation will be assuring that enough information is available to protect the public health while preserving the confidentiality of trade secrets.

  • Indian Gas Production Capacity Outlook: Supply Set to Satisfy Growth in Demand

    • IHS CERA
    • February 2011
    Authors: Jonathan Parry MEng (Oxon) MBA MIMechE SPE, Samia Razak

    Some of the world's largest gas discoveries have been made recently in India, with success concentrated in the Krishna-Godavari Basin. At the same time there has been unprecedented interest in India's sizeable unconventional gas resources. Total remaining recoverable reserves for conventional gas are 56.8 trillion cubic feet (Tcf), while coalbed methane (CBM) in-place resources stand at 300 Tcf. There is considerable potential to add to these reserves particularly from shale gas given the right investment climate and infrastructure development. Thus supply growth will remain strong, and India will meet a high percentage of its needs through indigenously sourced natural gas. However, a number of factors must fall into place to make this happen.

    ■ Total productive capacity is expected to rise to 13 billion cubic feet (Bcf) per day by 2030. This strong growth will be fueled by the large discoveries in underexplored offshore basins and emerging shale gas plays.
    ■ Domestic capacity will meet much of the anticipated gas demand increase but other sources will have to make up additional supply capacity. India must quadruple its current supply capacity to meet anticipated demand of 17.2 Bcf per day by 2030. Coalbed gas, shale gas, and liquefied natural gas (LNG) will play an important role to bridge the gap.
    ■ LNG has a critical role in bridging the gap between future supply and demand; therefore India will need to secure longer-term LNG contracts rather than purchase on the spot market.
    Shale gas will overtake coalbed gas. Given the domestic supply increase and the very high interest in India's shale gas potential, CBM's role is likely to be limited.
