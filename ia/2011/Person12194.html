Craig Dolder's Summary

My short-term professional goal is to work in industry on acoustics research. I feel that research will give me a chance to work on challenging projects that require both creativity and knowledge. I value a well-rounded education and enjoy when material from one area of study is applied to another. I worked on a research project for NASA during my undergraduate studies and that experience contributed to my desire to go into research. I like to be exposed to new ideas and concepts in the field of acoustics and am not daunted by the fact that that there is a possibility that these ideas may not prove possible. I look forward to finding new ways to approach the problems that may arise.
My primary interest outside of my field of study is renewable energy and recycling. I have a great interest in green technology and finding applications that make everyday processes more efficient and environmentally friendly.
Through Engineers Without Borders, I’ve been able to plan and implement a clean water solution for a small village in India. The project was started my freshman year of college and the planning and organization continued through the fall semester of my senior year when we started the implementation of the project. I was able to travel with a team of two technical mentors and four other students to India where we supervised the installation of a well with a solar powered pump and connected this system to a sealed water tank.
I am currently a Graduate Research Assistant at The University of Texas at Austin and am pursuing my PhD in Acoustical Engineering.

Specialties

Acoustical transmission, Mechanical vibration, Transmission of vibrations, Architectural acoustics.

Craig Dolder's Publications

  • A New Method for Breath Capture Inside a Spacesuit Helmet

    • 37th International Conference on Environmental Systems
    • 2007
    Authors: Craig Dolder

    This project investigates methods to capture an astronaut’s exhaled carbon dioxide (CO2) before it becomes diluted with the high volumetric oxygen flow present within a space suit. Typical expired breath contains CO2 partial pressures (pCO2) in the range of 20-35 mm Hg. This research investigates methods to capture the concentrated CO2 gas stream prior to its dilution with the low pCO2 ventilation flow. Specifically this research is looking at potential designs for a collection cup for use inside the space suit helmet.

    The collection cup concept is not the same as a breathing mask typical of that worn by firefighters and pilots. It is well known that most members of the astronaut corps view a mask as a serious deficiency in any space suit helmet design. Instead, the collection cup is a non-contact device that will be designed using a detailed Computational Fluid Dynamic (CFD) analysis of the ventilation flow environment within the helmet. The CFD code, Fluent, provides modeling of the various gas species (CO2, water vapor, and oxygen (O2)) as they pass through a helmet. This same model will be used to numerically evaluate several different collection cup designs for this same CO2 segregation effort. A new test rig will be built to test the results of the CFD analyses and validate the collection cup designs. This paper outlines the initial results and future plans of this work.
