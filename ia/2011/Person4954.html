Postdoctoral Candidate

Stanford University

Educational Institution; 10,001+ employees; Higher Education industry

March 2005 – Present (6 years 1 month)

"Selective degradation of an aggregated fragment of huntingtin by autophagy"
The ubiquitin proteasome system (UPS) is well equipped to degrade soluble proteins, however it is unlikely that this holds true for aggregated species. Macroautophagy (autophagy) on the other hand, has been demonstrated to be essential for the elimination of protein aggregates associated with neurodegenerative disorders such as Huntington's Disease. Classically defined as a means for cells to degrade, en masse cytoplasmic proteins in response to starvation, autophagy can also operate selectively to degrade organelles and, possibly, protein aggregates. To test the hypothesis that aggregated proteins are selectively degraded by autophagy, we developed a novel flow cytometry-based strategy to quantitatively measure selective autophagy in living cells. We are currently exploiting this cell-based assay to identify key molecules regulating this pathway.
