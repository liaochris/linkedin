Beth Shapiro’s Summary

I am an artist working primarily as a painter. My paintings are abstract, using a visual language similar to that of the abstract expressionist and color field painters of the mid-twentieth century, but created with materials that are very much of our current age.

I am interested in exhibition opportunities and am also currently investigating the possibility of pursuing an MFA degree.

Beth Shapiro’s Experience

  • Artist

    Independent Visual Artist

    (Information Services industry)

    2008 — Present (3 years )

    Jackson Pollock famously stated, “the modern painter cannot express this age, the airplane, the atom bomb, the radio, in the old forms of the Renaissance or of any other past culture,” while trying to explain the extraordinary breakthroughs of American Abstract Expressionism to the public. Today, these paintings seem an inevitable expression of the post-atomic age, and the artists of that era have changed the way we experience and understand art today. The work of abstract expressionists Jackson Pollock, Mark Rothko, Hans Hoffman, and the color field painter Helen Frankenthaler have had the greatest influence on my own work.

    Using the language of abstract expressionism and color field painting, with material insistently of this age, (manufactured acrylic sheeting, acrylic paint and glaze mediums) I create paintings that reflect contemporary society’s emphasis on appearances, “polish” and, above all, the intrusion of plastic. The surfaces of my paintings are lustrous, sensuous, and brilliantly luminous. By mixing acrylic paints with translucent glazes and using acrylic panels as my surface, I create paintings emphasizing the surface texture, with color juxtapositions and interactions that draw an emotional reaction from the viewer. The flow of color and line, shape and form become intricately interwoven as translucent and opaque layers of glaze are laid down. Each layer creates new forms that I respond to in making the painting. As light travels through each layer and into the surface, it creates a luminescence that appears to generate from within. The pieces speak to a contemporary age when plastic is everywhere and our most desired objects are sleek and shiny, minimalist in both design and even usage difficulty. But the substance under the “polish” of these shiny exteriors cannot hide the complexity and frailty of the human experience. I look for instances where I can realize an exquisiteness of beauty in combination with an underlying tension.
