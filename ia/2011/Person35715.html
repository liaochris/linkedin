Rachel Tortolini M.D., M.A.'s Summary

Broad range of education and experience not only in medicine but working in space technology before medical school:

Modelling communication channels for the Hubble, designing new HP spectrum analyzer, and was an advisor to the second Strategic Arms Limitation Talks. I was active in the search for extraterrestrial intelligence and built my own radio telescope which can be seen on mySpace/dr_rachel. Now I am Pacific Basin coordinator for the setileague.org.

I was on the medical staff of Hilo Medical Center for over ten years and served on the Credentials Committee and was chair of the Family Practice department. I subsequently also worked as Medical Director of Kau Hospital on Big Island, had three solo medical practices in Hawaii, worked as staff physician at the Bay Clinic in Hilo, and was concierge doctor for hotels on Kona coast of Hawaii.

In addition to board certification and residency in family medicine, I also worked in a Psychiatry residency for several years doing research on intersex disorders resulting in a published book marketed through amazon.com. Experimented with rat mRNA to bench test psychotropic drugs.

Worked for human rights for indigenous peoples of the Pacific (and appear in the PBS documentaries “and then there were none” and “The Tribunal.”)

In my spare time I am a novelist with my most recent novel published on Amazon.com and through other markets such as Target. I also have a book out on the medical aspects of intersex diseases. Recent review of book:

4.0 out of 5 stars A moving and informative read, July 18, 2010
By PdeC (Redmond, WA USA) - See all my reviews
This review is from: Transsexualism: a Medical Retrospective (1) (Kindle Edition)
I was moved by this book, so much so that I contacted the author, something that I have never done before. She asked me to write a review, so here it is. All the words are mine; she did not edit this review.
This book is part memoir, part medical science, and part political philosophy. The memoir part is a snapshot in time, the time frame being her transition in 1980 and the decade after that. The medical science part includes the physiology and anatomy of gender and sexuality. And the political philosophy part analyzes transsexuality from the perspective of privilege, stigma, oppression, persecutor and victim.
Being a transsexual is not easy. It wasn't easy for the author. She lost her career as a psychiatrist when she transitioned, and then found her way as a general practitioner. Although the social conditions for transsexuals have improved, much of this book is still relevant.
This little book is a worthwhile read, although difficult in parts, it is informative and moving.
