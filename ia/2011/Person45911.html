William (Billy) Smallen's Summary

I began my undergraduate career at the University of California, Santa Cruz with a purely scientific interest; studying Computer Engineering and Astrophysics I looked ahead to a life of research. By the end of college, my major had switched to Planetary Geology, where I combined a degree in Earth Science with my Astrophysics background. Through this course of study I became intimately familiar with the Earth and its systems. I learned about terrestrial and celestial climate histories and the tremendous affects that climate changes create. Planetary Geology also allowed me to study in-depth environmental changes occurring on the Earth and culminated with my senior thesis on the breakup of the Larsen B ice sheet in Antarctica. Seeing the consequences of environmental degradation and the effects they have on people and the natural world gave me the desire to have a hand in designing solutions to these problems. This led me to public policy and eventually to my Master’s Degree program in International Environmental Policy at the Monterey Institute of International Studies.

Climate change reaches into every corner of the globe, its echoes can be felt in the exacerbation of many environmental issues. The sheer magnitude of the problem entices me. At once a global and local problem, it will take the collaboration of a multitude of experts from disparate fields if it is to be solved.

The advances in standard of living that societies around the globe have experienced since the dawn of the industrial revolution are due in no small part to the development of masterfully designed infrastructure systems. The invisibility of these systems in our daily lives leads to overuse and waste of their output and masks the pollution problems associated with them. As few areas of the environment are left unaffected by these technologies, energy analysis, especially in the electricity and water sectors, is where I find my focus. With work, energy systems can be made more sustainable.

William (Billy) Smallen's Publications

  • Calculating Feasibility Of Codigestion At Wastewater Treatment Plants

    • BioCycle
    • November 2010
    Authors: William (Billy) Smallen, Matthew Ward, Laura Moreno

    Proposals to build new biogas facilities or to expand existing ones must demonstrate adequate source material as well as the potential for a reasonable return on investment. Recognizing the environmental value of anaerobic digestion and biogas use, the U.S. Environmental Protection Agency’s (EPA) Pacific Southwest Region developed two project planning and development tools that aim to bring more clarity to project developers. The first is an interactive map of the region that identifies sources and potential users of organic waste such as food processing facilities and wastewater treatment plants. The other tool, a downloadable Excel spreadsheet, is designed to help wastewater treatment plants determine the economic feasibility of codigesting food scraps as well as fats, oils, and grease (FOG). This article explores these two tools and introduces them to potential users.
