Katherine continued her education by achieving a second M.S. degree in Molecular and Nutritional Biochemistry at Case Western Reserve University as well as a PhD (ABD) in Nutrition and Cell and Molecular Biology at Virginia Tech.

    Katherine’s extensive education and research has molded her into a strong, dedicated and innovative scientist in a diverse array of fields, including biology, nutrition, metabolism, and food science. At GRN Katherine combines her innate communication and interpersonal skills with her scientific comprehension and savvy to successfully identify, attract, and recruit High Impact Talent in the Biotechnology field.

  • Graduate Research Assistant

    Virginia Tech

    (Biotechnology industry)

    July 2006 — August 2007 (1 year 2 months)

    I conducted research on the genetics of obesity. Duties and skills included; genotyping, cell culture, cell transfection, plasmid transformation, PCR, QPCR, EMSA, animal dissection, gel imager, experion, OD readings, RNA preparations, cDNA preparation, oligonucleotide design, primer design, Western blots and other general lab procedures. I demonstrated how Nhlh2 may regulate a-MSH in response to changes in energy balance and that Nhlh2 binds to the MC4R promoter and that this pathway may underlie certain genetic predispositions to obesity. Overall the coordinated transcriptional regulation of a-MSH and MC4R with specific hypothalamic neuropeptide genes results in the synthesis, processing and release of mature neuropeptides required for body weight regulation, in response to specific energy availability signals. The project required expertise in animal experiments, cell lines and transfections, in vitro assays, and complex molecular biology procedures.

  • Graduate Teaching Assistant

    Virginia Tech

    (Biotechnology industry)

    2002 — 2004 (2 years )

    I taught a senior level Metabolic Nutrition course and a writing intensive recitation as well as Human Nutrition Lab. For my thesis I performed research involving, measurement of LDL, HDL, TC, TG, Insulin, Glucose, CCK and dietary planning in clinical human research study. For the clinical study I came up with novel ideas and research techniques to help combat obesity. With our development of a protein pre-load food product designed to stimulate a naturally induced satiety hormone called Cholecystokinin, we were able to demonstrate that women on a low-fat, well rounded and healthy diet could in fact lose as much weight as women on a number of different “fad” diets. In addition, these women remained happy with and were able to stick to their diet throughout the study and at completion they had significantly improved markers of health including cholesterol, glucose tolerance and insulin levels.
