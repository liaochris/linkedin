Benjamin Abram's Summary

There are a lot of "low-hanging fruit" technologies out there that can make our lives more efficient, thereby reducing the amount of energy required to maintain our collective lifestyle. Particularly because of where I sit at The Westly Group, I'm particularly interested in hearing about companies that are tackling that issue.

I'm also personally interested in connecting with people who are recognizing our failing infrastructure in this country. (See Hurricane Katrina, the 7/19/07 steam pipe burst / the 8/8/07 subway floods in Manhattan, or this tragic I-35W bridge collapse, e.g.) I'm afraid we have not been investing enough time or energy in maintaining our infrastructure in this country. Back to the bigger picture, I think that these types of improvements will be crucial to maintain our way of life while reducing energy consumption, moving forward.

It may be that what I'm talking about is low-hanging fruits for giraffes, but hey-- you've got to reach!

My email is "ben" followed by "@westlygroup.com"

Benjamin Abram's Experience

Director

FACE AIDS

Nonprofit Organization Management industry

August 2010 – Present (8 months)

FACE AIDS is building a global movement of youth dedicated to health equity and social justice. With a focus on leadership capacity, peer community, and socio-economic opportunity, we empower youth to take action now and for the long-term.

FACE AIDS has over 200 chapters at U.S. college and high school campuses, engages more than 700 HIV-affected youth in Rwanda, and has provided employment, structured savings, and business training to over 200 HIV-affected Rwandans.

Board Observer

Glacier Bay

Privately Held; Mechanical or Industrial Engineering industry

September 2009 – Present (1 year 7 months)

Based in Union City, California, Glacier Bay has designed a battery-powered Auxiliary Power Unit (APU) that air conditions the cabs of long-haul trucks for at least ten hours. This breakthrough, branded as ClimaCab, enables fleets to keep their drivers cool in their cabs without idling their engines while they sleep overnight. Saving one gallon of diesel per hour, the units pay for themselves in under two years.
