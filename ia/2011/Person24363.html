Kevin Donahue's Summary

I have purposely sought and maintain a broad range of experiences and interests associated with Architecture. Be it building types such as multifamily, ecclesiastical, educational, commercial/service, and corporate, or architectural services such as space planning, design, interior design, building/exterior envelope assessments and studies, building research and restoration, and revisioning and reinvigorating the urban environment. The advantage of all of these experiences is that I am able to draw on them, think outside the box, and "cross pollenate" to arrive at a stronger design, issue resolution, or course of action. I enjoy learning, whether under the tutelage of an experienced and learned collegue, the mason striking a joint, or my client. This is my offer to my clients: Let's form a partnership. Teach me, guide me about who you are, what your needs and hopes are, and I will listen. In turn, allow me to explore, to guide, and maybe even to teach you as you listen to me, whether I speak with words or designs. Together, our dialogue will help create that work, that design, that solution for which you have come looking, be it a new structure, or the repurposing or saving of an old one.

Kevin Donahue's Education

The University of Texas at Austin

M. Arch, Archtiecture, Urban Design

1993 – 1995

Studied under Charles Moore and Professors Simon Atkinson, Andy Vernooy, and Susan Handy as part of a combine architectural theory/urban design program. Mebane Travel Scholarship. My studies focused on urban design and understanding how to keep our built environment human and humane. We traveled extensively throughout the southwestern US and throughout Mexico, seeking to study, understand, and apply the lessons of creating and developing an exciting and vibrant urban envrionment in light of history, culture, climate, transportation & communication. My studies culminated in a thesis seeking to tame the American auto oriented urban environment with traffic calming. Only through the act of traffic calming could "transportation balancing" be sustained and the demand for multiple transportation choices to the broadest segment of the population be recognized, planned for and fulfilled, thus making the urban environment more humancentric than autocentric.
