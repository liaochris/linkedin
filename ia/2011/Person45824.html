Ville Miettinen's Summary

I'm a serial entrepreneur and an investor. The last couple of years I've been particularly interested in crowd sourcing, cloud labor, and human computation -- disruptive technologies that are transforming the way people work all over the world.

A hacker at heart, I've spent way too many years banging out code in assembler, C, and other languages used by real cowboys. I've got a long background in computer graphics, and was the CTO and one of the founders of Hybrid Graphics, a pioneer in real-time 3D graphics technologies. We created some of the very first commercial 3D engines and licensed them to games companies all over the world. I was the co-author of the SurRender3D engine as well as the Umbra visibility optimization middleware.

Later on, we also wrote the first implementations of OpenGL ES, OpenVG, JSR-184 and other graphics standards, and licensed them to hundreds of millions of mobile phones. The company was acquired by NVIDIA in 2006.

Nowadays I'm the CEO & founder at Microtask (http://www.microtask.com), a company commercializing technologies for crowdsourcing and distributed work.

I am also a partner at Lots (http://www.lots.fi), a Nordic boutique VC and accelerator in the field of media technology. I am an investor and a board member in several high-tech, social media, and games companies in Finland and Silicon Valley. I am seriously involved in Finnish start-up scene, coaching and mentoring start-ups at the Aalto Venture Garage, and also acting as a referrer for the pan-European seed stage investment company HackFwd.

In my free time, I travel around the planet, and over the years have become a semi-professional photographer (http://www.flickr.com/photos/wili).

Ville Miettinen's Publications

  • Mobile 3D Graphics with OpenGL ES and M3G

    • Morgan Kaufmann
    • 2008
    Authors: Ville Miettinen, Kari Pulli, Tomi Aarnio, , jani vaarala

  • dPVS: An Occlusion Culling System for Massive Dynamic Environments

    • Computer Graphics & Algorithms
    • April 2004
    Authors: Ville Miettinen, Timo Aila

    Visibility optimization is currently the most effective technique for improving rendering performance in complex 3D environments. The primary reason for this is that during each frame the pixel processing subsystem needs to determine the visibility of each pixel individually. Currently, rendering performance in larger scenes is input sensitive, and most of the processing time is wasted on rendering geometry not visible in the final image. In this article we concentrate on real-time visualization using mainstream graphics hardware that has a z-buffer as a de facto standard for hidden surface removal. In an ideal system only the complexity of the geometry actually visible on the screen would significantly impact rendering time—3D application performance should be output sensitive. Furthermore, opportunities exist for using lower accuracy for artificial intelligence, physics computations, and collision detection in the hidden parts of the scene.

  • Delay Streams for Graphics Hardware

    • ACM Transactions on Graphics (Proc. SIGGRAPH)
    • August 2003
    Authors: Ville Miettinen, Timo Aila, Petri Nordlund

    In causal processes decisions do not depend on future data. Many well-known problems, such as occlusion culling, order-independent transparency and edge antialiasing cannot be properly solved using the traditional causal rendering architectures, because future data may change the interpretation of current events. We propose adding a delay stream between the vertex and pixel processing units. While a triangle resides in the delay stream, subsequent triangles generate occlusion information. As a result, the triangle may be culled by primitives that were submitted after it. We show two- to fourfold efficiency improvements in pixel processing and video memory bandwidth usage in common benchmark scenes. We also demonstrate how the memory requirements of order-independent transparency can be substantially reduced by using delay streams. Finally, we describe how discontinuity edges can be detected in hardware. Previously used heuristics for collapsing samples in adaptive supersampling are thus replaced by connectivity information.
