Guang Yang’s Experience

  • Senior Research Scientist / Engineer

    Nokia

    (Société cotée en bourse; NOK; Télécommunications industry)

    2007 — Present (4 years )

    I work for Nokia Research Center (NRC), Nokia's corporate research, in Palo Alto. I am deeply engaged in R&D activities in the following areas, working with Nokia business units as well as universities and industrial collaborators.

    * Personalized location based services. Instead of pursuing higher position accuracy or better content integration, we focus on learning and predicting a mobile user's long-term location patterns. Most people spend most time at a handful of routine places while occasionally deviating to more exotic ones. The ability to learn and predict individual location patterns will revolutionize location based services to be better personalized for one's unique needs.

    * Activity recognition and behavior modeling. Today’s mobile phones are equipped with various sensing components, as well as the ability to connect to external sensors. We apply machine learning to phone sensory data to recognize its user's physical activities and social contexts, which are useful to a wide range of applications including healthcare, wellness, location based services and smart UIs. We have built software APIs for application developers.

    * Mobile social networking. We already have the world's largest social network in pocket - the phone book. We have developed WebCall, a web-based, modernized social phone book to facilitate easy context/content sharing for family and friends. WebCall is a framework that allows third parties to develop plug-ins. It has a proximity networking module and is able to fuse one's virtual and physical social relationships in a unified way.

    * Proximity networking. Proximity networking is for close-by devices to quickly find each other and get connected. It is useful for serendipitous group events where the key is co-location rather than existing social relationships. We have built library tools to automatically handle service discovery, network management, authentication and content sharing on mobiles.

  • Technical Yahoo

    Yahoo!

    (Société cotée en bourse; yhoo; Internet industry)

    2006 — 2007 (1 year )

    I was part of Yahoo! Mobile that rolled out a series of revamped services including SMS, Mobile Web and Yahoo! Go in 2006 and 2007.

    * I was a key member of the engineer team designing, implementing and deploying the Yahoo! Mobile Sushi platform to connect millions of mobile users to dozens of Yahoo! properties. This platform consisted of many components written in C++/PHP, including a dispatcher, property connectors, a markup generator and several databases.

    * To handle thousands of phone models being used at the time, we invented a microformat called hMobile to generate customized XHTML markup based on device specifications from a device catalog database.

    * I was responsible for the platform scalability and performance issues. Due to the huge traffic (billions of monthly page views) and limited resources, the platform had to undergo vigorous scrutiny to ensure satisfactory performance and user experience.

    * I was responsible for WML support. WML was popular especially in parts of Asia-Pacific where Yahoo! had a strong foothold. To avoid writing a separate WML site, we developed sophisticated XSLT stylesheets to transcode XHTML into WML in real time.

    * As a platform engineer, I worked closely with front-end engineers and web developers to design and build new Yahoo! Mobile properties using Sushi and hMobile. Some of those are still being used at http://m.yahoo.com/.

  • Teaching Associate/Graduate Student Researcher

    University of California, Los Angeles

    (Établissement éducatif; Enseignement supérieur industry)

    2001 — 2006 (5 years )

    I taught undergraduate students how to program in C++ and assembly languages, as well as computer organization and performance analysis for 2+ years. I studied several problems in wireless/mobile networks, the solutions to which later became my dissertation:

    * How to improve data throughput without modifying the core Internet architecture? Mobile/wireless networks have higher error/loss rates than the wired Internet. Protocols initially designed for wired networks therefore suffered severe performance degradation. We proposed TCP Bulk Repeat to improve data throughput in a lossy environment. It may improve throughput by up to 10 times while behaving well to other protocols.

    * How to improve multimedia quality-of-service without modifying the core Internet architecture? We proposed the Video Transport Protocol (VTP) for adaptive real-time video in mobile/wireless networks. VTP has unique end-to-end rate control to remain smooth and efficient in the presence of errors/losses. VTP preserves convergence properties and converges to its fair share fast.

    * How to handle connection disruption effectively in highly wireless/mobile scenarios? We proposed the Ad-hoc Storage Overlay System (ASOS) to cope with connectivity disruption in mobile networks. It is a self-organized peer-to-peer overlay, consisting of storage-abundant nodes that provide distributed and reliable storage. In today's language, ASOS is a mobile cloud solution.

    I also collaborated with colleagues on the Universal Seamless Handoff Architecture (USHA) that maintains uninterrupted network connections and services when a mobile device switches from one wireless interface (e.g. Wi-Fi) to another (e.g. 3G).
