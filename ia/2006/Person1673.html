Holly Stevens’s Summary

My work dances among writing, editing and graphics elements; I was never content to focus on one more than the others. I was most motivated when doing work for causes I believed in, whether for free or for hire. My dream as a young mother was to attend seminary for the love of the learning; to use my M.Div as credentials to further my fledgling career as a religion writer; but to live for the weekends, opening my home to retreatants seeking solitude, or silence, or space, or safety to share one's stories. It was a dream incompatible with a marriage that was noisy, active, and enmeshed in the material world, and after much soul searching, I abandoned it and returned to school to become a special ed teacher. But when my world was shattered by the end of my marriage in 2000, I grew into relationship with a friend I'd known for 19 years, who had followed a similar dream. Bill and his first wife had acquired 40 acres of woods and meadows in the hills of North Carolina, creating a home/retreat center with 24 beds while he was a Friends pastor. She died, far too young, in 1979; Bill moved on alone, hosting spiritual and environmental retreats until we reconnected in 2001, marrying in 2004. My advanced cancer has ended the retreat ministry here at Glenagape, although Greensboro Montessori School now uses our "raw land" for a middle school land immersion program. So here I am, writing and editing and publishing inside the house via my keyboard and DSL connection, and watching students get to know the land and the stories and mystery it holds in store for those who observe it from close up with bare hands. My life is a circle; I am content. But in my next life, if there is one, I hope to come back as a troubadour -- singing and traveling and listening to others' stories and telling my own and making just enough dough to ward off fear and cold.

Holly Stevens’s Experience

Editor and Publisher at The Storyteller and the Listener Online (Self-employed)

(Self-Employed; Myself Only; Performing Arts industry)

August 2005 – Present (1 year 4 months)

It is high-falutin to call my enterprise an industry or to even say that I am gainfully employed, but though I am disabled from the effects of late-stage breast cancer, my mind is clear and my fingers are nimble, so I use my home computer to put out a twice-monthly newsletter on the role of story and narrative in peacemaking, healing, bridge building and reconciliation. The content takes the forms of essays that have come from all over the English speaking world, and my readers have come from every continent except Antarctica. (Anybody there online?) You're welcome to visit: http://storyteller-and-listener.blog-city.com.

Religion Writer/Columnist (Self-employed)

(Self-Employed; Myself Only; Writing and Editing industry)

January 1992 – January 1997 (5 years 1 month)

I was a stay-at-home mother during these years, but I wrote on the side, both for pay and for free. Among my assignments was lifestyle columnist for the Jamestown Sun and religion writer for the Yakima Herald-Republic. I also wrote various pieces for Quaker Life and other publications associated with the Religious Society of Friends.

Freelance Writer and Editor (Self-employed)

(Self-Employed; Myself Only; Writing and Editing industry)

September 1984 – December 1991 (7 years 4 months)

I was a writer and publications project manager working mostly on a freelance basis in the financial, medical and not-for-profit sectors. I specialized in case statements for not-for-profit capital campaigns. My favorite clients were Wolf Ridge Environmental Learning Center in northern Minnesota and Mount Sinai Hospital in Minneapolis. After my son was born in October 1987, I stopped working fulltime but continued to handle some work for those two organizations.

Manager of Publications at Wachovia Bank and Trust

(Public Company; 5001-10,000 employees; Banking industry)

July 1978 – August 1984 (6 years 2 months)

I started as a publications specialist, became a publications editor, and eventually manager of publications (employee and informational publications for specific client segments). I learned a lot, was treated well enough, and left on good terms, but I never understood the corporate culture. Looking back, I'm sure I stayed too long! In 2002, I contacted Wachovia (which is really First Union now, but that's another story...) to get proof of my past employment and was told that the bank doesn't keep employee data more than 10 years. It seems strange to have given so much of my spirit to a place for six years and for not a trace of me to remain there. (That's not a moan -- just a wonder at the transience of life.)
