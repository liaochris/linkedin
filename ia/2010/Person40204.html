Mark Chubb’s Summary

Public safety and emergency management have changed a great deal since I entered the profession 28 years ago. The things that remain the same -- the value of public service and the importance of being there to help people in times of need -- still motivate me. As a principal consultant with Manitou, Inc., I have the opportunity to "help the helpers", by sharing my skills and experience with clients worldwide.

Our practice focuses on bringing state-of-the-art analysis to bear for public, non-profit, and private sector clients. We help them refine their understanding of problems so they can make more informed choices, especially in situations that present no single best option.

For instance, we have become pioneers in the analysis of fire and emergency services deployment. Our approach not only looks for locations that will help service providers minimize their response time, but we also help them gain insights into the inherent tradeoffs between social equity and efficiency that arise in the real world. By considering such issues up-front, we help decision-makers avoid problems that could derail the implementation of the preferred solution.

Recently, we have begun working with clients to develop innovative public engagement processes that help them forge productive relationships with key constituencies to make informed decisions together. Our approach aims to foster social capital in communities while encouraging decision-makers to demonstrate respect for democratic values and civic virtues.

I'm very proud to be part of a firm that looks for lessons from other fields and disciplines. My colleagues' expertise in international development, planning, and policy analysis provide me with unique insights that help me bring innovative ideas and critical thinking to diverse issues. We look forward to an opportunity to share our expertise with you.

    (Public Safety industry)

    March 2009 — Present (1 year 8 months)

    The Regenhard Center undertakes important research for developing integrated, comprehensive approaches to the study emergency responses to large-scale disasters by studying the first-hand experiences of front-line emergency service workers. Established in 2008 with federal funding secured by the New York congressional delegation, the Center is named for FDNY probationary firefighter Christian Regenhard, who was killed at the World Trade Center on September 11, 2001. The Center serves as a research repository and information clearinghouse for the study of emergency responses to disasters such as the attacks on the World Trade Center and Hurricane Katrina as well as future threats such as a major earthquake on the West Coast.
