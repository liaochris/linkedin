Douglas Goodall’s Summary

My lifetime occupation has been the creation of software and firmware. I always preferred writing code that controlled hardware, whether embedded or not.

Having spent forty years writing code, it will be challenging to learn enough about digital electronics to enjoy my new hobby of working with vintage computers.

Douglas Goodall’s Specialties:

My primary interest has always been in the area of computer communications. I was an early adopter of SNMP with a private enterprise number of 204. My technical interests span the entire distance between 100 baud modems and gigabit networking, rs485, sdlc...

    Goodall Software Engineering

    (Computer Software industry)

I did some very fine work during this period and met a lot of great people in the industry. After Malia passed away in 2006, I closed the company and moved on.

    During its operation, GSE served a great many companies on a contract basis and provided solutions for many gnarly technical challenges. Well known clients included Stanford University, Motorola Corporation, Evergreen Systems, and Cubix.

  • Executive Vice President

    Concurrent Controls, Inc.

    (Computer Software industry)

    1987 — 1988 (1 year )

    My engineering company, Goodall Computer Systems, took on a business partner and was incorporated as Concurrent Controls, Inc. where my back stabbing business partner swindled me out of my share of the company through clever stock manipulations and gave my position to her husband whom I taught. I received a pittance and a kick in the ass and she went on to make millions off my code base and technology. Subsequent to my departure, what remained behind was my code, but not the understanding and wisdom about operating systems that I brought to the company.

  • Board Member

    Hudson & Supinger Computers, Inc.

    (Research industry)

    April 1986 — June 1988 (2 years 3 months)

  • President

    Goodall Computer Systems

    (Computer Software industry)

    1985 — 1986 (1 year )

    As the sole proprietor of a software company I consulted for various other companies in a troubleshooting capacity writing drivers and fixing bugs. I also developed a commercial version of Concurrent DOS for the Arnet SmartPort card. During the period I worked alone at the company, I developed the SCSI hard disk enhancement to the CP/M-80 BIOS for the Little Board, and subsequently developed an XIOS for the Ampro Little Board/186 (which I convinced Ampro to build) and which was later sold to Koor Industries for use in point of sale systems. Later the company incorporated as Concurrent Controls, Inc. I also wrote a Concurrent based run-time system for a high end graphics system developed for GE.

    I also worked with Ed Songy to create a hardware/software solution with the Little Board/186 to replace the IBM Series/1 as the only machine capable of monitoring natural gas wells for Exxon. A 24-state state-machine and the timer interrupt running at 7200 ticks per second allowed me to read a proprietary serial format unsupported by normal peripheral chips. BCH checksums for fun and profit :-)

    During this time I met Kevin Supinger and Jim Hudson, two ex-Intel employees that were wizards with microprocessor design. After they did the Little Board/186 for Ampro, I convinced them to build a 80186 based computer to run my Concurrent version. This board had SCSI hard disk and eight serial ports with fully decoded interrupts handled in 25 microseconds. A crazy fast machine at the time. I was on their board of directors also. I did finish the Concurrent port for their prototype system, but the release of the 80386 processor killed off the market for real-mode Concurrent.

    During this time I developed a generic driver technology that used state machines to manage a wide variety of third party serial cards and formed the basis for a line of products that sold well for many years.

  • Senior Operating Systems Specialist

    Digital Research, Inc.

    (Computer Software industry)

    May 1983 — 1985 (2 years )

    I worked in the OEM Systems division porting the Concurrent CP/M and Concurrent DOS operating system to a variety of platforms. Specifically the Olympia PEOPLE System, The Aprocot Personal Computer, the Mad 80186 system, the IBM 5550 in Japan...

    As the world troubleshooter for Concurrent CP/M and Concurrent DOS, I traveled extensively in Europe and the far east writing XIOS versions for customer machines and performing sales support in regional offices.

    In my spare time I wrote the first Concurrent version supporting the 80186 CPU from Intel. The system loaned to me by Mad Computers ran like lightning with my hand optimized SCSI routines, and was a great help in my day job, as it was the fastest development machine available to me.

    While there I was also able to adapt my Forth language system to CP/M-86 and Concurrent with some changes to the VMI. No Forth kernel changes were required. This was the first Forth system for CP/M-86.

    I was granted a "Key Employee" stock option for my diligent work supporting Concurrent around the world.

  • Systems Programmer

    Ampro Computers

    (Privately Held; Computer Hardware industry)

    1983 — 1983 (less than a year)

    I worked briefly at Ampro in order to modify the CP/M-80 BIOS for the Little Board Z80 to support SCSI hard disk operations. Once I completed this work, I suggested my skills would be better used working with a 16-bit card such as an 80186. After I completed the work, I taught a staff employee the principles of my driver architecture. He then left Ampro and went to work for Adaptec, where he reproduced my driver architecture and it ended up being called ASPI.

  • Software Development Manager

    Captain Software

    (Computer Software industry)

    1982 — 1982 (less than a year)

    I was the 8086 assembly language expert hired to support the companies migration from 8-bit 6502 to 16-bit x86 platforms. Eventually I ported the Easywriter program to the IBM-PC for IBM.

    I realize that John Draper has taken credit for the port, but I was the assembly expert working as Software Engineering Manager at the time, and I wrote the assembly portion of the Forth language system used as the base of the product. I extracted the machine dependent parts of the forth kernel into what I called the Virtual Machine Interface (VMI) which allowed this forth version to be easily ported from one operating system to another.

    I specified the S-100 based TEI computer and the Seattle Computer Products two card set and OS as the emulation platform for the development, which worked out well when IBM chose that OS to be the basis of DOS.I also wrote the SCSI drivers that allowed the Corvus hard drive to be shared among the various development and test machines. I also wrote the high performance subroutines that did not perform well in forth such as the justification and display routines. I also was responsible for the copy protection scheme that protected the Easywriter product disks from copying.

    When the actual IBM-PC hardware prototypes arrived, the port from the emulation platform to the IBM-PC only took one night because of my wise and fortuitous choice of 86DOS as the emulation operating system, and the encapsulation of the environmental dependencies in the VMI.

    After the product was released, I had enough of Draper and didn't speak to him for many years until he eventually hacked my netcom account and called me to take credit.

  • Systems Programmer

    Lesher Systems (later known as Oakleaf and Associates)

    (Computer Software industry)

    1980 — 1981 (1 year )

    Because of my strong experience in operating systems programming, Jim invited me back to Lesher Systems to develop his new operating system using an up and coming chip from Intel, the 8086. I wrote and supported language tools used to develop firmware for a finance calculator sold to auto dealers. Also I was the system operator for the IRIS timesharing system used in-house. I also design and implemented an 8086 based multitasking system to be used as the platform for a new line of finance calculators. I developed the software using an Intel MDS with 8" floppies, using plm-86 and asm-86. Due to my experience with preemptive multitasking, I was chosen to write the realtime kernel and context switching code. A colleague, Jeff Woolf, wrote the file system portion. Eventually the operating system was sold to Reynolds and Reynolds, and Fortune Systems. We called the operating system in-house, OOPS (Our Operating System). The system remained in use for many years. I had the honor to be taught a great deal more about programming by James Lesher who was one of the most important mentors of my life. Also I taught Steve Bishop about structured programming and he taught me about microprocessor circuitry. Subsequently I designed the microprocessor schematic used for the first Savin copier to use smart cards to control user access and accountability. I also helped develop a quality assurance program to verify the integrity of 2716 EPROMs that was later utilized at Intel.

  • Programmer Analyst (National IMP-16)

    Litton Automated Marine Systems

    (Computer Software industry)

    1977 — 1977 (less than a year)

    I wrote systems and application code for an embedded application. Specifically a general program debugger, and software design of a load monitoring system for product carriers (ships). I was the senior programmer working with 25 PHD's of Mathematics. When the project changed scope at the last minute from one ship to any ship, I designed a Ship Descriptive Language and a parser to create run-time tables to drive the higher mathematics. The Load Officer on the Product Carrier would load a Ship Tape, then begin modeling the load to avoid excessive shear forces and bending moment. The description would include tank locations and sensor specifics for automatic ullage calculations and resulting center of gravity results.

  • Wirewrap Geometry Programmer

    Data Connections, West

    (Computer Hardware industry)

    1976 — 1976 (less than a year)

    I worked at a company that used Gardner Denver automated wire wrapping machines to wrap limited run prototypes of digital circuitry. I wrote geometry descriptions that allowed the equipment to use custom wire wrap boards for specific projects.

    I also wrote a database program to keep track of contracts/geometries. It was written in Cobol and ran at Litton Mellonics down the street. The Mellonics system operators called me, "The Hawk", because I stood at the submissions desk and read the system monitor across the room for HASP completion status codes in order to run down the hall and punch more cards to fix problems. This was the only Cobol code I ever wrote for money. It made massive use of the "move corresponding" option in order to support six input card formats and twelve unique output formats. A large data division and a small procedure division. A clear case of the right language for the task.
