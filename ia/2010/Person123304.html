Dung Le’s Summary

Bioscience and Biotechnology have been contributing to the improvement of human health and well-being. Such improvements have been achieved via adequate and appropriate nutrition and excellent health care. Thus, my career objectives have been to use special knowledge and technical expertise in Biochemistry and Molecular Biology to improve human health and her well-being.

  • JSPS Postdoctoral Fellow

    National Agriculture Research Center - Japan

    (Biotechnology industry)

    November 2008 — March 2010 (1 year 5 months)

    My research here is aimed at developing RNAi-mediated virus-resistant rice and analysis of its resistant mechanism with emphasis on Rice Tungro viruses. I plan to deliver a 500-base inverted repeat of each of every gene of the viruses into japonica rice and then study how the transgenes can trigger the degradation of virus RNA when the transgenic plants is exposed to live viruses and finally how it could confer resistance to the viruses.

    My other interest is to look for evidence insight into the mechanism by which Rice Tungro Spherical Virus assists the Rice Tungro Bacilliform Virus to transmit via insect vector.

  • Researcher

    Vietnam Institute of Agricultural Engineering and Post-Harvest Technology

    (Research industry)

    October 1996 — May 2009 (12 years 8 months)

    I joined the Post-Harvest Technology Institute, which later merged with another Institute to form Vietnam Institute of Agricultural Engineering and Post-Harvest Technology (VIAEP), right after getting my Bachelor degree and worked mainly on biochemistry and physiology of fruits and vegetables. Since 1999, I took long-term leaves from the Institute (with occasional returns of few months each time) to pursue my career aboard.

    In April 2008 I returned to the Institute and was appointed as Vice Head of Department of Post-Harvest Biotechnology. I requested a resignation from the position at the end of Oct 2008 and by May 2009 my affiliation was transfer to the Agricultural Genetics Institute, VAAS, MARD.

  • Postdoctoral Research Associate

    University of Nebraska-Lincoln

    (Educational Institution; Higher Education industry)

    April 2006 — March 2008 (2 years )

    In certain proteins, methionine residues are positioning close in proximity to the active site, the binding site or sites that are critical for correct folding. Existing evidence proved that, when these methionine residues are oxidized, the proteins become malfunction and consequently lead to development of diseases.

    My studies in Dr. Gladyshev group (which now moved to Harvard Medical School) involved in both development of novel tools for the detection of methionine sulfoxide (including antibodies, biosensor, etc) and identification of enzymes that reduce methionine sulfoxide back to methionine. The successful stories had been published in Biochemistry and Journal of Biological Chemistry.

  • Overseas Business Advisor

    Bioland Korea

    (Biotechnology industry)

    November 2005 — March 2006 (5 months)

    Working mostly through online networking, I supported Dr. Chom-Kyu Chong, Director of the Human Science Division, to identify Vietnamese partners to establish a Join-Venture Company to produce rapid diagnostic kits. The Join-Venture company has been established in Jan 2007.

    In addition, I also assisted the Company to establish connections with several Institutions in Vietnam that later involved in collaborative researches on development and evaluation of rapid diagnostic kits for human diseases.

  • Postdoc

    National Food Research Institute - Japan

    (Biotechnology industry)

    May 2005 — March 2006 (11 months)

    In microorganisms several pathways leading to secondary metabolites are in dormant state and these pathways could be activated by targeting ribosome or RNA polymerases. My responsibility was to see which mutants in the Ribosomal proteins and/or RNA-polymerase could activate these pathways and to what extend the secondary metabolites could be produced. I also measured changes in binding affinity of mutants of RNA polymerase with promotors playing key roles in secondary metabolism.

  • Graduate Research Assistant

    (Biotechnology industry)

    September 2001 — December 2004 (3 years 4 months)

    Acetohydroxyacid synthase - molecular model-assisted protein engineering by site-directed mutagenesis. Designed a version strongly resistant to sulfonylurea class without significant effect on kinetic properties.
