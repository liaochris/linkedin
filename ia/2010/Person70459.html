Ken Follett’s Summary

I want to hear from you. My business has to do with fixing old buildings.

I tend to carry on conversation, ranting and raving and general foolery and befuddling attempts at entertainment as it pertains to 'doing virtual business' in 2 specific groups on LinkedIn:

1. "Means, Methods & Materials for Restoration of the Built Environment", hosted by my good friend Pat Morrissey, Greater New York City Area

2. "Cultural Heritage Conservation Science. Research and practice", hosted by the most gracious Jose Delgado Rodrigues, Lisbon Area, Portugal

Join these groups. Life is very serious, business is very serious and so that makes me laugh abundantly at the absurdity to remain too strenuously serious for too long of a time.

I bounce around different LinkedIn groups as the fancy takes me. I have experimented and created havoc with online social networks for nearly 2 decades. It all started with an old outhouse and a long train ride.

Rule #1: If someone says something in a social forum then someone needs to respond. Everyone in the world deserves to know that they are wanted.

I am insatiably curious and like to find out what I do not know, or never thought of, or could not possibly conceive as at all possible, within reason, of course. I do have opinions and they have feelings.

Lest you wonder if I am the Follett who wrote Cathedrals of Stone: NO, I am the real Ken Follett.

I have been active and reactive in construction for more than 40 yrs, with specific experience in historic conservation, exterior façade maintenance and waterproofing. I have worked on a bunch of compelling projects and it is always considered generous if you get to know me... through our company website.

As a writer I am a published poet, though I believe my strength is in the irrelevant essay form. There is a conversation going on in the world and I want in on it... I want to hear from you.

    (Architecture & Planning industry)

    As it has been said, the greenest building is the one that already exists.

    I have spent a lot of time trying to figure out why we save anything. I come from a line of collectors of stuff and besides my having too many books our house is full of tools and rocks.

    A comment that I heard one day has stuck with me, "When we preserve the past it is an act of hope and optimism for the future."

    If we want our youth and future generations to have a quality of life that we know then we need to work to maintain that quality that we have inherited. It is not solely that we may feel a responsibility to leave a clean planet with clean air and drinkable water behind us for our youth and future generations to enjoy their quality of life, but we also need to leave behind the evidence of our best and most worthy and noble cultural achievements, either in the built environment or in the arts.
