Tara Lumpkin’s Summary

Dr. Lumpkin is President of Perception, a non-profit organization that promotes biological, cultural, and perceptual diversity.

Tara has worked as a journalist, professor of writing and media, and in communications. She has published extensively in the areas of nonfiction (including travel writing), fiction, and poetry, and has been a reporter for the environmental newspaper High Country News. She is currently working on a memoir about healing.

In addition, she offers coaching with a focus on increasing her clients' consciousness by having them access their “inner animals” and inner nature. She calls this process “Izilwane.” It draws on perceptual diversity, somatic experiencing, shamanism, medical anthropology, meditation, body movement, and her own experiences as a medical anthropologist. Tara offers retreats, workshops, and travel workshops (“Izula”) in this area. She also is a certified somatic experiencing practitioner with a personal private practice.

Tara has a PhD in anthropology and uses her cross-cultural expertise to consult in the areas of sustainable tourism (with a focus on experiential tourism), community conservation, gender, ethno-medicine, and communication and media. Some of the organizations she has worked for are the United States Agency for International Development, Conservation International, and the United Nations Children’s Fund.

Tara Lumpkin’s Specialties:

non-profit management, intercultural communication, bridging communication between different cultures, community conservation, natural resource management, adventure travel, ecotourism, educational tourism, experiential tourism, voluntourism, Izilwane coach, retreat leader, somatic experiencing practitioner, shamanic healer

  • Izilwane, Founder & Director

    Perception International

    (Non-Profit; Non-Profit Organization Management industry)

    January 2009 — Present (1 year 7 months)

    Izilwane, which means “animal” in Zulu, explores the interrelationships between human beings, animals, and their ecosystems, and human perceptions of these interrelationships. Many of the principles of Izilwane are related to the concept of perceptual diversity, which states that perceiving reality through many different perspectives allows human beings to better understand, interact with, and develop a closer relationship with other species, nature, and the universe as a whole. This field is called “anthrozooecology.” The Izilwane project proposes that shifting how human beings see, sense, and experience the world affects not only their perceptions but also their actions. Izilwane believes that this shift will allow people to fully experience their interdependence with our global ecosystem, and, thus, “know” that they are an integral part of nature. This enhanced awareness could lead to more innovative solutions to the environmental problems facing the world today. www.izilwane.org
