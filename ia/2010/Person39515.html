  • Lead Developer & Designer

    Matter

    (Privately Held; Design industry)

    March 2008 — Present (2 years 3 months)

    I approach software as a design problem. Looking at the application as a system, finding its core objects, and respecting its constraints.

    As the lead developer of www.perqworks.com, my responsibilities encompassed a wide set of activities.

    At-the-keyboard responsibilities:
    1 - Writing thousands of lines of PHP (with Symfony), jQuery, CSS, and SQL code
    2 - Configuring Apache, Linux, and MySQL, aka LAMP stack
    3 - Configuring Postfix email server to send application emails
    4 - Configuring web application infrastructure like firewalls, networking, opcode caching, and backup procedures
    5 - Integrating the Paypal API to collect credit card payments and recurring subscription payments

    Away-from-the-keyboard responsibilities:
    1 - Designing and architecting an entire application. Discovering what the main objects of the system. Deciding what restraints to force the design team to adhere to and working to keep the design integrity of the system as new features are added.
    2 - Deciding scope, feature priorities, and product roadmaps by focusing on the business values of each decision and the speed of development execution.
    3 - Negotiating design, content, and other non-programming pieces of the project. By asking what the options are, rather than setting a design on a single solution, our team was more flexible as the project evolved.


    My experience as a UI Designer and Information Architect honed my attention to detail and helped me ask the right questions when vetting user flow diagrams and Photoshop mockups. By approaching development as a designer, my perspective respects the end users experience while balancing the reality of managing complex software.

    This experience, from discussing an idea, to launching the application has equipped me to tackle large, ugly, complex projects in the future. But for now I am focused on perfecting Perq.
