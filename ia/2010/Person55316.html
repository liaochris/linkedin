Desmond Pieri’s Summary

Though not by nature a short-term type person, for the last ten years I’ve decided to focus on interim CEO assignments, completing ten at startups or turnarounds. Four were in the States, while six were at global companies headquartered in Europe. In some cases the founder asked me in, while in others it was the venture capitalist that initiated the assignment. Engagements generally ran 3 to 12 months, though some much longer. Each has been a hands-on, operational role, in some cases advising a founding CEO. Five times I have been the first businessperson to join a startup team -- two in interim roles, and three in long-term roles. I hold US and EU passports.

Desmond Pieri’s Specialties:

My industry expertise is broad, including mobile devices/cell phones, wireless, medical devices, application software, middleware, computer-telephony, speech recognition, computer hardware, video games, and electronic ink. Functionally, I have experience at large and small companies in senior executive management, business plan creation, executive team building, fundraising, sales management, business development, product management, marketing, strategic alliances, and mergers and acquisitions.

  • Interim COO

    Everypoint

    (Privately Held; Wireless industry)

    May 2008 — September 2009 (1 year 5 months)

    General Management in a Turnaround: Reporting to the founder in the role of Interim COO of a 15-person company funded by Fairhaven Capital Partners, Prism VentureWorks, and Venrock. We created a game-changing mobile phone app development platform for ‘feature’ phones, with the goal of delivering on the long-time promise of ‘write once, run anywhere.’ To counter the disbelief of the cell phone companies, we utilized the remaining 15% of the $14 million the VCs had invested to complete the product. We delivered on the ‘write once’ promise but, unfortunately, the market no longer cared; the success of the new Apple iPhone App Store caused our target market – apps for feature phones – to dry up because the industry was laser-focused on delivering apps for high-end ‘smart’ phones. Positioned Company for an IP exit.

  • Board Member, Interim President & COO

    Cecropia, Inc.

    (Privately Held; Computer Games industry)

    March 2006 — April 2008 (2 years 2 months)

    General Management in a Turnaround: A two-phase assignment as Interim President & COO of a Video Game Developer, reporting to founder Omar Khudari. In phase one I managed a team of 75, creating a video game aimed at non-gamers, combining the appeal of an animated film with a simple game mechanic. Finished the game on time and within budget while simultaneously downsizing the operation. In phase two, we built on the IP to explore a new kind of interactive advertisement, a Flash Comedy Skit, intended to be part of the Facebook strategy of large consumer products companies. Prototypes had broad appeal, but we decided not to re-launch the Company into this new, still unproven market segment.

  • Interim COO

    CR2

    (Privately Held; Banking industry)

    2004 — 2004 (less than a year)

    General Management in a Turnaround: In an interim role as COO, I reported to the founding CEO of a seven year old, unprofitable, €15 million, 150 person developer of software sold to banks and other financial institutions around the world. Changed some of the senior management team, recruited three strong Vice Presidents, and hired a solid new sales force. Reorganized the business around five ‘centers of excellence’ in London, Dublin, Dubai, Bangalore, and Perth. Created a new business plan and the first detailed budget in the company’s history. Put into practice new processes to assure the Company only bid on profitable business. Changed forecasting methods so that both bookings and revenue forecasts became reliable. Introduced conservative US-style revenue recognition rules. Implemented and/or revamped numerous processes to allow the Company to operate efficiently with a more effective work force. The results were that bookings and revenue were increased and six months’ of profits were obtained, the first profits in the Company’s existence.

  • Interim General Manager

    (Computer Software industry)

    2003 — 2003 (less than a year)

    General Management in a Turnaround: Reporting to founder and CEO Chris Horn in an interim role as VP and General Manager of the 35-person Enterprise Integration business unit, I was asked to determine why the unit had not lived up to revenue expectations. Traveling to customers and prospects in China, India, Southeast Asia, Europe, and the States, I concluded that enterprise application integration (EAI) would converge with other integration products. I recommended to Dr. Horn that the Company merge the product’s EAI functionality into Artix, the Company’s new family of service-oriented integration products. In addition, I recommended that development and support of the current EAI product be moved to a new development center in Beijing. Both recommendations were implemented.

  • Interim General Manager

    Eink

    (Information Technology and Services industry)

    2000 — 2001 (1 year )

    General Management in a Turnaround: Reporting to the CEO, Jim Iuliano in an interim role as General Manager of a 22-person group within a 100-person Atlas Venture funded company that originated out of MIT. Was charged with determining why divisional revenue targets had not been achieved. Although I possessed no prior knowledge of the product space (electronic, centrally controlled signage) or the target markets (retailers and consumer package goods companies), within weeks determined that success could not be immediately achieved with the current product in the existing markets. Created and managed four SWAT teams which rapidly conducted exhaustive research to find a new market opportunity for the Company’s technologies, the Ink-In-Motion line which is still in use today. Currently, E Ink’s technology is the display in the Amazon Kindle and the Sony Reader wireless reading devices. Eink was sold for over $215 million.
