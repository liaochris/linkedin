Imran Mohammed’s Experience

  • Postdoctoral Researcher

    University of Pennsylvania School of Medicine

    (Educational Institution; Research industry)

    May 2010 — Present (8 months)

    The complement system is a branch of innate immunity comprising over thirty plasma and cell surface proteins. Complement opsonizes and lyses pathogen and injured cells, clears immune complexes and sends inflammatory signal to recruit other components of the immune system to sites of injury. Three pathways can activate the complement system: classical (antibody-mediated), mannose-binding lectin (pattern- recognition molecules) and alternative (constitutive, spontaneous activation). There are inhibitors at almost all stages of complement activation. Cleavage of C3 is considered the key step in complement activation. Not surprisingly, a large number of complement inhibitors can inactivate C3b and C3 convertases on host cell membranes and in the plasma. Fluid phase inhibitors, such as factor I and factor H, and membrane-bound inhibitors such as decay-accelerating factor (DAF), membrane cofactor protein (MCP), complement receptor 1 (CR1), CD59 and murine complement receptor 1-related gene/protein y (Crry) can accelerate the dissociation of C3 convertases or act as a cofactor in factor I-mediated enzymatic inactivation of C3 convertases. The potentially damaging effects of complement are clearly demonstrated by the problems that arise when it is inappropriately regulated. Autoimmune and inflammatory diseases such as rheumatoid arthritis and systemic lupus erythematosus (SLE) and transplantation rejection are associated with improper control of complement. In addition, polymorphism in factor H has been central to many disorders such as Age-related macular degeneration (AMD) and Atypical hemolytic uremic syndrome.

    Currently, I am working on the complement regulators with active interest in deciphering the role of the membrane bound regulators (DAF, Crry and CD59) in AMD pathology. Additionally, I am also investigating the role of various complement components in Candida albicans induced (vision-threatening) fungal corneal infection.

Imran Mohammed’s Education

  • University of Nottingham

    Ph.D. , Ocular immunology , 2006 — 2010

    Project Title: Signal transduction pathways involved in expression of novel Antimicrobial peptides.
    Background: "The mainstay of my research involved characterisation of signalling pathways required for the expression of newly identified AMPs (Defensin Beta 109 and Ribonuclease-7) in the human corneal epithelial cell line following treatment with agonist of innate immune receptors (TLRs, NLRs and IL-1R). The key signalling molecules identified could be potentially used as therapeutic targets to enhance the ocular defense mechanism during sight threatening eye infections. The study employed mammalian cell culture, proteomic (immuno-blotting, ELISA, bead-cytometric arrays, FACS and immuno-detection) analysis to characterise the secretome and phosphoproteome of the cells in different states, supported by siRNA knockdown and quantitative PCR methodologies to provide understanding of the interactions and signalling mechanisms in AMP expression"
    Supervisor's: Prof. HS Dua & Dr. A Hopkinson
