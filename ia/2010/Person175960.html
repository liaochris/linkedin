Rob Sentse’s Summary

My name is Rob Sentse, B-SW (Bachelor-Social Work), and I am a Major in the Royal Netherlands Army (RNLA). Serving at several branches, I worked, amongst others, as an intelligence analyst, as a trainer in Leadership and Didactic skills and as a project officer implementing Field Humint in the RNLA. Currently I work as an Information Operations officer for 13th Mechanized Brigade.
In 2006 I was deployed to the Canadian lead Regional Command HQ in Kandahar as J2PLANS where I implemented the Fusion Cell.
In 2008 I served as G2X for Taskforce Urzugan in Tarin Kowt.
In 1997 I earned my Bachelor of Social Works from the Eindhoven University of Applied Sciences and graduated with my project about prevention programs against bullying, (sexual) intimidation, blackmail and other types of violence, for Dutch scholars aging 12-18. The project had attention in national newspapers and on national television and was implemented at several Dutch schools.
I worked in Bosnia in 2001 (Bosnia and Herzegovina and Republika Serpska, RS) where I implemented a Bosnia wide project to bring into contact the several veteran unions with the local care attribution. I built a social network consisting of the Minister of Health RS, mayors, psychiatrics, psychologists and Croat/Serb/Bosniac veterans’unions. The Project had attention at BiH National television and other national media.
I am a NISA participant. Netherlands Intelligence Studies Association.
As an author of several articles about terrorism and political and economical relations, my articles were published in several (military) magazines and newspapers.

System Theory, Behaviour Influence Techniques and Behaviour Symptom Analysis.

  • Staffofficer Information Operations

    Army

    (Military industry)

    November 2008 — Present (1 year 8 months)

    Future conflicts will be complex and non transparent, which will require a nation’s military to respond with flexibility, creativity and speed. Our traditional military way of thinking has evolved into an interagency way of acting.

    In our present and future areas of operations it will be hard to find a clear distinction between permissive, semi-permissive and non-permissive elements, as these three concepts tend to emerge at the same moment. The primary behaviour we would like to influence lies in urban areas. Future conflicts arise in part from the need for political freedom, power, water, food, energy and living space.

    The limits of present line staff organization require changes be made. One solution may be a process organization consisting of modular units. One of the positive effects of this model would be a decrease of restraints whilst solutions and creativity will be magnified causing desired effects immediately.

    In an interagency environment armed forces are to shape the conditions to create a secure environment for economy, politic, development and diplomacy. The commitment of IO’s and NGO’s is, amongst others, key in this.
    The head of a taskforce is a member of Foreign Affairs and has, amongst others, a Development advisor and a Military advisor at his disposal. This will give a broader perspective and a broader commitment.
    In such a structure NGO’s and IO’s can be included at crucial stages without compromising them.

    An interdepartmental staff element consists of members of Foreign Affairs, Development, Defence, Economic affairs and this staff element is a permanent board.

    It can be completed with a Political advisor and a Cultural advisor.
    Do they come from “our own” government? Or from the “area of operations” (the "local" government)? Obviously it is “their” end state to reach?
