  • Senior Research Scientist

    Intel Research

    (Public Company; INTC; Semiconductors industry)

    February 2004 — July 2010 (6 years 6 months)

    I joined the Cambridge lab in 2004 and then moved to the Berkeley Lab in 2006.

    From mid 2008 onwards, I have been leading the "Dispute Finder" project - which aims to let people know when information they read online is disputed by sources they would trust. This work has been well covered in the media, including New Scientist, National Public Radio, The Guardian, The San Francisco Chronicle, and a load more.

    From 2007 to mid 2008 I worked on Mash Maker, a tool that tries to understand the meaning of structured information on web sites and automatically combines this information together to provide you with information you might find useful. This work was also well covered by the media, including Slashdot, Techcrunch, MIT Technology Review, The Guardian, and others.

    From 2004 to 2007 I worked primarily on programming languages. Notable work included developing what was then the fastest implementation of Software Transactional Memory, and helping develop tools that allowed one to give C code safety guarantees.

    I also represent Intel at the W3C, in the HTML and Web Applications Working Groups.

  • Intern - Unix Systems Group

    The Santa Cruz Operation

    (Public Company; SCOC; Computer Software industry)

    June 1998 — September 1999 (1 year 4 months)

    This is "old-good SCO", who built the Unixware operating system, not "new-evil SCO" who bought their IP and sued everybody.

    I worked for SCO for two summers while I was an undergrad at Cambridge. While there, I built a kernel profiler + user interface, built a parallel performance profiler, ported parts of the IBM AIX networking stack to UnixWare, and fixed some minor standards compliance bugs.
