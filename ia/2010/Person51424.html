David Braun’s Summary

Goal: Identify and produce quality, compelling content that generates a large loyal audience for advertising and other profit opportunities to support creation and growth of a viable editorial enterprise in digital media.

Passionate newsman for 30+ years, working in the U.S., UK, and South Africa on daily and weekly newspapers, magazines, newsletters, radio, international wire, and online news services.

Covered science, environment, space, aviation, city desk, police, criminal courts, business, politics, state/national legislatures, executive branch, and foreign affairs--as a reporter, copy desk editor, revise editor, news editor, bureau chief, executive editor, and editor in chief.

Devised and launched in-house National Geographic daily online news service in 2001, growing it to 40 million visits per year in 2009. Negotiated and produced 180-part special news series on earth systems science sponsored by National Science Foundation.

Member of National Geographic Expeditions Council, National Geographic Editorial Council, and media representative to the NG Committee for Research and Exploration (CRE). Accompanied CRE scientists to 53 field sites in eight countries and directed production of hundreds of news features about NGS-supported research and exploration. Coverage of one of these projects won the 2008 AAAS Science Journalism Award in the online category.

Long-time member/committee member of news guilds/trade unions, press clubs, and professional associations. Editorial committee member of Online Publishers Association (OPA).

Participated in Knight Digital Media Foundation seminar "Best Practices: Covering Science in Cyberspace" (2007). Guest speaker at National College Newspaper Convention in San Francisco (2008), OPA Editorial Day in NYC (2008), and Online News Association Conference in Washington, D.C. (2008). Lectured journalism students in South Africa, U.S., Russia.

David Braun’s Specialties:

Editorial solutions and management, adapting editorial enterprise to Web production and emerging technologies

  • Web Producer (News)

    Nationalgeographic.com

    (Non-Profit; Publishing industry)

    March 2000 — December 2003 (3 years 10 months)

    Devised and launched National Geographic News, a daily online news service that started publishing on 01.01.01. Prior to this date, Nationalgeographic.com licensed news from an editorial contractor. I was employed as a Web producer to manage this contractor as well as produce the "News Guide."

    I worked with a software engineer and a designer to create a new Web home for news, which we started to produce ourselves in 2001. I acted as both News Editor and Web Producer, appointing contributors across the world and assigning to them stories, editing their copy, and producing the content on the Web.

    When the news service gathered steam and generated significant and increasing page views and advertising revenue, an additional editor was added to the newsroom so that we could increase the volume of daily stories.

    By 2003 the National Geographic online newsroom employed three full-time editors and some three dozen contributors.

    Web producer skills for this job required a strong news sense, writing/editing, HTML editing, Photoshop, basic video editing, working in different publishing systems, knowledge of sources for images and rights, a very close working relationship with the Tech group, and a firm grasp of what content resonated best with the Web audience.

  • Public Affairs Editor

    National Geographic Society

    (Non-Profit; Publishing industry)

    November 1997 — February 2000 (2 years 4 months)

    The only dedicated writer/editor in the National Geographic Society's Communications department, responsible for all written materials used by publicists and senior executives.

    The work included drafting and editing news releases, internal communications, the annual report, and speeches for the Society's most senior executives.

    I was also the media contact for National Geographic Magazine's Hebrew edition and assisted the publicists with their duties whenever required.

    The work enabled me to become very familiar with every division of the National Geographic Society. I received a thorough grounding in the workings of a public affairs operation for a big and complex nonprofit--a very valuable practical extension of the theories of publicity and marketing strategies I studied to get my degree in marketing management.

  • Washington Correspondent

    I started my short career here as a financial editor covering technology companies, and then as Washington Correspondent, covering Capitol Hill, the White House, and the FCC on issues such as Internet privacy, encryption, gambling, taxation, Y2K, censorship.

    Attended and reported on Internet World conventions in New York and Los Angeles; Microsoft's "CEO Summit" in Seattle (attended by Vice President Al Gore and more than 100 business leaders from 25 countries to discuss how technology was changing the future); the International Conference of Data Protection and Privacy, in Montreal; the Air Force Technology Conference, in Montgomery, Alabama; and the Gartner Symposium/ITxpo 1997, in Disney World, Florida.

    Separately for CMP, I produced a series of special advertising print supplements of about 10,000 words each, featuring in-depth coverage of topics such as network storage.

  • Editorial executive, Washington bureau chief, political correspondent

    Independent Newspapers of South Africa

    (Privately Held; Online Media industry)

    1976 — 1995 (19 years )

    Started with Africa's largest newspaper group on the Pretoria News in the company's journalist cadet school. Six months' tuition by the company's most accomplished correspondents, editors, and photographers, was followed by 18 months of indentured newsroom training, mentored by veteran beat reporters covering the criminal justice system, police, and city government.

    I broke service for two years, working as an assistant editor on Orchestra World, an international monthly magazine in London, and then as chief reporter of a business news agency, specializing in personal finance.

    Returned to Independent Newspapers as a reporter on the Johannesburg Star, Africa's largest daily, covering general news. Transferred to the business desk and then back to the newsroom as political reporter. In 1984, at age 29, was appointed chief political correspondent, covering the Presidency, executive branch, and the national legislature. Covered Presidential visits to Zaire, Germany, Switzerland, Portugal, and Cote d'Ivoire and was hosted by governments of the U.S., UK, Germany, and Taiwan on journalism programs in those countries.

    Appointed Washington bureau chief for 13 newspapers in 1989. Traveled with Nelson Mandela on 11-city tour of North America after his release from prison. Covered peace talks between South Africa and Angola, hosted by Fidel Castro in Havanna, and by Boutros Boutros-Ghali in Cairo.

    Transferred back to South Africa in 1991 as senior editorial executive on a morning newspaper, was promoted to No. 3 editor on the group's largest weekend newspaper, and then named news editor of a start-up national morning financial daily, responsible for coordination of the business desks of four regional newspapers and a network of international correspondents.

    Final assignments for the group included coverage of Polisario refugees in Algeria and Western Sahara and the attendance of President Thabo Mbeki at United Nations General Assembly in New York.
