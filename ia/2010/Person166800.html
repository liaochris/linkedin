Paul Black’s Summary

I graduated from Georgia State University College of Law in May 2010. On September 1st, I begin a year-long elder law fellowship at the Georgia Senior Legal Hotline and the Atlanta Legal Aid Society.The bulk of my time will be spent designing and conducting a survey of those Georgians over 60 who have received legal advice from the Senior Hotline. I'll also be working with students and faculty at GSU law to organize a spring 2011 panel on the prevention of physical and financial abuse of the the elderly.

Long-term, I hope to practice Elder Law (which would incorporate long-term care planning, wills/trusts/estates planning, financial and medical powers of attorney, advanced directives, and possibly include probate and disability law as well.

I am also very interested in local governance issues, in particular:

- the need for expanded public transit and transit-oriented development (such as the BeltLine will bring to Atlanta);

-the potential for greenspace preservation via land trusts, community benefit agreements, and transferable development rights (TDRs);

-the need for affordable & accessible elderly housing via Section 202 and other federal laws and federal-state-local government cooperation.

I already have a great network of contacts and mentors in the legal profession that I have cultivated over the past seven years that I have lived in Atlanta, but am always eager to meet new ones, especially in the above-mentioned areas.

As a former high school teacher, I often the pleasure of seeing former students on MARTA, at GSU, and elsewhere, and hearing about what they are doing. I am also always glad to answer questions that any prospective law students or others who are friends of members of my network might have.

Paul Black’s Specialties:

My interest in elder care law derives from dealing with my father's radically changed healthcare and financial needs after a severe stroke. I hope to help the growing aging population meet their unique and often challenging legal needs.

I also believe that affordable housing- especially for the elderly- should be a priority, and that greenspace conservation, in tandem with public transit and smart growth planning, can improve the quality of life for all Georgians, urban or otherwise.
