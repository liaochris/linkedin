Daan Vreugdenhil’s Summary

Anyone linking as a friend, will be accepted!

Lifetime conservationist, Daan Vreugdenhil, spent 4 decades dedicated to the conservation of nature and the environment. His work in both tropical and temperate regions includes experience in a variety of fields: multi-disciplinary management and planning of natural resources, seas and marine coasts (incl. scuba reconnaissance), multiple-use fresh-water systems, forest ecosystems, environmental impact assessments, GIS forest cover and ecosystem mapping, environmental monitoring systems and databases.

He became IYF project officer in 1969 and forestry officer with FAO, in 1974, participating in projects in South America and Africa. From 1979, he worked himself up to the level of senior administrator with the Netherlands' Ministry of Works and Water (Rijkswaterstaat is the Dutch equivalent of the USA Corps of Engineers), where he was responsible for three different water management programs over a period of 10 years. In 1992, he joined the IADB. In 1989, he founded the forebear of the World Institute for Conservation and Environment, at first to provide ecotourism services in support of conservation, which in 1995, expended to integral conservation support, when Dr. Vreugdenhil became its full time director. His assignments spanned Eastern Europe, Latin America, Africa and Asia.

In preparation of the Vth World Parks Congress in South Africa he led the IUCN task force on: “Comprehensive Protected Areas System Planning And Monitoring”. He designed the first the computer based protected areas analysis tool, “MICOSYS”, originally developed in 1992 for the World Bank/GEF. The programme also includes a protected areas costing module for both investment needs and operational costs. With 10 years of high level government financing in the Netherlands, and 15 years of data collecting and financing analyses of protected areas systems of the world, Dr. Vreugdenhil has become a leading authority on protected areas costing.

  • President

    Adopts A Ranger Foundation

    (Non-Profit; Environmental Services industry)

    January 2006 — Present (4 years 2 months)

    I work on improving the staffing situation of the protected areas in developing countries. There, the worldwide staffing need is about 175,000, of which 140,000 rangers and 35,000 professional and administrative staff. Since half of the protected areas in those countries lack any staff at all, and the other half is at least 50% short, AAR estimates that the shortage of rangers is about 105,000 and of other staff about 27,000.

    This is the single greatest inmediate threat to biodiversity loss in the world, probably 10 times greater than that of climate change. The difference with the climate change problem, which no longer can be halted, that this problem CAN be solved, but it requires a different approach to conservation.

    Visit our website to understand the problem: http://www.adopt-a-ranger.org
