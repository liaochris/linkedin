Patrick Pinhero’s Experience

  • Associate Professor

    University of Missouri

    (Educational Institution; 10,001 or more employees; Higher Education industry)

    September 2007 — Present (2 years 10 months)

    In brief, my research group works in three teams: (1) Nanoassembly and fabrication processes; (2) Nuclear fuels and materials; and (3) Surface modification and characterization. Together these teams are tasked with the following:

    * Researching nanoantenna-based collectors for energy harvesting applications.

    * Researching the fundamental mechanisms of thin film and nanocomposite assembly.

    * Researching dry separation techniques for selective removal and processing of fission products from SNF.

    * Researching fast neutron interactions with matter.

    * Researching aging and environmental effects on structural alloys, reflectors, moderators, and claddings used in-core in nuclear reactors.

    * Researching novel coatings and other surface modifications for reducing thermomechanical wear.

  • Research Scientist

    Idaho National Laboratory

    (Government Agency; 1001-5000 employees; Research industry)

    January 1999 — July 2007 (8 years 7 months)

    At the INL, Dr. Pinhero was research scientist leading efforts in materials science characterization, the physical chemistry of materials, and biological processes at surfaces. Highlights include:

    * Developed of solar nanoantenna or nanoelectromagnetic collecors (NECs).

    * Researched molten salt electrochemistry and pyroelectrometallurgical processes.

    * Researched fundamental mechanisms for localized aqueous corrosion.

    * Researched and developed advanced neutron absorber materials for safe spent nuclear fuel (SNF) storage.

    * Demonstrated that biofilm-formers and other microorganisms often implicated in microbial influenced corrosion (MIC) can survive large radiation doses indicative of interim wet SNF storage.

Additional Information

Patrick Pinhero’s Interests:

Prof. Pinhero is primarily interested in materials used in energy applications. These interests range from the design and synthesis of novel catalytic materials for use in high tempreture processes, to the pyro-electrochemical separation of fission products in spent nuclear fuel (SNF), to the impact of service environments on the life-cycle of materials in nuclear plants. Most of his work can be loosely categorized as applied surface science.
