Terry Kremin’s Summary

Develop new techniques and protocols to discover normal information flow in the brain, and then how drugs of abuse alter this flow to cause addiction, and to cause relapse. This should also help understand the biological processes involved in addiction-like disorders and most perseverative behavior disorders such as sexual addiction, eating disorders, obsessive/compulsive disorder, compulsive gambling, etc..

Developed a memory model that helps explain why these perseverative disorders occur, and points to ways to help attenuate these behaviors.

Goals - to establish a research program that utilizes and harnesses the innate plasticity of the brain and provide a framework and basis for development of new therapeutic treatments, honing of existing treatments, and the development of new pharmaceuticals that work with the plasticity of the brain instead of against it.

  • Postdoctoral Fellow

    UCSF/ Gallo Clinic & Research Center

    (Educational Institution; 10,001 or more employees; Research industry)

    February 2004 — September 2008 (4 years 8 months)

    Developed multi-site tetrode recording. Developed a new contextual behavioral task for use in operant paradigms to explore connectivity and control of behavioral guidance.
    Normal animals show a high degree of correlated activity between (hippocampus) and (nucleus accumbens) in normal food rewarded behaviors. Now need to compare how drugs of addiction and stress alter communication between the structures and impact the ability to reverse prior behaviors (similar to addiction relapse - failure to reverse drug seeking behavior). Goals are to understand the basic communication between these structures, and how these structures (both shown to be altered by addictive drugs, stress, abuse) change communication and organization after chronic drug use or other trauma. A better understanding will allow the development of better therapies and therapeutics for addiction and addiction-like disorders, PTSD, Depressions, and other perseverative behaviors.
