Theo Chakkapark’s Summary

Connecting the Dots (October 30th, 2007)

For years, associates and family members have questioned my goal of completing five majors. Their arguments were that it would take forever to accomplish; I was wasting time by staying in school for so long.

When I found myself as a software engineer at NASA, my boss, Gaye Graves, noticed I had a wide away of interests and focuses, and she was instrumental in connecting my own dots together. Although my primary job at NASA was to develop web applications, I soon found myself using my psychology, sociology, and social sciences knowledge running a lab part-time where we studied 3D cockpit displays.

The concept of human factors or human computer interaction (which tied parts of my CS and psychology knowledge together) came later when I attended a seminar presented by cognitive scientist Don Norman, who talked about product design, and emotions - the very core of what I was interested in, but never fully realized it until then.

A picture blossomed when I was at Stanford, working in the psychiatry labs doing emotions research. My CS knowledge was handy in designing programs to analyze and collect data, and my psychology and sociology knowledge bridged me to researchers and the data they collected.

No one regrets my decisions anymore, and I don’t regret the awkward path I’ve taken with respect to college and the notions of a career. My life feels fulfilling in many ways, with many people who are genuinely interested in having me incorporated into their futures.

I’m frequently asked, “What do you plan to do in the future?”, and the honest answer is, “I don’t know.”

Those kinds of answers are often scary, but when I say this response, I say it with utmost confidence. I’m not afraid of not knowing my future, because I know even greater things lie in it; I can accomplish anything because I am up to discovering and conditioning my mind for that future.
