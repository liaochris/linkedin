Michael Skarzynski’s Summary

Michael Skarzynski's career as public and private company CEO has spanned multiple high-growth business areas including media, advertising optimization, predictive analytics, network management, communications software and systems and distributed systems. He is the consummate customer-centric 24 X 7 executive with a strong track record of creating and driving profitable revenue growth, expanding businesses globally, and optimizing operating metrics. He has raised significant capital in public and private markets and has led venture exits as well as mergers and acquisitions to deliver outstanding shareholder value.

Skarzynski was appointed by President George H W Bush (http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/George_H._W._Bush) and confirmed by US Senate as Assistant Secretary of Commerce for Trade Development under Secretary Robert Mosbacher with portfolio in export promotion, export finance and trade negotiations.

In 2009, Skarzynski served as CEO of Arbitron NYSE:ARB, the US audience measurement company in radio, out-of-home and television markets that via partner, WPP surveys 7 countries in radio and tv.

Skarzynski led ARB to increase:
sales by 4.4% to $385 M
net income by 13.4% to $42.4 M
shareholder equity by 70% to $621 M.

Skarzynski served as CEO and Board Director of 3 ventures:
(1) Iptivia, network management venture acquired by Circadence
(2) Xebeo, IP switch venture acquired by UTStarcom NASDAQ:UTSI
(3) Predictive Networks, online ad venture acquired by Comcast NASDAQ:CMCSA.

Skarzynski led Performance Technologies NASDAQ:PTIX as CEO and increased enterprise value by 50+% to $97M.

Skarzynski served as VP at AT&T Network Systems/Lucent/Bell Labs, the telecommunications systems supplier that merged with Alcatel NYSE:ALU. He served as VP and Director at Motorola, Inc. NYSE:MOT and in the general manager development program at G.D. Searle & Co. now owned by Pfizer NYSE:PFE.

Skarzynski started his career at CSIS.

CEO and President, market visionary, operational optimization, turnarounds and restructuring, executive coaching and leadership development, organizational transformation, international operations, mergers and acquisitions -- Communications, Wireless, Mobile, Database, Transaction Processing, Analytics, Data Centers, Cloud Infrastructure, SaaS

  • Vice President

    Bell Labs/Lucent/AT&T Network Systems-Now Known as: AT&T/ Alcatel-Lucent/Avaya/LSI/VTech

    (Public Company; LU; Telecommunications industry)

    1992 — 1999 (7 years )

    Lucent Technologies was a technology company composed in large part of AT&T Network Systems which included Western Electric and Bell Labs. It was spun off from AT&T. Lucent merged with Alcatel SA of France to form Alcatel-Lucent in 2006.

    One of the primary reasons AT&T chose to spin off its equipment business was to permit it to profit from sales to competing telecommunications providers; these customers had previously shown reluctance to purchase from a direct competitor. Bell Labs brought prestige to the new company, as well as revenue from commercialization of tens of thousands of patents.

    Lucent became a "darling" stock of the investment community in the late 1990s, rising from a split-adjusted spinoff price of $7.56/share to a high of $84. However, beginning in January 2000, Lucent made a string of announcements that it had missed its earnings targets, and Lucent fell from grace. By October 2002, Lucent stock bottomed at fifty five cents.

    Lucent had 165,000 employees at its zenith. In April 2000, Lucent sold its Consumer Products unit to VTECH. In October 2000, Lucent spun off its PBX / Business Systems arm into Avaya, Inc., and in June 2002, it spun off its microelectronics division into Agere Systems.
