Michal Legiewicz’s Summary

More than ten years of biomedical research expertise in RNA biochemistry with emphasis on RNA catalysis, RNAs capable of target binding, RNA engineering by in vitro selection, RNA structure, and visualization/animation of macromolecules.

Advances were achieved using state of the art biochemical and biophysical techniques, with innovations acknowledged twice in 2008 and 2010 by receipt of the National Cancer Institute Director's Innovation Award for Career Development.

Vice-President of Sigma Xi Frederick Chapter. Editor for the Fellows and Young Investigators Association Newsletter of Center for Cancer Research, National Cancer Institute.

Current scientific interest: structure and function of large non-coding RNA domains in relation to virus replication and cancer.

Michal Legiewicz’s Specialties:

Research into RNA structure and function - ribozymes, aptamers, large viral regulatory elements (RRE HIV-1), high throughput chemical footprinting (Selective 2'-Hydroxyl Acylation analyzed by Primer Extension; SHAPE), antisense interfered SHAPE

Michal Legiewicz’s Experience

  • Research Fellow

    National Cancer Institute

    (Government Agency; 10,001 or more employees; Research industry)

    October 2006 — Present (3 years 7 months)

    Research into structure and function of viral RNAs and their interactions with proteins with the aim of identification of new targets and development of novel anticancer/antiviral strategies

    RNA chemical footprinting with SHAPE technology of HIV-1 Rev Response Element (RRE) and other retroelements:
    Discovery of a substantial change in RNA secondary structure within HIV-1 RRE that emerged due to two mutations as response to gene therapy against HIV.

    Application of high throughput SHAPE (htSHAPE) to interrogate mRNA structure and probe interactions between mRNA of oncoproteins:
    Implemented high-throughput chemical footprinting by combining SHAPE with advantages of capillary electrophoresis.Analysis of regulatory elements within 5'-UTRs on messenger RNAs that, when deregulated, cause cancer.

    Visualization and 3D animation of viral macromolecules
    Developed procedures to model, morph and animate viral macromolecules in three dimensions

  • Research Associate

    University of Colorado at Boulder

    (Educational Institution; 5001-10,000 employees; Higher Education industry)

    January 2003 — September 2006 (3 years 9 months)

    Generating and investigation of RNA aptamers in terms of the origin of the genetic code

    Applied in vitro selection (or SELEX; Systematic Evolution of Ligands by Exponential Enrichment) to generate RNA molecules that are capable of target binding (aptamers)
    Optimized the length of randomized region for starting pools to create an optimal environment for selecting for the most valuable RNAs. Noticeably, many aptamers of clinical importance were selected from pools with randomized positions that were set to arbitrary values (e.g. 40 nucleotides). Investigation of a series of different randomized RNA pools has proven that small aptamers are mostly frequent within pools that contain 50-70 randomized positions.

    Obtained a more complex isoleucine aptamer with stronger binding

    Developed a novel method of calculation of information content of RNA aptamers

  • Graduate Student

    Institute of Bioorganic Chemistry, Polish Academy of Sciences

    (Research industry)

    March 1998 — December 2002 (4 years 10 months)

    Research into HDV ribozyme with potential applications in biotechnology and therapy

    Evolved active HDV (Hepatitis Delta Virus) ribozyme variants with SELEX
    Obtained a HDV ribozyme mutant with significantly improved activity, opening novel avenues for applied research in which regulated degradation of particular RNA (e.g. viral RNA) is desired in vivo.

    Applied HDV ribozyme as a molecular trimmer
    HDV ribozyme has been successfully engaged as a potent, precisely controllable RNA cutting tool that is especially useful for generating in vitro transcripts with homogenous ends (e.g., in NMR or crystallographic applications).

    Characterized structural variants of HDV ribozyme to explore its requirements

    Mapped accessible sites of HDV ribozyme for oligonucleotide hybridization using semi-randomized DNA oligomers and RNaseH
