Gabrielle Songe’s Interests:

After growing up in the suburbs of Seattle and having never visited a farm, in 1998 my publisher sent me to photograph several dairy farms, and I was present for the fortuitous birth of a half Brahma-half Holstein calf. Learning the calf was headed to market in 30 days, at 11 days old, I adopted the little critter. Named Sonny, he was the protagonist in a number of my columns that later appeared first in print and then at my former Web site, Life on the Farm. Currently, I am writing an accompanying narrative for a collection of previously published columns. An excerpt from the forthcoming book follows.

Life on the Farm by G. Songe

An excerpt from the column: "Sonny has been practicing for the big night" (first published November 27, 1998 in Ripley, Miss.)

In anticipation of the Walnut Christmas parade and with the closing of the old two lanes of U.S. 72, last week Sonny took a little jaunt with me to town. He traversed our hilly driveway just fine and walked the pavement like he’d been doing it every day for all five months of his existence.

Sucking several of my fingers, sniffing the crushed buoy at the outskirts of Walnut town limits, and every now and then with a spurt of speed, kicking up his heels, Sonny had an adventure. And, I did my best to keep up with him.

We met along the way an assortment of Mississippi Department of Transportation workers, surveyors and crews putting up barricades and moving mailboxes to the new highway. Among these, Sonny could tell who raised cattle and who didn’t. He had his way of knowing, and I took his word for it.

Gabrielle Songe’s Honors:

We stopped at Burgers & Stuff for a small vanilla shake, which was OK with the bull. However, my fingers were still his preferred taste treat.

At his grandmother’s restaurant, we met Matt Martindale, and Sonny liked him a lot. Martindale said it was the first time he could recall a calf stopping by the take-out window. Well, we didn’t want to mention it, but some of the menu items are not exactly ones that would draw a lot of bull customers. He was such a nice young man, and we didn’t want to offend him, so we didn’t point out the obvious.

From there we cut across the park and walked up Munn Ave. When we go to busy Miss. 15, Sonny balked at the traffic, lost his footing and went down....
