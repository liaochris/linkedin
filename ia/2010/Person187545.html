Shannon Savage’s Summary

As a Ph.D. candidate in Ecology and Environmental Sciences at MSU I am currently conducting research on the use of Landsat satellite imagery for mapping geothermally active areas (GAAs) in Yellowstone National Park and studying the spatial patterns of change in GAAs over the last three decades. Through this degree I intend to become an expert in remote sensing and computer modeling to compliment my expertise in GIS.

My story before starting my Ph.D. program:

After becoming smitten with the idea of studying large predators while in high school, I decided to learn everything I could about wildlife and their ecosystems by earning a degree in Wildlife Biology with a minor in Range Ecology at Colorado State University (CSU). During my last semester at CSU I took the first Geographic Information Systems (GIS) course offered at the University. There I discovered what an amazing tool GIS is and I chose to pursue a career in geospatial sciences with an emphasis on natural resources.

In 1995 I secured a job at Pacific Meridian Resources (PMR), an environmental consulting firm specializing in GIS and remote sensing, in Portland, Oregon. Through three years of on-the-job training, I was able to gain excellent experience in the use of GIS and remote sensing in the field of natural resource management. This experience qualified me for my dream job, a GIS position at Yellowstone National Park, starting in 1998.

I was a GIS specialist at Yellowstone for seven and a half years. This was an excellent opportunity for me to learn about managing natural resources and ecosystems and apply my GIS skills to that effect. Meanwhile, I began a graduate program at Montana State University (MSU) while working full time at Yellowstone. In December of 2005, I graduated from MSU with an M.S. in Land Resources and Environmental Sciences. My thesis specialized in remote sensing and vegetation mapping and has given me generous amounts of experience within these fields.
