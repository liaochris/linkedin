Steven Bird’s Summary

I'm investigating computational methods for analyzing human language, in both written and spoken form. This involves scalable techniques for collecting and annotating large amounts of data from many languages. A long-term focus is the preservation of endangered languages, and I have been involved in substantial efforts with undescribed "tone languages" in Africa and Papua New Guinea. I have taught undergraduate and postgraduate courses in informatics, philosophy of language, artificial intelligence, natural language processing, data mining, web technologies, and machine translation. Programming is an almost daily activity, and I recently published a book "Natural Language Processing with Python".

Steven Bird’s Specialties:

natural language processing, linguistic databases, language archives, speech prosody, open source software development.

  • Consultant

    Ellis Island Foundation

    (Non-Profit; Non-Profit Organization Management industry)

    2001 — 2001 (less than a year)

    Designed a high-performance approximate name matching algorithm which is used in searching for genealogical records. The algorithm models errors made when European surnames were transcribed from speech, when arrival logs were keyboarded, and when microfilm copies were OCRed.

  • Research Fellow

    University of Edinburgh

    (Educational Institution; Research industry)

    October 1990 — June 1998 (7 years 9 months)

    I conducted post-doctoral research in the Centre for Cognitive Science, in the area of computational phonology, with funding from SERC and ESRC. One of the ESRC grants involved 2.5 years of fieldwork in Cameroon, and analysis of the prosodic system of the Yemba language (Grassfields Bantu).

  • Linguistics Consultant

    Société International de Linguistique, Cameroun

    (Research industry)

    1995 — 1997 (2 years )

    SIL's linguists do fieldwork in partnership with mother-tongue speakers. Together they engage in the collection, analysis, and organization of language data, with the aim of promoting the use of the language in various domains. The tangible result of this cooperative research is publication of technical articles, dictionaries, practical grammars, and written literature, both native-authored and translated. I consulted on a variety of language projects, focussing on the Grassfields Bantu group, helped with the development of 10 writing systems, and published the first dictionary of Yemba (300,000 speakers).
