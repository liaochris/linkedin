Conrad Parker’s Experience

  • Linux multimedia architect

    Renesas

    (Privately Held; Semiconductors industry)

    October 2009 — Present (8 months)

    Designing a Linux multimedia architecture for the SH-Mobile processor series. This involves specifying userspace device driver interfaces and developing support infrastructure to use multimedia-related hardware blocks, and co-ordinating open source development with partners and contractors. This allows customers to use hardware codecs (encoding and decoding of MPEG-4, H.264 etc.) and video processing blocks. The software stack includes OpenMAX IL components, GStreamer plugins, and custom streaming servers.

  • Invited Expert, Media Fragments WG

    W3C

    (Non-Profit; Internet industry)

    January 2009 — Present (1 year 5 months)

    The Media Fragments Working Group is developing specifications for
    addressing temporal and spatial media fragments in the Web using Uniform Resource Identifiers (URI).

    In some ways, the scope of this WG is a logical progression from the temporal addressing that we developed in Annodex, extending that to addressing spatial regions of video and other meaningful units. This involves not just URI syntax, but also specification of HTTP and RTSP negotiation.

  • President

    Annodex Association

    (Internet industry)

    December 2005 — Present (4 years 6 months)

    The Annodex Association oversees the development and promotes the adoption of Annodex, a set of open specifications and open source software for hyperlinked video and audio on the Internet.

    We strive to work closely with groups of similar interest including Xiph.Org (on Ogg Vorbis audio and Ogg Theora video codecs), W3C/WHAT-WG (on specification of the HTML5 <video> element) and Mozilla (on implementation of these in Firefox).

  • Engineer / Linux Multimedia Specialist

    Jinzai

    (Semiconductors industry)

    January 2009 — September 2009 (9 months)

    Designing Linux multimedia architecture for Renesas SH-Mobile processor series (working directly with Renesas). I'm creating open source libraries and tools to allow customers to use the hardware codecs (encoding and decoding of MPEG-4, H.264 etc.) available on the SH-Mobile Video Processing Unit. The software stack also includes OpenMAX IL components and GStreamer plugins.

  • Graduate student

    Kyoto University

    (Educational Institution; Research industry)

    October 2006 — September 2009 (3 years )

    My research covers areas of video search, mobile interfaces, ubiquitous computing (computational devices embedded in everyday objects) and distributed content management. I enjoy modelling the formal semantics of these systems (by which I mean "implementing them in Haskell") because I believe that there is no excuse for software failure.

    Kyoto University is regarded as the second most prestigious university in Japan -- every Japanese person I have met has mentioned that. I prefer my own reasons for choosing to come here: the University is very encouraging of creative thought, I found a great professor, and the scenery is magnificent. Kyoto is home to some of the world's finest gardens, making it a great place to sit and think, and to code.

  • Engineer

    Wikimedia Foundation

    (Non-Profit; Internet industry)

    January 2009 — July 2009 (7 months)

    Implemented improvements in HTTP transport and seeking of Ogg media in Mozilla Firefox, and integration with the oggz-chop server. This also builds on earlier work from the Annodex project, where we specified an optimized byte-range redirection protocol for accessing temporal segments of video.

    This work is part of a larger project for Ogg support in Mozilla Firefox, sponsored by Mozilla Corporation. For more details, see

  • President

    Sydney Linux Users Group

    (Non-Profit; 201-500 employees; Computer Software industry)

    2000 — 2002 (2 years )

    The Sydney Linux Users Group (SLUG) is a community of hobbyists and professionals with an interest in Linux and Free Software. It is based in Sydney, Australia, with a mailing list membership of around 600. SLUG has been running since 1993, with regular monthly meetings and occasional workshops.

    I had the pleasure of being involved during the first boom in the Internet industry. The bubble had many interesting effects on the community, as we suddenly found ourselves working with many large and well-funded companies. When the bubble burst, we got back to writing code and enjoying software freedom.

    During that time we also hosted linux.conf.au. My part taught me a lot about forming teams and managing people's motivation. Co-ordinating the activities of dozens of volunteers is all about creating trust, and then delegating that. When there's no carrot, and no stick, all you can do is fuel passion. Actually, it turns out that that's not such a bad idea in any environment :-)
