  • Chief of R&D and Managing Partner

    WebGroup Media, LLC.

    (Computer Software industry)

    November 2000 — Present (10 years )

    As the original founder and managing partner of WebGroup Media LLC (WGM) I've had nearly a decade of accountable, hands-on experience in all aspects of running a small, bootstrapped, technology company.

    My greatest joys at WGM have been receiving immediate feedback from people all over the world about our daily, incremental progress; and being free to follow my passions through new ideas and technology. I've been able to hone a generalist skillset by always working on something I found intellectually challenging and creatively satisfying; while still being directly accountable to the customers who have kept us in business.

We share all our source code and offer "Community" editions of our applications. We've built a reliable source of income based on a mix of commercial licenses and services (On-Demand software, SaaS, support, training, consulting, custom development).

  • Software Engineer

    3Di Systems

    (Computer Software industry)

    January 2000 — November 2000 (11 months)

    I worked as a subcontracted programmer at various companies through 3Di. It was a brief but enlightening experience that exposed me to many different company cultures and technology options.

    Webvision
    During my first couple months with 3Di I commuted to Torrence, CA to work with Webvision's team on several web development projects. I received specs and artwork from designers and implemented them in HTML/CSS. I have a fond memory of using a 'dumb terminal' during my first few days at Webvision, which was running a UNIX variant with Emacs for me to do all my work. I think it was supposed to be a joke, but I loved figuring out all the keystrokes.

    LACOE (Los Angeles County Office of Education)
    I was the lead programmer on the first phase of a custom content management system for LACOE. This was before there were a lot of good open source options like Joomla and Drupal. They pushed for Coldfusion (scripting) and Microsoft SQL Server (database), which weren't my first choices, but it was an interesting experience. One interesting feature, for the time, was inline editing and rearranging of content blocks in a somewhat WYSIWYG manner; crude by today's standards but a fun challenge when Coldfusion was still <CF> tag-based.

    Day Software
    When I was involved in 2000, Day was working on an ECMA-based content management system called Communique. A really interesting feature at the time was the ability to generate graphics with layers and alpha transparency on-the-fly. Many e-commerce shops used this to print fancy-looking pricing information on product images. Layers could also be used to change product color schemes (which, again, was pushing the envelope in 2000). I worked at the Day offices on Wilshire Blvd in L.A. as a web developer taking advantage of their technology for several client projects.

    I left 3Di to start WebGroup Media LLC.

  • Web Developer

    Santiago Financial, Inc.

    (Financial Services industry)

    January 1999 — August 1999 (8 months)

    At Santiago Financial, Inc. (SFI) I served as a web developer, adding dynamic functionality to web sites which catered to the financial and real estate industries. We primarily used Coldfusion and Access. I started to see the limitations of those tools pretty early, but they motivated me to learn ASP and PHP on my own time so I could propose better alternatives.

    I have fond memories of SFI, the work was interesting enough that I used to stay after hours with some co-workers to draw flowcharts on the whiteboards and dream about starting a software company to tackle more complex projects (eventually, Ben Halsted and I did exactly that!)

    I left SFI to take on larger development projects at 3Di Systems.

    Later on, I collaborated with SFI again when Ben Halsted and I (as WebGroup Media, LLC) built them a custom application for managing their home loan process. It was written in PHP3 had a couple interesting features for the time: it could generate complex loan documents as PDFs using information from a database; and it auto-saved changes during data-entry using a clever pre-Ajax browser trick (an "invisible" 1 pixel frame at the bottom of the browser that synchronized information in the background).

  • System Operator

    Psykosis Online (BBS)

    (Information Technology and Services industry)

    January 1994 — August 1996 (2 years 8 months)

    In January 1994 I started a BBS called Psykosis Online in the 714 area code (Orange County, California). Over the two and a half years of operation, it grew from a single dial-up line shared among friends to 8 concurrent lines shared by over 1,000 users (a big deal in those days). We raised revenue by offering advertising space on the log-in screen to several local computer parts shops. We also distributed a printed newsletter called 'Psykosis Offline'.

    We synchronized mail and discussion forums with other BBSes around the United States, Puerto Rico and Barbados. We also offered about two dozen "door games" -- text-based games that were often multiplayer (Legend of the Red Dragon, Falcon's Eye, Barren Realms Elite, Usurper, Blackjack, Poker, etc.)

    Before ISPs were wide-spread, we also offered Internet e-mail (and Gopher/WAIS/Archie/FTP) through FidoNet.

    In early 1996, I also set up a small MINUX box (a precursor to Linux) to share a dial-up Internet connection, and to provide "shell" accounts to our BBS users for a small monthly fee.

    Eventually, the relevance of the BBS started to wane as ISPs became more prevalent. I was also spending most of my time engrossed in figuring out FTP/HTTP and MUDs (multi-user text-based games with hundreds of concurrent players). These factors led to me shutting down the BBS in late 1996 to focus on the new possibilities provided by the Internet.
