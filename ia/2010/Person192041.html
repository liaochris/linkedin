Jim Hunt’s Summary

I was born in Yorkshire, England. Over the years I've lived in various places in England and Wales. I'm now living in the Haldon Hills, not far from the beaches of South Devon and the Atlantic swell of North Cornwall.

My interest in cars began at an unusually early age. My Dad was a motor engineer who owned two Citroen Light 15s. Those Citroens were the world's first ever mass produced saloons that used front wheel drive. They were pretty ancient even in those dim and distant days, and my Dad was always swapping bits from one to the other so that at least one of them would always be running. He encouraged me to help him whenever I could.

I wanted a car of my own of course, but I was far too young to drive a real car. As a teenager I took to building 1:32 scale electric powered model cars. We raced them very seriously, sometimes for 24 hours at a stretch. From time to time the "grown ups" would buy a complete car off me. More often I just got paid to rewind their motors to deliver the maximum possible power.

I started programming computers in the late 1960s, while I was still at school. In those days there were no computers in schools, so we had to build our own. The main components were a large pile of Post Office surplus relays, and lots of wire. Programmes were entered via a set of switches on the front panel. It took about an hour to persuade it to add 2 and 2, but it was extremely satisfying when it eventually produced the correct answer!

  • Engineering Software Consultant

    Myself

    (Computer Software industry)

    October 1983 — October 1992 (9 years 1 month)

    I went for an interview at IBM Hursley. It was a nice day and the sun was shining brightly, so I cycled there. I was wearing a tee shirt and jeans. For some strange reason all the others were wearing dark suits. After a long wait I eventually received a "Dear Jim" letter, so I became a research fellow instead of a suit.

    When the IBM PC came out the first thing I did was write an interrupt driven serial driver.

    When the first AT clones emerged, the first thing I did was write a clean room 8042 keyboard controller that also worked with an XT keyboard.

    When the 386 came out the first thing I did was write a protected mode debugger.

    When Hitachi wanted a clean room VGA BIOS to go with their first TFT controller I used that 386 debugger to help me write one for them.

    When the first 8514/A clone came out the first thing I did was write an X driver for it.

    Then IBM called. They wanted to port the Doom engine to the PowerPC, but they didn't want to pay.
