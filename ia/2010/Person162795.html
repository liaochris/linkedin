Peter Honeyman’s Summary

I'm an experimental computer scientist, the "natural science" branch of computer science research.

I was trained as a theoretician at Princeton University, but quickly had that wrung out of me in my first job at Bell Labs.

As the cataclysmic breakup of AT&T approached, I retreated to Princeton and the stability of academic life, this time on the other side of the desk.

A few years of teaching convinced me that academic research absent the instructional load was best for me (and my students!), so after a decade in New Jersey, I returned to Ann Arbor as a full-time scientist at the University of Michigan's newly formed Center for Information Technology Integration. And there I have remained for more than 20 years.

And in time, I found that I love teaching; it has been my great fortune to be given that opportunity by the Computer Science and Engineering division, where I teach graduate and undergraduate courses and have supervised nine PhD students.

  • Engineer

    Ann Arbor Terminals

    (Privately Held; 51-200 employees; Computer Hardware industry)

    May 1975 — August 1976 (1 year 4 months)

    I started as an Electronic Test Technician Trainee making $3.65/hr. After a 90-day burn-in, I was promoted to Electronic Test Technician at $4.25/hr! W00t! Four months later, I was moved into Engineering and put on salary -- $16K/yr!

    I spent my final eight months at Ann Arbor Terminals as the one-man research department, designing and building a prototype terminal that used RAM instead of shift-register memory. My lab bench consisted of breadboards, a soldering iron, fluxcore, a heat gun, freeze spray (forgive me, Mother Earth), lots of SSI and MSI chips, a KIM-1 single-board computer and a cassette recorder (for backup!). Mike Levine, Ed Zimmer, and Jerry Liebler taught me everything I knew, especially Jerry, who could glance at a schematic, immediately understand its function, and quickly explain how to do it with fewer parts.

    After I left, my successful proof-of-concept developed into the Ann Arbor Ambassador, one of the most popular glass terminals ... back in the day.
