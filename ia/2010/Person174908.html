Robert Land’s Summary

As a backgrounder, I have served in leadership positions for a handful of public companies and developed several private ventures based in the US as well as in London and Dubai before moving to Nashville in 2005.

Along with my work focused on changing the face of consumer marketing through the use of mobile technology with my venture Social Nomads, I serve as a navigator to driven CEOs, business owners and solo-entrepreneurs with a focus on brand and revenue growth through my advisory business Vision2Venture.

Regardless of the business you're in, the best way to measure what your customers are really thinking is by evaluating bottom line results and then work backwards to understand why. You'll eventually determine the indicators for brand equity, revenue and bottom line performance is based on delivering exceptional customer experiences. And this business indicator alone separates great brands from everyone else. If you're in the everyone else category, recognizing the need to so something about is a good start. And that's where Vision2Venture comes into play.

My work represents the culmination of many years of hard knocks, idea testing, executing risky and time sensitive initiatives, learning from the successes and failures, operating with extreme integrity and most importantly- participating with the right people.

Robert Land’s Experience

  • Managing Partner

    Vision2Venture Inc.

    (Management Consulting industry)

    March 2007 — Present (3 years 2 months)

    Vision2Venture at its core delivers "Business Therapy". We have taken our global business experience and packaged it up into a series of relevant services that can be effectively utilized by small to medium sized companies. Our approach melds strategy, infused marketing, technology and operational effectiveness into a brand and revenue growth asset that will keep your competition guessing.

  • Chief Information Officer

    i-Trax, Inc.

    (Public Company; Health, Wellness and Fitness industry)

    June 2005 — February 2007 (1 year 9 months)

    I joined i-Trax, (a publicly traded holding company) in June 28, 2005 as a member of the leadership team with the goal of re-positioning iTrax's largest aquisition, CHD Meridian Healthcare. CHD Meridian was the largest corporate healthcare service providers in the US. As the CIO for the corporation my role involved managing the information technology side of Sarbanes-Oxley, driving lean Sigma and Six Sigma adoption and transforming the business to operate in an end-to-end Internet enabled information access model. In addition, I had executive responsibility for numerous clients in support of the company’s sales and customer experience disciplines. The Walgreens Co. ultimately acquired i-Trax and merged CHD Meridian into their Take Care Health Division.

  • Executive VP-Marketing & Technology

    Outercurve, Inc.

    (Wireless industry)

    March 2003 — May 2005 (2 years 3 months)

    Outercurve was one of the first RIM Blackberry remarketers In the US. The company was focused exclusively on the financial services industry. After acquiring the Outercurve out of bankruptcy, I was part of a management team that set out to recapitalize and reposition the company as a Blackberry application service provider. My role focused on managing the strategy, technology, product development and marketing functions of the business.   

    We launched InfoEdge, a technology driven service that extended the Bloomberg and Reuter’s financial desktop along with the Wall Street Journal and Financial Times web news service to a Blackberry. The service freed brokers, analysts and investment bankers from their desks and provided anywhere, anytime access to critical alert driven market-changing information.

    My position involved expanding the business globally. As a result it required me to travel and live between London, Amsterdam, Bahrain, Saudi Arabia and Dubai. As part of a planned exit strategy, the company was sold to a private concern based in the UAE in 2006.

  • Vice President

    InteliHealth, an Aetna Company

    (Health, Wellness and Fitness industry)

    2001 — 2002 (1 year )

    InteliHealth is an industry leading Internet health portal and wholly owned subsidiary of Aetna in association with the Harvard Medical School.

    My involvement at InteliHealth focused on the strategy, marketing and business development functions. The impact of the dotcom bust impaired the company’s existing strategy of selling web-based health content. Searching for new revenue producing options, we identified an opportunity to leverage InteliHealth’s information and technology assets to retain existing Aetna customers and attract new corporate accounts. The idea spawned the development of an Internet based, on-demand health and wellness service marketed as “Simple Steps to a Healthier Life”.

    With the launch of Simple Steps, my focus shifted to supporting the selling efforts of regional Vice Presidents in retaining and closing new business for Aetna. Today, Simple Steps continues to serve as a key component to Aetna’s marketing strategy. I left the company at the time InteliHealth was being formally merged into Aetna.

  • Founder & CEO

    OnlyBrands, Inc.

    (Marketing and Advertising industry)

    December 1998 — February 2002 (3 years 3 months)

    In 1998, I launched OnlyBrands.com, an Internet marketing company that developed a portfolio of over one hundred plus vertical “OnlyBrands” web sites. The site that gained the most attention was OnlyMusicians.com.

    The best way to describe OnlyMusicians.com is to compare it to MySpace for unsigned music Artists, but with a carefully architected technology framework where an Artist could have their own site within a site along with the ability to participate in virtual local music scenes, sell their digital music offering and promoting the bands live shows. OnlyMusicians.com competed predominately with MP3.com but our focus was showcasing and feeding talent to A&R executives.

    At it’s peak, OnlyMusicians.com had approximately 14,000 independent Artists from twelve countries as participants in the service. The company was sold along with the associated e-commerce technology assets in 2001 to one of the largest online retailers of musical instruments.

  • Founder & CEO

    Adaptive Strategies, Inc.

    (Information Technology and Services industry)

    November 1983 — November 1998 (15 years 1 month)

    In 1983, I launched Adaptive Strategies, Inc. as an information technology solutions company focused on developing “interoperability” solutions that used the PC as an access point to information across the enterprise. The company's distinct advantage and ultimate success was predicated on surrounding myself with the best technical talent available, delivering tangible and fixed priced based solutions to complex business situations and remaining focused on servicing the customer.

    By 1990, Adaptive Strategies had developed a solid reputation with the top Fortune 1000 companies in the New York, Philadelphia and Washington, DC corridor. It was at this point of the company’s evolution that we transitioned into Client/Server Computing. Then came the call from Microsoft that changed everything.

    In 1991, Adaptive Strategies was selected as one of the original nineteen Microsoft Solution Provider Partners. Our revenue growth prompted expanding the business operations nationally. We operated offices in New York, Chicago and San Francisco with our headquarters situated in the Philadelphia suburbs.

    The company was sold to a private investment group in 1995.

  • Regional and Commercial OEM Marketing

    Digital Equipment Corporation

    (Public Company; DEC; Computer Hardware industry)

    November 1980 — December 1982 (2 years 2 months)

    In 1980, Digital Equipment Corporation (DEC) was the second largest company in the information technology and computer industry. It's hard to believe thirty years later there's only remnants of a company that clearly dominated. And it all was a result of the stubbornness of the founders and executives being out of touch when it came to marketing.

    My position involved managing commercial OEM accounts in the Mid-Atlantic region as well as supporting the launch and marketing of the DEC Rainbow (IBM PC clone) and MicroVax Computing line.

  • IT Analyst/Buyer

    Campbell Soup Company

    (Public Company; CPB; Food & Beverages industry)

    June 1977 — October 1980 (3 years 5 months)

    I was initially hired as a summer intern in the position of Information Center analyst at Campbell Soup's World Headquarters in Camden, NJ. As a result of my performance, I was offered an opportunity to stay on with the company full time with the premise of completing my under graduate degree at Villanova University in the evening. After two years in IT, I was promoted to work in corporate purchasing as a buyer- responsible for IT, office systems and managing the Wharton Econometric Forecasting Associates (WEFA) forecasting modeling system for commodity buyers. My time at Campbell Soup served as a valuable foundation for my career and understanding of corproate and global business as a whole.
