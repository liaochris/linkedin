Paul Paradise’s Summary

I grew up in a family-owned business that started with 1 employee and has grown to 5, I've worked at the largest software company in the world, and now I'm having a blast at a small startup, while still being the part-time IT geek for my parents on the occasional weekend.

As a developer, I'm a strong proponent of the Agile Manifesto; I prefer the simplest solution that does the job right, but won't stand for a hack that makes it too simple at the expense of future work; I value the diversity of knowledge from my coworkers; I don't enjoy re-inventing the wheel; and I work best in small, focused groups with iterative development over a big-bang approach.

As an entrepreneur, I see myself one day in a family-owned business of my own, but I know there's a lot to learn before I get there.

  • Software Design Engineer

    Microsoft (Find My Stuff)

    (Public Company; 10,001 or more employees; MSFT; Computer Software industry)

    January 2006 — November 2006 (11 months)

    The Find My Stuff team produced two different products: Windows Desktop Search (known earlier as MSN Desktop Search), and an unreleased (but publicly disclosed) product called Windows Search Preview.

    I was a developer for Windows Desktop Search 3.0, which combined Microsoft's disparate search strategy across MSN, Windows Vista, Office, and Sharepoint into one unified search engine. I was the only developer working on the user interface for the Windows XP version of WDS 3.0, which uses the same underlying technology as the search engine built in to Windows Vista.

    In November 2006, the Find My Stuff team was merged in with the Windows Experience team, with an expanded charter to deliver a solution for finding and organizing data across all of Windows.

  • Software Design Engineer

    Microsoft (MSN Messenger)

    (Public Company; 10,001 or more employees; MSFT; Computer Software industry)

    June 2004 — January 2006 (1 year 8 months)

    MSN Messenger (now called Windows Live Messenger) is one of the largest instant messaging networks in the world, operating in 40 markets with millions of simultaneous logged-in users.

    During my time on the Messenger team:
    1. I was the primary developer for Winks, a feature first introduced in threedegrees allowing friends to send animations with sound to their friends. Unlike the threedegrees feature, Winks in Messenger introduced a new revenue source for Microsoft and third-party content partners.
    2. I worked on the video conversation feature, which allows full-screen webcam functionality with sound over Messenger's P2P platform.
    3. I worked on the replication engine of the sharing folders feature.
    4. I implemented a simple way for features within Messenger to deactivate themselves if signaled by the central servers, to prevent the spread of potential viruses and worms.
    5. I was one of the active bloggers on the team blog, MessengerSays.

  • Software Design Engineer

    Microsoft (Netgen)

    (Public Company; 10,001 or more employees; MSFT; Computer Software industry)

    August 2003 — May 2004 (10 months)

    The Netgen team was an incubation group at Microsoft responsible for creating products for the "Internet Generation" - 13-24 year olds. Coming here straight from college, I was the youngest employee on the team (by a whole 3 days) and had the awesome job of writing software for a target market that included me.

    We developed an application called threedegrees, which was a social application that helped people hang out online together. It was built on the windows P2P networking framework, and one of the first real IPv6 applications.

    I was responsible for winks, a feature that let you send animations and sounds to your friends; and the group folder functionality, a simple file sharing tool to broadcast files to your friends.

    The Netgen team was disbanded in 2004, with the majority of the ideas from the project and the employees moving into the MSN Messenger team.
