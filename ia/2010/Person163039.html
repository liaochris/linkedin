Peter Paul Bud’s Summary

Peter’s life has included three defining moments—his parent’s determination to escape with a four-year-old son from a worn torn Hungary; IBM’s decision to hire an ordinary, wet behind the ears, college graduate as part of its highly skilled sales force; and, ultimately, getting laid off thirty-one years later, presenting Peter with an opportunity to reinvent himself as an author.

KNOW OF A GOOD SALES GIMMICK OR STEAK PLACE?

I’m sure you enjoy a nice dinner out as much as I do. But, how would you feel if the manager took your steak back to the kitchen and said this after you informed the waiter your food tasted a bit odd? “We tasted your steak and it tastes the way it’s supposed to taste.” Unbelievable! That night, the restaurant we frequented lost six customers due to a lack of appreciation for the value of customer satisfaction. And, that’s just part of the story.

Understanding that “the customer is always right" is a key principle every successful sales person should understand. It’s a point as important as selling with honesty and integrity—like your mother is watching. If you believe sales gimmicks work, read no further.

These topics and others are in my new book, How To Be A Best Seller -18 Simple and Proven Steps You Must Know to Succeed, based on my thirty-one years in sales and marketing at IBM. As a recent pleased reader said, “It’s like good advice from a friend.” Learn from what one of the best sales organizations in the world taught me about selling; from my mistakes, successes and personal experiences.

Like someone once said, “Things may come to those who wait, but only what’s left by those who hustle.” Your success is waiting. Shouldn’t you start improving today? Please have a look at my book. Thank you.

    5 TRAITS Everyone Who Sells Something to Somebody Should Have:
    > A passion for what you do.
    > Enthusiasm every day.
    > Honesty and integrity.
    > Belief in your product or service.
    > Persistence...a never-give-up frame of mind.

    > Closer - properly asking for the order.
    > Partner - as a trustworthy source for further guidance and support.

  • Program Manager and Sales

    IBM Global Finance

    (Financial Services industry)

    2000 — 2007 (7 years )

    Continuing from my role noted below, my department was merged into the IBM Global Finance division of IBM and blended into the resale activities associated with off-lease equipment.

    For one year, I pursued a new business opportunity I had proposed - allowing end-users to utilize our industry knowledge, distribution channel and processes to sign on with us to dispose of "their" excess inventory, for a fee. The program was called eXIT (eXcess Inventory Transition) program. It was a creative concept and modestly successful. This was also the second time in my IBM career that IBM made an official brochure of a concept I developed.

    I took a primary position as a Program Manger/Trader (sales) for PC's, Point-of-Sale, Production Printers, Industrial Printers, ATM/Banking equipment, Terminals and misc OEM equipment - some as-is, some refurbished by IBM. Managing inventories, finding reseller partners and selling, were my primary responsibility until I was caught up in a round of lay-offs with a group of over-50 year old employees in June of 2007. After 31 successful years at a great company, it was a blessing in disguise, as this personal event lead to my re-inventing myself as an author who is able to share much of what I learned, experienced and believe with a vast audience of sales people. Please have a look at my book. Thank you.

  • Program Manager and Sales

    IBM Personal Computer Company

    (Computer Hardware industry)

    1993 — 2000 (7 years )

    This may have been the most challenging, fascinating and rewarding position in my career - I was invited to take a major role in building a program from scratch to dispose of the IBM PC Company's excess and surplus inventory. I love creating vs being the next person to do a job. I traveled extensively in the US to recruit quality reseller partners, developed the processes and procedures for selling our surplus and was actively involved with the new contract written to support our relationship. After the new program was set, I assumed the sole sales position of selling to our new (and growing) network of resellers who dedicated themselves to the secondary market. Many tens of millions of dollars (cost) of goods were sold annually. Our department was very successful.

  • Sales Account Executive

    IBM Personal Computer Company

    (Computer Hardware industry)

    1989 — 1992 (3 years )

    After voluntarily stepping down from second-line management for quality-of-life and family reasons (explained in my book for the benefit of others thinking along the same lines), I relocated to the Rochester, NY area as an IBM Sales Account Executive selling personal computers and associated products on a team to Kodak. I rediscovered the joys and rewards one can find in selling!

  • Branch Manager

    IBM National Distribution Division

    (Computer Hardware industry)

    1988 — 1988 (less than a year)

    The timing of this big, sought-after, step wasn't the best - not long after my twins were born and my dad passed away - but the six of us in my family relocated after I accepted a position in downtown Boston as a second line Branch Manager for New England (excluding CT) in my division. I had responsibility for sizable sales objectives and for managing many aspects of a sales office with about 80 managers, staff and sales people. Travel, meetings, customer sessions, long hours and one heck of an education in advanced manager's school at IBM was part of the role. This second-line management experience/position contributed to a challenging and rewarding assignment and ultimately to a career-changing personal decision (a chapter in my book is dedicated to this.)

  • Staff, then progressing into management positions

    IBM National Distribution Division

    (Business Supplies and Equipment industry)

    1983 — 1987 (4 years )

    During the early '80's, IBM changed from selling office equipment via direct sales reps to selling through Authorized Dealers. I moved to a staff assignment that was responsible for effectively communicating new product, T&C and program announcements to our US dealer network. From there, I was promoted to Marketing Manager with a responsibility for managing sales people, selling and supporting regional office equipment resellers. Then, I was moved to another group as a Marketing Manager for a team selling higher revenue PC's to our personal computer reseller network, while continuing to manage people. That led to another promotion - a stint at 590 Madison Avenue in NYC as Northeast Area Programs Manager in our regional office, responsible for all marketing/sales programs. I'll never forget the 2 hour and 20 minute commute, each way, in the hopes of earning a significant promotion to Branch Manager.

  • Sales Rep and staff positions

    IBM Office Products Division

    (Business Supplies and Equipment industry)

    1976 — 1982 (6 years )

    I started with IBM in Boston, selling typewriters, word processing and dictating equipment, supplies and copiers after extensive sales school and field training by IBM. They were the most "fun" years of my career, in hindsight. I progressed from having my own sales territory, to overlaying other reps as an "Educational Sales Specialist." After a couple years, I was invited back to IBM's sales school as a "guest instructor" during a session. From 1980-1982, I was promoted to a headquarters staff position with the responsibility of analyzing and reporting on competitors' products, recent announcements and marketing activities. This also included active participation in new product development. A headquarters staff marketing position gave me an entirely new perspective in contrast to being a face-to-face salesman, expanding my experience.

    During my tenure as an overlay "Education Sales Specialist," I developed a new concept for the classroom - The Graduated Learning Method. This approach encouraged schools to place basic typewriters in a classroom, and then advance students through a series of more sophisticated word processing equipment, all sourced from IBM. The program's success, which facilitated the sale of high end and more profitable equipment, rewarded me with a recognition bonus and an official IBM brochure (available nationally) that is framed and still on my home office wall today.
