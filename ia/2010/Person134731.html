Mark Trexler’s Summary

Why Climatography? Because I think it's a useful way to think about charting a course from "here" to "there" when it comes to successfully addressing climate change.

I've focused on bridging business and environmental objectives in addressing climate change, including 20 years working with companies ranging from electric utilities to Stonyfield Farm and Kinross Gold to anticipate and plan for both the risks and opportunities of a carbon-constrained world. An area of particular focus has been the development of decision-support tools to help companies understand climate-related risks and opportunities, to forecast carbon prices, and manage future risk.

I’ve been lucky enough to be at the cutting edge of the climate change mitigation field since it was "born" with Jim Hansen's testimony in 1988. In evaluating climate mitigation efforts at any level we need to ask the key question, namely "how well is it working or will it work, and can we do better?" It's a question we don't ask ourselves nearly often enough.

Carbon offset markets are a case in point, and have been a focus of mine since working on the first offset project in 1988 at the World Resources Institute. Carbon markets are not an "end" in and of themselves, and indeed that's a dangerous approach. Carbon markets are a means to an environmental end, and if we're going to use them we have to make sure we're truly advancing climate change mitigation efforts. That's the only way to accomplish both business and environmental objectives, and to make sure market mechanisms are available when we really need them for future compliance purposes. We haven't done a great job with carbon markets, and that's why they remain so contentious more than 20 years after the first offset projects.

Mark Trexler’s Education

  • University of California, Berkeley

    Ph.D. , International Environmental Policy , 1982 — 1990

    My doctoral work at the Goldman School of Public Policy specialized in implementation theory, with a focus on international environmental cooperation and international environmental treaties.

    Implementation theory seeks to anticipate and avoid predictable pitfalls in program design and implementation. Implementation theory asks difficult questions that we would often prefer to ignore. When starting my dissertation in 1983 on the Convention on International Trade in Endangered Species (CITES), I realized that while CITES was labeled a huge success, no one had ever asked “is it working" to help endangered species?

    If there’s anything implementation theory can reliably tell us about climate change policy, it’s that if we don’t constantly ask “is it working,” we will almost certainly end up failing to successfully address climate change. Asking the question is a key job for anyone wanting to be a climatographer.

    Activities and Societies:
    I served as the teaching assistant for the school's graduate level course in econometrics, was a member of the school's Admissions Committee, and was also active in the Environmental Law Society at UC Davis.
