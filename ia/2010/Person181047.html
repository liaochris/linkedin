Sam Dicker’s Summary

Principal Software Engineer with over 25 years of experience developing industry leading interactive multimedia products. Experienced in overcoming seemingly insurmountable odds to push the limits of hardware, with dramatic marketing results. For example, developed a software upgrade for the Creative Labs Sound Blaster Live!, which provided 32 sources of 3D sound on hardware only designed for 8, allowing Creative to maintain a market lead. Experienced in planning research and development tasks for teams of engineers to meet project milestones and delivery dates.

Sam Dicker’s Specialties:

3D audio spatialization, panning and mixing, algorithmic and sampled sound analysis, synthesis and music sequencing, voice communication and telephony, 3D graphics and animation with hierarchical models, motion blending, texture mapping, antialiasing, motion-blur and shaders, video game design, tuning, scoring, diagnostics and player internet integration, multi-tasking kernel, 2D graphics and image processing, 2D and 3D collision detection, sound, image and animation data compression

  • Senior Software Engineer

    Crystal Dynamics

    (Public Company; 51-200 employees; SEG.L; Computer Games industry)

    January 1994 — March 1996 (2 years 3 months)

    In the company's R & D Group, responsible for designing and implementing tools and runtime code for 32 bit console games. Designed and implemented a multi-platform 3D graphics engine and art processing tools. The challenge was to create a system that would allow artists and animators to port 3D models and animations from Alias PowerAnimator to the Sega Saturn and Sony Playstation, and to create an easy to program 3D graphics engine capable of efficiently displaying textured 3D models with perspective correction and hidden-surface removal and capable of blending compressed hierarchical animation sequences into continuous motion. Results were "3D Baseball" (http://tinyurl.com/2ck9jp) with "Real-Motion Control" and other 3D games developed at Crystal Dynamics which used this engine, tools and technology.

Designed and implemented a fully parameterized automatic gain control (AGC) algorithm that could process 16 bit, stereo samples at 44.1KHz using less than 10% of the 25MHz 68040 CPU. Designed and implemented a family of high-quality sample rate converters for 680X0 and PowerPC platforms.

  • Software Engineer

    Amiga Corporation and Commodore-Amiga Incorporated

    (Computer Games industry)

    June 1983 — June 1986 (3 years 1 month)

    Participated in the design of the sound hardware architecture and designed and implemented the sound driver. The driver was developed concurrently with the Amiga Exec multi-tasking kernel. It was designed for both total flexibility and high-performance. The Amiga Sound Driver provided shared access to the sound hardware with low overhead and sufficient performance to discourage the common practice of game programmers writing directly to the hardware. Participated in the design of the ROM kernel and produced a series of compelling demos of the technology. As a startup the Amiga demos were essential to the effort of attracting investors and provided a high-performance test bed for the ROM kernel. The demos including "Boing" the Amiga bouncing ball helped it receive rave review in the press and permanent cult status among sophisticated computer users and programmers.

  • Lead Game Programmer

    Williams Electronics

    (Computer Games industry)

    May 1980 — May 1983 (3 years 1 month)

    "Defender": Game Programmer responsible for special effects and sound on the company's first video game. Generated dramatic explosion effects and a scrolling terrain at 60Hz non-interlaced in a frame buffer with an unassisted 8 bit microprocessor. Programmed a wide range of sound effects using another 8 bit microprocessor and a DAC. Also programmed an extensive diagnostics package. Defender's cinematic graphics and sounds helped it become the 2nd highest earning coin-operated video game in history (estimated $1 billion US). It also earned the "1981 AMOA Video Game of the Year" award.

    "Sinistar": Lead Game Programmer responsible for multi-tasking kernel, game play and animation, some special effects and character animation. Although "Sinistar" was a difficult game to play, video game experts consider it a classic.
