Terrell Hull, MSIM, PMP’s Summary

A veteran IT leader with over 20 years' experience and a deep technical background, Terrell has earned a reputation as a "consummate software professional." His ruthless focus on results-oriented management, optimal business-IT alignment, and lean, evidence-driven IT investments "contribute directly to the client's bottom line." A "rare blend of intellect and action," his "approachable demeanor" helps him close gaps between executive management and IT. With a broad IT background ranging from financial, automotive, logistics, marketing, genetic research, and robotics, to defense and aerospace projects, Terrell is a leading authority on requirements engineering, managing projects, object technology, and enterprise architecture.

NORAD hired Terrell based on a referral from Grady Booch to oversee the Space Warning Systems Center (SWSC) upgrade, where he developed a set of OO design metrics to achieve quality control objectives, which were presented at the World Software Technology Conference by the team. In July, 2006, Terrell conducted a seminar at Northrop Grumman for the Software Process Improvement Network (SoCal SPIN) entitled "Use Cases: A Holistic Approach to Requirements Extraction for Aerospace Systems," attended by an audience of aerospace dignitaries and military leaders (see link).

Dr. Harvey Deitel, author of Java: How to Program, dedicated his 5th ed. to Terrell for his "commitment to excellence in teaching and writing about... object technology," and for his "extraordinary contributions [to Prentice Hall publications]." He has been featured as a distinguished speaker at numerous industry conferences, such as Int'l Advisor DevCon Java/Websphere Conference, ObjectWest, LogicWorks Intl, and elsewhere.

Designed and coded a "thunking" application (thunking is the transformation between 16-bit and 32-bit instruction formats ) to connect a 16-bit Windows 3.11 application (developed by Wheb systems) to a 32-bit Windows NT DLL (created by a third-party company), in fulfillment of an ultimatum from VISA (their largest client) to demonstrate the compatibility of their application with Windows NT, after Microsoft consultants had called the task impossible, and refused to participate.

    • The resulting thunking application did the "impossible," reconciling a 16-bit application with a 32-bit DLL in such a manner that the application remained untouched, as did the DLL, by introducing a series of marshaling layers standing in for the two components that coordinating complex interactions, including successfully transmitting 10,000+ element three-dimensional collections that often were allocated in the application and freed in the DLL, and vice-versa.
