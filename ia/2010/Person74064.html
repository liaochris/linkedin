Geoff Bristow’s Summary

Chairman/CEO with experience across IT industry, in particular in semiconductors, software and financial modelling.

By predominently working with small startups in the software industry, Geoff aims to keep himself available for fast-reaction turnaround/restructuring assignments of larger corporations in the IT space on an ad-hoc basis.

Having lived/worked across the USA and Europe, Geoff has now formed an operating "base camp" in Barcelona, Spain.

Career falls into 4 phases:

1) University (EE at Imperial, PhD from Cambridge)
2) Corporate (Texas Instruments, ICL)
3) Rescue/turnround (15 deals as CEO or Chairman)
4) Portfolio Chairman/Angel (since 1994)

Geoff has worked in all sizes of company (from small startup to large corporate) and in most aspects of the IT industry (hardware and software, from the smallest chips to the largest mainframes), and throughout the supply chain (from IP, to devices, to OEM and finally consumer devices)

He is passionate about the arts, is a keen runner and loves anthing that involves two wheels (with or without motor). He is a Chartered Engineer, a Fellow of the RSA, and a Freeman of the City of London.

Geoff Bristow’s Specialties:

Corporate turnaround in the IT sector, as CEO on contract or permanent assignment.

I am fluent in both finance and technology...enabling me to work with a top team to quickly create a new strategy and then execute on it.

For example, see case studies below on Arc International, Alphameric, and Wordplex, or click on press cuttings link above.

  • President and CEO

    ARC International

    (Public Company; ARK; Semiconductors industry)

    May 2009 — December 2009 (8 months)

    Became CEO at a time of great uncertainty for ARC, despite it being admired for its technology worldwide, with more than a billion consumer devices having been shipped with its intellectual property inside.

    Under the imaginative stewardship of the previous CEO the company had been transformed into a well-respected multimedia focussed solutions-IP company. Nevertheless, against the background of world recession, and in the shadow of a well established competitor more than 10x the size, the company had never made a profit and its cash resources were dwindling.

    By taking some previously unpalletable decisions, such as closing the company's flagship UK headquarters where many of the designs had been inspired and brought to life years earlier, the business was brought to a viable ongoing financial basis. Meanwhile the company's future engine of innovation was assured by focussing and further investing in its premium design centres in St Petersburg, Russia; San Jose, California; and Hyderabad, India.

    In October 2009 we were able to announce the unconditional sale of the company to Virage Logic via an all-cash offer to all of its 7000+ London Stock Exchange shareholders.

  • Chief Operating Officer

    Poqet Computer Corp

    (Computer Software industry)

    1991 — 1993 (2 years )

    Based in Santa Clara, California, Poqet invented and manufactured the Worlds first hand-held computer, fore-runner to the Palm Pilot and the Psion organiser.

    Subsequent innovations included redesigning the initial Poqet into the Worlds first wireless pen computer, fore-runner to today's PDAs, incorporating world's first 2.4GHz WiFi in a hand-held.

    Originally a $60m start-up, Poqet was successfully sold to Fujitsu in 1992.

  • Executive Chairman

    Alphameric

    (Public Company; Computer Software industry)

    December 1989 — August 1990 (9 months)

    In December 1989 this full-list plc on the London Stock Exchange was on the brink of receivership, having hit a cash crisis after making numerous acquisitions.

    Working against the clock through the Christmas break, Bristow enlisted the help of renowned industry strategists Robb Wilmot and Alex Reid to put together an emergency package of rescue finance and turnaround strategy.

    Following presentation of the rescue proposals in January 1990, Bristow was invited by the board to become Exec chairman on contract to put them into effect.

    A new top team was identified, including Rodney Hornstein as CEO, who led the recovery and subsequent growth over many years and subsequently became chairman until 2007. The company is still publicly traded on the London Stock Exchange.

  • Chief Executive

    Wordplex Information Systems plc

    (Computer Software industry)

    1986 — 1987 (1 year )

    A rescue pre-receivership prompted by a cash crisis. Wordplex, a full-list plc on the London Stock Exchange, was the UK's leading provider of word processing systems, especially to London's large legal practices. However, the company had not responded to the challenge from the newly emerging Personal Computer.

    Bristow was introduced to the board by the company's stockbrokers and invited to present a turnaround strategy, following the presentation of which he was asked to become CEO to put it into effect. Following a strategic redirection to refocus the company as a global systems integrator, it was sold to Norwegian public group, Norsk Data, at three times the original share price.

  • Director of Network Products

    ICL plc

    (Computer Software industry)

    1984 — 1986 (2 years )

    At that time ICL was UK's answer to IBM, a $2billion mainframe and computer systems group. Bristow's responsibility was the networking and communications products and software in all their guises, spreading across 10 sites with 650 staff.

    Projects managed by Bristow's team included the networking technology required for the initial computerisation of the UK government's PAYE (income tax) and DSS (social security) systems.

  • Profit Centre Manager

    Texas Instruments

    (Computer Software industry)

    1979 — 1984 (5 years )

    Five years at global semiconductor giant Texas Instruments during which Bristow set up and ran the "Marketing Business Centre" for North Europe and subsequently became the European Profit Centre manager for microsystems and custom silicon devices.

    Bristow's responsibility included the TMS1000, at the time the world's best selling single-chip microcontroller, surpassing 60million units already in 1982. In addition the TMS320 Digital Signal Processor was launched, which is now one of the mainstays of TI's portfolio.
