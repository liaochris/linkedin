Experienced microbiologist with a strong background in microbial physiology, genetics, molecular biology, microscopy, biochemistry and assay development. Demonstrate the ability to advance scientific projects both independently and in collaboration with other scientists, manage projects and draft manuscripts.

Zehra Tuzun Guvener’s Experience

  • Senior Research Scientist

    University of Wyoming

    (Educational Institution; Higher Education industry)

    April 2010 — Present (5 months)

    Cyclic-di-nucleotide signaling mechanisms in the Gram-positive bacterium and the opportunistic human pathogen Listeria monocytogenes. Conducting genetic and biochemical analysis of di-guanylate and adenylate cylases of Listeria monocytogenes.

  • Acting Instructor

    University of Washington

    (Educational Institution; Higher Education industry)

    January 2007 — August 2009 (2 years 8 months)

    Areas of investigation include identification of Pseudomonas aeruginosa novel type 6 secretion system (T6SS)-exported substrates with importance in disease biomarkers development by comparative proteomics approach, timing of expression by reporter gene fusions, RT-PCR and DNA microarray technologies, subcellular protein localization and characterization of post-translational “on” and “off” switch mechanism of the quorum-induced T6SS.

  • Senior Fellow

    University of Washington

    (Educational Institution; Higher Education industry)

    February 2005 — December 2006 (1 year 11 months)

    Investigation of an opportunistic human pathogen Pseudomonas aeruginosa, in particular, a systematic approach characterize subcellular localization of chemosensory-like signaling proteins by developing and implementing quantitative fluorescent protein fusions expression in live-cells by epifluorescence microscopy. Established distinct sensing and response capabilities of non-growing cells; starvation-induced homologous chemotaxis proteins (Che2) form complexes that localize to physically distinct foci inside cells. Discovery of unusual subcellular localization characteristics pertinent to novel signaling proteins called Wsp, involved in biofilm formation through modulation of cyclic-di-GMP production. Demonstrated unusual behavior of WspA, the dedicated sensor proteins trapped in multiple patches, and the output response regulator WspR self-aggregates when phosphorylated. Discovery of a stimulatory condition associated with growth on solid surfaces which activates Wsp signaling, a promising system in discovery of identification of environmental stimulus which triggers P. aeruginosa biofilm formation.

    Related links:
    http://www3.interscience.wiley.com/cgi- bin/fulltext/118630073/PDFSTART
    http://www3.interscience.wiley.com/cgi-bin/fulltext/118542282/PDFSTART

  • Postdoctoral Research Scholar

    University of Iowa

    (Educational Institution; Higher Education industry)

    January 2001 — January 2005 (4 years 1 month)

    Research on a marine bacterium and an emerging human pathogen Vibrio parahaemolyticus, in particular, identification of a gene cluster responsible for capsular polysaccharide (CPS) production in opaque and translucent V. parahaemolyticus variants and several unlinked regulatory genes upregulating and downregulating CPS production. Defined a role in CPS regulation for the newly identified AAA+ superfamily of an ATP-interacting protein, which participates in cyclic-di-GMP signaling pathway in V. parahaemolyticus. Collaborating with other scientists, discovered a large set of genetic elements important in biofilm matrix and biofilm development of the opaque and translucent V. parahaemolyticus variants.

    Related links:
    http://jb.asm.org/cgi/reprint/185/18/5431
    http://www3.interscience.wiley.com/cgi-bin/fulltext/118659665/PDFSTART

  • Research Assistant

    University of Wisconsin-Madison

    (Educational Institution; Higher Education industry)

    January 1991 — December 2000 (10 years )

    A doctoral work focused on how glucose represses gene expression in the soil bacterium Bacillus subtilis. Developed and optimized an in vitro biochemical assay enabling characterization of the specificity of DNA binding activity of CcpA, a key regulator in the B. subtilis glucose repression. The work established that CcpA binding to catabolite-responsive cis-acting element from alpha-amylase promoter is specific, the first evidence up to that date CcpA binding to a site-specific DNA. In focused with CcpA structure-function relationship, investigated the importance of several residues in the DNA binding and the N-terminal core subdomains in glucose repression.

    Related link:
    http://jb.asm.org/cgi/reprint/177/17/5129?view=long&pmid=7665492

  • Summer Intern

    Molecular biology of avermectin biosynthesis gene cluster from Streptomyces avermitilis.
