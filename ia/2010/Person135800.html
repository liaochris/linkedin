Martin (Marty) Smith’s Summary

You don’t know what you don’t know may seem a strange career summary. Jobs where new learning is available form a common thread. Spent five years at Vassar College: four as student, one as Assistant Director of Admissions. Left Vassar for Procter and Gamble to take advantage of their great sales training program. Traded training and P&G systems to M&M/Mars. Created first sales information system at M&M using a PC purchased with a personal loan (banker thought I was nuts). PC’s were expensive in 1982. Used VisiCalc and Dbase (remember those?). My territory management system got noticed. Accepted a position in M&M's National Office working with a great team developing Sales, Management, Analysis, Reporting and Telecommunications (SMART) system. Last job at M&M was first Region Marketing Manager working in Chicago office. Doug Wheeler, a former M&M VP, convinced me to sell NutraSweet for Monsanto. Selling food ingredients sounded different. Worked for Mary Kay O'Connor (great boss) as Director of Dairy Sales. We moved NutraSweet through patent expiration without losing Nabisco, Dannon or Nestle. Left NutraSweet to chase a dream starting Found Objects with Janet McKean. Found Objects sold funky gifts to specialty gifts stores. Found Objects distributed the first Magnetic Poetry Kits (words on magnets on refrigerators). Magnetic Poetry Kit enjoyed huge success becoming the “Pet Rock” of our time. We created FoundObjects.com, a B2C and B2B web site featured in O, Redbook and Elle Décor. The site is closed, but you can see our designs by typing FoundObjects.com into the Way Back Machine. Divorce closed Found Objects. I learned of a new chance to learn e-commerce lessons in Hillsborough NC not far from the old Found Objects' offices. I became Director of E-Commerce at Townsend Enterprises where I manage a great team, several million dollar+ e-commerce web sites and OPM (other people's money).

Martin (Marty) Smith’s Specialties:

All marketers are storytellers. I come from southern storytellers. Mixing Faulkner with Margaret Mitchell helped sell bar soap to Wegman's in upstate New York, sweetener to H. J. Heinz and strange gifts to millions. Now some of my stories spin out on web pages, get pulled into search engines and reach clean, well lighted places around the world.
