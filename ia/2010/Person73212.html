Gary Slack’s Summary

I am the founder of Slack Barshinger & Partners, one of the nation's first integrated marketing communications agencies and one of the very few worldwide that works exclusively with business marketers.

I also am the elected national chairman of the Business Marketing Association, the business marketing profession's leading professional society in the U.S., serving nearly 2,500 members via a 21-chapter network.

Gary Slack’s Specialties:

Integrated business-to-business marketing, including digital demand generation and digital customer engagement

Gary Slack’s Experience

  • Chairman

    Business Marketing Association

    (Non-Profit; Marketing and Advertising industry)

    June 2009 — Present (9 months)

    In June 2009, I was elected chairman of the Business Marketing Association, the business-marketing profession's leading professional society, serving the professional development and networking needs of nearly 2,500 members in 21 chapter markets across the U.S.

    In this role and working with a stellar 28-person board, we are continuing previous efforts to improve the BMA value proposition, increase membership, start new chapters, elevate the annual national conference and more. Learn more about BMA at http://www.marketing.org.

  • Chairman & Chief Experience Officer

    Slack Barshinger

    (Privately Held; Marketing and Advertising industry)

    April 1995 — Present (14 years 11 months)

    For nearly 22 years, I have led Slack Barshinger, one of the nation's first integrated marketing communications agencies and one of the very few worldwide that works exclusively with business-to-business marketers.

    While our name has changed several times, we have never veered one iota from this market position. In 2004, IMC authorities Don and Heidi Schultz singled us out as one of four "standout" IMC agencies in the U.S. Both the Business Marketing Association and BtoB magazine have accorded us national "Agency of the Year" honors many times in the past decade.

    Over the years, our agency has helped:

    • Dow Corning rebrand its Dow Corning® and Xiameter® brands
    • Fellowes launch the world's first jam-proof shredder
    • The NutraSweet Co. market NutraSweet® brand sweetener to food and beverage firms
    • The U.S. beverage industry promote recycling
    • eBay launch eBay Business (its business portal)
    • Dean Foods launch Milk Chugs into key sales channels
    • Illinois Power survive and thrive under electricity deregulation
    • Grainger launch a massive new fastener line
    • Silgan Containers launch Quick Top ends for steel food cans
    • Treasury Dept. launch the online version of the Electronic Federal Tax Payment System
    • The Department of Homeland Security promote emergency preparedness to businesses
    • Tetra Pak Inc. launch substitute beverage and food packaging in the U.S.

    Today, our principal clients include Aon, ArcelorMittal, the Association of Equipment Manufacturers, CCH, Diebold, Dow Corning, Fellowes, General Electric, Google, John Buck Company, National Starch, Nuveen Investments, Office Depot, PharMEDium, RedPrairie, Research in Motion, Siemens, Tellabs, Underwriters Laboratories and Wildman Harrold.

    We also have provided pro bono support to Chicago 2016, Economic Club of Chicago, Chicago Innovation Awards, Chicago Council for Science and Technology, Dartmouth College, Chicago Biotech Network, Ad Council and Sustain Our Great Lakes.

  • Co-Founder and Board Member

    B2BWorks

    (Privately Held; Marketing and Advertising industry)

    February 1999 — March 2003 (4 years 2 months)

    While keeping my day job, in 1999 I co-founded B2BWorks, the nation's first and largest online business-to-business advertising network and patterned after DoubleClick in its early days.

    Bill Furlong, who dreamed up the idea and served as CEO, and Bill Walker, a b-to-b agency executive, were the other co-founders. While I was never involved in day-to-day management, Slack Barshinger bootstrapped the operation for the first six months, and in October 1999 the fledgling company received $2.3 million in first-round funding from PRIMEDIA's IndustryClick, R.R. Donnelley & Sons, and IDG Ventures.

    Over the next three years, B2BWorks would attract new investors, including two Sand Hill Road venture capital firms (Convergence Partners and Menlo Ventures), and receive two more rounds of funding totaling more than $17 million from these and additional investors.

    By 2002, the digital media firm had built an unparalleled b-to-b advertising network delivering more than 17 million unique business executive visitors monthly through a network of 500+ site affiliates serving more than 60 vertical industry segments.

    Unfortunately, B2BWorks was ahead of its time and was challenged from day one by too many rudimentary business publisher sites, uneducated b-to-b buyers and pre broadband bandwidth. The two Bills and I relinquished our roles around 2004, and the firm morphed into a digital marketing services firm called ClearGauge.

    This experience, though, was highly valuable to Slack Barshinger in that it set the stage for major investments in digital marketing capabilities, which in later years proved helpful in attracting eBay and Google, among others, as agency clients.

  • President

    Slack Myers & Barshinger

    (Privately Held; Marketing and Advertising industry)

    May 1991 — March 1995 (3 years 11 months)

    These were years of steady, if not spectacular, growth for our agency, whose name changed again to reflect the departure of Jim Brown and the increased role of Don Barshinger.

    During these years, the agency expanded its food and beverage industry client base to include Nabisco, Procter & Gamble, Quaker Oats, Gatorade, Mott's, E.J. Brach and the International Dairy Foods Association, for which we served as the b-to-b agency for the larger Milk Mustache campaign from the mid-1990s to 2002.

    Also during this time, the agency diversified into new sectors, including the utility industry (Illinois Power), information services (ACNielsen, Newspaper Association of America), technology and IT services (Inforte and Bell + Howell) and environmental issues (Institute of Scrap Recycling Industries, Paperboard Packaging Council).

    In 1995, reflecting the departure of John Myers, we made one final name change that has stuck for nearly 15 years—Slack Barshinger.

  • Managing Director

    Slack Brown & Myers

    (Privately Held; Marketing and Advertising industry)

    October 1989 — April 1991 (1 year 7 months)

    Our agency name changed 18 months later to reflect Stu Lasky's departure and the significant contributions of John Myers, who helped us build client relationships with a variety of consumer packaged goods companies, including Tropicana, Kellogg's and Keebler.

    During this brief time, Don Barshinger joined the agency as a freelance copywriter and quickly established himself as a key contributor to the agency's success, becoming full-time within six months and promoted to an associate creative director soon after.

  • Managing Partner

    Slack Lasky Brown

    (Privately Held; Marketing and Advertising industry)

    June 1988 — September 1989 (1 year 4 months)

    Until one starts and has to run a business on his or her own, any and all previous GM experience in a bigger company, while helpful, does not fully prepare you for what is to come.

    We were luckier than most agency start-ups, able to found our new enterprise with more than $1 million in billings, thanks to The NutraSweet Company moving its account to us (eternal thanks, Tom, Kevin and Thym), and six employees.

    My initial two partners were Stuart Lasky, who'd been a terrific client at NutraSweet, and Jim Brown, an outstanding art director at DDB Needham who'd helped create some very effective early campaigns for NutraSweet targeting the food and beverage industry.

    With NutraSweet as our charter client, job one was both to grow NutraSweet and bring in other clients to reduce our reliance on any single client. We did so with a big assignment to launch the new environmental affairs division of the National Soft Drink Association and assignments from other food-ingredient and packaging clients, including the U.S. account of Tetra Pak Inc.

  • Sr. Vice President & General Manager

    Doremus & Company

    (Privately Held; OMC; Marketing and Advertising industry)

    September 1986 — June 1988 (1 year 10 months)

    By early 1986, I felt I was ready to run a business unit, and I also wanted to get more experience in related communications disciplines such as advertising and direct marketing.

    To their credit, Jack Porter and Bill Novelli uncovered an "in the (Omnicom) family" opportunity for me to head the Chicago office of New York-based Doremus & Company, the financial communications and business-to-business marketing arm of Omnicom.

    For 22 months, I helped revitalize the office, attract new clients and increase revenue. Along the way, I led the consolidation of Omnicom agency Fletcher Mayo into the Doremus fold. It was in the GM role at Doremus where I gained the business-management experience necessary to even think about breaking away to start my own firm, which I did in June 1988 at the age of 33.

  • Sr. Vice President, Group Account Director

    Doremus Porter Novelli

    (Privately Held; OMC; Marketing and Advertising industry)

    May 1984 — August 1986 (2 years 4 months)

    In 1984, I was made a senior vice president of the agency, whose name would soon evolve from Needham Porter Novelli to Doremus Porter Novelli as the powers that be at Omnicom saw a better fit with Doremus, a financial communications agency already in the fold.

    With offices established in New York and Los Angeles, the agency's attention turned to Chicago. In 1985 I was asked by Jack Porter and Bill Novelli to transfer to the Windy City to help with the integration of the Public Relations Board, a 30-person PR agency that Omnicom had acquired as part of the growth plan for Doremus Porter Novelli.

    Shorty before and while in Chicago, I helped attract and then lead The NutraSweet Company's business-to-business marketing account. I also managed the firm's Schering Plough business.

  • Vice President, Account Director

    Needham Porter Novelli

    (Public Company; OMC; Public Relations and Communications industry)

    July 1982 — April 1984 (1 year 10 months)

    After five years in writing and mid-level account-management positions in Porter Novelli's Washington, DC, office, Jack Porter and Bill Novelli promoted me to vice president and a member of the firm's board of managers at the age of 27.

    By this time, Porter Novelli was called Needham Porter Novelli as a result of the acquisition of the agency in 1981 by Needham Harper & Steers, the forerunner of Omnicom.

    With the help of Chet Burger, a Needham consultant who had matched Porter Novelli with Needham, the agency was formally repositioned as a public relations firm, even though for years to come social marketing and marketing communications would be two great Porter Novelli strengths and differentiators.

    During and after the acquisition, I was privileged to meet some true advertising greats in Paul Harper, Blair Vedder, Norval Stephens, Keith Reihard and Bob Savard.

  • Senior Associate

    Porter Novelli & Associates

    (Public Company; OMC; Public Relations and Communications industry)

    July 1980 — June 1982 (2 years )

    After three years as a writer and copywriter, in 1980 I moved into a full-time account management position, keeping most of my writing responsibilities.

    With Porter Novelli at this time making big push to diversify its client base, I focused almost exclusively on managing the firm's existing trade-association clients and helping land others.

    Despite my age, somewhat long hair and baby-face looks, I was able to develop close relationships with three brilliant but very different business leaders—Dwight Reed of NSDA, Michael Dunn of CMI and Henry Thomas and Chuck Elkins of the EPA..

    In these and later years, I was physically situated near Jack Porter, Bill Novelli, Bill Matassoni, Randi Thompson, Terry Baugh and future Porter Novelli global CEO Bob Druckenmiller—pretty heady company and all great mentors and great businesspeople.

    Recently, at a roast held in Washington, DC, for Bill Novelli, the outgoing CEO of AARP, I had the opportunity to reconnect with Bill, Randi, Terry and Bob: http://bit.ly/1tMVBP

  • Copywriter

    Porter Novelli & Associates

    (Public Company; OMC; Public Relations and Communications industry)

    March 1978 — June 1980 (2 years 4 months)

    In early 1978, I was made a copywriter and joined the agency's creative department. While I supported many of the firm's government and social marketing clients, my primary writing responsibilities were for the Environmental Protection Agency and our primary trade association clients at the time, the Can Manufacturers Institute and the National Soft Drink Association.

    In these years, Porter Novelli was still based on the Georgetown part of Washington, DC, close to the C&O Canal towpath along the Potomac River, the site of almost daily long-distance runs led by Mike Carberry and Bill Novellli. I was never in better shape!

    Still a small firm, Porter Novelli had two great traditions—a unique holiday card (a tradition I've continued) and a potluck holiday party where first-year employees staged skits making fun of the "oldsters." Sadly, these traditions have given way, but you can see 18 years' worth of our holiday cards at: http://www.slackbarshinger.com/tradition

  • Writer

    Porter Novelli & Associates

    (Public Company; OMC; Public Relations and Communications industry)

    April 1977 — March 1978 (1 year )

    While living (and freezing) in Vermont and trying to establish a freelance writing career, I was encouraged by Francis Xavier Slevin, my high school English teacher, to apply for a writing job with a "social marketing" firm in Washington, DC.

    Called Porter Novelli & Associaties, the firm was headed by two former Madison Avenue executives who had chucked their commercial marketing careers to run the department of public affairs of the Peace Corps and, in 1972, found their new firm, whose mission was to apply commercial marketing principles to social issues, causes and concerns.

    Traveling down to Washington, DC, I met Jack Porter, Bill Novelli, Bob Druckenmiller and Mike Carberry, then the firm's four principals, and took a writing test. Despite sending a thank-you note back to "Mike Porter," I evidently did well enough on the test to be hired.

  • Photographer

    Nat Kaplan Homes

    (Architecture & Planning industry)

    A Nat Kaplan home in Albuquerque is virtually priceless today, due to 100% adobe construction (nothing faked) and striking, spare, innovative designs. Gary Slack’s Education

  • Dartmouth College

    B.A. , Economics , 1972 — 1976

    Thanks to my high school soccer coach, Dick Reddington, who also was Dartmouth's New Mexico enrollment director, three out of a senior class of 50 boys were accepted, an amazing feat on Dick's part. My freshman year, I joined the staff of The Dartmouth, where my first story lead, "There is a shortage of workers at Thayer Dining Hall," still gets me kidded 35 years later. Thinking the beautiful Connecticut river would be a nice place to spend fall afternoons otherwise not occupied bugging administrators for quotes, I joined freshman crew only to quit four weeks later when I realized rowers have no time to enjoy the scenery. After one year in a dorm, I moved to the Coop House, an experiment in group living and the antithesis of a fraternity. One of my proudest moments was being named editor-in-chief and chairman of the board of proprietors of The Dartmouth. Many years later, our oldest son, Alex, followed in these footsteps by serving as editorial board chair of the Harvard Crimson.

    Activities and Societies:
    Editor-in-Chief and Chairman of the Board of Proprietors of The Dartmouth, the oldest daily college newspaper in America, 1974-75; member of Casque & Gauntlet honor society; Dow Jones intern, summer of 1975; freshman crew, 1972; resident of the Dartmouth Cooperative House (an experimental "anti-fraternity" group-living unit), 1974-76.

  • Albuquerque Academy

    1967 — 1972

    A truly unique secondary school, Albuquerque Academy produces more National Merit Scholarship finalists every year than do most private and secondary high schools in the U.S., despite drawing its student body from a relatively small geographic region. Thanks to one of the largest endowments of any secondary school in the U.S., it also is 100% needs blind and, as a result, has one of the most diverse student bodies of comparable schools. I was admitted as a 7th grader to the new campus, high up on the northeast mesa, when it opened in 1967, thanks to a Simms family cash and land grant, which has fueled the school's professionally managed endowment. Today I proudly serve as a member of the board of trustees and serve on the endowment management and governance committees.

    Activities and Societies:
    Yearbook editor, newspaper editor, captain of the varsity soccer and baseball teams, member of the junior varsity football and basketball teams, recipient of the Faculty Prize at graduation.
