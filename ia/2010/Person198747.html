  • Electrical and Test Engineer - Mafersa Push/Pull

    STV Incorporated / CDOT

    (Architecture & Planning industry)

    June 2006 — Present (4 years 3 months)

    Provided installation, technical support, and design solutions for compatibility testing for Mafersa push-pull cars for Connecticut Department of Transportation (CDOT). CDOT procured 33 push-pull cars from Virginia Railway Express that needed to be modified to work on the Connecticut infrastucture. Subsystems operated from different voltages (32VDC, 64VDC, 120VAC, 230VAC) and modification to the power distribution was required. Work included specifying and changing out contactors and protection devices as well as modifying general arrangements and corresponding drawings.

    Connecticut also required the addition of the Advanced Civil Speed Enforement System (ACSES), which is the train control system used on the Northeast-Corridor (NEC). ACSES equipment needed to be installed underneath the car and inside the equipment lockers. All new equipment required conformance to the Federal Railroad Administration (FRA) regulations found in 49 CFR 238.

    Due to the drastic difference in environmental conditions from Virginia to Connecticut, snow ingestion problems led to significant failures of the 480VAC to 74VDC low voltage power supply (LVPS) and battery charger (BC). The failure mode identified was failure of the transformer insulation, which caused 480VAC to flash over to the secondary side of the transformer. As a result, all cars experienced multiple failures of subsystem power supplies (wheel-slde, communications, lighting, and cab control circuits). A crowbar circuit was designed to apply a short circuit across the output of the LVPS and BC units when the voltage exceeded 95VDC. Careful consideration was taken to only fire the SCR in the crowbar during a true failure and not during the normal operation of the car battery voltage range (50-90VDC). The long term solution was to redesign the LVPS and BC systems. Power consumption and protection were re-evaluated. Currently the design review is complete and the units are entering production.

    (Entertainment industry)

    Provided engineering support for all electrical modifications to eight NJ Transit multi-level cars for the fitting of food and beverage equipment as part of a 3-year trial of the Atlantic City Express Service (ACES) to Atlantic City, NJ, from New York City. The modifications include additional 480VAC and 120VAC power reticulation to the on-board food service kiosk, together with the fitting and coordination of added protective devices.

All modifications are bolt-ons with careful attention to preserving original base car reliability and maintainability. Due to the power limits of the existing car 120VAC transformer, the cars were fitted with auxiliary transformers to operate coffee machines, multiple refrigerators, microwaves, and LCD monitor and DVD equipment. Equipment wiring was carefully routed to use existing cable trays and minimize car body interior paneling.

  • Electrical Engineer - Nippon Sharyo Gallery Cars

    STV Incorporated / VRE

    (Architecture & Planning industry)

    June 2006 — January 2009 (2 years 8 months)

    Provided design review and verification testing for the lighting and communications system equipment for Virginia Railway Express (VRE). The vehicle is based on the Chicago METRA gallery cars, with changes in the lighting and communication system for reliability reasons. Lighting system design was reviewed and went through two design reviews. The first used a single inverter to convert 32VDC to 120AC to provide power for the fluorescent ballasts. The first proposal was disapproved based on the reliability information calculated during the Failure Mode, Effects and Criticality Analysis (FMECA). The single point failure was not reliable enough for a safety critical system like emergency lighting. The second proposal used 32VDC inverter ballasts instead of 120VAC ballasts. The communications system was a completely new design with a new equipment supplier. VRE also did not have an automated passenger information system on their previous equipment. Typically GPS is used to identify vehicle location on the system, but with all of the tunnels in the infrastructure a GPS signal cannot be used alone. The alternative to GPS was to use a distance counting method. Multiple measurements were taken between each station on the Manassas Line and Fredericksburg Line. Once a train route has been entered by the crew, the system will synchronize each stop and count the distance traveled from the speed sensors installed on each axle. Multiple trigger points were used to coordinate the automatic announcements, door opening/closing signals, and additional passenger information. Verification testing required design review meetings in Montreal, QC and Washington, DC to identify shortcomings (mainly system integration between the door system, communications system, and monitoring system) and design field modifications to correct these integration issues.

  • Test Engineer - Comet II & V Push/Pull

    STV Incorporated / NJT

    (Architecture & Planning industry)

    January 2005 — April 2006 (1 year 4 months)

    Tested Comet II and Comet V cars to determine the cause of 10A circuit breaker trips on the fleet. Cab and trailer cars were outfitted with data recorders to monitor power quality of the low-voltage power supplies (LVPS) for static and dynamic testing. Circuit breaker sizing and timing curves were reviewed. When Comet II and Comet V coaches were coupled together, there were reports that all LVPS circuit breakers were tripped. The Comet II LVPS was a constant voltage charger with a current limit of 90A. Between 80-90A, the current tapered down to control the power consumption. The Comet V was a constant current type charger with two distinct outputs (car load and car battery). The current limit to the battery of 20A. During the winter season, the corresponding LVPS output voltage to provide a dead battery 20A was very low relative to the Comet II cars. This caused a car load imbalance due to the car battery trainline and the higher voltage on adjacent cars. The Comet II cars provided power to the Comet V car loads, which caused the 10A circuit breakers to trip. Once the Comet II circuit breakers tripped, the Comet V LVPS units would supply the car loads to the Comet II cars (Comet II LVPS is now offline) until the circuit breakers tripped. Two actions were required to remedy the problem. The Comet II and Comet V circuit breakers were changed out from 10A to 15A. The 10A circuit breakers tripped because the main 480VAC power supply from the locomotive is an inverter-based supply and the harmonic distortion (<5%) was enough to raise the current on one line to the point of tripping. The Comet V LVPS units required a software modification to raise the battery charging current from 20A to 50A to raise the cold-start voltage output to reduce the car load sharing between the two vehicle types.
