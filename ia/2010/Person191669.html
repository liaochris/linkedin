  • Materials Engineer

    GE Global Research

    (Public Company; GE; Research industry)

    December 2007 — March 2009 (1 year 4 months)

    Utilized nanofabrication approach to generate nanostructures for more efficient lighting and solar applications. Examined encapsulation methods for CdTe solar cells. Built, tested, and screened sodium-metal-halide battery cells for hybrid locomotive program. Produced low-impurity molten salt electrolyte for optimal charge transport using various chemical processes.

    Nanotechnology and Optoelectronic Devices (Lighting and Solar)
    • Designed and assessed tests for encapsulation materials for CdTe solar cells; utilized calcium test and permeability tests to determine moisture-resistant behavior of various sealants.
    • Analyzed and correlated spectroscopic data with dimensions of nanostructures; manipulated etching parameters and created nanostructures with optimal dimensions, improving emission by 20%.
    • Implemented experimental design approach to assess multiple factors in dipcoating system with various substrates. Identified 2 factors and 1 interaction that significantly effected quality of coating for solar and lighting applications. Results presented as part of Six Sigma Green Belt certification.
    • Created 2 technical documents for fabrication of light-manipulating nanostructures and dipcoating optimization based on prior experimentation. Documents used as nanofabrication manuals for other research center programs.

    Batteries
    • Performed electrical characterization of battery cells; screened chemistries based on electrical performance and selected those that met fuel savings requirement, ensuring that program resources would be used for high potential cell chemistry options.

  • Materials and Processes Engineer

    Lockheed Martin Space Systems Company

    (Public Company; LMT; Defense & Space industry)

    October 2006 — November 2007 (1 year 2 months)

    Created and executed test plans to ensure structural integrity of solid-state welds and composites, and for accuracy of nondestructive evaluation (NDE) methods on Crew Exploration Vehicle (CEV). Designed solutions for electromagnetic interference (EMI) and electromagnetic capability (EMC) issues of CEV structure. Interacted with vendors for procurement of materials and forming of various components.

    • Led friction plug weld (FPW) development by examining aluminum-lithium alloy plug properties and implemented experimental design approach for process optimization which increased pass rate of welds
    • Conducted trade study to decide between conventional friction stir welding and self-reacting friction stir welding as the primary joining method; determined cost and schedule impacts by examining equipment, personnel, and process lead times required to execute each method. Study resulted in $1M award from NASA for phased-array ultrasonic testing development.
    • Devised designs to protect the CEV’s structure and electronic components from direct lightning strikes and EMI. Contributed to presentation for electrical bonding and corrosion evaluation that won $1.8M award from NASA for necessary testing.

  • Research Assistant

    Center for Materials Research, Norfolk State University

    (Semiconductors industry)

    June 2004 — August 2004 (3 months)

    Thin Film Deposition:

    Pulsed Laser Deposition (PLD) and characterization of colossal magnetoresistive (CMR) oxides on Si such as the LBMO/STO/Si system and other films, e.g., Y2O3:Eu3+ and Lu2O3:Eu3+ on sapphire and Si substrates.

    • Determined that the LBMO/STO/Si system exhibits epitaxial growth, and displays remarkable magnetic hysteresis, ferromagnetic transition, and resonance at and above room temperature; results showed that the system would be useful for magnetic memory and sensor applications.
    • Concluded that the Y2O3:Eu3+ and Lu2O3:Eu3+ films exhibit superior spectroscopic performance on sapphire due to the high quality and epitaxial growth of the film. Films shown to be useful for gate dielectric and X-ray imaging applications.

  • Research Assistant

    Thomas Jefferson National Accelerator Facility (Jefferson Lab)

    (Government Agency; Research industry)

    June 2003 — August 2003 (3 months)

    Performed topographical analysis of GaN thin films using AFM and optical microscopy.

    • Confirmed that an etching treatment with aqua regia to remove metal contacts does not damage the thin film surface.

Som Mohanty’s Education

  • Virginia Polytechnic Institute and State University

    M.S. , Materials Science and Engineering , 2004 — 2005

    Performed and analyzed molecular dynamics (MD) simulations to examine grain growth and grain boundary behavior in nanocrystalline nickel. Research group used results in subsequent projects to better understand nanocrystalline nickel behavior.

    • Applied tensile stress to samples and determined that the tensile deformation mechanisms in nanocrystalline nickel with grain size of 4 nm are grain boundary sliding and stress-induced grain rotation.
    • Performed thermal annealing on samples at various temperatures and determined that grain growth follows a linear growth law.
    • Utilized simulations to prove that thermal grain growth is facilitated by grain rotation-coalescence.
