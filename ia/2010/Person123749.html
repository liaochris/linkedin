  • Manufacturing Engineering Internship

    Cherry Aerospace

    (Higher Education industry)

    May 2009 — August 2009 (4 months)

    In the Maxibolt focus factory I worked on a time study project in the band anneal operation. My report suggested changing the piece rate targets for their log boards. From my analysis, I realized that the operators were expected to produce more pieces per hour then was sometimes physically possible, and often their inability to hit the targets was caused by factors outside of their control. For example, operators were expected to produce 1,250 parts per minute, but they would often get poor quality parts and spend all their time trying to set up their machine. In the end, they might only be able to produce 950 pieces per minute on average, and thus have to record all of their pieces produced in red on their production chart. I realized that on top of being demoralizing for the employees who felt like they were under performing, the engineers weren’t able to distinguish real manufacturing problems from external variables. To solve the problem, I was able to make and implement a grid of time targets based on three variables – lot size, run rate, and part type using a series of regression of historical records from the company database.

    Another major accomplishment was initiating a new production scheduling system for the Maxibolt area. Prior to my revamp of the system, engineers and production coordinators would spend upwards of 5 hours a week choosing which orders would go onto which machines. Often we would have over 150 orders waiting to go on 13 different machines, and the system of manually assigning them was tedious and inefficient. By utilizing my existing excel skills and learning how to use macros on the job, I was able to make a spreadsheet to organize every order on each machine in their optimal arrangement. I helped find lost orders in the factory, and decreased production set up time while working to eliminate past due orders. By implementing the new system I was able to reduce major setups by 15% for an approximate savings of $93,000 for the company.

  • Case Competition

    KPMG

    (Partnership; Management Consulting industry)

    April 2009 — April 2009 (1 month)

    The background of the project was that we were consulting for Ringtone Communications to come up with solutions for their business. The project was left intentionally vague to spur our creativity in applying the concepts we had learned. In order to differentiate our project from other groups and provide a more concrete argument, I added a statistical dimension to the project using knowledge from my 310 Statistics class and a financial dimension using knowledge from the 421 Financial Valuations and Analysis class. By using the methods from these classes, I was able to illustrate the 250B ideas we wanted to implement with concrete numbers. I used my statistical background to project data about the cell phone market for the future five years and from those numbers create a master budget. For example, one of our ideas was to use withheld shareholder dividends to invest in Research and Development. To illustrate the concept concretely to the KPMG executives, I produced a diagram consisting of possible outcome and their respective probabilities. Using the financial concept of an internal rate of return, I was able to create a model of the revenue benefit of investing in research projects compared to the opportunity cost of cutting dividends.

    By combining my multidiscipline analysis with the strengths of the other group members, we were ultimately able to win the case competition. The experience taught me the significance of pooling resources, knowledge from the various Marshall Business School departments, to the skills of every individual.

  • CEO / Book Business Manager

    Self Employed

    (Higher Education industry)

    June 2006 — August 2007 (1 year 3 months)

    When my high school bookstore was taken over by a private textbook enterprise, Ike Pono, my textbook prices rose over 500 dollars. Tired of hearing my friends complain about the prices and angered about the unjust increase, I decided to start my own textbook business called “Leland’s Honest Books.”

    The philosophy of Leland’s Honest Books was simple; buy books back from students for a higher price the Ike Pono, and sell students their next year’s books at a discount while offering better customer service and convenience. The undertaking grew beyond my imagination, and my house became a warehouse with over 90 stacks of books piled shoulder high while my email box was flooded with customer inquires and orders. Time management became essential as the venture became a full time job I had to balance with my part time TA position and summer courses.

    Reflecting on my experience, I realize now that I was providing myself a crash course in marketing, sales, branding, cash flow management, inventory turnover, inventory management, market trending, pricing strategy, and competitor analysis. From my first to second year, I was able to improve in all of these levels of understanding the business. Through constant expenses of 6,000 a month, I was able to carve out a unique niche, eventually allowing my profits to double from 4,000 to 8,000 dollars from my first to second year of operation.
