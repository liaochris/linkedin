  • Assistant Scientist

    DLR

    (Government Agency; Aviation & Aerospace industry)

    August 2000 — September 2000 (2 months)

    In my second internship at he DLR I developed a system for automatically adjusting the amperages of 10x10 VCSEL laser arrays. It consisted of a mainboard with 14 expansion slots for circuit boards that could each precisely control the amperages for 8 lasers.

    My work consisted of planning the entire system, board design and manual assembly of a prototype board. I also created an electronic version of the board design to be used to manufacture the circuit boards with milling machines.

    Before the internship ended I received the 14 circuit boards and the mainboard and assembled the system. I verified that the hardware worked as designed and started developing the necessary control software in LabVIEW. Before I left he DLR I handed my work over to a colleague who continued the software work.

  • Assistant Scientist

    German Aerospace Center (DLR)

    (Government Agency; Aviation & Aerospace industry)

    February 2000 — March 2000 (2 months)

    I worked in a group that researched the coherent coupling of vertical-cavity surface-emitting laser arrays (VCSEL). The goal was to couple multiple VCSELs coherently, so that the resulting laser beam had the maximum power density. A system to measure the power density to evaluate the quality of the coupling had to be developed.

    During my internship I evaluated and finally purchased high quality microstepping motors. I implemented software for controlling these motors using National Instruments LabVIEW. The goal of the program was to perform a precise scan of the laser beam. It automatically determined the measurable area of the beam and performed the scan automatically, while dynamically adjusting the step size in order to get higher resolutions in "interesting areas". As a result the program generated a 3D profile of the scanned laser beam, which had a significantly higher dynamic range compared to results produced by previous approaches.

  • Intern

    Stadt Olsberg

    (Government Administration industry)

    April 1999 — September 1999 (6 months)

    While still in high school, I developed an election results presentation
    program for the Kommunalwahlen 1999 in Olsberg, Germany. The software was used on the election day at the official event in the town-hall, presenting results to hundreds of people.

    The program ran on 7 different machines in a network. Two projectors presented the results; one showed presentations with charts, visual and sound effects for each electoral district, the other one displayed tables summarizing all results in real time.
    I could control the entire system, such as scheduling the different presentations, from my PC.

    On three other computers the audience could use an interactive map to watch presentations from the different districts again. Links for the districts appeared on the map as soon as the corresponding results were available.

    The biggest challenge for me was to develop this network-based system on two computers at home without having any network programming experience.
